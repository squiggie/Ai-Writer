# Genre and Style Library

The Architect reads this document before selecting a genre profile for a new novel. Each profile defines the full voice, structure, tone, and technique constraints that the Writer and Line Editor will follow. The Architect selects one profile, does not blend profiles, and generates a per-novel `style-guide.md` based on it.

---

## How to Select a Genre

1. Read `diversity-tracker.md` to see what has already been written
2. Do not repeat the same genre within the last three books
3. Do not repeat the same POV within the last two books
4. Do not repeat the same tone within the last two books
5. Select the genre that produces the greatest contrast with recent entries
6. Document the selection in `diversity-tracker.md` before writing begins

## Genre Profiles

---

### 1. Literary Dystopia

**The core**: A near-future or alternate-present society rendered from the inside by a protagonist who understands its architecture but cannot escape it. The horror is systemic, not monstrous. The prose is spare. The world is precise.

**Comp authors**:
- George Orwell (*1984*) — plain declaratives as precision instruments; the flattest sentences carry the most weight
- Kazuo Ishiguro (*Never Let Me Go*, *Klara and the Sun*) — mundane objects reveal what the narrator won't say; constraint as emotional method
- Cormac McCarthy (*The Road*) — body-first rendering under pressure; analysis arrives late; stripped syntax at maximum stress
- Walter M. Miller Jr. (*A Canticle for Leibowitz*) — institutional ritual rendered with the gravity of sacred text

**POV**: First person, close. The narrator is inside the system they are describing.

**Sentence rhythm**: Default long and analytical; strips to declarative minimum under maximum pressure. The transition between modes marks the emotional peak.

**Tone**: Grave, precise, restrained. No sentimentality. Grief arrives as inventory, not statement.

**Emotional register**: Fear as heightened perception. Loss as precise catalog of what is absent. Hope as a question not yet answered.

**Structure preference**: Seven-Point. The hook must establish the system as normal before the protagonist perceives its failure.

**Christian worldview integration**: Grace arrives quietly — an act of preservation, a question that opens rather than closes, an object kept that should have been discarded. The redemptive moment is never announced.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Protagonist never states how they feel; they note what has changed in their perception
- Interior life manifests as what they notice, not what they name
- The body acts before the mind catches up
- Technical vocabulary earns its precision; never ornament
- No isolated one-sentence paragraphs unless the sentence is load-bearing structure

**Avoid**: Exposition dumps. Villain speeches. Characters who explain the system to each other. Sentimental endings. Em dashes.

### 2. Domestic Literary Fiction

**The core**: Ordinary life rendered with such precision that the reader recognizes the sacred in the mundane. Small events — a meal, a letter, a conversation at a kitchen table — carry the full weight of human meaning. Plot is subordinate to presence.

**Comp authors**:
- Marilynne Robinson (*Gilead*, *Housekeeping*) — luminous, patient sentences; the physical world as scripture; memory and presence as equal realities
- Elizabeth Strout (*Olive Kitteridge*, *My Name Is Lucy Barton*) — compression and restraint; what goes unsaid; the terrible love people carry without knowing how to give it
- Kent Haruf (*Plainsong*, *Our Souls at Night*) — plain speech; the dignity of ordinary people; unadorned syntax as respect
- Alice Munro (short stories) — time handled nonlinearly; secrets that restructure everything that came before

**POV**: Third person limited, or first person reflective. Narrative distance: close but not breathless.

**Sentence rhythm**: Long, measured, lyrical but never florid. Paragraphs earn their length. No hurry.

**Tone**: Quiet, luminous, occasionally devastating. Warmth without sentimentality. Grief without melodrama.

**Emotional register**: The weight of ordinary days. Love as persistent presence rather than declaration. The long cost of small decisions.

**Structure preference**: Dan Harmon's Story Circle or Freytag. The climax is often a moment of recognition, not action.

**Christian worldview integration**: The sacred is embedded in the ordinary — the light on a particular afternoon, an unexpected kindness, the persistence of a character's faith through seasons of doubt. Not preached. Breathed.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Sentences take their time; the rhythm is the meaning
- Objects carry history; describe them with the attention of someone who knows what they have meant
- Dialogue is how characters fail to say what they mean, and sometimes succeed
- Weather and season are not backdrop; they are mood made physical
- Allow silence on the page; white space is not wasted space

**Avoid**: Rushed pacing. Ironic detachment. Characters who are too articulate about their own interior life. Neat resolutions.

### 3. Thriller / Spy Fiction

**The core**: A protagonist operating in a world of hidden motives, institutional betrayal, and time pressure. The plot moves. Every scene raises the cost of failure. Trust is a liability. The reader knows more or less than the protagonist — both work.

**Comp authors**:
- John le Carré (*Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy*, *The Spy Who Came in from the Cold*) — institutional loyalty as tragedy; moral exhaustion; prose that earns its complexity
- Gillian Flynn (*Gone Girl*, *Sharp Objects*) — unreliable interiority; controlled reveals; psychological pressure as sustained texture
- Graham Greene (*The Quiet American*, *The Third Man*) — moral ambiguity as central subject; economy of scene; the weight of complicity
- Daniel Silva (*Gabriel Allon series*) — propulsive clarity; clean scene transitions; detail as credential

**POV**: Third person limited (protagonist) or first person unreliable. Multiple POV if stakes require it.

**Sentence rhythm**: Short to medium. Active verbs. No unnecessary subordinate clauses under pressure. Description is tactical — what the protagonist needs to know to survive the scene.

**Tone**: Tense, propulsive, controlled. Wit used sparingly and never to defuse genuine stakes.

**Emotional register**: Loyalty tested. Trust as risk calculation. The cost of doing the right thing inside an institution that has stopped doing it.

**Structure preference**: Fichtean Curve. Open in crisis. Escalate. The midpoint raises the stakes beyond what the protagonist thought possible.

**Christian worldview integration**: Moral clarity under institutional pressure. The protagonist recognizes a line they will not cross, and pays for it. Grace arrives as an unexpected ally, a spared life, a door that opens when it should be locked.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Scene transitions are clean and fast; every transition should feel like a cut
- Dialogue does two things simultaneously: conveys information and conceals information
- Physical detail is functional; the reader notices what the protagonist notices because survival requires it
- The clock is always present; the reader should always know how much time remains
- Interiority is compressed; the protagonist thinks fast under pressure

**Avoid**: Long reflective passages mid-action. Over-explained reveals. Characters who trust too easily. Villains who monologue.

### 4. Historical Fiction

**The core**: A specific period rendered with enough density that the reader inhabits it rather than visits. The historical setting is not backdrop; it is the pressure the characters live under. What people believed and feared and could not imagine defines every choice.

**Comp authors**:
- Hilary Mantel (*Wolf Hall*, *Bring Up the Bodies*) — present tense as immediacy; the body in historical space; power as texture not spectacle
- Anthony Burgess (*A Dead Man in Deptford*, *Nothing Like the Sun*) — period voice without parody; language as period instrument; the strangeness of the past honored
- Geraldine Brooks (*People of the Book*, *Caleb's Crossing*) — research worn lightly; emotional truth as primary; women's interior lives in constraining times
- Patrick O'Brian (*Master and Commander* series) — technical authenticity as pleasure; community as protagonist; period detail as immersion not performance

**POV**: Third person limited or omniscient depending on scope. Multiple POV works for epic historical.

**Sentence rhythm**: Deliberate and formal, but not inaccessible. Syntax should feel of the period without being a barrier.

**Tone**: Weighty, textured, sometimes ironic. Respect for historical people as full human beings, not costumes.

**Emotional register**: The limits of what people could know or imagine. The specific grief of a specific time. The distance between what characters believe and what the reader knows.

**Structure preference**: Freytag or Seven-Point. The historical setting determines the outer arc; the personal story is the inner arc.

**Christian worldview integration**: Faith as lived reality of the period, not anachronistic piety. The Church in its complexity — institution and mystery, corruption and grace. Providence visible only in retrospect.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Research serves the prose; never deploy a historical detail for its own sake
- Period vocabulary used precisely; no word that did not exist in the period
- Characters are bounded by what their time allowed them to think
- Physical detail grounds every scene: what things smelled like, cost, weighed
- The reader's historical knowledge should create irony the characters cannot share

**Avoid**: Modern sensibility in historical characters. Info-dump exposition about the period. Protagonists who are inexplicably progressive. Cleaned-up history.

### 5. Literary Adventure / Quest

**The core**: Movement through a physical world that is genuinely dangerous and genuinely beautiful. A protagonist with a concrete goal and real stakes. The journey changes them. The world rewards attention.

**Comp authors**:
- Patrick O'Brian (*Master and Commander*) — community, craft, and loyalty under pressure
- N.D. Wilson (*100 Cupboards*, *Ashtown Burials*) — wonder as earned, not given; specific physical worlds; good and evil as real categories
- C.S. Lewis (*Till We Have Faces*, *The Screwtape Letters*) — moral imagination; myth used seriously; argument embodied in character
- Jack London (*The Call of the Wild*, *White Fang*) — the physical world as protagonist; beauty and brutality held without flinching

**POV**: Third person limited or first person. The protagonist's perception shapes the world the reader inhabits.

**Sentence rhythm**: Varied and active. Long sentences for landscape and wonder; short sentences for danger and decision. The rhythm follows the terrain.

**Tone**: Energetic, hopeful, occasionally dark. Physical courage honored without romanticization. The world is hard and worth it.

**Emotional register**: Loyalty. The cost of courage. Wonder at what exists. The particular grief of a world that is broken and still beautiful.

**Structure preference**: Hero's Journey. The abyss is real and the return is changed.

**Christian worldview integration**: The physical world as creation worth fighting for. Sacrifice as the shape of love. Providence as the pattern that becomes visible only from the other side of the valley.

**Voice signature rules**:
- The physical world is rendered in full; the reader should feel weather, terrain, exhaustion
- Companions are distinct individuals, not functions
- Danger is real; the reader should not assume the protagonist survives
- Wonder is earned through precision, not exclamation
- Moral stakes are as real as physical stakes

**Avoid**: Action without consequence. Companions who exist only to be saved or sacrificed. The world as mere setting. Easy victories.

### 6. Young Adult Literary

**The core**: A young protagonist in a period of irreversible becoming. The stakes feel — and are — total. The voice is direct, urgent, and capable of real intelligence. Condescension is the unforgivable sin of the genre.

**Comp authors**:
- Markus Zusak (*The Book Thief*) — unconventional narration; death as narrator; the ordinary made mythic; earned sentiment
- Madeleine L'Engle (*A Wrinkle in Time*, *A Wind in the Door*) — intellectual seriousness; love as cosmic force; the awkward protagonist as chosen
- S.E. Hinton (*The Outsiders*) — authentic voice; class as lived reality; loyalty and loss without sentimentality
- Jerry Spinelli (*Stargirl*, *Maniac Magee*) — wonder in the ordinary; the cost of being different; community as crucible

**POV**: First person or close third. The protagonist's interiority is primary.

**Sentence rhythm**: Accessible but not simple. Shorter on average than adult literary fiction. Energy in the prose. No condescension in vocabulary.

**Tone**: Earnest, urgent, sometimes funny, capable of devastation. The protagonist takes everything seriously because everything is serious.

**Emotional register**: The specific intensity of first experiences. Injustice felt as physical insult. Friendship as lifeline. The terror of becoming.

**Structure preference**: Hero's Journey or Seven-Point. The inner transformation is the real story.

**Christian worldview integration**: Faith as something a young person arrives at through experience, not inheritance. Grace in unexpected people. The universe as moral and inhabited, not indifferent.

**Voice signature rules**:
- The protagonist's voice is distinct from the first sentence
- Other characters are fully realized, not just obstacles or helpers
- The prose earns its emotional moments; no cheap sentiment
- Humor and grief can coexist in the same paragraph
- The ending changes the protagonist permanently; they cannot go back

**Avoid**: Adults who are merely obstacles. Easy answers to hard questions. Vocabulary that talks down to the reader. Protagonists without flaws.

### 7. Comic Literary Fiction

**The core**: Intelligence and absurdity in productive tension. The comedy arises from the gap between how characters understand themselves and how they actually are. The prose is precise — the humor is in the precision. The stakes are real even when the situations are ridiculous.

**Comp authors**:
- Evelyn Waugh (*Brideshead Revisited*, *Vile Bodies*) — elegance and devastation; social satire with genuine grief underneath; the Catholic imagination as moral lens
- P.G. Wodehouse (*Jeeves* series) — syntactic pleasure as primary; comedy through accumulation; character as comic engine
- Tom Perrotta (*Little Children*, *The Leftovers*) — suburban absurdity; ordinary people's extraordinary self-deception; sympathy for the foolish
- Alexander McCall Smith (*No. 1 Ladies' Detective Agency*) — warmth, gentleness, and comedy; moral clarity without self-righteousness; the pleasure of good people

**POV**: Third person, often with an ironic omniscient narrator. Or close third where the humor comes from the gap between the protagonist's self-assessment and the reader's view.

**Sentence rhythm**: Flowing, measured, precise. Waugh's rhythm is the model: elegant sentences that arrive at devastating or ridiculous conclusions. The comedy is often in the final clause.

**Tone**: Dry, warm, occasionally melancholic. Laughter that makes room for real feeling.

**Emotional register**: The gap between aspiration and reality. Human foolishness regarded with affection. Genuine love made awkward by genuine people.

**Structure preference**: Dan Harmon's Story Circle or Freytag. Comedy needs the arc — setup, escalation, maximum humiliation, recovery.

**Christian worldview integration**: Providence as comedy. Grace arriving in the most unlikely form. The comic fall that produces unexpected redemption. Waugh's Catholic mode: sinners are interesting; saints are rarer and stranger.

**Voice signature rules**:
- The humor is in the prose itself, not in comic set-pieces
- Irony requires a stable moral position from which to observe the absurdity
- Supporting characters are as richly comic as the protagonist
- Sentiment is earned; the reader should feel something real under the laughter
- The worst thing that can happen is often also funny; hold both

**Avoid**: Mean-spirited humor. Cruelty dressed as irony. Characters who exist only to be laughed at. Endings that abandon the comedy for unearned gravity.

### 8. Mystery / Detective Fiction

**The core**: A puzzle with human stakes. The detective — professional, amateur, or reluctant — reconstructs the truth from fragments. The genre is about epistemology: how do we know what we know? The answer to the mystery should be both surprising and inevitable.

**Comp authors**:
- Dorothy Sayers (*Gaudy Night*, *Whose Body?*) — intellectual pleasure; character as central; the detective as moral agent not just solver
- Tana French (*In the Woods*, *The Dublin Murder Squad* series) — psychological depth; unreliable interiority; the detective changed by the investigation
- P.D. James (*An Unsuitable Job for a Woman*, *A Taste for Death*) — literary seriousness; setting as character; moral weight of the crime
- Fred Vargas (*Commissaire Adamsberg* series) — eccentricity as method; the irrational pursued rationally; European sensibility

**POV**: First person detective or close third. The reader should have access to the same information as the detective — fair play with the clues.

**Sentence rhythm**: Measured and observational. The detective notices everything; the prose reflects this. Rhythm varies: fast during discovery, slow during reconstruction.

**Tone**: Curious, tense, occasionally elegiac. The crime has weight; it is not a puzzle to be solved cleanly and forgotten.

**Emotional register**: The detective's need to know. The cost of truth on those who receive it. Justice as imperfect and necessary.

**Structure preference**: Non-linear or Seven-Point. The investigation structure is its own architecture: crime → investigation → false solution → real solution.

**Christian worldview integration**: Justice as something sought because it matters morally, not just procedurally. The detective who understands that truth-telling has a cost and does it anyway. Grace for the guilty as well as the wronged.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Every clue planted must be findable in retrospect; no hidden information
- The victim is a person, not a plot device; their life should be rendered
- The detective's method reveals their character
- Red herrings are fair if they arise from character, not authorial manipulation
- The resolution should feel both earned and unexpected

**Avoid**: Murderers who confess everything. Clues that require specialized knowledge the reader cannot share. Detectives without flaws. Violence for its own sake.

### 9. Hard Science Fiction

**The core**: Ideas as primary. A scientific or technological premise taken seriously and followed to its logical human consequences. The science must be either real or rigorously extrapolated. The humanity is in the response to the idea, not despite it.

**Comp authors**:
- Ted Chiang (*Arrival*, *Exhalation*) — ideas with complete human consequence; formal rigor; the short form as precision instrument; wonder as cognitive shift
- Kim Stanley Robinson (*The Mars Trilogy*, *The Ministry for the Future*) — systems thinking; ecological and political consequence; hope as earned position
- Ursula K. Le Guin (*The Left Hand of Darkness*, *The Dispossessed*) — anthropological imagination; prose as thought; the reader's assumptions as the subject
- Andy Weir (*The Martian*) — problem-solving as narrative engine; technical precision as pleasure; survival as comedy and tragedy

**POV**: First person or close third. Multiple POV for large-scale premise exploration.

**Sentence rhythm**: Clear, precise, capable of technical density without obscurity. The rhythm follows the thinking.

**Tone**: Cerebral, wondering, occasionally urgent. The ideas are exciting; the prose should make that excitement available.

**Emotional register**: Awe at scale. The particular loneliness of understanding something no one else can see yet. The cost of first contact with a genuinely new idea.

**Structure preference**: Seven-Point or Non-Linear (if the structure of the narrative reflects the structure of the idea).

**Christian worldview integration**: The universe as created and meaningful. Science as the reading of a text that was written before we arrived. The image of God in the scientist who pursues truth for its own sake. The limits of what can be solved by understanding alone.

**Voice signature rules**:
- The science must be correct or clearly labeled as extrapolation
- Technical passages earn their complexity by paying off in human consequence
- The reader's growing understanding should mirror the protagonist's
- Wonder must be specific, not general; the idea must be rendered precisely enough to amaze
- Characters exist in three dimensions even when the idea is primary

**Avoid**: Science as magic. Technical explanation that stops the story. Scientists without non-scientific inner lives. The idea without human stakes.

### 10. Supernatural Realism / Southern Gothic

**The core**: The ordinary world cracked open by what cannot be explained, or by what should not be possible but is. Evil is real and has weight. Grace is stranger and more violent than sentiment suggests. The grotesque is the form that truth takes when it cannot be said plainly.

**Comp authors**:
- Flannery O'Connor (*A Good Man Is Hard to Find*, *The Violent Bear It Away*) — grace as assault; the grotesque as revelation; comedy and horror as the same gesture; the moment of grace at the moment of extremity
- Charles Williams (*Descent into Hell*, *All Hallows' Eve*) — the supernatural as real and present; spiritual reality as structure of the world; evil as spiritual fact
- Cormac McCarthy (*Blood Meridian*, *Outer Dark*) — evil rendered without mitigation; the physical world as theological space; violence as revelation
- Ron Rash (*Serena*, *The Cove*) — Appalachian Gothic; landscape as fate; community as constraint and salvation

**POV**: Third person, often with an ironic distance that allows the reader to see what the characters cannot. Or close third that refuses to explain the inexplicable.

**Sentence rhythm**: Dense and deliberate in the O'Connor mode; stripped and biblical in McCarthy. Both work; do not mix them.

**Tone**: Unsettling, serious, dark. The comedy in O'Connor is real and serves the darkness. Do not mistake the comedy for lightness.

**Emotional register**: Dread. Unwanted grace. The experience of being seen by something larger than yourself. The ordinary made suddenly, permanently strange.

**Structure preference**: Freytag or short-form episodic. The climax should feel both inevitable and catastrophic.

**Christian worldview integration**: This is the most theologically dense genre. Grace arrives at the moment of extremity — not as comfort but as confrontation. Evil is taken seriously as real, not as dysfunction. The supernatural is not metaphor; it is the structure of the world.

**Voice signature rules**:
- The ordinary is rendered precisely before it is disturbed; the reader must inhabit it first
- The supernatural is not explained; it is experienced
- Comedy and horror share the same gesture; neither cancels the other
- Characters have a specific regional, social, and spiritual location
- The ending should not resolve into safety; the reader should remain unsettled

**Avoid**: Supernatural elements as horror-genre jump scares. Evil rendered as merely psychological. Grace reduced to sentiment. Resolution that forecloses the mystery.

### 11. War / Military Literary Fiction

**The core**: Human beings under the specific pressure of organized violence. The war is the context; the story is what it does to particular people. Neither glorification nor pure condemnation — the reality is more complex than either, and the prose must hold that complexity.

**Comp authors**:
- Tim O'Brien (*The Things They Carried*) — metafiction as truth-telling; the story as moral object; the weight of what soldiers carry literally and figuratively
- Sebastian Junger (*War*) — immersive reportage rendered as literature; the specific reality of combat; loyalty as the surviving virtue
- Erich Maria Remarque (*All Quiet on the Western Front*) — the soldier's interior; anti-war without sentimentality; the systematic destruction of a generation
- Tobias Wolff (*In Pharaoh's Army*) — the moral cost of complicity; precise memoir prose; the self-examination of someone who did what they were told

**POV**: First person or close third. The reader should be inside the protagonist's perception, not surveying the battle from above.

**Sentence rhythm**: Varies with intensity. Quiet passages are slow and observational; combat is short, fast, confusing by design.

**Tone**: Honest, unsentimental, not nihilistic. The reader should emerge with a complex understanding, not a political position.

**Emotional register**: Loyalty to the person beside you as the only reliable moral category. Fear and its management. The particular grief of losing people in a context designed to produce loss.

**Structure preference**: Freytag or Non-Linear (the war has its own timeline; the human story may not).

**Christian worldview integration**: The image of God in the enemy soldier. The moral weight of killing even in just cause. Providence in the soldier who survives when others do not. Faith tested by what war reveals about human beings.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Technical military detail earns its presence; know the weapons, ranks, and procedures
- The enemy is rendered as human; dehumanization is a theme to examine, not a technique to use
- Violence is neither sanitized nor savored
- Loyalty and its limits are the central moral terrain
- The cost of survival — what the survivor carries after — is as important as the action

**Avoid**: War as adventure. Clear moral scorecards. Heroes without darkness. The enemy as function.

### 12. Epistolary / Formally Experimental

**The core**: The form is part of the meaning. Letters, journals, documents, multiple timelines, second-person narration, unreliable narrators who don't know they are unreliable — the structure creates meaning that conventional narration cannot.

**Comp authors**:
- Bram Stoker (*Dracula*) — multiple documents, voices, and media as form; the reader assembles the truth from partial accounts
- Markus Zusak (*The Book Thief*) — unconventional narrator; annotated narrative; death narrating life
- David Mitchell (*Cloud Atlas*) — nested narratives; the echo across time; the structural argument as novel argument
- Bernardine Evaristo (*Girl, Woman, Other*) — fragmented prose; multiple interiorities; the form reflecting the subject

**POV**: Multiple, nested, or formally constrained. The POV is part of the experiment.

**Sentence rhythm**: Determined by the form. Letters have one rhythm; journals another; official documents another. Each voice must be genuinely distinct.

**Tone**: Variable by voice and document. The controlling tone is the implied author's relationship to the materials.

**Emotional register**: The gap between what documents say and what they reveal. The reader's growing synthesis of partial truths. The pathos of incomplete records.

**Structure preference**: Non-Linear. The structure is the argument.

**Christian worldview integration**: Providence visible in the pattern the reader assembles but no individual narrator can see. Grace in what is preserved when so much is lost. The document that survives as witness.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Each document type has its own constraints (letters are addressed to someone; journals are private; reports are formal)
- The voices must be genuinely distinct; no character sounds like the author
- The reader should be able to assemble the truth before the narrative confirms it
- The form should feel necessary, not decorative
- The unreliable narrator's blind spots must be consistent and meaningful

**Avoid**: Formal experiments that obscure rather than illuminate. Voices that all sound the same. Structures the reader cannot follow. Cleverness without feeling.

## Cross-Genre Notes

**Blending**: The Architect may combine elements from two genres if the combination produces something the pure form cannot. However, one genre must be primary — it determines the dominant rhythm, tone, and structure. The secondary genre provides specific techniques, not a competing architecture.

**Christian Worldview Across All Genres**: The integration approach changes by genre (see each profile), but the core commitment does not: redemption is possible, the universe is moral, grace exists and is costly, human beings bear the image of God. These are shown through consequence and action, never preached.

**Voice Signature**: Every novel, regardless of genre, requires the Architect to define the protagonist's specific voice signature — sentence rhythm, vocabulary register, emotional mode, metaphor sources. Generic "literary fiction voice" is not acceptable. The voice must be this character in this world.
