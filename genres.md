# Genre and Style Library

The Architect reads this document before selecting a genre profile for a new novel. Each profile defines the full voice, structure, tone, and technique constraints that the Writer and Line Editor will follow. The Architect selects one profile, does not blend profiles, and generates a per-novel `style-guide.md` based on it.

Only the profiles in this file are approved for automatic generation. Every new novel must fall under one of these categories:
- Sci-Fi
- Fantasy
- Thriller
- Military SF / Space

---

## How to Select a Genre

1. Read `diversity-tracker.md` to see what has already been written
2. Select only from the approved profiles below
3. Do not repeat the same genre profile within the last three books when a different approved profile would serve the story
4. Do not repeat the same POV within the last two books
5. Do not repeat the same tone within the last two books
6. Prefer contrast in setting, protagonist type, and sentence rhythm whenever possible
7. Document the selection in `diversity-tracker.md` before writing begins

## Approved Genre Profiles

---

### 1. Literary Dystopia / Social Science Fiction

**Category**: Sci-Fi

**The core**: A near-future or alternate-present society rendered from the inside by a protagonist who understands part of its architecture but not all of it. The horror is systemic, not monstrous. The prose is spare, exact, and morally awake.

**Comp authors**:
- George Orwell (*1984*) — plain declaratives as pressure instruments; institutional evil rendered without ornament
- Kazuo Ishiguro (*Never Let Me Go*, *Klara and the Sun*) — emotional repression made legible through object detail and omission
- Walter M. Miller Jr. (*A Canticle for Leibowitz*) — institutions, liturgy, and endurance under civilizational pressure
- Emily St. John Mandel (*Station Eleven*) — quiet human persistence inside broken systems

**POV**: First person close or third person limited. The protagonist's understanding should be partial, earned, and costly.

**Sentence rhythm**: Analytical medium-to-long lines by default, stripping to bare declaratives under pressure. The shift itself marks fear.

**Tone**: Grave, restrained, lucid.

**Emotional register**: Fear as sharpened perception. Grief as catalog. Hope as a question kept alive.

**Structure preference**: Seven-Point or Fichtean Curve.

**Christian worldview integration**: Grace arrives quietly through preservation, mercy, repentance, or a refusal to surrender moral clarity to the system.

**Voice signature rules**:
- The narrator does not announce feelings; they reveal them through what has changed in perception
- Technical or bureaucratic language must earn its place
- Institutions are rendered concretely, not abstractly
- No speechifying about oppression; the reader must feel it in procedure, scarcity, and consequence
- Endings should open toward costly hope, not easy triumph

**Avoid**: Villain monologues. Exposition dumps. Decorative jargon. Sentimental rebellion. Em dashes.

### 2. Hard Science Fiction

**Category**: Sci-Fi

**The core**: Discovery, survival, engineering, and consequence inside a physically coherent world. The science matters because failure has measurable cost. Wonder is inseparable from constraint.

**Comp authors**:
- Andy Weir (*The Martian*, *Project Hail Mary*) — systems problem-solving under pressure; clarity of process
- Arthur C. Clarke (*Rendezvous with Rama*) — awe earned through scale, rigor, and disciplined observation
- Kim Stanley Robinson (*Mars Trilogy*) — political, ecological, and technical systems interacting over time
- Robert Charles Wilson (*Spin*) — conceptual ambition anchored in human stakes

**POV**: First person or third person limited. The protagonist notices the world as a builder, scientist, pilot, medic, or operator would.

**Sentence rhythm**: Clean, precise, medium-length lines. Under acute danger the prose shortens; during exploration it expands just enough to carry wonder.

**Tone**: Intelligent, disciplined, urgent when needed.

**Emotional register**: Competence under stress. Awe bounded by physics. Loyalty tested by material limits.

**Structure preference**: Hero's Journey, Seven-Point, or Freytag for disaster arcs.

**Christian worldview integration**: Creation is intelligible and worth stewarding. Human limits matter. Pride in mastery must yield to humility, sacrifice, and dependence.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Scientific detail must always change the scene, not decorate it
- The body is present: fatigue, pressure, weight, heat loss, hunger, acceleration
- Solutions require tradeoffs; no miracle-tech escapes
- Dialogue should sound like professionals under load, not explainers for the audience
- Wonder must arise from precision rather than adjectives

**Avoid**: Hand-waved physics. Omnipotent technology. TED-talk exposition. Snark as a substitute for courage.

### 3. Epic / High Fantasy

**Category**: Fantasy

**The core**: A secondary world with moral, political, and spiritual weight. The landscape is old, beautiful, wounded, and worth defending. The story operates at the level of kingdoms, quests, households, and oaths without losing the intimacy of character.

**Comp authors**:
- J.R.R. Tolkien (*The Lord of the Rings*) — linguistic gravity, moral seriousness, and beauty under shadow
- Guy Gavriel Kay (*The Fionavar Tapestry*, *Tigana*) — elegiac grandeur, political memory, and emotional consequence
- Tad Williams (*Memory, Sorrow, and Thorn*) — patient worldbuilding and deepening danger across large-scale arcs
- Stephen Lawhead (*The Pendragon Cycle*) — mythic sweep with Christian resonance and sacrificial stakes

**POV**: Third person limited or multiple third person limited. Multiple viewpoints must each justify their place in the large design.

**Sentence rhythm**: Elevated but readable. Longer lines for landscape, ceremony, and memory; clipped lines for battle, pursuit, and terror.

**Tone**: Noble, weathered, luminous at the edges of sorrow.

**Emotional register**: Wonder, loyalty, lament, courage, and the cost of keeping faith.

**Structure preference**: Hero's Journey or Seven-Point with epic scale.

**Christian worldview integration**: Evil is real, sacrifice is the shape of love, providence works through weakness, and creation retains goodness even under corruption.

**Voice signature rules**:
- The world must feel inhabited before it feels explained
- Customs, songs, vows, and lineages should have consequence, not flavor-only value
- Companions must be distinct persons, not party slots
- Battles turn on fear, terrain, logistics, and moral decision, not spectacle alone
- The ending should carry earned restoration or costly endurance, never a glib reset

**Avoid**: Generic medieval wallpaper. Video-game quest structure. Lore dumps. Cynical nihilism posing as maturity.

### 4. Technology Thriller

**Category**: Thriller

**The core**: High-stakes suspense driven by surveillance, networks, sabotage, institutional secrecy, and time pressure. The protagonist is trying to expose, stop, escape, or outmaneuver a technological threat before it compounds into catastrophe.

**Comp authors**:
- Daniel Silva (*Gabriel Allon* series) — propulsive scene movement and controlled intelligence tradecraft
- Michael Crichton (*Prey*, *State of Fear*) — technical threat architecture and cascading consequence
- Mark Greaney (*Gray Man* series) — kinetic action with practical procedural texture
- Joel C. Rosenberg (*The Kremlin Conspiracy*) — Christian suspense pacing with geopolitical stakes

**POV**: Third person limited or tight first person. Multiple POVs only when they sharpen suspense instead of diffusing it.

**Sentence rhythm**: Short to medium. Active verbs. Fast transitions. Every paragraph should either tighten danger or sharpen the tactical picture.

**Tone**: Tense, controlled, relentless.

**Emotional register**: Distrust, urgency, moral pressure, and the cost of acting before certainty is complete.

**Structure preference**: Fichtean Curve.

**Christian worldview integration**: Moral lines matter under pressure. The protagonist may be frightened or compromised, but must confront the question of what they will not do to win.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Scene entries are late and exits are early
- Technology is operational and risky, never magical
- Dialogue should conceal as much as it reveals
- Time pressure must remain legible throughout the novel
- Action sequences must track space, body, and decision clearly

**Avoid**: Info-dump cyber jargon. Cleverness without cost. Invincible protagonists. Revelations explained three times.

### 5. Christian Suspense Thriller

**Category**: Thriller

**The core**: A suspense novel where danger, pursuit, conspiracy, or criminal pressure forces characters into choices about truth, courage, repentance, and protection. The faith element is active in conscience and consequence, not bolted on.

**Comp authors**:
- Terri Blackstock (*If I Run*, *Intervention*) — clean suspense velocity with moral pressure
- Charles Martin (*The Letter Keeper*, *The Water Keeper*) — emotionally grounded danger and sacrificial masculine courage
- Dee Henderson (*O'Malley* series) — Christian suspense structure with operational stakes
- Creston Mapes (*Nobody*) — threat escalation and faith under direct social pressure

**POV**: Third person limited or dual POV. The distance should stay close enough for fear to remain bodily.

**Sentence rhythm**: Medium, tightening hard in pursuit or confrontation scenes. Reflective beats are brief and earned.

**Tone**: Suspenseful, sober, redemptive without softness.

**Emotional register**: Fear, guilt, conviction, costly trust, guarded hope.

**Structure preference**: Fichtean Curve or Seven-Point.

**Christian worldview integration**: Prayer, repentance, forgiveness, and moral courage belong inside the action of the plot. Faith is lived under duress, not discussed in detached speeches.

**Voice signature rules**:
- The threat must stay concrete and active
- Redemptive moments should arise from sacrifice, confession, truth-telling, or mercy
- Supporting characters need operational roles, not just spiritual commentary roles
- Romantic tension, if present, remains subordinate to the suspense engine
- Endings may bring rescue or justice, but they must also acknowledge scars and cost

**Avoid**: Sermon scenes. Sanitized danger. Easy forgiveness without consequence. Flat villains defined only by evil intent.

### 6. Military Science Fiction

**Category**: Military SF / Space

**The core**: War, readiness, command, unit cohesion, and sacrifice inside a future military or resistance structure. Tactics, logistics, and chain of command matter. The protagonist is shaped by duty and tested by what duty demands.

**Comp authors**:
- Joe Haldeman (*The Forever War*) — military experience rendered through exhaustion, estrangement, and cost
- David Weber (*Honor Harrington* series) — command pressure, fleet consequence, and operational clarity
- John Scalzi (*Old Man's War*) — soldierly immediacy and technology integrated into combat reality
- Timothy Zahn (*Cobra*, *Conquerors* trilogy) — disciplined tactical storytelling and military problem-solving

**POV**: First person or close third. Multiple POVs may be used for command-level and ground-level contrast if each adds operational value.

**Sentence rhythm**: Controlled, efficient, mission-focused. Under combat the prose gets shorter and more positional; under planning it becomes crisp and procedural.

**Tone**: Hardened, disciplined, humane under strain.

**Emotional register**: Duty, fear, loyalty, grief, endurance, and the moral burden of command.

**Structure preference**: Seven-Point or Hero's Journey.

**Christian worldview integration**: Courage is not bloodlust. Leadership carries accountability. Sacrifice, restraint, mercy, and stewardship of lives matter inside the machinery of war.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Tactics and logistics must shape plot outcomes
- Units need distinct culture, hierarchy, and interpersonal strain
- Combat has aftermath: injury, fatigue, error, grief, paperwork, replacement
- Command decisions should reveal character more than speeches do
- Victories must cost something real

**Avoid**: Power fantasy slaughter. Weightless warfare. Rank structures that exist only cosmetically. Invulnerable heroes.

### 7. Space Opera / Fleet Epic

**Category**: Military SF / Space

**The core**: Large-scale interstellar stakes with political, spiritual, and military consequence. The canvas is wide, but the reader stays anchored in personal loyalties, rivalries, vows, and decisions that can re-order worlds.

**Comp authors**:
- Lois McMaster Bujold (*Vorkosigan Saga*) — velocity, wit under pressure, and character-driven political consequence
- C.J. Cherryh (*Alliance-Union*, *Foreigner*) — dense political systems and hard-earned trust
- James S.A. Corey (*The Expanse*) — interlocking viewpoints, escalating system-wide stakes, and grounded momentum
- David Weber (*Safehold*, *Honorverse*) — fleet movement, hierarchy, and conflict at civilizational scale

**POV**: Multiple third person limited or close first person with carefully rationed alternation. Every POV must expand the scale without weakening momentum.

**Sentence rhythm**: Flexible. Fast in conflict, broader in political or strategic scenes, but always legible.

**Tone**: Grand, tense, serious, occasionally awe-struck.

**Emotional register**: Loyalty under expansion pressure. The loneliness of command. Civilization at risk. Wonder that does not erase danger.

**Structure preference**: Hero's Journey, Seven-Point, or a disciplined multi-arc epic.

**Christian worldview integration**: Power is accountable to truth. Empire, conquest, and governance are moral questions, not neutral abstractions. Hope persists through remnant faithfulness and costly leadership.

**Voice signature rules**:
- Keep the political and the personal tightly coupled
- Ships, stations, worlds, and factions must have distinct operational identity
- Strategic scenes must still carry human stakes
- Scale should increase consequence, not blur it
- The final act must feel like culmination, not sequel bait disguised as closure

**Avoid**: Lore encyclopedias. Endless faction names with no emotional anchor. Empty grandeur. World-ending stakes that do not register at the human level.
