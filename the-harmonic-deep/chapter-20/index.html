Chapter 20

Burn Window

Serran’s thumb remained over the authorization field while the extraction countdown turned over another minute in the corner of the wall. The room had gone quiet in the wrong way, not settled, not resolved, only compressed around his hand. Beside the vote pane, Enceladus rotated under us in blue-white bands and hard shadow, south-polar fractures bright against the dark. Burn lock in thirty-two minutes. Array phase stabilization in twenty-seven. Final extraction geometry commit in nineteen. The numbers moved with the indifference of orbital mechanics. We were the only unstable system in the room.

“Director,” Xu said through feed lag, “if you are going to reject shield geometry, reject it now and release propulsion to clean the current solution. If you are going to authorize the retune, I need Ekwueme and burn control inside the next three minutes.”

Serran did not answer him.

Juno’s legality pane still pulsed amber. Nadir kept her eyes on the mortality curves, perhaps because they were the only numbers in the room that still supported her. Anik had folded her hands on the table. Solis stood behind me with the same measured stillness she had carried through custody, but I could feel attention coming off her like heat from a processor bank.

“Mission command,” Juno said carefully, “the charter recognizes delay itself as a decision once burn prep crosses hard timing thresholds.”

“I know what the charter recognizes,” Serran said.

He lowered his hand. Not onto the field. Onto the table.

For one irrational second I thought he might step back from it, let time force the current geometry, and call the result procedural necessity. That would have been consistent with everything he had done since Day 1. Not a lie exactly. Only a preference translated into passive grammar until the harm appeared self-generated.

The hatch behind the wall console opened so fast it struck its stop. Everyone in the room turned except Solis, who had already half turned before the sound finished. Linh came through with two propulsion techs and a control systems officer from the array deck, her restriction band still lit red around one wrist. Her hair looked as if someone had tried to make custody look orderly and failed. There was a bruise darkening along one forearm. She carried a portable control slate under her arm and did not slow for Juno’s protest.

“You wanted engineering,” she said to the room at large. Her breathing was high but controlled. “You have it.”

“Engineer Ekwueme is under restriction,” Juno said.

“Then update the paperwork after the station survives.”

The propulsion tech beside her, Malik from burn control, put his slate on the side console without asking permission. I recognized him only because Linh had once complained he labeled his thermal buffers like a devotional practice. The control systems officer, Reyes, was younger than I expected and pale enough that the overhead light showed each fatigue line under her eyes.

“Commander Xu pulled us,” Malik said. “We have the retune envelope loaded but not committed. Burn control needs a command path in the next ninety seconds or we lose the shallow solution and the propellant curve gets ugly.”

Nadir stood. “This proceeding is not a corridor raid.”

“No,” Linh said. “It is the part where your delay becomes load.”

Serran looked at Linh’s red restriction band, then at Xu’s feed. “You overrode custody.”

Xu’s image flickered once. “I overrode engineering stupidity. If you want a shield maneuver executed without the person who built half the array compensation routines, document the choice.”

No one moved for a beat. The extraction countdown kept moving. I felt my pulse in the base of my throat and in the weak tremor that had started again in my calves. Fatigue had not left. It had only made room for a clearer kind of fear. If Serran held the room much longer, there would be no viable shield geometry to fight over.

I stepped toward the main console before Juno could decide whether I was still a detainee or an analyst. “Director. The current geometry commits in eighteen minutes. If you refuse shield configuration, say it into the record and let them clear the current burn. If you intend to preserve even the possibility of lower harm, unfreeze engineering now.”

Nadir looked at me as if I had asked her to sign away stored blood. “You continue to talk about lower harm as if the missing compounds will not produce bodies.”

“They may,” I said. “And striking the same injured corridor again will produce bodies here and continue the injury below us. Those are the terms we have.”

“You are not the only one allowed to count the dead.”

She meant Earth, and because she meant it sincerely the sentence carried. I took one breath before answering. “Then count all of them.”

Serran’s jaw tightened. It was not anger alone. Something more expensive had entered the room, the stage at which principle begins to resemble confession because the cost has become exact. He looked from the damage cascade to the mortality curves to Linh’s still-lit restriction band. Then at Solis.

“If I release Ekwueme to engineering authority,” he said, “does security maintain the rest of custody.”

Solis answered without hesitation. “Yes.”

“And if she uses that release for anything except execution.”

“Then that enters my log. Right now my log says command is withholding necessary array expertise during a live threshold decision.”

That was as near to mutiny as Solis was likely to come, which meant we had crossed further than I had realized. Serran heard it too. So did Juno, who stopped arranging tablets and simply watched.

Linh set her slate on the table and keyed the local display alive. The shield geometry rotated above the tribunal record, no longer my clean analytical model but a working control layout with actuator delays, thermal warnings, and rollback branches branching like fractures through glass.

“You do not have nineteen minutes,” she said. “You have less than six before array preload starts making the thirty-one-degree rephase unstable. After that, you can still do something, but it stops being Mara’s model and starts being triage.”

Reyes added, “And triage means asymmetric shutdown. We save some sectors, maybe, but the collector spine takes a hit.”

“Maybe?” Nadir said.

Reyes swallowed. “Maybe.”

The word entered the room like contamination. It was the same word command had used against probable intelligence, against injury, against restraint. Now it belonged to hardware and revenue.

Serran looked at the rotating geometry. “If we authorize shield configuration and the spine destabilizes.”

“Then you lose the window and probably the array’s profitable life,” Linh said. “If you preserve current geometry and Mara is right, you deepen the coupled injury and may lose sectors anyway after the next propagation event. Those are the options left after five days of pretending the options were better.”

“Engineer.”

“Director.”

They held each other’s eyes across the table. Linh had none of Mara’s procedural cushioning and none of Pavel’s instinct to protect future access. She had reached the end of patience days ago and was now running on stripped conductors. In another room that would have worked against her. Here, with the burn timer red over all our heads, it made her sound like the station itself had decided to answer.

Juno found his voice first. “Mission command still requires an authorization path.”

Serran’s hand returned to the console. This time he did not pause over the preserve-current field. He opened emergency operations, pulled the shield package into live authority, and entered a command key that promoted the maneuver from analytical proposal to executable station action.

Nadir said, very evenly, “I object for the record.”

“Recorded,” Juno said.

“I object on procurement grounds, contractual grounds, and humanitarian triage grounds.”

Serran did not look at her. “Recorded.”

Then he said, “Authorize shield geometry. Release Ekwueme to temporary engineering execution authority. Restore Dr. Vale’s analytic access under custody supervision. Commander Xu, burn control now. Reyes, Malik, local. Counsel Juno, annotate that command adopts emergency shield maneuver due to unresolved biological harm risk, station propagation risk, and narrowed timing envelope.”

The room changed state in less than a breath. My stomach dropped with it. One second I had been standing under detention status and contested evidence; the next my suspended access was being restored because the station had run out of safer people to trust. The shift should have felt like vindication. Instead it landed like being pushed back into a pressure suit that still held the shape of the last near-failure.

Voices overlapped. Xu’s feed expanded and replaced the vote pane. Malik was already routing burn-control channels to the side wall. Solis touched my elbow once, guiding rather than restraining, and moved with me toward the command console as if we had rehearsed it. The contact was light, almost impersonal, but it steadied the first misstep my body wanted to make. Linh was beside the array layout before I reached the station, fingers moving hard and fast over actuator tables. Reyes brought up collector-spine tolerances. Juno started speaking into the record at legal speed, his voice flattening to keep pace with too many consequential verbs.

Nadir remained standing at the far side of the table, one hand braced against its edge. She did not leave. I respected that more than I wanted to.

“Mara,” Linh said, not looking at me, “I need your elasticity term live. If the shell lag shifts during retune, I want the threshold update before the array sees it.”

“Pulling it.”

My access token opened under Serran’s emergency release. The console recognized my handprint, hesitated on suspended credentials, then yielded. Harmonic forecast windows bloomed over propulsion timing. Burn vector, array phase, coupled-load estimate, projected shell transfer. My body remembered the station’s working interfaces faster than it remembered exhaustion. That was dangerous. Competence always made it easier to mistake action for absolution.

Xu’s voice cut through three open channels. “Burn control, you are inside the shallow envelope. Commit in one hundred ten seconds or not at all.”

Malik answered, “Copy. Main thrusters to fractional retune only. No reserve correction after commit unless command signs off on propellant burn-through.”

“You will not have reserve correction,” Nadir said quietly.

No one answered her because everyone already knew.

I overlaid the distress recurrence on the live tidal relation and watched the low-transfer band crawl half a degree west as Enceladus turned under us. “Linh, the corridor is moving.”

“How much.”

“Half a degree and accelerating with local shell response.”

“Can you hold the threshold.”

“If Reyes gives me collector-spine flex on updated arc power.”

Reyes shoved the data across without speaking. I fed it into the model. The first output came back unstable, which was my fault and not the station’s. I had left the pre-hearing inertia correction in place. My hand stopped above the slate long enough for memory to try its old cruelty. Elias’s seal review. Incomplete load assumptions. One field accepted because the launch window was already narrower than reason. The sensation was so familiar it almost felt protective, as if failure repeated often enough could become a method instead of a wound.

I dragged the inherited correction out of the stack, rebuilt from current burn mass, and reran. New numbers resolved. Collector spine within tolerance if arcs two and three power down three seconds earlier than Xu’s draft sequence. Without that lead, thermal stress crossed the red line during retune.

“Xu, early drop on arcs two and three. Three seconds.”

“That cuts preload.”

“It keeps the spine inside tolerance.”

Linh glanced once at my output and nodded. “Do it.”

Xu said, “Confirmed. Updating sequence.”

The countdown in the burn-control pane rolled under sixty seconds. Around us the tribunal room no longer resembled a room designed for judgment. It was what every room aboard Anselm became under enough pressure: a control space assembled from imperfect specialties and tired bodies. Anik’s feed stayed open, tracking crew-risk projections and med readiness in case the retune went wrong. Solis had moved to the hatch and was speaking low into security comms, likely clearing corridors and locking down anyone who might decide now was the time to contest authority with force. Juno continued building the record that would ruin all of us in precise legal prose.

Nadir came around the table at last and stopped beside the mortality curves she had opened earlier. They still glowed on the auxiliary wall, untouched by our work.

“If this holds,” she said, speaking to no one and everyone, “there will be treatment chains on Earth that do not receive what they were promised.”

I kept my eyes on the threshold window. “Yes.”

“I want that in the same record as your probable intelligence.”

“It already is.”

She looked at me then, really looked, as if checking whether I understood the shape of the damage I had demanded. Perhaps she found enough understanding there, or perhaps there was no use testing it at T minus forty-three seconds. She gave one short nod and turned to Malik.

“If you are burning my reserve margin,” she said, “do not miss.”

Malik blinked once, surprised into almost-human levity, then let it go. “No plan for it, Director.”

Linh’s slate flashed yellow. “Array preload beginning. Mara, live threshold?”

I checked the updated model against the distress band one last time. The recurrence still held in the low-transfer corridor. Not safety. Not innocence. Room enough, if we acted cleanly. “Threshold holds.”

“Xu?”

“Retune holds.”

“Reyes?”

“Collector spine holds if Malik doesn’t improvise.”

“I never improvise,” Malik said, which was a lie so obvious it almost steadied the room.

Serran stood at the head of the table with one hand on the command rail and the other at his side. He had become still again, but not with indecision. This was the posture of a man who had accepted that choosing did not purify anything. It only assigned the loss. His fingers tightened once against the rail, a small private betrayal of the calm he was trying to lend the room.

“Execute,” he said.

The word went to every system at once. For a fraction of a second nothing visible changed, and that interval was worse than motion. My knees locked against the deck. My next breath stalled halfway in, held there by the old superstition that if I stayed perfectly still the station might pass through consequence without selecting us. Then burn control committed.

I felt the retune before the console confirmed it, a low shove through the deck and a momentary shift in my inner ear as Anselm spent propellant to move itself a fraction of a degree into a different moral universe. The wall display replaced countdown with live vector deviation. Arc two and three dropped early. The collector spine flexed. For six seconds every tolerance bar on Reyes’s panel rose together, yellow hardening toward red. Linh killed adjacent load with both hands moving across separate control branches, bypassing a safety confirmation Juno had not yet finished objecting to. The array phase slewed off throughput alignment. Stress transfer spiked in the shell model and then hung there long enough for my pulse to turn each refresh into a verdict.

“Threshold?” Linh said.

I heard my own breathing over the open channels. “Below breach. Still falling.”

A new alarm cut through the room. Not propagation. Collector-spine thermal warning. Reyes swore under her breath and pushed coolant reroute. Malik countered with a burn taper he had probably been planning since Nadir told him not to miss. Xu barked three clipped commands from his lagged feed. For a second the vector deviation widened beyond the planned shallow envelope and I saw, with laboratory clarity, the two ways a station can fail: by clean explosion or by a chain of managed compromises that each call themselves acceptable until the sum becomes fatal.

“Mara,” Linh said, “talk to me.”

The stress map refreshed. The low-transfer band held. Distress recurrence attenuated instead of climbing. Sector 4 sympathetic load dropped below the post-breach floor for the first time since the breach. I stared at it because my body refused the meaning before my mind did. The muscles across my shoulders unlocked so abruptly they hurt. My hands had been gripping the console hard enough to numb my fingers. Not healed. No honest model would call it that. But no longer under immediate strike from us.

“The corridor is taking it,” I said, and had to swallow before I could finish. “Propagation down. It is taking it.”

No one cheered. This was not that kind of station. The thermal alarm diminished to warning. Vector deviation narrowed back into planned range. Reyes exhaled through her teeth and kept both hands on the coolant reroute as if success could still break if she relaxed.

Malik looked up first. “Retune complete.”

Xu confirmed a beat later through lag. “Burn stable. Shallow solution holds.”

Linh was already drilling deeper into the array telemetry. “Collector spine?”

Reyes’s silence lasted too long.

“Reyes.”

“Spine survived,” she said. “Array did not.”

She pushed the damage map to the main wall. Adjacent collector branches along the original throughput corridor lit in dead gray. Two sector lances had safed hard and failed to reacquire. A third was technically alive but thermally warped past anything procurement would ever call reliable. The first-pass capture projection recalculated itself downward with bureaucratic calm. Forty-one percent. Thirty-six. Thirty-three. Final projected economically recoverable fraction: below viability threshold.

No one needed Nadir’s model to interpret that number.

The room emptied of velocity all at once. The work was done. The cost had only begun reporting.

Serran looked at the crippled array map as if it had translated him into a language he had spent the last week refusing to learn. Juno’s record light still blinked. Solis stepped away from the hatch and did not reassert custody. Anik’s feed remained open, but now she was watching my vitals rather than casualty projections. Xu removed his headset on the wall screen and rubbed both eyes before remembering the channel was still live.

Linh leaned both palms on the console. The red restriction band still encircled her wrist, absurd now that she had just helped save the station by violating it in full view of command. “It lives,” she said, but she was looking at the dead sectors when she said it, as if the sentence could not yet decide what its subject was.

I brought the harmonic overlay back over the damaged array map. The distress recurrence had not vanished. It had broadened, slowed, lost the jagged overload climb that had marked the injured corridor under current geometry. My pulse was still beating too hard to call the room steady. Relief came in thin increments, each one checked by the dead sectors on the wall and by the knowledge of what Earth would read in that damage. We had not saved innocence. We had only stopped adding one more injury fast enough to count.

Below viability threshold, the procurement display repeated.

Nadir read it once and closed her eyes. When she opened them, she did not argue the number. “Earth will call this preventable.”

Serran answered without turning from the wall. “Yes.”

He said it as a commander, as an admission, and as the first sentence in whatever inquiry would follow when the lagged oversight queue finally became a tribunal with more power than this room. The station had survived. The ocean-linked field under the ice had likely survived the window. The mission that had brought us here in its present form had not.

No one spoke for several seconds. Enceladus continued its rotation beneath us, indifferent to contracts, hearings, moral vocabulary, and the structures we had just broken to avoid breaking something else. On the wall, the damaged sectors remained dark while the attenuated recurrence moved through the low-transfer band with the steadiness of a signal that had stopped crying out and returned to some harder, quieter form of persistence.

I became aware of Solis still near my shoulder, of the ache in my locked knees, of the taste of adrenaline thinning at the back of my throat. My clearance had been restored because the room needed my hands. Custody would return when usefulness did. That should have shamed me, or angered me, or brought some cleaner satisfaction that the model had held. Instead I felt only the terrible narrowness of stewardship. We had chosen damage in public and called it duty because the alternatives were worse. The ocean below us was quieter now. Earth would not be.

Then the first delayed Earthside priority packet entered the queue, tagged for command review, casualty forecasting, legal hold, and incident attribution, and every person in the room had to look at the same question from a different side: what, exactly, had we saved, and who would be made to pay for it now?
