Chapter 15

Cost Curve

Triage overflow had been set up in the centrifuge-adjacent equipment bay because it was the nearest volume with drains, clean lighting, and enough anchor points to keep injured people from colliding when station handling shifted. By the time Solis brought me there from plume analytics, antiseptic aerosol had mixed with scorched polymer and the mineral-metal cold that flash-frozen ejecta carried in on fabric and skin. A medic had commandeered the maintenance bench as a procedure table. Two other crew lay strapped to emergency cots against the wall under warming sheets, conscious and trying to look less frightened than they were. A fourth patient sat half-upright on the deck grid while a nurse cut away the outer sleeve of his pressure underlayer.

He was one of the collector-boom technicians from Sector 3. I recognized the patch and not the name. Blood had not had time to decide what state it wanted. Dark beads clung near the edge of a forearm laceration, while farther up the sleeve the exposed skin had already gone wax-pale around scattered impact burns where supercooled particulate had struck and adhered before melting into damage. His respirations were fast and shallow. Pain guarding and cold shock, probably. The medic looked up, saw me, and pointed with her chin toward the thermal cartridge warming in its cradle.

“Hold that at forty-two and do not let him flex the arm,” she said. “He keeps trying to help.”

I took the cartridge and knelt beside him. The deck chill came straight through my knees. Up close, ejecta residue glittered in the weave of his undersuit like ground salt. He was younger than I had guessed from across the room, maybe twenty-six, with the raw stunned expression people get when machinery turns from abstract risk to local fact. His left hand kept trying to close and failing halfway.

“What is your name?”

“Deren.”

“Mara Vale. Do not move the arm unless she tells you.”

He let out one short breath that might have been a laugh if pain had not interrupted it. “You are the scientist they keep dragging through every bad room.”

“Today, apparently.”

I set the warming cartridge near but not on the damaged tissue and watched the display climb by tenths. Controlled reheating, then debridement, then graft decision if the microfracture scan looked as bad as the skin suggested. Procedure first. My hands knew that order and were grateful for it. Deren’s pulse fluttered visibly at the base of his throat. Each time the medic irrigated a new patch of skin he clenched his jaw so hard the muscles stood out in both cheeks.

“What hit you?” I asked.

“Boom kicked when alignment went.” He swallowed. “Something came through the visor seam. Arm took most of it.”

The nurse cut away the glove seal. Three fingers were already swelling. The pattern on the forearm looked almost beautiful in the way frost sometimes does when it forms too quickly on a failed window, branching outward from points of impact in pale crystalline fans. I had to stop my own mind from classifying grain size and deposition geometry. A person’s arm is not a specimen tray just because injury writes itself in readable structure.

The medic passed me a restraint strip. “Anchor his wrist.”

I did. Deren hissed through his teeth and shut his eyes. Behind me, the wall display had already been hijacked from medical queueing to station command updates. Sector 3 cold-safe in progress. Two collector booms offline. One lance assembly damaged. Three personnel injured, none critical. Hardware, personnel, schedule. The categories had arrived faster than the dressings.

Solis came to stand at my shoulder, not close enough to interfere. She had taken off one glove and was holding it by the fingers, a detail so small and human it unsettled me more than her sidearm. “They are routing nonessential witnesses out of plume analytics,” she said quietly. “Director Serran wants a narrowed incident statement in twenty minutes.”

“From whom?”

“From everyone.”

The medic heard and did not bother hiding her expression. “Narrowed. Useful.”

Solis did not answer. She watched while I adjusted the cartridge position and checked Deren’s fingertips for capillary response. Slow, but present. He followed the movement with the effortful concentration of a man trying to understand whether his own body still belonged to him.

“Was it the lance?” he asked.

I looked at him. Under pain and shock, he had still found the correct noun first.

“Yes.”

“They said test pulse.”

“They say a number of things.”

His mouth tightened. “I heard the boom line sing before it kicked.”

The word hit with more force than it should have. Elias had used the same word in his final maintenance note because engineers reached for acoustics when systems crossed from ordinary behavior into warning. Machines always tell on themselves. The memory altered my breathing for one second, then left the old ache behind between my ribs.

“How?” I asked.

“Not metal resonance. Too low. Came through the frame.” His eyelids fluttered once. “Like it was already there and then got shoved sideways.”

The medic glanced at me over Deren’s shoulder. She did not know the larger argument, only that the answer mattered to me in a way medical history usually did not. “Save the rest for recording,” she said. “You need him still conscious, not explanatory.”

I held the restraint and kept the warming cartridge steady while the nurse irrigated frozen debris from the wound margin. The bay kept filling and emptying in clipped emergencies. Someone rolled in another supply cart. Someone argued for more analgesic authority over the local channel. Overhead, the ventilation adjusted to the extra bodies and sent a colder draft along the floor.

Linh arrived six minutes later with her curls damp at the temples and a data slate under one arm. She had changed gloves but not shirt, so a faint rust-colored streak still marked one cuff where she had braced against the analytics rail during the breach. Her eyes went first to Deren’s arm, then to me, then to Solis.

“They pulled Pavel into command consult,” she said. “Nadir wants spread estimates rewritten in operational terms. Dae Juno is already drafting incident language.”

“Of course he is.”

Linh looked at the wall summary and exhaled through her nose. “Equipment loss. That is what they chose.”

The medic said, “Unless one of you can spare me another pair of hands, choose it somewhere else.”

Linh set down the slate and took the irrigation line without offense. She had the kind of competence that moved into a room and lowered the panic ceiling by existing. Deren relaxed by a degree when she steadied the line and told him exactly what the next thirty seconds would feel like.

While she worked, I picked up her abandoned slate with my free hand. The plume overlays were still open. Sector 3 showed the same compressed recoveries and clipped returns I had watched in analytics, though the amplitude had fallen since shutdown began. Adjacent Sector 4 remained distorted. Sector 2 now showed a weaker sympathetic phase drift that had not been present ten minutes earlier. Not active collapse. Not safe. The pattern kept redistributing load through the shell-ocean coupling path faster than command could rename it.

“They are still holding staged isolation?” I asked.

“Yes,” Linh said. “Serran wants a station-wide propagation model before full suspension.”

“He wants a number that can carry blame for him.”

She rinsed another patch of tissue. When she spoke again, her voice had gone quieter, which in her usually meant anger approaching a more durable state. “I copied the live cascade before they locked the room. Off-net and redundant. If they bury the injury language, we can still move the record.”

Solis’s posture shifted almost invisibly at that. Not surprise. Registration.

“You are saying that in front of security,” I said.

“Security already watched the thing break.”

Solis met my eyes. “I did.”

The medic taped a sensor mesh over Deren’s wrist and finally let someone dose him properly. His shoulders softened by small increments as the analgesic took. The nurse covered the wound with a clear thermal film that clouded as it sealed. Temporary. Nothing in the bay looked final except the fact that machinery had reached flesh before command had finished arguing over nouns.

When Deren was stable enough to move, the medic sent him toward imaging with a transport orderly and the warning that anyone who touched the dressing before scan results came back would answer to her personally. I stood too fast after helping lift him to the cot and had to catch the bench edge while the room shifted around me. Lack of food, sleep debt, adrenaline dump. The body always submitted its report after the event, never before. Linh noticed and handed me a squeeze pouch from her pocket without comment. I took it because refusing would have been theatrical and stupid.

The pouch was lukewarm protein paste flavored in theory as coffee. I had swallowed two mouthfuls when Pavel came through the hatch.

He looked older than he had forty minutes earlier. Not transformed. Advanced by stress the way paper changes around a flame before it catches. His lab coat was gone. Both sleeves had been rolled to the elbow, and one remained twisted inside out at the cuff. A fresh command badge rode on his chest clip, temporary consult access. He saw the treatment bay, the remaining patients, Linh with bloodless irrigation fluid on one glove, me with the empty pouch in my hand, and stopped.

“How many injured?” he asked.

“Three from Sector 3,” Linh said. “None dead, which I assume means command is calling itself vindicated.”

Pavel absorbed the strike without answering it. “Mara. I need to speak with you.”

Linh looked at me first. Not asking permission. Checking geometry. The medic was busy at the far cot; Solis remained close enough to hear anything said at ordinary volume. Privacy aboard stations was usually a matter of deciding who would pretend not to listen.

“Here is fine,” I said.

Pavel glanced at Solis and lowered his voice anyway. “No. Not here.”

Solis answered before I did. “The consult alcove is empty. Hatch stays open.”

We followed him into the adjacent bay, a narrow supply recess reclassified by emergency need into meeting space because it had two fold-down seats and a functioning air return. The hatch remained open exactly as Solis had said. Through it I could still see the edge of triage activity and Linh moving between cot and bench with efficient contained anger.

Pavel did not sit. He braced one hand against the wall rail and stared at the opposite bulkhead for a moment before turning to me. “I told Serran the passive models are dead. I told him the injury interpretation is the most honest one available.”

“I know.”

“He is still not authorizing full suspension without a propagation estimate he thinks can survive Earthside review.”

“I know that too.”

He looked directly at me then, and the thing in his face was not retreat from the science. That would almost have been easier. It was a different sort of failure line, one that opened after belief had already arrived. “Mara, if you take this outside command control now, you will turn one damaged sector into a system-wide political event before anyone has bounded the consequences.”

“The consequences are already bounded. They are in triage.”

“No. The local consequences are in triage.” He took one breath, held it, and went on more carefully. “The station is one node in a larger chain. If you force probable nonhuman intelligence plus injury into the public record during an active extraction emergency, the consortium does not just lose this window. It loses off-world scientific legitimacy for a generation. Every mission with ambiguous environmental signatures becomes litigation bait. Every extraction program gets flattened together whether it deserves to or not. Earthside scarcity does not pause while we learn moral precision.”

The language was his and not his. Pavel, when frightened, retreated into structure, but the casualty curve inside the structure belonged to rooms Serran and Nadir had already built. I leaned back against the folded seat because my calves had started to tremble. My grip on the pouch crimped the foil hard enough to crease it.

“You sound like procurement,” I said.

“I sound like someone who has spent the last hour watching three truths collide at once.” His hand tightened on the rail. “Yes, we may be looking at distributed intelligence. Yes, Sector 3 likely injured it. Yes, there are still millions of recognized human lives downstream of whether stations like this remain politically possible. Those facts do not cancel one another because one of them horrifies us more.”

“So what do you want?”

“Hold the line on internal evidence. Force full suspension locally if you can. But do not transmit, do not leak, do not turn this into a moral spectacle while the data are still outrunning the model. If you make them choose between public scandal and institutional survival, they will bury everything, and the backlash will not stop with Serran. It will hit every outer-system lab, every biosignature mission, every fragile research platform already one budget cycle from death.”

Through the open hatch I could hear a patient groan as a dressing came free in the next bay. The sound traveled with ugly clarity through the ventilation wash. Pavel heard it too. His eyes closed once, briefly, then opened on a look more exhausted than defensive.

“You think I do not see the human cost in front of us,” he said. “I do. I also see the cost curve beyond this room. If Earth decides the lesson of Enceladus is that science withheld relief compounds to protect an unconfirmed alien claimant, what follows will not be stewardship. It will be retaliation, cuts, secrecy, and worse actors with fewer brakes.”

“Unconfirmed.”

“Provisionally confirmed, then. The adjective does not solve the governance problem.”

“It solves whether we tell the truth.”

“Not if truth arrives in a form that guarantees it will be used to kill the field and save nothing.”

The sentence landed between us and stayed there. I had known, abstractly, that agreement on the evidence did not guarantee agreement on obligation. Knowing a structural principle is different from feeling the exact point where it bears weight. Pressure had started behind my sternum and spread outward, a hard shallow band that made every inhale feel borrowed. Pavel had walked with me all the way to probable intelligence. He had signed the sentence command most wanted reduced. In plume analytics he had refused an inert explanation in front of the room. None of that protected this next step.

“Three days ago,” I said, and had to wet my lips before the rest would come, “you were asking me not to outrun the data.”

“And now I am asking you not to outrun consequence.”

“Consequence outran us at 07:41.”

He flinched, barely, at the timestamp.

“Mara, listen to me. Public alarm is not witness if it only hardens the machine on a wider scale. There may still be a route through injunction, through delayed review, through a station-side suspension that does not make every desperate government on Earth see Enceladus as the place where scientists chose alien possibility over human certainty.”

“You think silence keeps the comparison from being made.”

“I think timing changes whether the comparison becomes a weapon.”

Behind him, across the open hatch, Linh looked up from the triage bench and met my eyes. She could not hear every word from there, only posture and cadence. It was enough. She crossed to the hatch, pulled off one glove finger by finger, and stayed where Pavel would have to see her if he turned.

“Better timing for whom?” she asked.

Pavel’s shoulders drew tight, though he did not fully look back. “This is not helping.”

“No,” Linh said. “It is just making the room honest.”

I said, “If they bury this injury report, what exactly are we preserving for your better timing?”

“Enough institutional survivability to matter later.”

“Later is what Serran has said at every gate in this story.”

“Serran uses later to get to skim. I am using later to keep this from becoming the last serious off-world biology program anyone funds.”

The distinction was real. That was what made it dangerous. A lesser man would have hidden inside cowardice or denial. Pavel was trying to protect multiple futures at once and had decided witness was the expendable variable. My hands had gone cold. Not numb. Worse. Exact. I could feel each heartbeat in the pads of my fingers where they pressed into the wrinkled foil pouch.

“What will you sign?” I asked.

“A full internal injury finding. A local suspension order. A direct challenge to Juno’s narrowed language. I will not sign an unauthorized external release while the event model is still unstable.”

“And if command rejects the suspension?”

He hesitated. Not long. Long enough.

“Then I keep fighting in the lanes still open.”

That was the break. Not the science, not even the conscience. The lanes. He still believed there were lanes left that power had not already chosen for him. Or maybe he did not believe it and needed the belief to keep functioning. Something in my chest seemed to shift a fraction lower, as if the station had changed spin again and only my body had noticed.

From triage came Linh’s voice, sharpened by distance and restraint. “Mara. You need to see this.”

Pavel turned halfway toward the hatch but did not move. He was waiting for me to say something that restored the earlier geometry. I had nothing fit for that purpose.

“If I decide your timing is only another form of permission,” I said, “do not mistake that for confusion.”

His face altered then, a clean small grief moving through it before discipline sealed over. “I won’t.”

I stepped past him into the triage bay. Linh had my slate in one hand and Deren’s preliminary imaging in the other. On the scan, the soft-tissue damage ran deeper than the surface pattern had suggested. Fine cryofracture branched toward the extensor sheath, damage tracking load through the arm the way stress propagates through a compromised shell. On the slate beside it, Sector 4’s distortion had strengthened another measurable increment despite staged isolation.

Two curves, human and nonhuman, both steepening after contact with the same decision. Command would call one manageable injury and the other unresolved anomaly until the phrasing failed under its own weight. Pavel had already chosen the range of costs he thought history could survive. Linh looked at me as if she knew the answer had narrowed again.

Across the bay, Solis stood by the hatch with my custody tablet in her hand and did not tell me to return to restricted review. Through the overhead speaker, command requested revised incident language from all scientific personnel within ten minutes. The room kept moving around that demand. Dressings. Scans. Coolant alarms. A man on the far cot biting the heel of his hand while someone rewrapped his fingers.

I looked from Deren’s damaged arm to the spreading distortion on the slate. Evidence had brought us this far. It would not make the next decision for me. The speaker clicked again, waiting for obedient language. No one in triage answered it. For one breath the only sounds were the ventilators, the medic’s tape tearing free, and Deren’s scan still open in Linh’s hand like a second wound.
