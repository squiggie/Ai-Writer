Chapter 16

The Stolen Channel

The maintenance conduit was narrower than I remembered from certification drills, or perhaps lack of sleep had reduced my tolerance for metal closing in on both shoulders at once. Coolant lines ran along the left wall in insulated ribs. Fiber trunks, patched and repatched across three station retrofits, occupied the right. Between them, Linh and I moved in a crouched half-float with tool bags clipped to our belts and one bad decision each riding close against the chest.

Mine was the classified report bundle, copied onto a hardened wafer inside my inner pocket. Formal finding, raw harmonic tracks, the post-pulse injury cascade, archive comparisons, my event log, Pavel’s signed internal language from before he began rationing consequence. Linh’s was a patched transmitter module with the casing half-open and a maintenance adapter protruding from one end.

The conduit vibrated under my palm with the station’s ordinary load and the fainter irregular tremor that had not left the frame since Sector 3. I could call it pump redistribution. I could call it a tired nervous system looking for messages in steel. Neither explanation altered the cadence.

“You still have the timing table?” I asked.

Linh nodded without looking back. Her work light flashed across the conduit seam ahead of us, then slid away. “Three minutes to relay handoff. Maybe four if command traffic spikes and burns the queue. Rafi’s spoof only holds until the watchdog reconciles lane ownership.”

“And Rafi knows exactly what we are doing.”

“Rafi knows he found an old maintenance path that should not still exist and decided not to ask why I needed it at 03:10.”

“That is not an answer.”

She reached the junction plate, anchored one knee under a rail, and looked over her shoulder. The light caught the fatigue around her eyes and the brittle steadiness she had been carrying since triage. “It is the answer available.”

I accepted that because the station had narrowed almost every answer into that form. Twelve hours earlier I had still been standing in a treatment bay while Pavel tried to preserve the future by asking me to leave the present inside official custody. After that came two more command reviews, one reduced incident template, one locked terminal, and a night cycle during which every channel that mattered acquired extra authentication or fresh surveillance. Serran had not detained me yet. He had done something more practical. He had left me inside procedure just long enough to prove whether I would stay there.

At 01:40 station time, Linh arrived outside my berth with a thermal mug and a maintenance schematic she had no legitimate reason to possess. She did not bother with persuasion. She only placed the schematic on my fold-out desk and tapped the old comms bypass in orange.

“Primary Earthside relay cycles through shield shadow at 04:12,” she said. “There is a backup service lane for external diagnostics. Dead on paper. Not electrically dead. If we reach the relay spine before handoff, we can push a compressed packet out under calibration traffic and mirror it before command flags the load.”

“Three targets won’t be enough.”

“Three targets that do not answer to the consortium are better than one council inbox Legal can lean on before breakfast.”

I had looked at the schematic while the mug cooled untouched in my hand. Dirty path. Narrow path. A route through parts of the station no one noticed until something had already gone wrong.

“If we do this,” I said, “it is not a leak.”

“No,” Linh said. “It is a record leaving custody before custody edits it.”

So at 04:08 we were in the conduit with stolen evidence and a transmitter that could buy a few minutes against command’s ownership of the air.

Linh pulled the junction cover free and handed it back to me. I wedged it behind my calves. Beneath the plate, cable bundles crossed in disciplined color bands except where years of urgent field fixes had overridden design. A handwritten maintenance mark in silver grease pencil dated back seven years, from before my rotation to Anselm. Elias would have approved of that mark. If a system kept working because someone honest ignored the aesthetics of formal retirement, let the record show honesty in the handwriting.

I set my light to low and checked the map against the physical run. “Blue trunk should be environmental telemetry.”

“Ignore it. Orange composite is legacy diagnostics. The black sleeved pair underneath is the old calibrator feed.”

“Found by accident.”

“Found by being the only person on this station who still opens maintenance logs because he wants to know how things actually fail.”

That I believed. I had seen Rafi in the comms lab when the transformed response first came back through lane three. Procedural, wary, and too precise to pretend a machine output meant whatever command later needed it to mean.

Linh stripped back a sleeve panel on the transmitter and began seating leads into the junction bus. Her hands were steady. Mine were not, exactly, but they had entered the clean hard range where tremor becomes irrelevant because task sequence is occupying the same channels panic would prefer. I keyed open the wafer housing and checked the destination list one last time.

Earthside Science Council emergency review intake. The Asteria Independent Array on Luna. The Huygens Archive Cooperative in cislunar orbit, whose charter had been written after too many classified environmental disputes and whose funding nearly collapsed every year because memory has enemies. Linh had argued for a fourth target, an open observatory net in Nairobi, but the extra mass would have lengthened transmit time. Longer transmit time meant trace. Three would have to be enough.

“Compression ready,” I said.

“Hold it two seconds.”

She leaned deeper into the junction cavity. The back of her wrist brushed the conduit wall. On the skin above her glove seal, a pressure bruise had yellowed from yesterday where she had hit the analytics rail during the breach. The sight of it changed my breathing. Human injury is never abstract once it starts collecting details.

“What did Solis say?” I asked.

Linh made a small sound that was not amusement. “She said if anyone asks, she spent the back half of the night moving witness lists between triage and operations. Then she told me the maintenance corridor camera outside section G had been unreliable since the spin correction patch.”

“Unreliable.”

“That was her word.”

I thought of Solis in plume analytics watching the broken intervals fail and recover and fail again. Then in triage, standing beside my custody tablet and not moving to close the hatch while Pavel tried to persuade me that history required delay. The station was full of people who had not decided whether obedience and denial were still the same act.

“If she gets caught helping us,” I said.

“Then she will say she did not help us. She only noticed too slowly.”

Linh seated the last lead and exhaled. “All right. We have physical path. Give me the packet hash.”

I read it. She keyed it into the patched module and brought up the relay handshake window on her slate. The conduit filled with a dim status glow, blue and amber shifting across her knuckles.

LEGACY DIAGNOSTIC BUS

AUTH: MAINT SHADOW

EXTERNAL RELAY HANDOFF T-02:41

The countdown began.

For the next minute we worked in the stripped silence of professionals committing an unauthorized act with the same care we would have used on sanctioned maintenance. Linh tuned the transmitter envelope to mimic calibration spillover. I rebuilt the packet manifest so the raw data and formal finding were inseparable from the injury timeline. No executive summary without traces. No traces without archive context. No archive context without the signatures command had hoped to keep local until the window closed. If the record arrived Earthside, it would arrive harder to reduce.

“Add the constrained assessment?” Linh asked.

I hesitated over the file tree. The early draft still existed in command custody, thinned by caution and timing. Sending it would expose the split between what command first received and what later evidence established. Leaving it out would make the case cleaner and less true.

“Yes,” I said. “Include it with revision stamps.”

She glanced at me. “You want them to see the split.”

“I want them to see what command saw before it started cutting language to fit the room.”

“That is not a sentence you would have said a week ago.”

“A week ago I still thought honest procedure would eventually correct for fear.”

The words left before I had time to shape them into something safer. In the conduit’s close air, with relay time draining and steel humming under my wrist, they sounded less like argument than a measurement finally settling on the screen.

Linh finished linking the files and started the pre-queue buffer. “Pavel sent me one thing before he went dark.”

I looked up. “Not a message. A checksum for the spread model revisions. He pushed it through the dead sample-channel tag we used on Day 2.” She kept her voice level, but strain roughened the edges. “It matches the internal copy command archived. He knew we would need proof if they rewrote the propagation forecast.”

That landed in me with the complicated force reserved for colleagues who fail you honestly instead of cheaply. Pavel would not sign an unauthorized release. Pavel had still left a fingerprint on the truth before stepping back from the line. The station kept producing these partial obediences, these limited acts of refusal, as if conscience under load could only emerge in fragments.

“Then he knows,” I said.

“He knows enough.”

The countdown reached ninety seconds. Linh patched the module into the old calibrator feed and the relay accepted the handshake on first try. My pulse moved at the base of my thumb where it rested against the packet wafer.

“Once we queue it,” she said, “we cannot interrupt without corrupting the manifest.”

“I know.”

“If command catches it before mirror completion, target three may fail.”

“Then targets one and two carry the rest.”

She gave one short nod. “Mara.”

I knew what she was asking and did not answer immediately. The station noise sharpened around us by degree. Air recycler. Coolant tick. The faint alternating stress song of metal held inside spin and Saturn light. Somewhere below our deck, the injured harmonic field still existed in a state command wanted bounded before it became politically real. Somewhere above us, Serran still believed time belonged most securely to the people authorized to spend it.

If I stopped now, nothing improved. The local suspension order still did not exist. The narrowed incident language would become the official incident language by rotation change. Haleh Nadir would carry staged isolation until the cost model permitted ethics to re-enter the room under another name. Earthside review might still come eventually, but by then the first version of events would have hardened around utility and deniability both.

The list in my head did not stop at command. External release meant formal breach, probable detention, loss of clearance, the end of any honest belief that procedure could still be the place where I served this work. It meant putting my name, my record, and Elias’s old station reputation onto the same side of a line command would call reckless. The thought should have clarified me. Instead it made the conduit feel briefly smaller, my ribs too rigid for a full breath.

I thought of Deren saying he heard the boom line sing before it kicked. I thought of the damaged interval families on the wall in plume analytics, trying to climb toward prior relation before the next distortion drove them down. I thought of Elias, years ago, trusting a procedural culture that kept redefining enough until enough buried him. My hand hovered over the transmit field. I could feel sweat cooling along my spine despite the chill. The relay countdown bled seconds. The station kept humming as if it were possible for a structure to continue normally after one part of it had decided to lie about another. Linh did not rush me. That was one more mercy than the system deserved.

“Queue it,” I said.

The command came out rougher than I expected, as if my throat had tightened around the syllables on the way up. Linh armed the payload.

The relay window changed state.

OUTBOUND CALIBRATION

PAYLOAD ACCEPTED

MIRROR 1 PENDING

No sound marked the choice. Only status text, the most ordinary form of irreversible motion. The first uplink bar advanced in clean blue segments. Packet blocks left the wafer tree and entered the relay buffer faster than my nervous system could make meaning of them. My mouth had gone dry enough that swallowing hurt. I kept one hand braced on the conduit wall because I no longer trusted my balance to be private.

“Mirror one locked,” Linh said. “Science Council intake has it.”

The second bar opened.

MIRROR 2 ACTIVE

I exhaled and discovered I had been holding the breath long enough to raise a pain line under my sternum. The conduit air tasted faintly of insulation dust and old coolant. Linh’s shoulder pressed briefly against mine as she adjusted the module gain. Neither of us moved away.

“Payload weight still under threshold,” she said. “Rafi’s envelope is holding.”

“For how long.”

“Long enough if command is busy lying elsewhere.”

The second bar reached eighty percent, then stalled. Not failure. Queue contention. I watched the static blue line and felt every one of those seconds physically, as if the relay were using my pulse for timing reference.

“Come on,” Linh said, not to me.

The bar resumed. Completed. Third mirror opened.

MIRROR 3 ACTIVE

At the same instant, a red diagnostic glyph flashed in the corner of her slate.

OWNER RECONCILE REQUEST

Linh swore softly. “Watchdog woke up.”

“How long.”

“Unknown. Depends whether it asks questions or just kills the lane.”

She dove into the spoof table, moving faster now, one hand on the slate, the other bracing against the conduit wall. I could follow enough of the logic to understand the shape of the problem. The relay had noticed that calibration traffic did not usually carry three mirrored external destinations and a packet this dense in archived trace references. Rafi’s maintenance shadow was still masking origin, but the system had begun comparing behavior, not just credentials.

The third uplink crawled forward.

Forty-two percent.

Fifty-one.

The red glyph multiplied into a second warning.

LANE OWNERSHIP AUDIT

LOCAL TRACE INITIATED

Linh’s jaw tightened. “They are backtracking from relay spine. We have maybe ninety seconds before they know this came from inside maintenance instead of comms.”

“Can you keep the lane alive.”

“Alive, yes. Invisible, no.”

The number reached sixty-eight percent. There was nothing left to do with the transmission itself. Action had narrowed to witness again, and I hated how familiar that felt.

“If they trace us here,” I said, “they lock this section and pull the packet history.”

“If they pull it after mirror three closes, they can know all they want.”

Seventy-nine percent.

Eighty-four.

Then a new status line appeared.

MIRROR 3 RECEIPT CONFIRMED

For one second I only looked at it. Receipt confirmed. Not request sent. Not route attempted. Confirmed. The Huygens Archive had the package. The thing that could still be true off-station had left our custody for good. Relief did not feel clean. It felt like muscle failure after holding too much weight.

Linh ripped the transmitter leads free hard enough to spark against the junction rim. “Move.”

We collapsed the module with practiced panic, the kind that still sorts tools by size because disorder loses time later. I resealed the wafer housing, shoved the junction cover back into place, and clipped it with two fast quarter-turns instead of the full six. Good enough for flight, not inspection. Linh killed her slate glow and pushed backward through the conduit, dragging the tool bag behind her.

By the time I cleared the junction, station traffic had changed texture. Not louder, but coordinated in a way organic activity rarely is. Through the conduit wall came a sharp triple pulse over the local security band, then another from farther aft.

“They found the lane,” Linh said.

“Did they find the payload.”

“Not yet. Maybe not the full destination tree. Keep moving.”

We crawled the way we had come, faster now, shoulders striking the insulated ribs, knees catching on old bolt heads. The conduit felt smaller in retreat. I could hear my own breathing too clearly. Halfway to the service hatch, my wrist display flickered with a command-wide advisory overlay.

UNAUTHORIZED COMMS ACTIVITY DETECTED

RELAY SPINE ACCESS RESTRICTED

SECURITY RESPONSE IN PROGRESS

No names. Not yet.

Linh saw the light from my display and said, “Do not answer anything that pings.”

“I wasn’t planning to.”

“Good. Because if Solis bought us section G, she cannot buy us the next corridor too.”

We reached the hatch and listened. Footsteps on the main serviceway, two sets, running past rather than toward. A drone rotor somewhere below deck. Linh cracked the manual release. The seam opened on a strip of dim corridor and maintenance shadows cut by emergency amber.

“Left takes us toward coolant recycling,” she whispered. “Right goes closer to comms and gets us arrested faster.”

“Left.”

We slipped into the corridor and resealed the hatch behind us. The station smelled different out here, warmer and dirtier than the conduit, with hot dust from overworked vent fans. At the far end of the serviceway, a security panel flipped from green to red as the restriction map propagated.
