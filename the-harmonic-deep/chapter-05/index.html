I made it back to my berth without deciding to go there. One minute I was still in the acoustic bay with the transformed sequence held on the wall in amber, and the next I was in the station night corridor with my tablet tucked under one arm, moving through the reduced-light cycle as if some lower procedural layer had taken control. Anselm dimmed by sectors after 22:00 to save power and to suggest, politely and without much effect, that human bodies had limits. The corridor strips had gone to the low blue setting. Air handlers ran slower. The day-shift traffic had thinned to maintenance crews, med techs, and the insomniacs who learned long ago to make their wakefulness look mission-relevant.

My tablet kept the anomaly track open in the upper pane. The new offset held in replay after replay. Old interval, icequake, altered interval, preserved relation. I had already checked the lag correction twice in the bay and once more while waiting for the lift. The result stayed where it was. That should have narrowed the next action. Instead it widened it. Every explanation still available looked worse than it had three hours earlier, and the path from worse explanations to institutional consequence remained obstructed by the same clock counting down in the corner of the screen.

Thirty hours, thirty-six minutes to initial skim.

My berth module was half the width of the plume lab airlock and perhaps one twentieth as useful, with a fold-down bunk, a wall screen, two netted storage pockets, and a restraint strap I used only when station attitude jitter made sleep physically impossible. The ventilation outlet above the door clicked on and off with a cycling fault maintenance had promised to fix six months ago. I could usually ignore it. Tonight each click sounded like a cursor selecting a field.

I set the tablet on the wall shelf, drank from the water bulb without tasting it, and opened the anomaly summary memo I had been revising since the first calibration run. The text was still where I had left it after the briefing and the acoustic bay.

Repeatable nontrivial harmonic structure observed across separate sensor paths. Current source undetermined. Operational significance unresolved. No present basis for classifying plume field as unsafe on available evidence. Continued accelerated validation recommended under anomaly-response workflow.

The final sentence had not been there this morning. I had written it after Serran formalized the track, thinking that if I placed enough caution around the edges of the finding I could preserve room to keep working. It was disciplined language. It was also beginning to resemble another kind of document too closely.

I did not sleep. I lay down once, because training teaches respect for any available recovery window, and felt my pulse continuing in my jaw and wrists as if rest had been proposed to the wrong organism. The berth wall carried a faint transmitted vibration from the station spine, regular enough to read as comfort on better nights. Tonight the frequency kept resolving into the altered interval from the icequake sequence. Not actually, of course. The station was full of mechanical cycles that could mimic whatever pattern the brain had been feeding on too long. I counted six full breaths, then ten, then sat up before I could begin pretending this counted as an attempt.

There are stations of fatigue beyond which the body stops asking for permission and starts using memory as a second environment. By 23:17 I was no longer arguing with myself about whether to pull the old file. I was already inside the secure archive partition, entering a mission code I had not typed in eleven years and still knew at finger level.

The expedition had been called Khepri on paper and something blunter in ordinary speech. Rock-route survey. Ice interface prospecting. Outer Jovian auxiliary. It had gone out lean and underfunded with a hull older than the mission planners admitted and a safety packet whose unresolved items had been designated tractable because the alternative was cancellation. My brother Elias had signed on as thermal systems engineer because he liked broken machinery, difficult frontiers, and the kind of work where competence was still visible to the naked eye. I had signed as external systems analyst on the review board because I knew the hull stress model, trusted the lead package, and believed incomplete was not the same as unsafe if the incompleteness was bounded properly.

That last phrase had a long afterlife.

The archive accepted my clearance and opened the old incident tree in austere gray tabs. Mission timelines never look like grief. They look like sequence markers, fault summaries, and notarized restraint. I selected the pre-event review packet first, not because it would hurt less than the casualty file, but because the casualty file was too easy. People who want punishment go there first. People who want truth start with the paperwork that made the punishment possible.

Version history populated down the left side of the wall screen. Review notes. Engineering exceptions. Thermal bleed concerns flagged by junior systems. Compensating procedures added. Two unresolved coupler response anomalies deferred pending in-flight validation. Then the sign-off chain. Twelve names. Mine seventh.

I enlarged the page until only the certification block remained. The berth seemed to contract around the white field. My hand found the shelf edge and locked there hard enough that the tendon at my wrist stood out. For a moment I could hear nothing except the intermittent vent click and the dry movement of my own swallow.

Residual uncertainty judged acceptable within current operational envelope. No evidence at present of failure cascade under planned load conditions. Recommend conditional proceed with active monitoring and authority to revise after first live stress confirmation.

The words hit with the delayed force of an impact traveling through structure. Heat moved up the back of my neck. My stomach drew tight, then tighter. For several seconds I looked only at the phrase at present, not because it was the worst part, though it was close, but because it was the hinge. A small linguistic bearing designed to let responsibility rotate away from the signer and into time. At present, given current evidence, no basis at this stage, pending further validation. The forms changed by mission and decade. The structure did not.

My right hand had gone so still it was beginning to throb. I flexed the fingers once. The knuckles ached as if I had spent the evening gripping tool rails instead of language.

I opened the event packet from Khepri. The thermal fault had propagated twelve hours after the first live load test. Not from the exact coupler response anomaly we had logged, which had made the review board look better for almost a week, but through an adjacent transfer manifold whose tolerance stack had narrowed under conditions our envelope estimate had treated as low probability. The cascade was fast once it began. A seal failed. Heat ran where it should not have. Emergency partition doors closed one segment too slowly. Elias and two others were in the wrong compartment at the wrong three minutes of the mission.

The official summary remained precise and bloodless after all this time. Contributing factors: underestimated coupled response under live stress environment; deferred anomaly resolution; cumulative procedural optimism.

Cumulative procedural optimism. That was what institutional guilt called itself when it wanted to remain employable.

I moved to Elias’s maintenance notes because they were the only part of the file that still sounded human. He had written the way he repaired things, directly and without decorative patience. The final maintenance log before the live test was short.

Manifold B still sings on thermal rise. Not enough to fail spec, enough to watch. If the first stress pass comes back ugly, stop pretending the noise is character.

My breath caught high and stayed there. I had a sudden, useless memory of standing with him in Dock Three before launch while he pressed two knuckles against a coolant line and listened through the metal with his eyes half closed, grinning when he found the vibration he wanted me to hear. “There,” he had said then, not looking up. “Machines always tell on themselves.” In the berth, the vent clicked again. For half a beat the sound seemed to come through that older dock air, carrying hot alloy, sealant, and the bitter steam from the service cart he drank instead of coffee.

The berth was too warm suddenly. Or I was. I reached up and widened the vent two increments. Dry station air fell across my face carrying the usual trace of polymer dust and scrubber ozone. It did nothing useful.

I should have closed the archive there. The relevant point had already been made more clearly than I deserved. Instead I opened the board exchange attached to the sign-off cycle and found my own response to Elias’s note.

Logged. Current assessment does not support hold. Recommend proceed with thermal watch and live validation.

My chest tightened before the meaning fully landed, as if the body recognized the sentence faster than the mind that had built it. I felt the pulse jump at the base of my throat. The skin along my forearms prickled in the station air. I had not forgotten writing the line. I had forgotten how ordinary it looked. Clean, professional, nearly kind in its refusal.

On the wall shelf my current anomaly memo remained open on the tablet. Two documents, eleven years apart, each using uncertainty as both tool and shelter. The similarities were not exact. That mattered. Enceladus was not a failing manifold. The harmonic structure was not a known hardware defect. I was not about to sign people into a sealed thermal segment on a damaged ship. If I let fatigue erase those differences, I would stop being useful. Precision still mattered. But precision was not helping me ignore the underlying mechanics of the choice now in front of me.

The old Khepri language had been built around a testable machine whose failure modes we thought we understood. The language on my current memo was being built around something for which the category itself might be wrong. Geological process. Environmental pattern. Adaptive response. Possible life. Possible intelligence. I could defend every caveat individually. Taken together, the memo was starting to perform a more dangerous function. It was preparing the station to continue until the truth became expensive enough to classify.

I dragged the current draft onto the wall beside the old sign-off. The parallel columns made my stomach tighten with an efficiency no rhetoric could improve.

No present basis for classifying plume field as unsafe on available evidence.

Current assessment does not support hold.

Different problem. Same engineered humility. Same distribution of risk toward whatever could not yet speak in the meeting.

I closed my eyes for one breath and heard Orlov from the plume lab two shifts earlier, quiet enough that at the time I had treated the words as an interference pattern instead of an instruction: lack of a name does not remove responsibility. He had not meant paperwork. But that was where responsibility liked to disguise itself on stations like this, in the fields where one phrase widened and another narrowed what the next person could do.

The night cycle made private sounds larger. Vent click. Panel hum. Footsteps out in the corridor attenuated by composite and distance. Somewhere down the module row a sink valve chattered twice. In the old days, before Elias died, I used to trust enclosed technical spaces because they narrowed moral questions into maintenance questions. Something either held pressure or it didn’t. A seal either passed or failed. Then I watched three people die under a sign-off chain full of accurate partial statements, and ever since then I had treated certainty as the only clean solvent strong enough to remove self-deception. That worked well enough right up to the point where certainty arrived too late to prevent the thing it had been waiting to name.

The terminal clock rolled to 23:46.

I opened a fresh memo instead of revising the old one. Better to see what sentence I reached for when the page offered me no precedent. The header auto-filled from Serran’s anomaly workflow, formal and ugly.

Preliminary Classification Recommendation

My hands hovered over the keys longer than they should have. The tendons along the backs of them had drawn tight. A faint tremor moved through my left index finger and would not settle until I pressed it against the desk lip. Then I wrote the sentence command wanted because I needed to see whether I still could.

Current data do not justify immediate suspension of initial skim preparation.

I stopped. Deleted immediate. Left the rest.

Current data do not justify suspension of initial skim preparation.

Technically arguable. Operationally disastrous. The station would read the sentence the way water reads a pressure gradient and move through it at once.

I kept writing.

Observed harmonic anomaly remains scientifically significant but unproven as a hazard source, legal life-detection trigger, or extraction-prohibitive condition. Continued collection during scheduled readiness operations is recommended while validation proceeds.

There it was. A complete path from wonder to paperwork. Clean syntax. Measured uncertainty. Built-in future regret.

My chest had tightened so gradually during the drafting that I only noticed it when I leaned back and the restraint strap bracket pressed between my shoulder blades. I read the paragraph once, then a second time aloud under my breath to hear whether the words at least sounded like me. They did. That was the problem. Voice can carry truth. It can also carry habit with higher resolution.

I highlighted continued collection during scheduled readiness operations is recommended. My thumb paused over the delete control and stayed there. Thirty hours to initial skim. Less now. The transformed sequence from the icequake still lacked the kind of proof Serran could not narrow. Pavel would say, correctly, that the paragraph preserved the anomaly on record while avoiding claims we could not yet defend. Linh would say, also correctly, that the paragraph functioned as clearance no matter what I named it. Both assessments could be exact. Exactness was no refuge if the next action still damaged what the data were trying to protect.

I reopened the acoustic event file and ran the altered sequence again in the corner of the screen. Old interval. Disturbance. Shifted interval. Preserved relation. Nine pulses under changed load. The pattern did not look like any machine language I knew. It looked like persistence under pressure. I found myself attending to it with the same care I had once given Elias’s voice messages after Khepri, when I was afraid repetition would wear the human trace out of them. I stopped the replay before that comparison could harden into something sentimental and therefore sloppy. The ocean under the ice was not my brother. The point was not resemblance. The point was that I was already calibrating my language around the possibility of harming something I did not yet know how to classify, and that calibration was making me write like a survivor of one failure preparing a second.

I went back to the old board exchange one more time. There was a reply from Elias beneath my recommendation, one I had not remembered at all.

Understood. Logging my objection so nobody has to reconstruct it later.

The line blurred before it sharpened again. I realized I had leaned so far toward the screen that my ribs were pressed against the desk edge. A pulse beat hard behind my eyes. I could see him with painful clarity, not dying, not even frightened, just standing square to a panel with one shoulder angled toward the heat, entering the objection because somebody had to leave a true thing where the record would not be able to pretend surprise later.

No accusation. No plea. Just a line placed in the record because he understood, better than I had, that reconstruction comes after harm and always sounds wiser than timing allowed.

My forearm brushed the tablet on the shelf and woke the extraction countdown. Thirty hours, seven minutes. Beneath it Serran’s workflow queue remained active, awaiting the preliminary classification his office would want before station morning. If I sent the draft now, command would have what it needed to keep the anomaly scientifically interesting and operationally ignorable. If I withheld it, I bought time measured in hours, maybe less, while also making myself easier to isolate once they asked for the deliverable I had not provided.

I copied the draft paragraph into the queue field and watched the send control turn from gray to white.

That was the worst moment, not the old archive, not Elias’s note, not the casualty summary. A form almost complete. A cursor waiting. The body recognizes some thresholds before the mind agrees to name them. The muscles along my back had locked into a hard vertical line. My jaw hurt. Two fingers on my right hand had gone numb from how long they had held themselves above the panel without moving.

I thought of the sign-off block on Khepri, of the phrase at present, of how many dead forms begin with honest uncertainty and end with preventable permission. I thought of Linh in the acoustic bay asking which failure mode I preferred. I thought of Pavel trying to keep a stronger denial off the record and perhaps not understanding that a softer one could still do enough damage. Then I looked again at the send control and understood with unusual clarity that if I pressed it, the sentence would stop belonging to me and start belonging to the station.

I selected the entire recommendation block.

Deleted it.

The wall screen went blank except for the header and the waiting field beneath it. On the shelf the older memo still sat open with its defensible cautions and balanced verbs. I deleted that too, line by line this time, feeling each phrase resist because I had built them carefully.

The cursor remained at the top of an empty report.

Outside the berth, a station-night announcement chimed softly over the local speaker net: shift transition in forty minutes, maintenance pause on ring C, routine reminder to log fatigue impairments before hazardous duty. The voice was synthetic and almost gentle. I sat in the dim berth light with the old mission archive on one screen, the blank preliminary classification field on the other, and the altered harmonic sequence suspended in the corner like a question no procedure aboard Anselm had been designed to hold.
