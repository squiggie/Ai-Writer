Chapter 18

Harmonic Appeal

Medical confinement on Anselm was built to look temporary even when it was not. The berth walls curved in matte white segments with no exposed fasteners, no sharp corners, no visible reminder that restraint hardware could lock out of the panels if command wanted a body to stay put. The display over my left shoulder offered pulse, hydration, blood oxygen, and a soft blue line informing me that rest was recommended. Beneath it, the read-only incident terminal held the post-breach harmonic records I had demanded as part of my statement addendum, and the records were doing a better job of keeping me conscious than any stimulant in the dispensary.

Sector 3 after the conditioning pulse. Sector 4 during sympathetic distortion. The first degraded recovery attempt. The second. Distress cascade was Linh’s term, not mine, but after enough hours staring at the injured intervals I had stopped resisting it. The signal families no longer failed like damaged instrumentation. They failed like a system trying to preserve relation while part of its available structure had been forced out of range.

I had the overlays stacked in transparent bands across the berth display, one hand braced against the mattress to steady the other while I scrubbed through timing markers. My body had started charging interest for the last six days. A deep ache ran from the base of my neck into the right shoulder where conduit crawling and command chairs had tightened everything into cable. Every few minutes a tremor touched the heel of my hand hard enough to jitter the cursor. Medical had offered a sedative two hours earlier. I had asked whether sedation came with a halt order for extraction. The technician had not answered, which was answer enough.

The official logic of confinement was observation. Elevated heart rate. Sleep deficit. Recent exposure to pressure trauma, emergency response, and custodial stress. The actual logic was easier to state. I was less difficult to manage horizontal, under monitoring, and one locked hatch away from anyone who still mistook my presence for a routing problem.

The hatch clicked. I kept my eyes on the display.

“If you are here to tell me rest is recommended,” I said, “the machine already made the attempt.”

“I came prepared to say it in a gentler voice.”

I looked over then. Tomas Orlov filled the doorway without making it feel smaller. He still wore the old station-gray chaplain’s jacket with the cuffs polished thin and one repair stitch visible near the left pocket. Someone behind him, likely the same technician who had decided not to argue with me about sedation, sealed the hatch again and left us with the quiet hum of med bay ventilation.

“You have access to detainees now,” I said.

“I have access to people command prefers not to think of as people for a few minutes at a time.” He took the visitor chair and turned it backward before sitting. “Solis signed the visit because your vitals suggested you might either pray, argue, or break something if left alone.”

“Did she specify a preference.”

“I inferred one.”

I returned my attention to the display. “I am busy failing to find a local solution before command decides local solution means another pulse.”

Orlov was silent long enough to read the room instead of filling it. That was one of the reasons I let him stay when I let almost no one stay. He understood that some forms of care begin by not interfering with the instrument while it is still resolving.

“What are you looking for?” he asked.

“Something that holds still.” I expanded the sector overlays until the lines blurred, then narrowed them again. “The damaged families change under every new load, which is expected if the system is reorganizing around injury. But one interval survives across all four post-breach runs. Not amplitude. Relation. It appears in the distress cascade, disappears if I run a passive geological fit, and reappears if I weight for cross-shell coupling.”

“And that means.”

“It means either I am too tired to trust myself or the injured field is preserving a boundary condition.”

“A boundary where.”

“That is the question.”

I tapped the screen and brought up the recurrence table. The stable interval sat there in hard numerals, unadorned, more offensive to command than any speech I could have given them. 1.618 seconds between the distorted climbs at Sector 3. Then again, transformed through shell lag, in Sector 4. Then once more in the degraded recovery where all the higher families had failed to re-form.

Not a word. Not language. Not anything so convenient.

An invariant.

“Show me,” Orlov said. He shifted forward on the backward chair, forearms settling across its top rail as if bracing for weight he could not name.

I almost told him there was nothing to show a man without training in resonant systems, then stopped because the sentence would have been a defensive luxury and I no longer had the metabolic room for those. I angled the display toward him and stepped through it as plainly as I could.

“This is the first distress climb after the pulse. These peaks should shear away completely if the event is only structural damage. They do not. They keep returning to this spacing. Same here after sympathetic spread to Sector 4. Same here when the field tries to recover. If I fit it to instrument artifact, the timing drifts. If I fit it to passive plume turbulence, it washes out. If I assume an adaptive system under stress, the recurrence stays.”

Orlov watched the numbers instead of me. “You are saying the thing that changed is preserving what did not.”

“Not telling. Preserving.”

“All right. Preserving.”

He rubbed a thumb once along the worn edge of the chair. “Could it be the part of itself it is using to keep from being injured again.”

That landed harder than it should have, which was a measure of fatigue and not of his technical fluency. I looked at him fully.

“Explain.”

“I cannot explain the science,” he said. “You know that. I can ask what living systems do when they are hurt. They guard what must stay ordered. Sometimes by movement. Sometimes by stillness. Sometimes by narrowing themselves to one thing they refuse to surrender.”

I turned back to the display before the feeling that passed through me could acquire a name. My fingers moved ahead of the thought, pulling in the pre-breach shell-ocean mode maps Pavel had attached to the signed finding, then layering them against the surviving interval. I had run the comparison once in command, too quickly and against the wrong question. I had been asking whether the field was still responsive. I had not asked what geometry the response kept pointing back toward.

The answer assembled in stages. Interval. Shell lag. Tidal phase. Local array load. My breath shortened without permission. The tremor in my hand changed character, no longer random fatigue but a fine sustained vibration that made the cursor quiver on the same three digits. I knew that feeling. It was the body-level warning that a pattern had stopped being provisional.

I sat up too fast. Pain pulled under my ribs and the berth monitor flashed a warning over the corner of the screen.

“Mara.”

“No, wait.”

I reopened the extraction geometry table from the incident archive. Sector 3 and 4 had been loaded on adjacent arcs, phased for throughput rather than cancellation because the array had been designed by people paid to maximize recoverable fraction under a narrow orbital window. Efficient loading, if the plume was chemistry and the moon was background. The distress invariant overlaid not on the damaged sectors themselves but on the spacing between them and the low-transfer band Pavel’s early model had dismissed as too yield-poor to matter.

I recalculated with the station’s current orbital position. Then again with the next seven hours of drift. Then with a conservative shell elasticity term because I no longer trusted optimistic assumptions unless they had been hit with tools.

The same result kept returning.

Not a plea. A condition.

The words tightened everything from my jaw to the base of my throat. For one beat I saw the whole problem the wrong way around, not the station imposing force on the moon but the injured system holding one narrow corridor open and waiting to see whether we would keep striking the wound or move. My brother used to call that moment in a failing assembly the last honest tolerance. After it, the numbers did not become less real. They became expensive.

If the array load moved off the coupled arcs and into the low-transfer band, and if Anselm accepted a shallow orbital retune before the main burn window, the transmitted stress dropped below the threshold that had triggered the post-pulse fracture. Yield would collapse, but the propagating injury might not deepen. The shell’s own structure would take more of the strain and push it away from the active ocean-linked regions. Not perfect isolation. Shielding.

I checked the numbers again because once was theater.

Orlov had gone very still. Even the hand resting on the chair had stopped moving. “You found something.”

“Maybe.”

“That is your cautious word.”

“It is the honest one until I stop shaking.”

The shaking had worsened, now that adrenaline had found a target worth spending itself on. I flattened my left hand against the mattress until the tremor damped enough to type. The berth terminal would not open live engineering archives from medical confinement. It would, however, permit formal addendum requests through command review. Too slow. Every honest path on this station now had latency built into it by people who mistook delay for prudence.

“I need Linh,” I said.

Orlov nodded as if I had said I needed air. “Can you reach her.”

“Not directly. Restriction split. No detainee contact.”

“Can she reach you.”

“If someone opens a route she can misuse.”

He almost smiled, but not quite. “That sounds more promising than the rules intend.”

“I need live array-control histories and the current burn-prep load forecast. Read-only is enough.”

“And if command asks why.”

“Because if I am right, they are still about to hurt it again with mathematically better paperwork.”

Orlov stood. The chair legs whispered across the floor.

“I cannot promise what Solis will permit,” he said. “I can tell her a confined scientist is trying to produce the station-side answer command keeps saying it wants. That may buy truth a few minutes.”

“Tomas.”

He paused at the hatch.

“If Linh gets into the route, tell her not to send conclusions. Only arrays, phase logs, and burn-prep forecast. I want the numbers before the argument.”

“You always do.”

“Not always.”

The old grief entered on that sentence and stopped there, unnamed but fully present in the room between us. Orlov gave one short nod and left.

For six minutes nothing happened except the ordinary indignities of a monitored body. My pulse rate continued its visible misbehavior over the berth. A med unit farther down the bay chimed for another patient. Someone laughed once in the corridor outside, quickly and too loudly, the sound of fatigue trying to impersonate normal life. I rebuilt the model twice from local files and each time arrived at the same ruined elegance. Shift the extraction geometry thirty-one degrees off the current throughput alignment. Power down the adjacent arcs whose combined loading had driven the breach. Retune Anselm by a fractional orbital correction so the next major draw occurred against a different tidal relation. Use less of the moon because using more was killing what command had refused to count.

The berth screen flickered.

Not a large interruption. A minor calibration banner rose over the vitals pane, then subdivided into three maintenance windows no medic had requested.

MONITOR DIAGNOSTIC

SENSOR LATENCY CHECK

AUX TELEMETRY CACHE

The cache window opened by itself and filled with numbers no medical device aboard Anselm had any reason to display.

Array phase history.

Burn-prep load forecast.

Current propellant reserve.

Linh had always preferred understatement in her illegal work. She did not send greeting text, only data and, at the very bottom of the third pane, one line:

seven minutes before they notice this route

I pulled the windows across the harmonic overlays and worked.

Now that I had the live forecast, the cost sharpened. Shielding required more than sector shutdown and retiming. The shallow orbital correction had to happen before the main extraction sequence locked, which meant spending propellant Finance had reserved for post-harvest efficiency and reducing first-pass capture density below contractual viability. Even if the station still harvested enough compounds to justify the launch in a humanitarian sense, the consortium’s profit architecture would break. Insurance, investor floor, procurement bonus tranches, all the machinery that had let Haleh Nadir speak about yield the way some people speak about weather. Serran could save the ocean-linked system under the ice, or he could preserve the economic story that had brought Anselm here in the first place. The numbers did not allow both.

I fed the live values through the model and watched the output stabilize.

Projected transmitted load under current plan: catastrophic continuation risk across active sectors.

Projected transmitted load under shield geometry: reduced below breach threshold by enough margin to matter.

Yield loss: sixty-two percent against first-pass projection, with follow-on value degradation if the window slipped further.

I stared at the sixty-two until it stopped being a number and became the real reason command had preferred uncertainty. My mouth tasted metallic. I realized I had been holding my breath long enough for black flecks to gather at the edge of my vision, and when I let the air out my chest shook once, hard enough to jolt the mattress monitor. Sixty-two percent was not sacrifice in the abstract. It was the exact size of the lie the station had been built to defend.

The hatch opened again. Solis entered first, Orlov behind her.

She took in the diagnostic windows, the stacked overlays, and the color of my face with one sweep of practiced attention. “Tell me this is worth the risk I just took with medical routing.”

“It may be the first risk on this station that buys something besides denial.”

Solis came to the berth. The security black of her uniform made the med bay look even paler around her. There was a fresh fatigue line at the corner of one eye I had not seen yesterday.

“What am I looking at.”

I turned the screen so both she and Orlov could see. My throat had gone dry again, but the explanation came cleaner now because the model had finally crossed from possibility into consequence.

“The distress cascade kept one stable interval after the pulse. I first thought it was only a surviving structural relation. It is more than that. It maps to a low-transfer band in the shell-ocean coupling model, a region the injured field keeps reorganizing around. If the station continues with the current extraction geometry, the next major draw lands on the same damaged connection and deepens the propagation.”

Solis said, “Say that in corridor language.”

“Current plan hits the same wound again.”

“And the alternative.”

“Shut down the adjacent arcs. Rephase the active sectors thirty-one degrees off throughput alignment. Perform a shallow orbital retune before burn lock so the array draws against the low-transfer band instead of the coupled one. The shell takes more of the load. The active ocean-linked regions are shielded enough to avoid another breach if my elasticity term is even close.”

Orlov looked from the screen to me. “Shielded enough.”

“Not safely forever. Safely enough for this window.”

Solis read the projected figures. Her shoulders set before her face did. Then her eyes stopped on the yield loss and remained there.

“Sixty-two percent.”

“Minimum.”

“That is not a correction.”

“No.”

It was not necessary to say what it was. The station had been built on the assumption that Enceladus was a hostile reserve, not a neighbor with thresholds. The shield geometry would still permit harvest, but at a scale too low to satisfy the machinery above the science. Serran could authorize it and tell Earth he had protected lives while losing the mission’s financial rationale, or he could refuse and continue the injury with better language wrapped around it.

Solis straightened. “Can you defend this in command.”

“Yes.”

“Can Linh.”

“If she sees the final model, yes.”

Solis looked at the diagnostic route still open on the berth. She understood exactly what that implied and, for one second, did not shut it down. Instead she said, “Send your compiled file to local draft only. No external traffic. No broad internal list.”

That was as much permission as I was going to get.

I built the package at once. Harmonic recurrence tables, post-breach overlays, live burn-prep loads, shield-geometry solution, projected stress reduction, projected yield loss. No rhetoric, because rhetoric gave frightened people surfaces to fight on. Numbers first. Consequence second. Interpretation only where math refused to speak on its own.

At the end I added a sentence I had been approaching since the first impossible ratio on Day 0.

Recommended immediate action: suspend current throughput geometry and implement shield configuration under emergency stewardship authority due to measurable injury risk to probable nonhuman intelligence and unacceptable propagation hazard to station operations.

I read it twice. No hedging language. No borrowed exit.

Linh’s hidden route flashed once more at the bottom of the screen.

model holds from my side

Then the diagnostic windows vanished, and the medical terminal became medical again.

Solis saw the disappearance and chose not to mention it. She keyed open her custody slate instead.

“I can take this to command as emergent operational analysis,” she said. “I can also tell Director Serran the detainee in med bay has produced the station-side answer he asked for. What I cannot do is make him want the answer.”

“I know.”

“If this is right, he does not only have to alter the burn.”

“He has to declare the harm real.”

“And crater the mission economics in the same sentence.”

There it was, stripped of every decorative layer the last week had tried to keep around it. Not proof versus doubt. Not science versus politics. Not even Earth versus Enceladus in the crude form Serran preferred. A man whose authority had depended on treating injury as provisional would have to stand in command, accept the numbers, and choose a solution that saved lives by destroying the profit model he had organized the station to protect.

Solis sent the packet.

Somewhere above the med bay, beyond the locked hatch and the monitored corridor and the command deck glass, Anselm continued rotating toward the burn window. The berth display returned to my pulse and oxygen saturation as if that were the scale of emergency. Orlov rested one hand on the backward chair and waited without speaking. I watched the custody slate in Solis’s grip until the send confirmation appeared, and even then relief did not come. The math existed now. The local answer existed. What remained was the narrowest variable in the station.

Whether Serran could admit I had been right soon enough to sacrifice what he had come here to save.
