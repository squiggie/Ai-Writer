The command summons did not come through the anomaly queue. It came through logistics.

That was the first sign I had misunderstood the shape of the room closing around me.

At 12:07 station time, eleven minutes after the upload demand, my wall display in plume analytics blanked the local lattice maps and replaced them with a route header instructing me to report to Procurement Annex B with full sortie telemetry, particulate findings, and provisional extraction-impact assessment. The header carried Serran’s authorization string and the procurement director’s countersign beneath it. No security escort. No compliance language. Just a meeting location I had never been asked to enter in person.

Linh read over my shoulder and made a short sound through her nose.

“They’re widening the audience,” she said.

“Or narrowing the purpose.”

The telemetry bundle sat in a sealed local partition awaiting transfer. I had already split out the raw skiff stream from the annotated findings package. The difference mattered. Raw data still had time in it. Interpretation could be pinned down and cross-examined. The command request had asked for both. The procurement summons asked specifically for extraction impact.

Linh pushed off the bench and caught the overhead rail. “You want me there.”

“Yes.”

“You think they’ll let me stay.”

“No.”

She watched me lift the sample case from the lock drawer and slot my tablet beside it. The muscles at her jaw had gone hard under the skin. “If they separate us, don’t let them make this about instrumentation reliability. That’s where they’ll start.”

“I know where they’ll start.”

“Not what I meant.” She let go of the rail and dropped lightly to the deck. “They’ll try to get you to admit the particulate result only matters if someone gives it agency language. Don’t take that bargain. Phase-locked transport is enough to hurt them.”

Enough to hurt the schedule, perhaps. I was less certain it would hurt the underlying argument. The part of my career that once assumed good data necessarily altered bad decisions had worn thin.

We left the lab together. The corridor outside plume analytics smelled of scrubbed air, hot electronics, and the faint metallic wetness that clung to sample bays no matter how often the filters cycled. Shift traffic had thickened. A maintenance crew floated past with a folded radiator panel between them, boots grazing the deck only when station spin gave them enough weight to remember to walk. Two med techs crossed toward central lift with cold packs clipped to a tray harness. Every face we passed carried the ordinary compression of a station inside deadline. Nobody knew that my sample case might become evidence in an argument they had not been invited to hear.

The route header pulled us down a branch off central command that I had used only for data handoffs. Procurement Annex B sat beyond the supply arbitration rooms and budget offices, in a section of Anselm whose walls were cleaner than the science decks and whose light was tuned a fraction warmer, as if administrative decisions benefited from pretending they had not been made inside a machine. The overhead displays here did not show plume maps or orbital hazard bands. They showed cargo tonnage, reserve curves, launch windows, and demand projections scrolling in disciplined columns.

At the annex threshold, a logistics aide with command silver at the collar held up a hand. “Dr. Vale only.”

Linh did not slow. “Systems engineer Ekwueme is attached to the validation track and witnessed the sortie.”

“The director requested Dr. Vale.”

“Then the director requested an incomplete description of the evidence.”

The aide kept her voice even. “You may wait outside.”

Linh looked at me. It was not a plea. It was calibration. How much open resistance now, how much later. I felt the answer arrive in the same place that had hesitated over the upload authorization.

“Wait here,” I said. “If I’m not out in thirty minutes, pull Pavel in and make noise.”

“I can make noise much sooner than that.”

“I know.”

She handed me a slim encrypted wafer from her wrist slate. “Second copy of the phase-separated collector trace. Off the command net.”

I palmed it without looking down and slid it into my sleeve pocket. The aide pretended not to notice. That told me more than an objection would have. They did not want a scene in the corridor. Not yet.

The annex door opened on a room wider than it needed to be, built around a recessed table and a curved wall display three meters high. I stopped just inside because the display had already populated and because, for one disorienting second, I thought I had walked into a casualty briefing.

Human silhouettes rotated in pale blue columns across the screen. Not names. Not faces. Stacked demographic abstractions labeled by region and projected survivability under treatment-availability scenarios. Beneath them ran supply curves for the compounds Anselm had been commissioned to extract from Enceladus plume fractions: catalytic bio-reactives, lattice-templated antivirals, immune-modulating substrates used in organ salvage and post-collapse waterborne disease management.

I knew, in outline, what the harvest was for. Seeing the need rendered as comparative deaths per missed quarter was different. My hand shifted on the sample-case handle before I told it to. The polymer seam pressed into my palm. I adjusted my grip and felt the tendons answer up my wrist, small mechanical protest against a number I had not agreed to carry.

A subtitle at the lower edge of the display read:

Estimated preventable mortality increase under one-window harvest delay: 13.8 to 21.4 million Earthside over 30 months.

The digits held steady. My vestibular sense gave a brief false dip, the kind I usually got when station spin and visual motion disagreed. The room had not moved. Only the scale had.

Voices carried from the far side of the room. I had entered through a side access, not the main door, and for a few seconds nobody turned. Serran stood at the head of the table in command black, one hand resting flat against the polished edge as if steadying the numbers into place. Beside him sat procurement director Haleh Nadir, gaunt and exact in station gray, with three logistics officers, a legal analyst, and two people on delayed Earthside feed whose faces lagged half a beat behind their own expressions.

Nadir was speaking when I came in. “The conservative model still assumes successful interpolation from prior harvest purity, which this window may not match. If quality drops by seven percent, downstream casualty reduction falls out of tolerance by midyear.”

One of the Earthside figures flickered. “We can source partial substitution from Ganymede synthesis for pediatrics.”

“For pediatrics in treaty districts,” Nadir said. “Not for flood-belt outbreaks or organ preservation chains. Substitution capacity is already oversubscribed.”

Serran looked up then and saw me. He did not appear surprised. That annoyed me more than if he had staged the moment with visible intent.

“Dr. Vale,” he said. “Come in.”

The room shifted just enough to acknowledge me as a variable and not as a person. Chairs angled. A legal slate dimmed. One of the logistics officers reached to receive the sample case. I kept hold of it until he met my eyes and said, quietly enough to sound procedural rather than acquisitive, “Cold storage only.”

I handed it over.

Serran gestured to the empty place halfway down the table. “You are in time for context.”

“I was told to bring extraction-impact assessment, not attend procurement.”

“Today those are the same meeting.”

I took the chair but not the offered data port. My tablet remained offline in front of me. On the wall, the mortality projections continued cycling by region, treatment chain, and delay interval. Basin fever. Renal collapse sequelae. Cryogenic tissue salvage failures. Waterborne parasite resistance. The language was technical, actuarial, cleaner than blood and harder to dismiss because it had passed through so many hands before reaching this room.

Serran touched the display and the silhouettes resolved into a rotating Earth with disease-burden bands layered over population density. “You know the science side of the harvest,” he said. “I am not sure you have ever been required to sit inside the full dependency model.”

“If this is meant to instruct me in urgency, the countdown displays have been adequate.”

“Urgency is the wrong category. Tradeoff is the category.”

That landed more honestly than I expected. I looked at him then, really looked. Lack of sleep had begun to mark him too, at the eyes and mouth, though command grooming and anger control hid it better than mine did. He was not performing compassion for the room. He was carrying a burden he believed he had already accepted.

Nadir slid a data block toward me across the table. “Delayed harvest scenarios, adjusted for revised particulate uncertainty if your findings imply reduced extraction efficiency.”

I did not touch the block. “My findings are not about efficiency.”

“Everything aboard this station is about efficiency once it reaches procurement,” she said.

Serran let that stand. “Dr. Vale, before you present, I want the operating frame plain. If your new particulate result indicates an unresolved anomaly with possible impact on skim geometry, pressure response, or legal exposure, we will assess it. We assess it inside the fact that a lost window is not an abstract inconvenience. It is a measurable increase in human death.”

The legal analyst to my right brought up a second screen. Lines of policy text populated beside the mortality map.

Recognized life-detection trigger

Operational biosignature threshold

Extraction suspension standard under environmental ambiguity

I felt the room settle into its actual geometry. Not around truth. Around admissibility.

“Environmental ambiguity,” I said. “That is the phrase you use for the possibility that something under the ice is organized enough to respond to load.”

The legal analyst answered, not Serran. “That is the phrase the consortium charter uses until recognized criteria are met.”

“Recognized by whom.”

“By standards ratified before this mission launched.”

Meaning by a framework built to govern dead oceans.

Serran folded his hands. “Present your findings.”

I brought the tablet online on local power only and projected the phase-separated lattice maps onto the lower third of the wall beneath the mortality curves. The juxtaposition was obscene in a way I could not challenge procedurally. Human loss above. Mineral morphology below. Competing claims entering the same arithmetic.

“Near-field particulate sampling from the unauthorized maintenance sortie shows phase-dependent variation in mineral transport,” I said. “Silicate lattice regularity, brine-film retention, and electrostatic behavior shift in lock with the harmonic interval already observed across sensor paths and stress-transfer modeling. The relation survives local turbulence and reorganizes after shear disturbance without degrading into stochastic spread.”

One logistics officer frowned at the wall. “Translate.”

“The plume is not carrying matter as a statistically indifferent outflow. At specific harmonic phases, particulate populations arrive sorted and structured in ways bulk turbulence does not explain.”

“Structured by geology,” Nadir said.

“Possibly. Not adequately on current models.”

Serran spoke before she could continue. “Operational consequence.”

There it was again. Not what is true. What does it force us to do.

“If the relation reflects adaptive or load-responsive organization below the shell, extraction may not be interacting with an inert environment.”

“May,” Nadir repeated.

“Yes.”

“Could also reflect coupled fracture mechanics under tidal stress,” the legal analyst said. “Unusual, scientifically important, operationally inconvenient, but not protected.”

“Our geophysical edge cases are shrinking.”

“Shrinking is not crossing.”

The Earthside figures watched without speaking. Delay turned them into witnesses from a slower moral weather.

Serran stepped away from the head of the table and moved nearer the wall display. He touched the mortality projection and collapsed the ranges into a single line trending upward across thirty months.

“I want this explicit,” he said. “Suppose you are right in the stronger interpretation you have not formally advanced. Suppose the ocean expresses some distributed patterned activity more complex than geology and more coordinated than our present categories are prepared to name. We still remain responsible for people whose deaths are not hypothetical. That is the problem. Whether ambiguity below the ice outweighs certainty here.”

He tapped the rising line on the wall with one finger. The gesture was controlled, almost gentle. That made it worse.

“Thirteen point eight million at the low end,” he said. “Twenty-one point four at the high. That is one missed window before substitution failures cascade. Not all on us. The world is larger than this station. But enough on us that nobody at this table gets to pretend the harvest is greed dressed as necessity.”

The air system whispered at the vents. Somewhere in the ceiling, a relay ticked. I heard Elias then, not as memory exactly, but as a caution embedded in my habits: when a system tells you the load path, believe the load path. This room was a load path. Any evidence entering it would come out shaped for whatever burden the structure had been built to carry.

“I am not calling the harvest greed,” I said.

“Good,” Serran said. “Then we can proceed like adults.”

I kept my hands flat on the table because I did not trust what they would reveal if I let them move. “The issue is not greed. The issue is whether you are counting only deaths that fit existing ledgers.”

Nadir’s gaze sharpened. “That is rhetoric.”

“No. It is classification pressure. If harm can only count once a category exists for it, then the category becomes a permit.”

The legal analyst looked almost sympathetic, which in that room was another kind of resistance. “Standards exist because action under uncertainty can destroy human systems as quickly as negligence can.”

“And delayed action under uncertainty can destroy everything else.”

Serran turned from the wall. “Everything else. You hear yourself, I assume.”

“I hear the limits of the available language.”

“Then let me improve it.” He returned to the table and rested both palms on its edge. “You have evidence of a repeatable anomaly. Strong evidence. You have evidence the anomaly couples to stress transfer and particulate behavior. You do not have recognized life detection. You do not have demonstrated sentience, legal personhood, or even a defensible biological architecture. What you have is a scientifically serious ambiguity emerging at the exact point in the schedule where delay carries a known human cost. My responsibility is to hold that asymmetry.”

I could see every joint in the argument as he laid it down. Known deaths. Unknown life. Existing standard. Speculative interpretation. Burden of proof assigned toward the silent party because the speaking party had bodies, votes, families, treaties, supply chains. It was clean enough to persuade decent people. An older version of me, the one who signed Khepri inside bounded uncertainty, would have admired its discipline.

“Your responsibility,” I said, “is also to keep us from damaging something before the standard learns how to count it.”

“Within reason.”

“Whose reason.”

“Mission reason. Civil reason. The kind that keeps children alive when philosophical purity would rather delay shipment for one more paper.”

The word purity landed like contamination. Not an insult. Placement. He had put me on the side of abstraction and himself on the side of triage.

I opened the collector trace Linh had given me off-net and projected it beside the lattice maps. The secondary periodicity brightened on the wall, phase-locked where it had no right to be if transport were merely noisy matter under load.

“This is not one more paper,” I said. “The relation is surviving phase separation, charge sorting, stress disturbance, and export into vacuum. If extraction hardware enters the plume field before we understand what it is coupling to, we are not only observing. We are intervening.”

Nadir leaned forward at last and studied the trace with actual concentration. Her right thumb moved once over the edge of her slate, a small habitual stroke, as if she were already sorting the question into columns. “Could intervention degrade yield without broader environmental consequence.”

“Yes.”

“Could it increase yield by collapsing the structured fraction into more recoverable particulate.”

The room went still in a new way. My jaw locked first. Then my pulse hit hard under it, a blunt count I could feel in the hinge below my ear. I turned to her too quickly and felt the tendons in my neck answer.

“You are asking whether disruption might be profitable.”

“I am asking for the complete operational envelope.”

No one else spoke. A logistics officer stopped with his stylus half a centimeter above the table display. One of the Earthside feeds glitched and caught on a frozen blink. Nadir herself did not look embarrassed. She looked efficient.

“No,” I said, hearing how flat my own voice had gone. “You are asking the question you would ask if the anomaly’s value becomes easier to defend than its protection.”

Serran cut in before she answered. “Enough.”

But the floor had already shown through. Procurement had not been brought in merely because my data might threaten the schedule. They were here because any patterned behavior in the plume would be parsed at once for throughput, legal risk, and recoverable fraction. If the ocean below the ice was organizing matter, the station’s first institutional reflex would be to model that organization as process variance.

I could not make that simple by calling it evil. It was colder than that. It was administration.

Serran’s expression changed by less than a degree, and yet the meeting hardened. “Dr. Vale, I invited you here because I need your science attached to the real burden it imposes. Not because procurement is entitled to every speculative fear you can generate from an unfinished dataset.”

“It is not unfinished in the direction that matters.”

“Everything is unfinished in the direction that matters.” He straightened. “Here is my decision. The sortie telemetry and particulate findings move under classification hold. Extraction geometry remains on schedule pending internal review. You will prepare, by 18:00, a constrained scientific assessment that states exactly what the new data support without interpretive expansion into intelligence claims or legal trigger language. Director Nadir will model operational adjustments if your particulate result affects skim efficiency or hardware tolerance. We do not suspend readiness on this evidence.”

The words arrived in clauses, each one seating itself before the next. Classification hold. On schedule. Constrained assessment. My throat tightened hard enough that the first breath scraped. I took another and tasted recirculated air, old coffee from someone’s cup at the far end of the table, the sterile cold leaking from the sample case now out of my hands. I kept my fingers flat against the table until feeling returned to the tips.

“You had this decision before I entered the room,” I said.

Serran did not answer at once. That was answer enough.

The Earthside feed crackled with lag. One of the distant advisers, a woman with flood-zone insignia at her collar, looked from the mortality line to my particulate maps and said quietly, as if speaking from another weather system, “If she is even partly right, delay may not be the only cost.”

The channel delay swallowed the room for half a second after she finished. By the time anyone here could answer, the remark had already become past tense.

Serran inclined his head to the screen with formal respect and no concession. “Noted.”

Then he looked back at me. “You understand the argument now,” he said.

Yes. With a clarity I had not wanted.

Not the public argument. Not the visible shell about proof standards and disciplined interpretation, though that would be the version entered into record. The deeper one. Human need, quantified early and often enough that every other form of life must survive translation into utility before it could enter the ledger at all. If I brought him stronger evidence tomorrow, he would ask whether it had legal force. If it had legal force, procurement would ask whether accommodation was cheaper than delay. If accommodation failed, the dead on Earth would be summoned again until uncertainty itself looked indecent.

I looked at the mortality line, at the blue silhouettes rotating without faces, at my own particulate maps beneath them like a smaller claim to reality waiting for permission to count. The station had built the case against me before I had chosen the words I would use to make it. That changed the problem. More data alone would not be enough. The next proof would have to arrive somewhere their ledger could not reduce it before anyone else saw what it was.
