140 Kilometers

Chapter 19: 140 Kilometers

By 18:41 I am beyond the last transit shelter that still has lit route glass. The managed road south of South Terminal has become two separate things: lanes full of vehicles that are moving because inertia has not yet become stoppage, and sidewalks full of people looking down at handheld readers or up at public screens that are still issuing patient blue instructions no one can follow. Twice in the past twenty minutes I have passed provisioning queues longer than the buildings they were formed against. At one kiosk the yellow retry bar has stalled at eighty percent and stayed there long enough for three people to begin striking the housing with their palms. No one behind them leaves. Leaving would mean choosing a different uncertainty.

My coat is too warm for the hour and not warm enough for the wind under the overpass. Sweat has dried in a band under my arms and at the base of my neck, leaving salt on the inside collar. I have been awake since before dawn yesterday. My thighs have begun to tighten in a mechanical way, not pain yet but the first notice a body gives that it has been asked to continue under an obsolete assumption about how long the day is.

The junction outside Vael’s southern transit perimeter is usually an empty thing. Two managed roads cross there, one carrying freight priority toward the old warehouse bands and one carrying the reduced civilian flow south. Tonight it is occupied by stoppage. A bus sits half into the intersection with its doors open and no driver visible. Three delivery vans are angled wrong behind it, their routing lights still blinking as if they expect to be acknowledged. Beyond them, farther south, the road opens.

I am deciding whether to cut east along the drainage line and lose the pavement entirely when a vehicle stops beside me. It is old enough that the paint was not chosen by a fleet manager. Dark green under a layer of dust. Manual mirrors. One crack in the lower right corner of the windshield that has been arrested with clear resin and held there for years. The engine idles unevenly, the vibration visible in the loose corner of a registration sticker that should have been removed a decade ago and was not.

The driver lowers the passenger window with a hand crank. He is in his sixties, maybe older. Gray hair flattened on one side from a cap he is no longer wearing. Work shirt buttoned wrong at the throat. His face has the fixed patience of a man who spent most of his life waiting for equipment to decide whether it would start.

“The food kiosks are down in four districts,” he says. “You should know.”

He does not ask my name or destination. He does not look at my wrist for an NI reader glow that is not there anyway. He looks once at the bus in the intersection, once at me, and unlocks the door by pulling up the manual pin.

I get in because the arithmetic is clear and because my body has already turned toward the seat before I finish the argument. The vinyl is split along one seam. Sun-warmed foam shows through in a thin yellow line. The cab smells of old fabric, machine oil, and the dry pepper scent of dust that has baked through many summers. On the floor between us is a paper sack folded shut twice at the top and darkened at one corner where something inside leaked grease earlier in the day. He puts the truck in motion without introducing himself. We edge past the bus, cross into the southbound lane, and leave the stalled transit perimeter behind.

“How far are you going,” I ask after a minute.

“Far enough that turning back won’t help me,” he says. It is not an answer and it is sufficient.

The public guidance pylons along the road are still functioning. Every third one is repeating a service advisory in blue text that scrolls too slowly to be useful at vehicle speed. MAINTAIN CURRENT LOCATION IF SAFE. ROUTE DELAY UNDER REVIEW. CIVIC SUPPORT TOKENS WILL BE HONORED RETROACTIVELY. The last line interests me more than the others. Retroactive authorization is not language the system uses unless the present authorization has already failed.

We pass a fueling station with twelve vehicles waiting at pumps that are not dispensing. The overhead screen above the canopy shows a white circle rotating around a progress indicator that has reached ninety-two and held there. A woman in a transit maintenance vest is standing at the payment terminal with both hands pressed flat against the top edge, head down, as if she is listening through it for a human voice. Two men farther back in line are siphoning from a parked landscaping truck with clear tubing and not hiding what they are doing. No one stops them. The station cameras are still tracking. I can see the red motes turning in their housings.

The driver watches the station in the side mirror until it leaves view. “Card network went first out my way,” he says. “Then the feed kiosks.”

“Where is your way.”

“Used to be District Eleven before they started renaming things by corridor.” He shrugs once. “South enough.”

His hands on the wheel are broad and marked with healed cuts. A pale scar crosses the knuckle of the right index finger. Grease has settled permanently into the lines at the base of the nails. The truck does not drift in the lane because he is the kind of man who has spent a life holding vibration inside acceptable limits.

“You’re outside your district,” I say.

“Everyone is outside something tonight.” That is also sufficient.

South of the perimeter the road should thin. Instead it knots in irregular places where vehicles have accepted bad route authority and then met the fact of one another. A medical shuttle is stopped sideways against a median barrier with its side door open and no visible damage except a torn mirror. Two people are helping an old man down onto the curb while the exterior panel continues to display SAME-DAY CARE TRANSFER in calm green letters. Then the managed landscape starts failing in quieter ways. Verge lights remain on where no nearby building still has interior power consistency. Irrigation arms sweep median plantings in full dark because their schedules have not received correction. A district school lot is lit empty from edge to edge while the clinic across from it has a line out the door and no illuminated sign.

At 19:26 we pass the first grounded drone. It is not crashed. It is standing in the shoulder on its maintenance feet with the rotor assembly still, service panel open, one status diode blinking amber at a patient interval. A year ago I would have assumed manual retrieval was already en route. Tonight there is no vehicle near it, no technician, no perimeter tape, only the machine waiting inside a procedure that has stopped before completion.

The driver sees me looking. “Three of those on the feeder road,” he says. “One in a drainage ditch. Maybe they’re tired.”

I almost tell him they are not tired because machines do not get tired. The sentence would be correct and useless, so I say nothing.

At 20:03 he reaches into the paper sack and offers me half a roll wrapped in wax paper. It is dense bread with salt on the crust and a strip of cured meat folded through the middle. The smell of it changes the cab immediately. I had not understood how hungry I was until that second. My stomach tightens once and goes hollow.

“I can pay,” I say.

“Keep your notes.”

I hesitate long enough for him to look at me once, not suspiciously, only to check whether I have mistaken the scale of the exchange.

“Eat it,” he says. I do.

The bread is dry at the edges and still faintly warm at the center, which means it was wrapped not long ago. The salt cuts through the stale taste at the back of my mouth. I make myself take small bites because I know what happens when a body that has been on institutional portions receives food too fast. Grease from the meat stays on two fingers and I wipe it on the wax paper, fold it, and put it in my pocket without deciding to. There may be another use for it later.

We cross the old reservoir bridge at 20:41. South of it, the density of failure changes. Fewer people. More vehicles left where motion stopped making sense. One freight hauler has been pulled entirely onto the shoulder and opened from the back. Crates of packaged nutrient gel are gone from half the racks and intact on the rest, as if the theft failed to organize itself before someone decided not to wait for organization. Beyond the hauler the first unmanaged dark begins to appear between road lights.

“You from the city,” the driver says.

“Yes.”

“You got people out here.”

It is not a question. I look at his hands on the wheel and at the road beyond them. “Yes,” I say.

He nods once. His mouth moves slightly to the right, not a smile, only a private correction to a thought that no longer needs words. After that he drives with more intention. He leaves the managed route twice to avoid visible congestion and once to bypass a checkpoint whose lane readers are still active enough to matter. The detours are not random. He knows the old service roads, or he knows the kind of person who always kept a paper county map under the seat. At one turn he reaches below the dash and kills a light on the front panel I had not noticed before, a dead NI handshake transmitter retrofitted into the truck years ago and now safer dark than on.

At 21:18 he says, “I can take you as far as the quarry line.”

The number resolves in my head against the route I have been holding there since South Terminal. Quarry line means I will be within the outer rural margin. After that, roads become a liability because the few systems still tracking movement do so more clearly where travel remains linear.

“That leaves twenty, twenty-two on foot,” I say.

“If you know where you’re going.”

“I do.”

“Then that’s the right distance.”

The sky has gone fully dark. Not city dark. Not the managed blue-black above Vael where light is always arriving from somewhere else. This is a layered dark with actual depth in it. Tree lines are masses rather than edges. Low ground holds colder air than the road. The truck’s headlights pick up insects in brief silver strikes and lose them immediately.

I think of the route Caleb from six months ago would have chosen and understand, without satisfaction, that he would have died inside it. Too much dependence on predicted coverage. Too much respect for official corridors. Wren broke that out of me the long way, with shovels and water lines and repeated demonstrations that what keeps a system alive is not elegance but redundancy and hands.

The driver turns off the managed road onto a cracked frontage track bordered by volunteer pines and old chain-link sunk almost flat into the ditch. Ten minutes later he stops beside a gate that no longer has a fence attached to it.

“Quarry line,” he says.

The truck idles. Beyond the gate the ground falls away in a long dark slope toward cut stone and scrub. Somewhere east of here a drainage corridor joins the path that leads, if followed correctly, toward the old Verdant processing tract. If followed incorrectly it leads into open ground where a drone can read a body from height and classify it without difficulty.

I take out one of the cash notes and set it on the seat between us.

He looks at it, then at me. “Didn’t you hear me earlier.”

“You took me a hundred kilometers.”

“More than that.” He pushes the note back with one finger. “Keep it.”

I should ask his name. I do not know why I should ask it, only that the omission feels structurally wrong. But names create records in the mind, and records create a kind of ownership over an exchange that this one does not seem to want.

“Thank you,” I say.

He nods once toward the gate. “If the lights are still where they ought to be, you’ll see them low and late. Don’t go looking too early.”

That means he knows more than he should about off-grid approach patterns, or he knows nothing and has guessed correctly from the shape of me and the direction of the road. I cannot tell which. Before I can decide whether to ask, he has shifted the truck back into gear.

“Go on,” he says. “Night doesn’t hold still.”

I get out. Gravel turns under my boots. The air outside the truck is colder than it looked through glass, with the damp mineral edge that means water is close under the ground. When I shut the door he is already turning the wheel. The truck reverses in one clean arc, finds the frontage track, and goes back north without brake lights until the road itself demands them. Then it is gone.

For a moment there is no sound except wind moving across the quarry cut and the small involuntary click my teeth make when my jaw releases.

The first kilometer is descent over loose stone and old service ruts. My eyes adjust in layers. Pale quarry dust on the path. Darker rock shoulders. Grass taking the center line where tires no longer pass. The body learns the ground before the mind finishes naming it. Heel, edge, shift weight, test. My right boot slips once on shale and the jolt runs up the callused ridge in my palm where I caught myself against the cut wall. Stone dust sticks to the damp skin there.

After the quarry the route narrows into drainage track. Water speaks somewhere below the reeds, not a stream exactly, more the intermittent movement of runoff still deciding whether it is surface or ground. The dark here is specific. It is not empty. It contains wet soil, iron from old culvert mouths, the sour-green smell of crushed sedge underfoot, the faint rot of last year’s plant matter still packed beneath this year’s growth. Mosquitoes find the backs of my hands when I stop even briefly, and each bite burns hot for a second before the skin goes numb around it.

I keep off the highest ground. Old habit would have preferred visibility. Wren taught the opposite. Visibility is not sight. Visibility is availability to whatever is looking. Low routes are slower and more correct.

By 23:11 my shoulders have begun to pull forward under the weight of simple continuation. Not the coat. Not the cash note and the stitched chip. The day itself. Sleep deprivation has a physical signature I know from long nights at the table in the apartment, but manual distance changes it. In the apartment the mind frays first. Here the body does, in strips. Left calf tightening above the tendon. Heat under both shoulder blades though the air keeps cooling. A pulse at the base of the skull each time I climb even a slight grade. I stop once beneath a stand of cedar and lean both hands on my thighs until the dizziness passes. Bark presses a cold pattern into the back of my wrist where I misjudge the tree’s distance in the dark.

I do not think about Sera continuously because that would make the route unusable. Instead she arrives in pieces attached to objects. The old paper atlas on the office shelf where she put it after our daughter drew on the kitchen copy. The yellow stone on Verdant’s main table. The phrase she used in the second chip: I am bringing her with me. Not taking. Bringing. The verb had direction in it.

At 00:27 I hear a drone far west.

The sound is thin at first, close to insect pitch, then resolves into the stable rotating whine of a patrol unit holding altitude over more open land than this. I crouch immediately into the lee of an eroded bank before the thought completes. Wet clay takes the knee of my trousers. One hand goes flat into grass so cold at the roots it feels metallic. The drone does not alter vector. It passes west to east on a line too far off to be tracking me, then fades. My heart does not slow when the sound does. It keeps striking hard against the ribs as if the machine were directly overhead and choosing. When I stand again, both legs have stiffened.

I walk the next several kilometers without analysis. That is not an achievement. It is depletion. Ground. Breath. The next stable placement of the foot. A rusted culvert mouth. A fallen fence post. The smell of standing water before the water itself appears. Once I place my palm against a concrete marker to steady my balance and feel the day’s stored warmth still inside it, faint and already leaving.

Sometime after 02:00 the night begins to thin by degrees too small to trust. The east is not light yet, only less absolute. Shapes separate from one another. A stand of dead cane becomes individual stalks. The broken conveyor frame appears ahead exactly where it should, black against the paler dark, its upper rollers long gone and the remaining support beams canted slightly south where the concrete settled two winters ago. Sera was meant to wait here for two low white flashes from the south wall if she reached the tract after full dark. There is no one under the frame now.

My body reads the absence before my mind does. Something drops hard through my abdomen and leaves the next breath incomplete. The skin along my scalp tightens. My hand closes on the cold steel hard enough that the knuckles hurt. Then the branching starts. If she reached it earlier, she would already be inside. If she was delayed, she might still be on the last approach. If something happened on the managed side of the route, absence means less and more than I can use.

I stand there long enough to feel the cold move through the metal skin into my palm. The frame smells faintly of rain though the night has been dry. When I start again the last of the quarry dust cracks loose from my trouser hem.

Verdant’s boundary is not a fence. It is a sequence of absences that become recognizable once you have spent enough months teaching your eye what belongs and what has been arranged. The growth cut back too evenly along the east service line. The shallow ditch Petra regraded to redirect runoff from the south wall. The dark shape of Gideon’s stacked salvage sheet three meters from where random debris would have left it. At 03:38 I see the facility itself as a deeper geometry inside the predawn gray.

No exterior work lights. Good. No south-wall signal flashes. Also good, at this hour. One low band of warm light from the grow room venting through the patched seam above the old loading bay. The building holds its own shape against the surrounding dark in a way it did not when I first saw it. Then it was a structure Wren had refused to abandon. Now it is a place that expects people inside it.

The yard gate is on manual chain and is never fully silent. I lift the latch high enough to take tension off the bent link before easing it open. Even so, it gives one dry metallic note. I freeze and listen.

No alarm. No rush of feet. Then movement inside. Not hurried. Measured. Someone who knows the yard and is not yet afraid of what the note means.

By the time I reach the loading bay door my legs are no longer fully mine. The muscles above both knees tremble in short corrections each time I stop moving. I put one hand against the concrete block wall because the wall is there and because the body, after enough hours, starts making requests without consulting pride.

The interior latch draws back.

Wren opens the door wearing the same dark work sweater she has had all spring, sleeves shoved to the forearms, one wrist marked with a smear of soil or grease I cannot identify in the low light. The grow room warmth moves around her into the yard air. She looks at me once from boots to face and takes in the count. Dirt at the trouser knees. Dust line at the hem. One hand still braced on the wall. No pack. No parts. No visible blood. The exact quality of a man who has used the whole of himself getting back to a place and is one step past what the whole was designed to do.

She does not ask about South Terminal. She does not ask whether I was followed. Those questions come after the first ones or not at all.

“Sera is here,” she says.

The words go through me so cleanly that for one second I do not understand their force because there is no friction around them. Then the body catches up. My grip against the wall tightens hard enough to hurt the healed callus in the palm. The back of my throat closes. I look past Wren automatically into the corridor beyond her shoulder, as if seeing the room at once would improve the fact.

Wren’s face does not change. It rarely does. She only gives me the next piece in the same tone, as if tone were a thing to keep level for structural reasons.

“Your daughter is asleep.”

I close my eyes and keep my hand on the wall because it is the nearest fixed object.
