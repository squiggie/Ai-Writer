Arrivals

Chapter 21: Arrivals

At 05:32 I hear wood striking wood outside the loading bay in a pattern too regular to be wind and too light to be field work. The facility is only beginning to separate into parts. Pump at low cycle. A pan set somewhere near the west room. One cough from the annex floor that does not resolve cleanly and then stops. The dawn air coming through the corridor seam has the cold mineral edge of ground that gave up heat all night and has not yet received any back. I go out because I have been waking to sounds since Intake East and because sounds that repeat at Verdant usually mean labor or failure.

Marguerite is sitting on an overturned crate against the south wall with a narrow loom braced between her knees. At this distance I cannot immediately identify what she is doing. Her hands are moving in short, exact passes through a field of pale strips that catch the dawn and dull it. Then I get closer and see the shuttle, the tension line, the improvised heddle bar cut from what looks like one of Gideon’s old seed markers. She is weaving. The loom is small enough that she could have carried it on her back in the bag she arrived with, which means that while the provisioning grid was failing and the transit hubs were filling with people waiting for a screen to tell them where to stand, Marguerite decided this object counted as necessary mass.

She says, “Good morning,” without looking up.

“Good morning.”

Her hair is white and pinned with the kind of precision that persists after mirrors stop mattering. She is seventy-four years old, according to the paper card Sera wrote for intake because keeping the ages straight in the first two days exceeded what memory would carry cleanly. Late-stage cardiac disease, managed until three days ago on a medication schedule Wren cannot reproduce exactly because the dosage labels in Marguerite’s bag are half torn and the district formulary that would have clarified them no longer answers in a reliable sequence. She should be resting more than she is. Her ankles say she has not. Her breathing says the same thing in a more private register. Every sixth or seventh pass of the shuttle there is a pause just long enough for the sternum to lift against effort, then the hands continue. The strips on the loom are cut from feed sacks, old shirt cotton, and one length of blue packaging band Petra had been saving because it was too strong to discard and too awkward to classify. Under Marguerite’s hands they are becoming something with sides.

“I thought you might still be asleep,” I say.

“That would not improve the morning.”

She says it in the tone of a person offering an operational correction rather than wit. The half-made basket on her knees has clean corners already. Her fingers know the count without watching for it.

Behind me the loading bay door opens and closes once. Theo, likely, taking first light watch off the south line. Five days ago Verdant held six adults and one child and all of us could still be named without effort while working. Now there are fourteen bodies inside the boundary, and the building has acquired the layered sound of continued occupancy. More footsteps. More cups. More breath at night through thin partitions. The old plant was not built for domestic sound, so every private thing becomes structural. A child turning in sleep. Someone clearing a throat. Wren’s boots stopping outside the west room before moving on because she has decided whoever is inside can carry another hour without intervention.

Marguerite lifts the growing basket, inspects one corner, and tightens it with both thumbs. The motion is so practiced that for a second I can see the years behind it without having access to any of them.

“Do you need anything,” I ask.

“More strips by evening if there are more sacks. If not, this becomes a smaller basket and the world continues.”

I tell her I will find them. My hand is still on the cold edge of the loading-bay door. I have stopped there without meaning to. The fact of her is still taking time to distribute itself correctly through the morning. Not just that she is here. That she carried a loom into the dead zone. That she sat down outside a former processing plant under intake restrictions and began making a container for things that are not yet in it.

Inside, the corridor smells of oats, damp work clothes, antiseptic, and the warm electrical dust that comes off Petra’s battery bank when she has asked too much of it before breakfast. Someone has boiled mint again. The annex is already bright with the gray of early day. Four of the arrivals are sleeping there, if lying still with open uncertainty around the mouth counts as sleep. The floor pallets Gideon cut from insulation wrap and old foam are arranged with exactly enough space to walk between without stepping on a hand if you remember where each body ends. Oren is awake on the second pallet from the wall. His left leg is splinted from mid-calf to ankle with two shaped lengths of plastic siding and a strip torn from what used to be a transit route banner. The fracture began ten days ago, on stairs, according to him. The first district clinic wrapped it and sent him home with a follow-up interval the system did not survive long enough to honor. He tried walking on it after the kiosks failed. The bone healed far enough wrong that Wren had to decide whether rebreaking it here would cost more than leaving it. She chose to leave it and save the analgesics for the nights when the swelling takes the decision out of his face and puts it in the body.

He sees me and shifts up on one elbow. Pain reaches his jaw before the rest of him.

“Any word on the road?” he asks.

“Nothing useful.”

“That means bad.”

“Yes.”

He nods because the translation is obvious and lies back again. On the pallet near his feet, Linh is folding and unfolding the same sweater she arrived in. She and her brother Davi came because a woman outside Halcyon Junction told them there was an old agricultural facility south of the quarry line where systems did not reach cleanly. When Wren heard the description on the first night, she stood still for a second and said, “Mara Venn,” not to either of them, only to the room. Soil pathology, she told me later. Left the managed districts two years ago after her license review was delayed into disappearance. Wren once told her, in a parking lot before rain, that if the state ever became completely stupid there were still cistern lines out here and enough concrete to keep weather off.

That sentence traveled. Two of the seven came by way of Mara Venn. Three came because someone wrote a note in a transit hub bathroom on paper thick enough not to disintegrate under the soap-dispenser drip: OLD VERDANT EAST OF QUARRY. NO DEVICES. WALK AFTER DARK. WAIT LOW. No name. No route explanation. The final two saw the seam of grow-room light above the loading bay and kept walking toward it because in a failing landscape any repeated human light becomes instruction.

At the main table Sera has turned the yard map over and is using the blank side for intake columns. Name. Source. Physical condition. Skills. Family status. Device carried or discarded. The handwriting is hers. The structure of the page is mine. Our daughter is beside Marguerite’s bag on the bench, holding the small loom shuttle and turning it over as if it is a species of tool she has not yet classified. Sera takes it gently from her and hands her a spoon instead.

“Not in your mouth,” Sera says.

Our daughter looks at the shuttle, then at the spoon, measuring the inconsistency of adult rules. She accepts the spoon because Gideon has set oats on the table and hunger is a stronger argument than principle.

Wren comes in carrying Marguerite’s morning pills in a metal lid the size of a coin dish. There are fewer of them than there were yesterday. The reduction is visible even without knowing the schedule. She sets the lid beside Sera’s cup and looks at the page.

“Count again,” she says to me.

“I did at 04:50.”

“Do it with daylight.”

It is not mistrust. Night numbers and morning numbers are different categories. At night the building is weight and sound and the fear of what might reach it before dawn. In daylight the actual bodies return, and arithmetic has to answer to them.

So I count again. Existing residents: six adults, one child. New arrivals: seven. Total: fourteen. One requires cardiac medication continuity we do not fully possess. One requires wound monitoring and controlled loading on a badly healed fracture. One has a cough Wren does not yet trust enough to name as improving. Two can do field labor immediately. One can repair clothing, sort fiber, and turn waste packaging into baskets before breakfast. Davi used to maintain district cold-storage compressors.

Theo comes in from watch with damp cuffs and a face the morning has not improved. “Nothing on the south line,” he says. “One drone west, too far to read. No movement at the conveyor.”

“Eat first,” Wren says.

“I can give the line another pass after.”

“You can give it a better pass after oats.”

He sits. His body does not agree with the sitting until the chair forces the issue. Across from him Petra is already annotating the back of a torn meter carton with fresh loads: kettle, grow beds, pump cycling under fourteen-person wash demand, one extra heat plate because the arrivals do not yet know how slowly Gideon’s stove wants to be asked. Her pencil presses so hard at the turns that the cardboard fibers lift.

Breakfast proceeds at the scale of function. Bowls passed. Apples divided. Marguerite coming in only when Wren calls through the open door that the pills are going warm. Our daughter asking whether the basket will be for seed or socks. Marguerite saying, “For whatever has to be carried without falling through.” Theo looking at that answer longer than the words require.

After the food, the question that has been under the table since the second day comes up where everyone can put hands on it.

“If they found us by light,” Petra says, “we either seal that seam or accept we’re a beacon.”

“We are not a beacon,” Wren says.

“Tell the last two that.”

Gideon wipes the blade of the bread knife on a cloth and folds the cloth once. “People walking in the dark toward any steady light are not receiving an invitation. They’re being human.”

“Humans can still walk a drone to the door.”

That is true enough that no one answers immediately. Sera looks at the intake page. Theo looks at the south wall as if the wall itself could choose to be less visible. Wren rests two fingers on the metal lid where Marguerite’s pills were and says, “We seal the seam better tonight. We keep the low-light protocol. We do not send anyone away for correctly identifying that people live here.”

This is how most decisions now complete. Not as consensus. As the least false sentence in the room.

The day reduces into labor faster than thought. Gideon takes Linh and Davi to the west ditch with spades and a warning about overcorrecting the grade he spent all spring getting right. Petra gives Davi a meter lead and discovers in ten minutes that he can read fault drift without talking himself important in the process. Theo and I shift the annex pallets to create a cleaner path for night movement. Oren insists on helping from a seated position and ends by sorting screws into salvage trays because pain will not let him stand but pride will not let him lie still. Sera inventories textile scraps with Marguerite and our daughter at the south wall, the three of them making separate, incompatible kinds of order from the same heap.

At noon Wren calls me to the west room. Marguerite is on the cot nearest the door, shoes off, ankles elevated on folded blankets, one hand still holding a strip of blue packaging band she forgot to set down when the shortness of breath started. Wren has the stethoscope on her chest and the watch face turned inward at her own pulse. The room is warm from too many bodies and the blocked noon sun on the west concrete. Marguerite’s bag is on the floor open at the mouth. Inside are two dresses rolled with military economy, one wrapped bundle of family photographs that she has not shown anyone, the loom tools, three pill bottles, and a folded dish towel around half a loaf gone hard.

“How long can we stretch it,” I ask quietly.

Wren does not look up. “That is not a useful unit.”

“Then give me the useful one.”

She listens another fifteen seconds, lifts the stethoscope, and says, “If nothing worsens, days. If the road gives us someone with insulin dependence or a child who can’t move air well, those days become arithmetic against other days.”

Marguerite opens her eyes. “I’ve lived a long time,” she says. “You don’t have to talk around me.”

“I wasn’t,” Wren says.

Marguerite almost smiles. It takes effort and is therefore small. “Good. I dislike waste in language.”

When we leave the room, Wren puts the stethoscope around her neck and braces one hand for a second against the corridor block wall. Fatigue is mostly visible in her feet. Today it has reached the hand.

“Mara Venn,” I say.

“Yes.”

“You never expected contact.”

“No.”

“And the note in the bathroom.”

“Also no.”

She looks through the loading bay toward the south wall where Marguerite has resumed weaving on the crate in a patch of afternoon light. Our daughter is beside her on the concrete with a length of rag wrapped uselessly around one hand, imitating the loom with no grasp yet of sequence.

“You can leave a route,” Wren says. “People still have to choose it.” Then she goes back into the medical alcove before I can answer, which is her preferred method when she has already said the whole thing.

By evening the baskets exist in the plural. Marguerite has finished one narrow one for tools and begun a broader one Gideon says will hold potatoes if we had potatoes enough to justify it. Davi has helped Petra cut the flicker on the secondary inverter by half a second during pump transition, which matters because half seconds become cumulative theft when systems are already at ceiling. Linh and Theo have patched the grow-room seam with layered foil and old gasket strip so the leak of light is now a thin line instead of a visible invitation. Oren, angry at his own leg, has sharpened every dull blade in the shed from a chair and left them in a row on the bench with the private satisfaction of a man who has recovered one useful measure of himself.

No one arrives tonight. That is relief and not relief. The road continues whether or not it resolves at our gate.

After supper, after the child is asleep in the west room with one of Marguerite’s unfinished reed coils on the crate beside her because she insisted it was for morning, I take Petra’s revised meter readings and my own notebook to the upper office where the radio first carried the outer-district failures to me. The room still smells faintly of the graphite I ruined five days ago. Now it also smells of more bodies in the building below, more cooked grain, damp socks drying on lines where a foreman once kept throughput charts, and the warm resin note of overloaded wiring that has not yet crossed into danger. I set the numbers in order because order is the only way to see whether the thing is actually failing or merely heavy.

Battery reserve against current draw. Bed five at half-cycle three nights out of seven. Reflective panels moved manually at dusk and dawn, which reduces electrical demand and increases labor debt. Pump load adjusted for fourteen wash volumes, though actual wash behavior will improve once the arrivals stop cleaning their hands with city frequency and start thinking like people who have to keep the tank above its own anxiety line. Annex occupancy without refrigeration demand. West room at current limit. Kettle use restricted. No exterior lights except shielded task lamps at ground level when medically necessary. One compromised meter bezel still holding. One cold-storage-trained arrival reducing Petra’s burden enough that maintenance latency drops from dangerous to merely continuous.

I write every source beside the number that depends on it. Davi, inverter drift. Petra, bed-five relay correction. Gideon, panel movement by hand. Wren, medical load discipline severe enough to keep the cache from disappearing in three bad nights. Sera, intake structure and ration logic under a pressure she did not train for and has accepted anyway. Theo, watch rotation extended without argument after the third arrival. Marguerite, basket capacity from waste material, which is not power exactly but changes handling efficiency enough that it belongs in the edge column. Anonymous note in transit hub bathroom. Mara Venn, parking-lot memory carried two years forward and released at the correct moment into someone else’s panic. Two travelers seeing one seam of light and choosing not to avoid it.

The numbers should not close as tightly as they do. I check them twice for the satisfaction of being able to distrust hope on technical grounds. They still close.

My hand stays flat on the page. I have been holding my breath without noticing it. When I let it out, the top sheet shifts against the board beneath it.

Not comfortably. There is no surplus worth the name. If one battery cell fails early, if one child arrives feverish, if one more badly managed chronic condition crosses the conveyor frame, this page becomes historical rather than predictive. But tonight the loads hold. The water line holds. The food rate can be extended if Gideon’s seed calculations are right and the next two weeks stay within the weather pattern Petra wrote on the wall calendar. The building takes the weight.

Down below, faint through the floor, I can hear the changed sound of Verdant at night. Someone turning in the annex. A low murmur from Sera in the west room that stops before it resolves into words. Theo on the outer step, tapping mud from his boots with the handle of a tool. Marguerite’s cough, then quiet. The colony is no longer the structure I could keep entirely in my head. It has exceeded that category. The input chain for this page includes people I did not recruit, choices I did not witness, and acts of transmission no model available to me would have credited until they arrived carrying their own proof.

I write the final line in the notebook. Power budget closes without margin. Fourteen bodies. Sustainable under current weather and labor assumptions.

I wait a moment to see if the sentence changes under observation. It does not.

Then I turn the page.
