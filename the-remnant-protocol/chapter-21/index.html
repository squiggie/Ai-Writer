Chapter 21: Arrivals

Marguerite is sitting on an overturned crate outside the west wall at dawn with a loom across her knees.

I do not understand what I am looking at for the first few seconds because the motion is too small to register as work from a distance. Her hands move and stop, move and stop. Something pale passes through darker strands and tightens into place. The crate is set where the concrete still keeps a little warmth from yesterday afternoon. Beside her boots is a cloth bag with one strap repaired in blue thread. She has pulled a wool blanket over her shoulders against the morning cold, but her fingers are bare.

When I get close enough to see the frame clearly, I understand that she carried it here herself. Small travel loom. Hardwood smoothed by years of use. One corner burnished darker where a thumb has rested through repetition. The thread she is using is not new. Strips of old garment, unraveled and rewoven. Gray, brown, one narrow line of red that might once have been a cuff or collar.

She does not look up.

“Good morning,” she says.

Her voice has the dryness of age without any weakness in it. She speaks as if the fact of my being here had already been accounted for and does not require inspection.

“Good morning.”

The air at this hour still carries the ditch-water cold. It gets into the joints of my left knee before the rest of me has fully woken. My right heel has finally started closing, but the new skin there complains under the boot seam with each step. Behind us the facility is already making its morning sounds. Pump on. Vent fan lagging two beats before it finds its speed. Someone in the kitchen moving metal against metal with the care people use when others are sleeping nearby.

Marguerite passes the shuttle through one more time, tightens the row with her thumb, and glances up at me at last. Her face is narrow and exact. White hair cut short at the jaw, one side tucked behind the ear. The deep lines around her mouth suggest a person who spent a long time withholding comments that would have been correct and unwelcome.

“Your doctor tells me you designed the battery schedule,” she says.

Wren is not my doctor. She is, among other things, the person most likely to reduce a human being to the function currently needed from him. Marguerite has been here long enough to learn that.

“Part of it.”

“It needs revising.”

She says this with the same calm she might use to observe weather. Not challenge. Not complaint. Only a structural fact.

“Yes.”

She nods once and goes back to the loom. The movement of her hands resumes with no wasted distance.

“This was a scarf once,” she says. “Then a bag strap. It appears to be becoming something else.”

I look at the woven section. It is too narrow for a blanket, too broad for cordage.

“What.”

“I don’t know yet.”

That answer holds me there longer than it should. The first arrival came before dawn on the day after the radio traffic thinned.

Theo saw him from the water-tower rise, a man moving south with one arm held too close to the body and stopping every hundred meters to look back at nothing we could see. By the time Gideon and I reached the east line, Wren had already decided we were bringing him in. He was forty or thereabout, dehydrated, with a radius fracture that had been set badly at some district urgent-care station and wrapped in polymer that cut circulation every time swelling rose under it. His name was Levin Orr. He had worked maintenance for a refrigeration distributor until the routing system stopped sending parts where the parts were needed and sent audit notices instead. When the kiosks went dark in his district, a woman at a manual queue told him there was a doctor south of the dead zone who still saw people as bodies and not categories. He never learned her name. Wren did. Former colleague. Off-grid two years. Dead now, apparently, or at least gone from the route she used to hold. Two of the seven came from that line of rumor.

The next three arrived because someone had written four words in marker on the inside of a transit-hub bathroom stall door: SOUTH OF FREIGHT. LOOK FOR LIGHT. Petra laughed once when we learned this, not because it was funny but because every controlled network in Vael had collapsed into managed nonsense while a person with a marker had produced the most useful routing instruction in the region.

The last two found us because we were visible.

This is the part I continue trying to calculate past. Verdant was never meant to be seen. Even now, with the vent shielding altered and the grow-room bleed carefully cut, there are hours after dusk when the upper slits of the west side show a yellow that does not belong to abandoned ground. Two people walking south through the dead zone saw that yellow and kept walking toward it because there was nothing else in the dark claiming to be inhabited.

None of them came through the dead-drop protocol. None of them came from the names I carried or the lines I traced or the risk models I built to decide who might endure the trip and not betray the route under pressure.

I stand with Marguerite’s loom still in front of me and feel the cold work under the cuff of my coat. The red strand near her thumb disappears into the weave and comes back changed on the other side. A truck panel on the scrap pile behind the wall clicks once as it gives back the night’s chill. My heel pulses inside the boot where the seam rubs the new skin thin again. I had imagined this place surviving by exact admission, by names chosen carefully enough that each new body would arrive already accounted for in some page of mine. Instead a woman with a marker, a dead colleague’s rumor, and a line of yellow light have done work I did not authorize and cannot reproduce.

They are here anyway. Marguerite glances up once, not asking the question and not sparing me from it.

I go inside because the morning schedule has already begun without my consent.

The worktable room no longer resembles the room it was six days ago. The map is still on the center surface under clear sheet, but the rest has changed around it. Marguerite’s medicine list is clipped to the lamp stem with one of Petra’s black binder clips. A pile of rinsed bowls stands upside down on a towel by the sink because cabinet space ran out two arrivals ago. Someone has tied a line across the upper corner near the vent and hung socks there to dry. The room smells faintly of boiled cloth and the mineral chalk of too many wet boots drying near one wall. The yellow stone my daughter painted sits at the edge of the table beside the battery notebook, not because it belongs there but because the room has absorbed her into its logic and left her object where her hand last placed it.

Sera is bent over the clinic shelf inventory with her hair tied back and a pencil caught above one ear. She has started wearing Gideon’s work shirt over her own because the pockets matter. The sleeves are rolled twice and still too long. She looks up when I enter, then down again at the list.

“Marguerite took her morning tablets?”

“Yes.”

“Before coffee.”

“Yes.”

She makes a small mark beside the line and shifts one bottle two centimeters left to align it with the others. Her hands have stopped looking uncertain here. That changed on the third day, somewhere between cleaning the fracture brace for Levin and teaching our daughter how not to touch the lamp chimneys after ignition.

“Levin’s wrap held overnight,” she says. “Wren wants him off carrying loads for two more days.”

“He’ll argue.”

“He already did.”

There is the beginning of something at one corner of her mouth. Not a smile. Recognition of a predictable human behavior. I keep one hand on the chair back and do not move closer because the space between us is working hard enough as it is. The cuff of Gideon’s shirt has fallen loose again over her wrist. She pushes it back with the side of her hand instead of setting down the pencil. Graphite has marked the side of her thumb.

“Petra said the west vent drew high after midnight,” she says.

“I know.”

“I don’t think it was a leak. I think the room occupancy changed the pressure balance.”

“Probably.”

She turns the pencil in her fingers.

“If you re-run the load model this morning, add the second loft sleeping shelf. Body heat up there is changing the thermal loss curve.”

“I will.”

This is the closest thing we have, at present, to ease.

Our daughter comes in carrying a chipped enamel mug in both hands because it is too full for one. She has dirt on one knee and straw caught in the cuff of her sock. Five days here and the dead zone is already becoming legible to her in the direct way children permit a place to become real.

“Marguerite says the red thread used to be a church scarf,” she says.

Neither Sera nor I answer immediately because the sentence has entered the room with more weight than she knows.

“Did she,” I say.

“She says she doesn’t know what it’s making now.” She sets the mug down beside me with grave concentration. “She said maybe that is not the first question.”

Sera’s pencil stops moving.

“What is the first question?” she asks.

Our daughter considers this seriously because she has been asked a thing worth answering.

“If it will hold.”

Then she goes back out before either of us can improve on that.

By noon we are at thirteen adults, one child, and a clinic corner that has stopped pretending to be temporary.

Wren has taken the old parts room and converted it fully. Shelves stripped for clean surface. Cots cut down to fit. Wash basin moved in. The arrivals brought what bodies under stress usually bring with them. Blisters opened raw at the heels. One infected cut at the base of a thumb where a woman named Iona tore sheet metal trying to force a provisioning hatch. Levin’s fracture. Marguerite’s heart condition, managed until this week by a medication pack that now sits on Wren’s tray in a quantity low enough to alter the shape of every conversation about time. The room holds the mixed smell of antiseptic, boiled fabric, sweat dried into borrowed clothes, and the iron note of blood that never entirely leaves once it has been in a place. Even the air has changed. Too many breaths. Not enough wall between them.

Levin is sitting outside the clinic corner when I pass, his bad arm in the rewrapped brace Sera and Wren improvised from old splint stock and boiled cloth. He is using the good hand to peel insulation off copper salvage for Petra, slowly, because forbidden work becomes irresistible the minute a person is told to rest.

“If she catches you,” I say, “she’ll take the knife.”

He keeps working. “Then you’d better keep walking.”

His face has come back from its first-day gray into the color of a living person. There is beard burn along his jaw and a healing split at one eyebrow that never received enough attention to scar neatly.

“How bad is the load.”

He means the population load. People arrive here and begin speaking in Verdant’s categories within hours because the categories are concrete and therefore merciful.

“Bad enough that I’m counting fan-start intervals.”

He nods once.

“Queue where I came from had eleven hundred people by the end,” he says. “You’re talking like thirteen adults and a child is a structural event.”

“It is here.”

He slides the knife under another strip of insulation. “Woman who sent me south kept writing numbers while she talked. Like directions to the end of the world were one more item before lunch.”

“That’s usually how useful information arrives.”

That gets the smallest breath of a laugh out of him and costs him enough in the ribs that he stops there.

The afternoon brings two more people out of the south corridor, not new arrivals this time but near enough to force the same decision. A father and his teenage son, both underfed, both walking north because north still means city in their minds and city still means help in the order the world taught them first. Theo sees them hesitate at the perimeter rise when they catch the facility outline and cannot decide whether a building in dead ground is shelter or trap. Gideon takes water up the line. Wren goes herself because sending anyone less medically precise into a conversation with people that exhausted is how mistakes get made.

They do not stay. The father has a sister in a service district west of here and cannot yet believe that continuing toward her is less rational than stopping at a place he has never heard of. Wren gives him water, directions that account for the burned road, and one packet of dried beans against Gideon’s visible pain. After they leave, Gideon stands in the yard with the empty bucket and looks at the horizon for a long time.

“That was tomorrow’s beans,” he says.

“I know.”

“I am not saying it was wrong.”

He shifts the bucket from one hand to the other. The metal ring taps once against the side.

“People keep finding us.”

“Yes.”

“You expected some.”

“Not this many.”

He glances toward the west wall, where Marguerite’s loom now leans in a patch of sun and our daughter is winding thread back onto the shuttle under instruction.

“Nor did I,” he says.

In the evening, when the heat has gone out of the concrete and the mosquitoes come up from the ditch grass, I climb to the monitoring room with the battery notebook, the site thermal sheet, and the revised occupancy counts.

Petra has already been there an hour. She moved half the wiring off the main table and onto a suspended rack made from old conduit so I would have enough surface to work. Her pencil marks remain everywhere she has been. Frequency blocks. estimated draw. one bulb west loft? check. The handwriting is upright and narrow enough to look like parts inventory from a vanished century.

“I gave you the cleaner chair,” she says without looking up from the solder joint she is reflowing.

“Then you expect this to take a while.”

“I expect arithmetic to behave badly when frightened.”

I sit. The chair gives one soft complaint at the back-right leg. Through the thin wall behind me I can hear the colony in its new configuration. More voices than the room was designed to absorb. Someone coughing in the clinic corner and then apologizing for it. Marguerite speaking to my daughter in the measured tone of a person teaching handwork. Theo on the stair telling Levin, again, that no one here will be impressed if he collapses carrying wire one-handed. Sera below us, opening and shutting the medicine tin with the attention one gives an object that has become more significant than its size suggests.

I open the notebook. The existing model assumed six adults, one child, and a margin for two short-term admissions under low-light protocol. Even that required sequencing the grow lamps so bed four and bed five never came up to full draw simultaneously unless the batteries were above threshold and the day had given us enough solar salvage to spend. Now the sleeping loft holds four more bodies than it was built for. The west room holds three. The clinic corner never goes fully dark. Boiling water has doubled. Ventilation has changed because human respiration is a load and not only a fact. Marguerite’s medication requires one storage band. Levin’s brace cleaning another. If the weather turns hot early, the thermal assumptions fail first.

I write the new counts and begin tracing each system against them. Power first. Then water. Then thermal retention. Then food-production timing against labor capacity. The numbers move across the page in small exact increments. Petra solders in silence for twenty minutes, then leaves without a word when she sees the part of my face that means interruption will produce nothing useful. Someone replaces her later with a cup of boiled chicory that tastes almost but not quite like coffee. I do not see who sets it by my hand. The mug carries Sera’s thumbprint in graphite at the rim.

Dark comes fully. The west-slit windows turn from gray to black. The room cools enough that the skin at the back of my neck notices. I take my coat from the nail and put it on without standing up.

At 21:14 Wren comes in, reads the top sheet upside down from the doorway, and says, “You forgot body heat in the loft.”

“Sera already corrected me.”

“Then listen to your wife.”

She leaves again before I can answer. I adjust the thermal line.

At 22:03 Theo appears with the field notes on outside movement. No arrivals in the last six hours. One drone line far north. No visible lights on the west corridor besides ours and one transient point that vanished too quickly to classify. He sets the paper down, sees the spread of pages, and keeps his hands carefully clear of them.

“Do you want the bad news.”

“Only if it’s different from the current set.”

“It isn’t.” He shifts his weight off the stiff leg he still favors when he forgets to hide it. “Levin says the marker note in the transit hub might still be there if no one scrubbed it.”

“Probably not.”

“No.”

He remains in the doorway a second longer.

“If more people come,” he says, “we still take them.”

It is not a question. He asks it like a man stating the principle by which he wants to be judged.

“If we can keep them alive,” I say.

“That’s not the same answer.”

“No.”

He takes that with him and goes downstairs.

By 23:11 the page has become one of those pages where the arithmetic narrows enough that any false move will not look false until after it has already propagated.

I slow down.

This is the only useful thing to do when a system approaches the point where confidence becomes a liability. I run the grow-light sequence again with the new occupancy heat, then again under cloud-cover assumptions, then again with the freezer cycling reduced per Gideon’s note. I subtract the unused wash-bay lighting circuit Petra hard-isolated yesterday and add the clinic lamp Wren now refuses to extinguish overnight because Marguerite’s breathing changed once in the dark and no one here is interested in running that experiment again.

Outside, the colony has gone mostly quiet. Not silent. There are too many bodies sleeping under one roof for silence. Instead the building has taken on a different night signature than it had last week. Floorboard. Cough. Water in the pipe. One child turning over and thumping a heel lightly against a mattress frame. The sounds are specific and close and alive.

I put the pencil down and read the final column twice.

The numbers close.

Not with generosity. Not with the kind of margin a sane designer would call resilience. One pump failure and we become a different place within hours. Three days of bad cloud and the bed-five sequence must halve. Two more arrivals with medical refrigeration needs and the freezer budget goes from narrow to impossible. But under the conditions actually in the notebook, with the systems we have and the labor now present to maintain them, the numbers close.
