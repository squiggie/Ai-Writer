Chapter 10: What the Water Told Noah

By the time my NI moved from Restricted pending to full Restricted, I had already stopped sleeping through the sync window. The third miss triggered the standard ladder: kiosk refusals, out-of-residence authentication failures, transit friction, then provisioning non-availability, what people inside Nexus used to call hard eject when the door was closed. The language is careful. The outcome is not. You eat where you planned ahead, or where someone already outside the system planned ahead for you.

I made three trips to Verdant after the reclassification. Night routes only. Old maintenance links that disappeared from current maps after corridor expansion and mesh regularization. Wren gave me her sweep table for the rural boundary: three years of dates, cloud cover, vehicle noise, and the kind of silence that means one unit has passed and the next has not started yet. Accurate data. Field data. Never perfect.

On the fourth trip, I leave the apartment at 2:11 a.m. The hall lights in Threshold East are at low-power night settings. The entry lock resolves on the first cycle because I am still authenticated inside registered residence. Outside, the air has the dry metallic edge of reduced overnight particulate suppression. My bicycle chain ticks once on the first full pedal turn and then settles.

I count six full mesh passes between the north corridor and the old service route at Sector Twelve. I wait through two behind a concrete barrier that used to shield a distribution yard and now shields gravel, weeds, and a rusted sign worn down to matte gray. Forty-three minutes from the boundary marker, the managed surface ends and the old agricultural cut begins. The temperature drops all at once. Ground cold. Drainage cold. The cold that sits in low places and stays there. My gloves are thin at the fingertips from two weeks of night riding. The brake lever bites into that thin place. I can feel the metal shape through the fabric.

I kill my light before the final rise and walk the bicycle the last three hundred meters. No engine noise. No voice traffic. No rotor note. One dog, far east, then quiet. Verdant sits low from this angle, dark except for one panel of muted green light where the lowest grow setting keeps winter trays alive. I do not call out. We do not use names outside walls.

The cache is in the cistern annex, in the old concrete chamber where Wren’s first filtration array failed before she replaced it with the current gravity sequence. We use it when one of us is on a route the other cannot risk that night. The chamber smells of iron, wet stone, and old algae caught in seams no brush reaches. I kneel, unseat the second panel, and reach into the dry section behind the intake pipe.

My fingers touch canvas first, then paper.

For a moment I think it is one of Wren’s notebooks wrapped for moisture, then I pull it clear and the shape is wrong. Too thick at the spine, too narrow in height, sewn signature bulge visible through the oilcloth. Heavier than expected for its size. I know what it is before I unwrap it. There are not many objects built this way.

The wrapper is two layers, oilcloth over waxed paper, tied with ordinary twine in a knot you can open without cutting. I untie it with gloves on, then remove one glove because I cannot feel enough through the fabric. The cover is dark, nearly black, no jacket, edges worn pale where hands have taken it on and off shelves for years. The spine has been repaired once with cloth tape the color of old bone. The title is embossed and almost gone. Holy Bible.

I stand in the chamber with the book in my hand and do nothing for longer than I intend. Water moves through the active pipe to my left with the low continuous sound of pressure and stone. The floor is cold through my soles. I have not held a Bible in twenty years. I have seen scripture as archived text blocks and digitized fragments and obituary quotations before moderation removes them. Not this. This is an object meant to be opened. I rewrap the oilcloth loosely, keep the book under my coat, and go inside.

The main room is in night configuration. Three overhead fixtures off, one at minimum. Grow banks along the interior wall, each strip running low enough to hold seedlings in maintenance without draining battery reserve. The air has two temperatures: cold above shoulder height, warm near the tables where compost heat and ballast collect. Humidity carries damp soil, copper from old intercom coils, yeast from yesterday’s bread rack. Wren’s boots are by the inner door. She is here. I hear no movement.

I set the book on the steel worktable under the weakest light that still allows reading. The table has knife marks, wrench slips, and one burn where a soldering iron rolled and lay for six seconds before someone moved it. There is an empty cup with a dry tea ring at the base. A page clipped behind the sink shows Wren’s valve rotation schedule with tomorrow already filled in. She did not leave this book here. Someone else reached our compartment.

I look back toward the annex door. If someone can place an object in the cache, someone can map our exchange rhythm. I check the panel seam in my head, the scrape pattern on the floor, the hook point where the twine caught when I pulled it free. No forced entry. No sign of a rushed hand. No marker I can trust.

I sit. My knees complain when I bend past ninety degrees, the stiffness from repeated cold exposure and too little recovery. I hold my palms around the tin cup even though it is empty. The metal warms slowly. I open the book.

The first pages I see are not the beginning. Maps, then thin columns of names, then a header with the top corner torn away. I turn forward, then back, then stop and let the pages fall where wear wants them to fall. The book opens near the middle of the first quarter. Genesis 6.

I read standing, then sit again because my balance feels wrong. The words are familiar in shape before they are familiar in meaning. I remember my grandfather’s kitchen table, pine top worn smooth where elbows polished it over decades, his thumb flattening a page while he read aloud in a voice like gravel and summer dust. He tapped lines with the knuckle of his free hand when he wanted to mark structure. Specific instruction, he used to say, before reading a number. He liked numbers in scripture. Said you could test a person by what they did with numbers.

I read the passage once through. Corruption. Violence. Decision. Then instructions: make an ark of gopher wood, rooms, covering, length, breadth, height, window, door, levels. Three hundred cubits. Fifty. Thirty. I read it again.

The first read for me is architectural. The dimensions are presented as work. Material class, envelope treatment, structural sequence. Seal it inside and outside with pitch. Place the door in the side. Build three levels. Enough detail to begin. Not enough detail to remove judgment.

I put my index finger on the dimension line and calculate rough scale from memory. Cubit conversion varies by source, but the ratio holds. Long enough for distribution. Narrow enough for integrity in moving water. Height for deck separation and air volume. Not random. Not poetic. Work.

The south water line knocks once and settles. A hinge moves in the inner room, then stillness again. My hands are on the page and I realize I have tightened them enough to crease the margin. I release pressure and smooth the paper with the side of my palm.

I have spent fifteen years tracing outputs to inputs. The phrase in my reports was input chain. In a valid system, the chain terminates in a source you can name, test, question. In PACEM’s audit deception, it terminates in null.

Here, the source is named. Instruction first. Action second. Build this, then enter it.

I close my eyes and the room does not go dark. The grow lights are visible through my eyelids, a diffuse green-red field that shifts when I move my head. My back hurts from the ride and from a month of sleeping in positions that keep one ear available for the hall lock, stairwell, and any purposeful footsteps after midnight. My jaw is tight. I unclench it. The muscle jumps once.

At my kitchen table in Vael I asked: What would it mean to stop trying to be believed.

I had treated that as an audience question. If not Sone, then who. If not archive contacts, then who. If not formal channels, then who can receive evidence and act. Wrong variable.

Not who will believe.

What to build before belief matters.

I open my notebook.

It is the same one I have carried since October: weatherproof cover, graph paper, half the pages filled with route times, lock-cycle observations, provisioning counts, battery draw estimates, rough material lists from the months when query flags still mattered to me in a different way. The last page holds numbers from two nights ago, pump output versus barrel loss at near-freeze temperature. My cold-weather handwriting narrows and leans right as the hand stiffens.

I turn to the next blank page. I start to write what I always write: objectives, dependencies, constraints. I stop. I write one word: Build.

The pen catches at the end of the d where the paper fibers rise. I press down again and darken the line until the word is almost black compared to the rest of the page. There is no list under it. No numbered items. Not confidence. Not certainty. Sequence. One action before the others.

I sit with the notebook open and the book beside it and listen to the building. The inverter hum. Water moving through the gravity line as the tank equalizes. A branch on the north side touching sheet metal each time wind pushes. Two small clicks from the heating relay cycling to keep minimum tray temperature on the east wall. The room has the quiet of a place kept alive by work that does not stop because no one is watching.

At 5:02 a.m. by my watch, first light starts to unseal the top edge of the east windows. The interior is still dark, but the dark has direction. I stand, close the notebook, and read the passage once more, slower. I do not search for argument against it. I do not search for emotional confirmation. I read the dimensions again. Length. Breadth. Height. I close the book.

For a moment I hold it the way my grandfather held his: palm under the spine, thumb on the page edge, weight centered so the object does not slide. My hand adjusts before I decide to adjust it. He used to pause like this before setting it down at the end of morning reading, making sure the page he wanted would open easily the next day.

I do not know who put this book in the cistern annex. I do not know if it was meant for me or for whoever reached into that compartment first. I do not know if there is another note I have not found. I do not know if Wren knows more and chose not to say.

I know someone carried a physical Bible across monitored ground and placed it where our hands would meet in the dark.

I slide it inside my coat, against the inner layer where body heat will keep the paper above the cold point on the ride back. The cover presses flat against my chest. I can feel the corners when I breathe.

Outside, the sky over the eastern fields lifts from black to iron gray. The route back to Vael is still possible for another hour before traffic and light narrow the options. I pick up my gloves, my notebook, and the wrench I forgot to return to the pegboard last week. I set the wrench back where it belongs.

Then I step into the morning with the book under my coat and the word in my notebook and no plan beyond it.
