Chapter 22: What Wren Said

Wren is already outside when I come through the loading-bay door. The morning has not yet finished becoming distinct. The field beyond the south wall is still in that early state where distance exists without detail, the row lines only barely separated from the dark between them. She is standing in the strip of hard ground between the wall and the first bed with twenty meters of black irrigation line looped at her boots, one end in her hands, the other running back through the doorway into the utility room. She does not wave or explain. She only looks at me once and lifts the coil slightly, enough to indicate division of labor.

“Take that end,” she says.

I am carrying my notebook, Petra’s revised load sheet folded into it, and three pages of counts I meant to put into a cleaner order before breakfast. I set them on the concrete sill just inside the door and take the line from her. The outer surface is wet with condensation and cold enough that my fingers tighten around it before the grip has fully decided to close. There is still water inside. I can feel its weight shifting when I lift.

“What’s wrong with the current run?”

Wren turns and starts walking south along the wall. I follow with the line over one shoulder. “It clicks at the slab seam when the tank equalizes. It clicked twice at dawn yesterday and three times this morning.”

“Expansion at the coupling?”

“Probably. Or the old concrete has shifted again and is asking the coupling to solve a problem it can’t solve.” She steps over the narrow trench where the existing line disappears under the cracked edge of the loading bay. The trench was cut in winter, back when moving water from the header tank to the wash barrel and lower grow beds still counted as a problem with only two hands available to answer it. “We’ll know when it’s out.”

I look back toward the main room. Through the open door I can see the edge of the table, the yellow stone holding one corner of the map flat, my notebook where I left it. I had intended to spend the first hour on the full architecture. Water distribution, battery replacement intervals, medical threshold logic, harvest-rate assumptions against fourteen bodies instead of seven. The numbers from last night close, but only if the sources beneath them keep behaving. There are pages to write. There is a proper order to put them in.

Wren has already taken hold of a mattock leaning against the wall. “You can count later,” she says.

I do not remember saying the thought aloud, but it does not matter. She puts the blade into the ground six inches south of the old trench line and drives it down with the heel of her boot. The soil here is denser than the first beds and still holding some night cold below the top dry layer. When I kneel to clear the loosened dirt by hand, the underside of it is dark and wet and smells of clay, root fiber, and the mineral stagnant note you get where water has been passing through a space too tight for too long.

The old run comes up reluctantly. We uncover it by degrees, Wren cutting ahead of the line with short exact strikes, me pulling back the soil and lifting the pipe clear as it surfaces. The coupling fails in the manner she predicted. Not split yet, but whitening at the stress ring where the material has been asked the same question too many times. When I bend it lightly, a thread of water beads at the seam and gathers on my thumb.

“How long has it been taking load?” I ask.

“Three days that I heard. Longer than that in fact.”

“You should have said something.”

She does not stop working. “I am saying something.”

The answer is not sharp. It is only correct. She hands me the old section once the coupling is free and I feel at once how much of the line’s problem was invisible while it was buried. The pipe has stiffened in one arc where the slab edge kept it under pressure. A line of grit has worked its way into the collar. The old seal smells faintly of algae and old rubber when I bring it close enough to inspect.

Wren starts opening the new trench. Her pace is not fast. Fast is the wrong category for labor that needs to be done once and not revisited. Each strike goes where it should. I take the shovel and widen behind her. The first ten minutes live in the shoulders. The next ten drop into the lower back and thighs. By the time we reach the south corner of the building my shirt is damp between the shoulder blades and the cold on the line has become a different fact, something that now feels almost welcome when I lift it and settle it into the trench.

Verdant wakes around us by layers. Pump at low cycle from the utility room. The secondary inverter giving its brief morning complaint before Petra quiets it from the annex. A pan set on wood. Theo crossing the yard toward the north line with the rolled map tube under one arm. Somewhere near the south wall, Marguerite’s loom beginning its patient wooden pattern: shuttle, draw, tighten, pause, then again.

When we stop to reposition the coil, I straighten too quickly and feel the blood move out of my hands in a rush of pins. Wren is kneeling by the trench wall, scraping loose stone away from the base so the line will bed evenly when we set it in. The cuffs of her coat are dark with damp soil. Her hands are bare. The skin across the knuckles is split in two old places and fresh dirt has settled into both.

“I was going to map the full system this morning,” I say.

“You still might.”

“It needs doing.”

“Yes.”

She says yes to the sentence and keeps scraping stone from the trench as if agreement and redirection are not mutually exclusive operations. I stand there with the shovel for a moment longer than the task requires, then step down into the trench and clear the section she has indicated with the toe of her boot.

Our daughter comes out of the west room carrying one of Marguerite’s unfinished baskets by the handle. The basket is too wide for her wrist, so it slides down over the hand and bumps against her knee as she walks. Sera follows with the blanket from the cot folded over one forearm and a cup in the other hand. Neither of them is dressed for speed. The day has not yet asked for it.

“Is that for a rabbit,” our daughter asks, looking into the trench, “or for water.”

“Water,” Wren says, without looking up.

The child considers this, nods once, and goes on. Sera’s eyes move from the open trench to the line in my hands to the load sheets on the sill inside the doorway. She takes all three in, does not speak, and keeps walking toward the south wall where Marguerite has begun weaving in the first strip of direct light. The blanket over her arm still holds the crease of the cot.

We set the new run at midmorning. Wren wants a broader curve at the south corner than I first allow for, and when I tell her the bend radius will hold as I have it, she says, “Not after six hot days and one cold night it won’t,” and makes me lift it again. She is right. Once I stop arguing with the physical facts and look where she is looking, the pressure point is obvious. The line wants more room than the geometry on paper would have given it. The ground here settles differently after rain. The south wall throws shade until nearly nine. The old building keeps its own temperatures.

By the time the line is sleeved and set on sand, my wrists are carrying that faint internal ache that arrives after repetitive force. Wren threads the new coupling onto the tank-side run, checks the cut edge by touch, and nods for me to hold the far section level while she seats the seal.

“First winter,” she says, as if continuing a conversation that has been silent for half an hour, “this line split at 03:00. Different route. Different mistake. I carried water from the utility room to the west beds in stock pots until dawn.”

“How long before you replaced it?”

“By noon.”

“Alone.”

“Yes.”

The coupling seats with a dull soft click. She tightens the collar, stops, listens to the line though no water is moving yet, then tells me to open the valve a little when she says.

I go to the utility room and turn the brass handle until resistance changes. Water enters the new run with a sound I feel through the pipe before I hear it in the wall. Wren is still crouched over the coupling when I come back. She has one hand resting lightly on the line, not gripping, only receiving the pressure through the material.

“More,” she says.

I give it more. This time the line settles. No knocking at the slab seam because the slab seam no longer has anything to say about it. No shiver at the collar. When I return, Wren is already backfilling the first section with both hands, pulling damp soil over the sleeve and pressing it down flat with her palms so no void remains beneath.

We eat standing at noon because the trench is open and because sitting would make standing again less efficient than continuing. Gideon leaves bread on the sill and a jar of preserved pears with a spoon still in it. Sera is at the main table with the intake pages turned sideways and our daughter beside her drawing circles that are, at this distance, either seeds or people or both.

I wipe my hands on a rag, pick up the notebook from the sill, and open to the blank page after last night’s counts. The paper buckles slightly under a drop of water from my wrist. I start a heading: SOUTH DISTRIBUTION REROUTE. Date. Materials. Revised path. I list the depth, the sleeve type, the new coupling model scavenged from Petra’s fourth drawer because Wren had known it was there before I did.

Wren tears the bread in half with her teeth and watches me write for a moment.

“That part is useful,” she says.

“Only that part?”

“The next person with cold hands at dawn will not care what you called the system. They’ll care where the valve is.”

“If we write only separate corrections, we leave the whole thing in people’s bodies. That’s fragile.”

“Everything here is in people’s bodies.”

She says it with the same neutrality she would bring to blood pressure or a weather front. I look up from the page. On the far side of the yard Oren is seated at the bench with his splinted leg stretched out and three dull knives laid in a row beside him, working each one against a stone. Our daughter is holding the basket steady while Marguerite shows her how to turn the strip at a corner without breaking it. Petra is on the annex step reading meter drift over Davi’s shoulder.

“I’m not talking about abstraction,” I say. “I mean structure. Water, power, grow load, medical limits, intake logic. The dependencies between them. If this place outlasts us, the order should outlast us too.”

Wren swallows, drinks from the jar lid she has used as a cup, and nods once toward the trench. “Then finish burying the water first.”

The day warms while we work. By one the south wall has begun reflecting heat back into the yard, and the top layer of soil dries on the shovel blade in a thin skin that has to be knocked free every few passes. The line disappears by degrees. First the long black curve gone under sand and soil. Then the sleeve. Then the bright new collar under the access box at the valve point. When the trench is level again, the yard looks only slightly changed, which is the correct outcome for infrastructure.

Wren sends me to check flow at the lower bed and the wash barrel. The lower bed takes water evenly across the first manifold. No sputter. No pressure loss. At the wash barrel, the fill note is cleaner than it was on the old line, the brief metal rattle in the standpipe gone. When I tell her, she grunts once and begins packing the removed section of old pipe into salvage lengths.

I should let the thing end there. The line is moved. The pressure holds. The next task has already begun presenting itself in other parts of the yard. Petra wants Davi in the upper room to help trace a fault that only appears under pump transition. Gideon is calling for Theo at the west wall. Sera has our daughter by the shoulders, turning the child toward the basin because the basket strips have left a brown pattern of dust across both palms.

I take the notebook back out.

“Wren.”

She is cutting the failed coupling free from the old run with a hacksaw, slow strokes so the brittle collar does not fracture and throw shards. “What.”

“I want to do the full record. Not just today’s correction. All of it. The architecture, the maintenance intervals, the capacities, the things that fail in sequence and the things that fail all at once. The intake thresholds. The cache logic. What Petra knows about the batteries. What you know about the medical line. If more people find this place, or another place like it, they should not have to rebuild the whole thing by trial.”

She keeps sawing until the collar drops into her palm. Only then does she set the old pipe down and look at me. Her face in work is difficult to read because most of what would count as expression in other people has long ago been reassigned to function. Dirt has dried at the edge of one cheekbone. There is a pale line where the bridge of her glasses has pressed all morning. Behind her, through the open door, my load sheets are still on the sill under a stone because the breeze from the corridor would take them otherwise.

“Write down the valve intervals,” she says. “Write down Petra’s replacement order for the cells. Write down the dose schedule for anyone whose labels have gone missing. Write down the way Theo checks the north line and the way Gideon rotates the panels when cloud cover changes. All of that matters.”

“Yes.”

“But don’t talk about it like you’re the architect.”

The page flexes once under my thumb. Sweat that had dried in my shirt cools across the spine. I feel the sentence first in the center of the chest, not pain exactly, but the same hollow impact you get when a step is not where the foot expected floor. “I didn’t mean that.”

“You did.”

For a second I hear only separate things. The hacksaw frame touching concrete where she set it down. A bucket knocked once against the basin near Sera and our daughter. The new water line moving under pressure inside the wall. My mouth opens and the breath that goes in tastes of hot dust and the wet rubber smell of the cut pipe at my feet.

Then the argument arrives. Not about the record. A list. I brought the first maps from the library. I counted the drone cycles. I found Verdant. I brought Theo. I came back with Sera and our daughter. I set intake rules. I ran the power model last night until the numbers closed. The list is accurate in the limited way lists often are.

Wren reaches for the coil of salvage pipe and loops it once around her forearm.

“You didn’t build this,” she says. “You just showed up.”

The back of my neck goes cold though the yard is hot. My grip tightens on the notebook hard enough to bow the cover. There is dried soil at the crease of my palm and a fresh blister ridge under the right thumb from the shovel. I can feel both before I can find an answer. “That isn’t all I did.”

“No,” she says. “It isn’t.”

There is no correction after it. No softening clause. She turns toward the utility room with the salvage lengths under one arm and the failed collar in her pocket because metal is metal and will become something later if it survives long enough. By the time I have assembled the next reply she is already at the doorway, already bending to pick up the box of spare clamps we left there at dawn.

I stay where I am. Petra is calling for the seven-millimeter wrench. Our daughter is laughing at something Marguerite has shown her in the half-made basket. Water moves through the new run inside the wall with no visible ceremony, only the steadier fill note at the lower bed and the absence of the old knock at the slab seam.

The notebook is open in my hand to a page headed with a system name and a clean line beneath it, ready for the larger order I meant to impose. My thumb has left a damp print at the bottom corner. I look down at the page, then at the trench we have already erased back into yard, and then toward the utility room where Wren has gone on to the next thing without waiting to see what her sentence does.

The cut length of failed pipe is still leaning against my boot. I pick it up because leaving material in the weather is an error and because my hands need an instruction they can follow while the rest of me catches up. The old line is lighter now that it is empty. Water is moving through the new run without it.
