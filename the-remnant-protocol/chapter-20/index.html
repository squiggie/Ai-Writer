Chapter 20: What He Predicted

When the first sound comes out of me, it is small enough that I do not at first identify it as mine.

My hand is still braced against the doorframe. The cut in the palm has opened and put a dark smear on the chipped paint. Wren is standing three steps away beside the shelf where she set the seedling tray. Soil is drying on the side of her thumb. The pump keeps moving water through the wall. The ballast above us gives its regular faint tick on the half-second delay that means Petra still has not replaced it. Nothing in the room changes to accommodate what is happening in my body. The second sound is worse because it has structure.

I bend without deciding to. One hand catches my knee. The other stays on the frame because if it leaves, I am not certain I remain upright. Heat has gone behind my eyes so quickly that the corridor blurs at the edges. I have spent months wanting the system to admit what it was doing. I have spent months wanting one sentence from one person in authority that would convert fact into recognized fact. What arrives instead is my wife here, my daughter asleep somewhere under this roof, the city beginning to fail in the exact pattern I tried to warn them about, and my body deciding before my mind does what category that belongs to.

Wren does not come toward me at once. She waits one breath. Two. Then she steps close enough to put her hand once, firmly, on the back of my coat between the shoulders.

“Easy,” she says.

The word is practical. Not comfort. Not ceremony. Only instruction.

I keep my eyes shut because opening them would not change the room. Moisture has reached my mouth. My breathing has broken into short attempts that do not complete. There is no dignity in this that I can locate. There is also no way around it.

“I need,” I say, and stop there because I do not know the rest of the sentence.

“You need to sit down.”

I nod once, or think I do. She takes the tray from the shelf again with one hand, sets it on the floor out of the way, and then guides me, not gently but with exact pressure, toward the chair at the map table. My left knee threatens to go on the second step. I catch the edge of the table hard enough to make the pencils rattle in the jar.

The yellow stone is there. My daughter’s. Bright painted color chipped at one side. Under it is a sheet of Petra’s numbers in her narrow upright hand. Battery draw. Grow-light intervals. Pump relay notes. I sit because the chair is behind my legs and because Wren is right that there is no other useful thing to do.

By the time I can take one full breath without it tearing, the front of my shirt is damp. I look down at my hands. Mud in the knuckles. Blood drying across the palm crease. The tremor still visible in two fingers. I know enough about bodies to understand that there are thresholds beyond which control is an argument and not a fact.

Wren pours water from the steel jug into a cup and sets it by my hand.

“Drink.”

I do. The water tastes faintly of the north tank. Mineral and old pipe. It is colder than the room and steadier than I am.

“Sera,” I say after a while.

“In the west room. She got in after midnight. Petra took first watch with your daughter so Sera could sleep for an hour. Theo and Gideon stayed on the east line until morning. Nobody was followed.”

“You checked.”

She gives me a look that means the question is beneath both of us.

“Yes.”

I drink again. The cup shakes against my teeth only once.

“Did she see the city.”

“Enough of it.”

That answer contains more than she says. Wren pulls the other chair out with her foot and sits across from me, forearms on her knees.

“You can see her now,” she says. “Or you can wash first and not frighten your daughter if she wakes. Those are your two reasonable options.”

I look at the blood on my hand. The mud. The coat stiff with old rain.

“Wash first,” I say.

“Good.”

She stands. “Then after that you come upstairs. Petra has the receiver running. You should hear what they’re hearing.”

I nod. She picks up the seedling tray and goes back through the grow-room door, leaving me with the cup, the yellow stone, and the fact of my own breathing returning in increments.

The wash basin in the side room is a salvaged utility sink with a hairline crack under the drain collar that leaks only when the outflow is restricted. I know this because I sealed it twice in March and it still opens under cold contraction. The water from the tap is warmer than the cup Wren gave me and carries a green smell from the storage barrel. I rinse the blood from my palm and watch the diluted red spin once around the basin before it goes. The cut is shallow. Reopened, not new. My face in the steel mirror above the sink looks older than it did four days ago. Mud dried across the jaw. Eyes bloodshot from rain, lack of sleep, whatever the body does after it has been held together by task and then released from it.

I take off the coat. The stitched section in the hem remains intact. I hang it on the pipe hook by the door and leave my hand there longer than the coat requires. The pipe is cold through the skin of my fingers. Somewhere past the wall I can hear the receiver upstairs bleeding static in thin bursts between voices. Under that is the ordinary house noise of Verdant: water moving, a drawer shut carefully, someone crossing a floorboard that complains at the same joint every time. The place has continued without me. I stand at the sink until my breathing is level enough to pass for it.

When I step into the west room, the light is low and angled toward the wall so it does not spill onto the mattress.

Sera is asleep on her side with one hand under her cheek. Our daughter is curled against her back in the blue sweater with the yellow cuff turned inward now, only a line of it visible at the wrist. There is dried mud on the hem of Sera’s trouser leg where she must have knelt somewhere outside. Her boots are on the floor beside the bed, unlaced and tipped over. One sock hangs half out of one boot. Her hair is tied back badly and has come loose at the neck. I stand in the doorway and look because there is nothing to say in a room where the two people I spent the last nine hours trying to reach are asleep and breathing.

My daughter has one hand open on the blanket. The fingers twitch once. Sera’s shoulder rises and falls under the wool shirt she took from the storage shelf. Not mine. Gideon’s, I think. The sleeve is too long.

The room smells of damp clothes, plant air from the corridor, and the faint soap Petra uses when she washes equipment because it leaves less residue. I do not go farther in. Waking them would convert the fact into speech before I am ready for speech. I stay until the details stop changing under my eyes. Then I put two fingers against the doorframe where the paint has worn smooth from use, feel the wood catch slightly at one old split in the grain, and step back into the corridor.

On the stairs I have to stop once. Not because of the climb. Because halfway up, the sound from the receiver resolves into a human voice asking for transport that is not coming, and my foot remains on the same step until the message ends and static takes it. By the time I reach the monitoring room, my hand has left a damp mark on the rail.

Upstairs, the room is warmer than the rest of the facility because the old receiver throws off heat and Petra has insulated the broken north window with three layers of clear packing sheet that hold air badly but better than nothing.

Petra is at the table with one headset half on and a pencil in her mouth, writing frequencies on the back of packaging cardboard because the good notebook pages are being saved for inventories that matter more. Theo is by the window with the field glasses. Gideon stands over the battery monitor with both hands on his hips, reading numbers no one can improve by looking harder.

All three turn when I come in. Theo straightens too fast.

“You made it.”

“Yes.”

He looks as if there are twelve other things he could say and has chosen the only one that does not presume. Petra removes the pencil from her mouth.

“The west-band emergency repeater is still live,” she says. “Barely. Voice quality drops every six minutes and then recovers. The civic service band is repeating advisories with no timestamp. And one of the outer clinic networks is broadcasting queue states over open audio because their internal routing collapsed.”

She says it as if she is handing me tool measurements. That is kindness here.

Gideon points to the monitor. “Battery took the night load. We’re all right if nobody decides they need heroics.”

“No heroics,” Wren says from the doorway behind me. She has come up silently enough that only the sentence marks her arrival. “He sits. He listens. The rest of you continue being useful.”

Theo’s mouth shifts at one corner. Petra hands me the second headset without comment.

I sit. The receiver hum is low and physical through the table. Old equipment. Analog stages. One transformer somewhere in the chassis running hotter than its rating and not yet dead. Static lies under every voice like weather.

The first channel Petra puts in my ear is an outer-district provisioning desk on manual relay.

“District Three South allocation point at thirty-eight percent throughput,” a man says. His voice is flat with repetition. “Manual reconciliation unresolved. Shelf-stable protein exhausted at 04:10. Infant formula unavailable. Repeat, unavailable. Citizens are advised to remain in queue for updated guidance.”

He says it again thirty seconds later. The second time his voice is hoarser. In the background a child is crying with the steady, exhausted pitch of a child past ordinary hunger.

Petra changes the frequency.

This one is a healthcare dispatch band, still carrying because whoever set it up decades ago assumed voice redundancy mattered more than efficiency. The speaker is trying to stay in protocol and failing by degrees.

“Respiratory triage overflow to corridor B. Corridor B only. Do not route noncritical fever to pediatric. I said do not route.” A burst of static wipes out the next line. Then: “No estimate on insulin resupply. Manual queue only. If home refrigeration failed, mark urgent. Mark urgent.”

Another voice comes in over him. “Urgent queue is at two hundred twelve.”

“Then make another queue.”

There is a silence after that in which no one on the channel seems to know whether another queue is something language can make.

I take the headset off one ear and put my free hand flat on the table. The wood under the varnish is swollen along one seam where humidity got into it last winter. I remember planing that seam down so the receiver would stop rocking on its base.

“How long,” I ask Petra.

“Started before dawn. Worse every hour.”

“District spread.”

“Three confirmed on provisioning. Healthcare wider. Transit is gone in sections. Civic layer is posting generic congestion text over service categories it was never built to show.”

Theo says, from the window, “People are moving south along the old freight corridor. Not near us yet. I saw two separate groups at first light from the water tower rise.”

Wren comes far enough into the room to set a bowl beside me. Bread torn into thick pieces. A hard-cooked egg. Beet stems cooked down with onion. Food from this place, not from the city.

“Eat while you listen,” she says.

I obey because argument would waste energy we do not have.

The bread is dense and slightly sour from the starter Petra keeps alive in a jar near the warm battery housing. The egg has a trace of sulfur at the yolk. The beet stems carry grit because Gideon rinses as if water is free and Wren does not. I taste all of it. That, more than anything on the radio, is what undoes me.

It is not dramatic. No sound. No movement anyone in the room could call attention to without choosing cruelty. My throat closes on the second bite of bread and will not open again for a moment. Water gathers in my eyes and runs before I can convert it into something else. I set the bread down carefully because crushing it in my hand would be childish and because it came from labor I know by name.

No one speaks.

The dispatch voice in my ear says, “We have nonresponsive in queue and no transport authority. Repeat, no transport authority.”

I put the heel of my hand against my eyes. The reopened cut stings with salt.

I was right. The sentence is true. It is also worthless.

The city did not need me to be correct. The children in the provisioning line did not need me to be correct. The man at the clinic trying to make another queue where the first queue had already become inhuman did not need my vindication. The colony mattered before this morning. It matters now for exactly the same reason. Being right has arrived too late to save the people still standing under yellow screens waiting for green.

The tears stop because bodies cannot keep every valve open indefinitely. I breathe through my nose and lower my hand. Theo is looking deliberately at the window. Petra is writing as if the graphite itself needs managing. Gideon sets a fresh number beside the battery curve and says to no one in particular, “If arrivals increase, the freezer becomes the first fight.”

That is the right sentence to enter after grief. Plain and exact.

“Water first,” I say. My voice comes back rough. “Then fuel draw. Then clinic isolation space.”

“How many arrivals are we preparing for,” Theo asks. His field glasses remain in his hand. He has not put them back to his face.

I listen to the static another moment before answering because the number should come from conditions and not fear.

“Twelve without structural changes,” I say. “More if we collapse room assignments and move food production outside faster than schedule.”

Petra writes it down. Gideon says, “Freezer load changes before then.” Wren answers, “I know.” Theo turns back to the window and says, “Drone window is still favorable.” I say, “Not for long.” That is enough to set the next hours in motion.

The work settles into sequence after that. Not calm. Sequence. Petra keeps the frequencies cycling and marks which voices recur, which districts go silent, which advisories begin losing timestamps and coherence. Gideon strips the battery budget down to numbers that matter over the next three days and no farther. Theo tracks movement where the corridor can be seen, then comes back to the map and shifts likely approach lines in pencil when the radio suggests pressure somewhere north has broken loose.

I do what I can still do. I listen. I sort signal from panic. I cross out plans built for a colony that expected more warning than this. When Sera wakes, I hear her once below us in the corridor asking Petra for the compliance printout she carried in under her coat. Later the stair boards sound under her weight and stop outside the room, but she does not enter. The pause is brief. Then her steps move away again toward the worktable downstairs. The fact of her being one floor below me alters the air more than the receiver heat does.

By midday the room smells of dust warmed by electronics, boiled roots from the kitchen below, and the wool on my coat drying on the hook outside the stairwell. My knee has stiffened. My right heel has opened the skin under the failed seam and left a dark crescent on the sock. Wren notices and says only, “Boots off when we’re done.” She has seen enough of men working past avoidable damage to know which instructions might still land.

The municipal band continues to narrow. At 14:37 a voice reads district outages in a tone so controlled it becomes almost liturgical.

“Provisioning access unstable in districts one, three, five, and six. Same-day clinical services suspended in sectors west and central pending manual redistribution. Civic identity verification may be delayed. Citizens are instructed to remain in their assigned residential bands for updated guidance.”

Assigned residential bands. The language has narrowed. People have become tokens to be held in place while the system decides what category of failure to call them.

My daughter comes upstairs once in the middle of it all carrying the yellow stone in both hands as if it is something official she has been asked to deliver.

“Mama said this was mine,” she says.

“It is.”

She studies my face with the directness children reserve for damage and says, “Wren said your knee is bad.”

“Wren says many things.”

This produces the smallest change in her mouth. Not a smile. Near it.

She sets the stone down beside the receiver, touches two fingers very lightly to my wrist, and goes back downstairs without another question. The place on my skin where she touched me stays present after the rest of the room has resumed.

Toward evening the radio traffic thins, which is worse than if it had increased. Fewer voices does not mean fewer problems. It means fewer people free to narrate them. The room has taken on the flattened quality of places where everyone has been using the same air too long. Pencil shavings lie beside Petra’s elbow. Theo has set the glasses down at last and is rubbing the bridge of his nose with two fingers. Gideon has stopped muttering the battery numbers aloud and simply writes them, harder each hour, as if pressure on the pencil could supply amperage.

When Petra lifts one side of the headset and says, “Nothing new for six minutes,” no one answers. The silence that follows is full of apparatus. Receiver hum. The fan belt below us slipping once under load. A pot lid from the kitchen set down too hard and then corrected. My own pulse in the heel of the reopened hand where it rests against the table edge.

I take the headset off and set it on the cardboard frequency sheet. The imprint on my ear remains. “Call me if the west-band repeater changes,” I say. No one objects. Wren, who has been sorting dried stores by the shelves, looks up once, reads whatever is visible on my face, and says only, “Boots.”

I sit on the stair halfway down and pull them off because doing it standing proves briefly beyond me. The skin at the right heel has gone white at the edges around the split and red in the center. The air on it hurts more than the boot did. I leave the boots by the wall, step the rest of the way down in my socks, and wash my face at the sink without turning on the full light. By the time I open the back door, the day has gone from gray to the color that comes just before true dusk.

The concrete behind the west wall still holds a little warmth. The sky over the dead zone is the color of old aluminum. Far to the north there is a low dirty brightness where Vael sits beyond the rise, too diffuse to call light and too constant to mistake for weather. No drones pass overhead. Insects have begun in the ditch grass. The facility behind me gives off its evening sounds: one fan belt with a soft whine under load, the click of the intercom relay, Gideon’s slow tread crossing the upper hall.

I hear the door open and close. Sera comes to stand beside me, not close enough to touch. She has changed into a dry sweater, but the cuffs are dark where she must have washed her hands and not dried them fully. There is graphite on the side of one finger. Her hair is still tied back badly. A loose section at the neck moves once in the wind and settles against the wool.

For a long time neither of us speaks.

The silence is not empty. It contains the west room with the mattress on the floor, her boots tipped over beneath it, our daughter asleep there in the morning. It contains the apartment in Vael and the steel dish rack and the blue sweater by the door and all the sentences that were true too late. It contains the city to the north and the channels upstairs carrying pieces of its failure into this yard.

When she speaks, her voice is low and worn down to plainness.

“I’ve been running the suppression data on PACEM’s healthcare allocation,” she says. She keeps her eyes on the northern glow. Her thumb moves once against the graphite on her finger, rubbing it darker instead of clean. “The populations they deprioritized most aggressively. They’re the ones the provisioning grid failed first.”

I keep my eyes on the same point.

“I know.”

She nods once, though the motion is mostly visible in the shift of her shoulder. Her hands have gone into the sleeves, then back out again. She is still learning where to put them.

“I helped build the suppression model.”

The words stay in the air between us. No defense attached to them. No explanation. Only fact.

“I know that too,” I say.

She closes her eyes. Only for a second. When they open again, she turns her face slightly away from the city and toward the dark field to our left, as if the angle might let her continue standing here.

“I told myself it was bounded,” she says. “A triage layer. Temporary. Something they would have to justify every week. Then they stopped having to justify it.”

The concrete has cooled through the soles of my socks. Somewhere in the ditch a night insect changes pitch and holds it.

“I know,” I say again. It is not absolution. It is only the fact that I saw the same architecture from the other side.

Her hand lifts once and stops at the height of my sleeve before touching. Two fingers close on nothing. Then the hand lowers.

“I do not know what to do with that,” she says.

Neither do I. The answer is in my throat and does not become speech. I can hear her breathing now. Not even. Checked once in the middle, as if her body is trying not to let too much through.

So I do the only thing available that is not false. I turn my hand on the concrete between us until the palm faces up.

She looks at it. The reopened cut runs dark across the crease. Soil remains in one knuckle I missed at the sink. For three breaths she does not move. Then she sets her hand in mine carefully, as if she is not certain the contact will hold. Her skin is cold from the evening air. The graphite smudges once against my thumb.

We stand there after that in the dark with the facility at our backs and the city still failing somewhere beyond sight. Neither of us has any more words for it. Her hand remains in mine. That is not enough. It is what there is.
