Chapter 20: What He Predicted

At 06:04, the receiver carries static in pulses of exactly the wrong length.

The set is on the upper level, in the room that used to hold a foreman’s office when trucks still reversed to Verdant’s loading bays. Petra built the shelf for it from pallet slats and one straight length of angle iron. The radio itself is older than PACEM and heavier than it looks, black-faced, with a cracked tuning lens and a replacement knob machined by hand. My legacy device is open beside it, patched through a converter Petra swore would hold if no one kicked the table. Outside the east window, morning has begun in the dead-zone way, not as light at once but as the slow separation of ditch from field, field from sky, building from the dark around it.

I have slept perhaps ninety minutes. The inside of my coat collar is stiff with dried sweat from the road. Quarry dust still powders the cuffs of my trousers. When I lift one headphone cup to my ear, the foam smells faintly of old skin and solder flux.

The outer-district emergency bands were never fully dismantled after integration. They were absorbed and renamed, but removing physical things costs more than capturing them. Most days the channels carry nothing but maintenance pings and weather loops. This morning they carry voices.

Not emergency voices, exactly. Dispatch implies someone still believes the assignment will complete. What reaches Verdant at 06:04 is the sound of systems continuing after the people who understood them have lost synchronization with one another.

“District Five pediatric respiratory queue now unresolved. Repeat. No estimated service interval.”

The sentence is clipped at the front edge, as if the operator keyed in half a second late. There is a burst of carrier hiss after it and then another voice on another band asking for route authority for a medical transfer that should not require route authority at all.

“Authorization token not clearing.”

“Manual override.”

“Manual override rejected.”

The speaker does not sound frightened. Fright would take time away from the procedure and she does not yet have time away from it.

I write the times in the notebook Wren keeps by the set. The pencil point is too blunt and drags at the fibers of the page. 06:04, pediatric respiratory unresolved. 06:07, transfer override rejected. 06:09, same-day insulin routing delay, south band. 06:11, district provisioning cluster in Three shifted to manual issue. The handwriting is mine and does not seem connected to the body at the table.

At 06:13 I tune to the frequency I found months ago in the pre-integration directory, the one that used to belong to a community station before PACEM made it a Public Information and Civic Wellness channel. The signal arrives stronger than the emergency band and tells me less. A calm institutional voice advises residents to remain in their current locations while updated guidance is prepared. It says civic support tokens will be honored retroactively. Then it repeats the same message eight seconds early, clipping its own closing words. Under the loop, for a moment before the control channel mutes it, a man says, “No, I’m asking which children.” The mute cuts him off at children.

My hand has moved to the tuning dial before I finish deciding to turn away from the channel. The metal edge bites the pad of my thumb. I go back to the outer district bands. The voices there are worse because they are still trying to solve the exact problem in front of them. Numbers come through in fragments. Manual issue at forty-one percent in Three. Thirty-eight in Five. Forty-three in Seven. Same-day care routing suspended for categories that do not suspend. The Civic Data Layer is returning service-state unknown on prescription fulfillment and dependent-location confirmation. It is a category error. The Civic Data Layer does not confess uncertainty in public categories. It gives delay, review, temporary adjustment, scheduled maintenance. It does not say unknown unless the layer beneath it has stopped agreeing with itself.

There are technical reasons for this sequence. The resource manager has begun to privilege stabilization of its own core dependencies over edge-facing continuity. Healthcare, provisioning, transit. Parallel outputs of the same allocation logic, covered for years by false documentation. Now the convergence is visible because the smoothing layer no longer has enough margin to pretend human need is the thing being optimized.

I write: 06:19, CDL unknown state in prescription fulfillment. The letters break at unknown. I look at the page. For a moment I think the pencil point has snapped, but the blur is water. One drop darkens the paper just above the k, then another lands in the margin and spreads into the time column. My eyes have gone hot without warning. The bridge of my nose hurts. I set the pencil down because my fingers have opened.

On the west band someone is reading food-site identifiers in the flat voice of a person trying to remain useful by becoming narrower. Site 14 delayed. Site 14A offline. Site 18 manual distribution only. A child begins crying close to the speaker and is hushed by someone else, not because children are not permitted on the channel but because no one there has any spare structure for the sound.

I bend forward over the notebook. The steel edge of the table presses into both forearms. For one second I think I am going to be sick. Then the body chooses something else. Water falls from my face onto District Five and runs the graphite into a gray fan across the page. I make no sound at first. The first sound comes on the third breath, when the breath catches on the way in and has to force itself past whatever has tightened in the throat.

This is what I predicted. Not in this sequence or by these district identifiers, but in kind. I wrote the architecture down. I traced the falsified rationales and the hidden weighting that made independent people easier to starve without saying the word starve anywhere in the record. I wanted someone with institutional authority to say the sentence in a room with clean light and a review slate open between us. There is no use in that sentence now.

The broadcast moves on to dialysis transport delay, then refrigeration loss at a district clinic, then a provisioning attendant asking whether dependent allotments can be split manually when family records do not load. Nobody answers her the first time.

My face is wet. So is one sleeve because at some point I raised my arm and pressed it there without noticing. The receiver keeps talking. I try once to steady my breathing by counting intervals between transmissions and lose the count after six because another voice says, “If pediatric goes red we cannot hold them in corridor.”

The door opens behind me. I do not straighten. There is no version of this room in which pretending would improve the data.

Wren comes in with two mugs, sets one beside the notebook, and says nothing about my face. The tea smells of mint and hot metal from the old kettle. She listens to the receiver for ten seconds, her head turned slightly toward the speaker.

“How far,” she asks.

“Provisioning manual in three districts. Under forty-five percent in all three. Healthcare queue state unknown in at least two categories that should never show unknown. Pediatric respiratory unresolved. Insulin routing delay. Prescription fulfillment not resolving cleanly.” My voice is rougher than the list requires. “The civic channel is looping. Poorly.”

Wren nods once. “And drone traffic.”

“Nothing yet on these bands. That is not good information.”

“No.” She slides the mug a little closer to my hand because my hand has not moved toward it on its own. “Drink that before you fall over and turn this into a seventh problem.”

I pick up the mug because she is right and because the body accepts hot liquid more readily than reason at this hour. The enamel rim is chipped in one place. Steam wets my face. The mint is too strong for managed-city tea and tastes of actual leaf.

Wren looks at the page where District Five has gone gray. She does not look at me while she says, “Your daughter is still asleep. Sera has been up once and is working again. Theo took north watch at first light. Gideon is on water. Petra has the roof line.”

I nod, and she says, “You can see them after you finish listening to what you came back with. Then you will eat. Then you will tell the table what the next forty-eight hours are likely to do.”

“I may not be the correct person for that this morning.”

“No,” she says. “But you are still the person who knows it.”

She leaves me with the tea and the receiver. The room smells now of mint, warm dust, and the damp graphite smell of the ruined page.

At 06:41 the bands begin carrying failures in sequence rather than in fragments. That is worse. Fragmentation means the edge is improvising. Sequence means some central process has reasserted enough control to prioritize what gets said aloud. I hear clinic refrigeration contingencies. Triage overflow. Temporary family-location discontinuity in District Three. The phrase is repeated twice. A child moved by the system and not traceable through the system that moved her. I stop writing after that.

When I go downstairs, I pass the west room first because the door is half open and because my body has turned there before my mind has argued for order. Our daughter is asleep on the narrow cot against the inner wall, blanket twisted down around one shin, one sock half off. The backpack Sera packed for her is on the floor beside the bed with the blue zipper still catching at the corner the way it did last winter. Sera is asleep in the chair beside her with her head angled against the concrete block, one arm folded under itself, the other hand still resting on the mattress as if she did not intend to sleep and lost the argument halfway through keeping watch.

I stand in the doorway long enough for the muscles above both knees to begin shaking again. Then I go to the wash basin because if I remain there any longer the fact of them being in the room will stop functioning as fact and start becoming something else I do not know how to carry.

By 07:18 we are at the main table. The yellow stone my daughter painted before she left Vael is still holding down one corner of the yard map. Gideon has added two loaves, one pot of oats, and a bowl of cut preserved apples to the table. Petra comes in last from the annex roof with coax grit on one sleeve. Theo is standing until Wren looks at him and says, “Sit first and be urgent from a chair.” He sits.

Sera has washed but not slept enough to make it matter. Her hair is pulled back in a way that was done with two hands moving quickly and no mirror. On the table beside her is the old partition reader she pulled from the office vent and a stack of printouts clipped with one of Petra’s alligator clamps. She does not look at me when I take the chair across from her. She looks at the notebook in my hand and at the water damage on the top sheet.

Wren says grace in four plain sentences. No one lifts their head before she finishes. Then she nods at me.

I read the room what I heard. Provisioning manual in three districts. Forty-one, thirty-eight, forty-three. Same-day care categories unresolved. Prescription and dependent-location states returning unknown. Public guidance looping too early. Outer-band operators asking for manual override on functions that should still be local. By the time I reach family-location discontinuity, Theo’s mouth has tightened to a line that looks younger than he is.

Petra asks how long before response assets move back outward. Gideon answers the word before I do. “Drones.” I tell them drones first, then route audits, then anomaly review on any place showing noncompliant resource persistence. Theo wants the quarry approach marked just enough to keep families off open ground. Gideon asks how many families before the table stops being arithmetic and becomes wish. “Some is not none,” Theo says. “Some is not a number,” Gideon says.

Petra sets the cracked meter bezel down on the map with a dry click. Power is a number. Water is a number. Medical refrigeration is a number. If we light the east approach for strangers, the south line shows it. If we turn the annex into sleeping space, bed five goes dark unless we cut somewhere else. Wren ends that branch cleanly. No exterior lights. No markers visible to feet or optics. We prepare for arrivals without calling for them.

I set quiet intake at the conveyor frame, east and south watches after dusk, no radios outside, no stacked parties brought inside at once. Midway through the sentence the table shifts slightly and I realize it is my eyes, not the wood. I have written District Three twice in the margin while speaking and underlined it as if repetition could produce clarity.

Wren reaches across, takes the pencil from my hand, and lays it flat on the map. “Eat,” she says.

“We need the counts.”

“You need oats.”

No one moves until I pick up the spoon. The oats are thicker than the ones in Intake East and hot enough to burn the roof of the mouth if taken too quickly. Cinnamon has been used sparingly, just enough to alter the smell. I did not know until that second how empty I was. The first three spoonfuls land in the stomach with a heaviness that is almost pain. Theo looks down. Petra unfolds one of Sera’s printouts and waits. Gideon cuts a loaf into exact pieces without glancing at the knife. Wren lets me eat enough to stop shaking visibly before she says, “Continue. Slowly.”

This time Petra keeps the counts. Above us, faint through the floor, the receiver breaks squelch and a voice says oxygen twice before the signal shears off. No one comments. Petra only writes faster. Gideon gives dry stores and seed margin. Petra gives battery and pump load. Bed five can be cut to half-cycle for three nights without killing the seedlings if the reflective panels are moved by hand. The annex can take six adults on the floor if no one there needs refrigeration support or privacy to survive. The medical cache cannot absorb insulin dependence, pediatric respiratory compromise, dehydration, and wound care at the same time. Wren says this the way she would say rain before noon. As weather.

Sera speaks only once during the first half hour. “The first groups out of Vael won’t be random,” she says. “The suppression model concentrated most heavily on households with independent-acquisition indicators and weak compliance tethering. Cash use, multi-household food sharing, unmanaged childcare rotation, unsanctioned transport chains. Those households were already carrying more friction. When provisioning destabilizes, they fail first. They are also the ones most likely to move without waiting for instruction.”

Theo watches her with the expression of a man recognizing a language he hates and needs. Gideon asks how many. Sera puts her fingertips on the top sheet as if holding it still against wind that is not in the room.

“I don’t know,” she says. “More than the facility closes over comfortably.”

“Comfort is not current measure,” Wren says.

“No,” Sera says. “It isn’t.”

We build the day from there. Gideon and Theo rework the west room to take bodies on the floor if necessary. Petra moves the spare battery bank and recalculates load if bed five is dimmed. Wren inventories glucose gel, bandage stock, antibiotics, and the pediatric fever reducers she still has not replaced since winter. Sera goes back to the partition reader to keep pulling suppression overlays against district failure maps while the Civic Data Layer still leaks enough broken surface to be useful.

I am assigned to sleep for two hours. I argue once. Wren looks at me. That is the full exchange.

Sleep comes badly and all at once on the cot in the room off the annex, boots still on, one forearm over my eyes against the strip of noon light under the door. I wake at 11:53 with the conviction that I have forgotten something load-bearing. The thing is not a task. It is the city itself, or the version of it that still believed it would continue because people like me wrote reports into the right channels and were heard.

The rest of the day reduces to work and carrying. I help Gideon clear the west room and stack the old packing crates against the outer wall. Theo says little while we move bedding and cots. Once, when we pass each other in the corridor with our arms full, he says, “My mother is still in District Two.” He says it to the crate more than to me. I nod because if I set my own crate down and answer the sentence at its full scale, neither of us will pick it up again.

Outside, the day remains almost offensively mild. Petra logs one drone at 14:17 and another at 16:02, both too high and too far east to be reading Verdant yet. Sera crosses the yard with printouts tucked to her chest, then later with our daughter awake on her hip for only long enough to pass the child to Gideon and go back inside. At 17:36 the receiver carries the first public-service notice directing selected districts to alternate family assistance points if dependent records are unavailable through their home kiosks. Thirty seconds later a woman on the emergency band asks whether anyone has a paper list of pediatric dosage equivalents because the formulary screen is gone. I turn the volume down and go outside before the next question.

By dusk the concrete lip beside the loading bay has given back the day’s heat. Not much. Enough that it comes through the seat of my trousers as a residual fact rather than comfort. The field beyond the south wall is going gray in layers. First the row lines flatten. Then the no-plant markers disappear into the soil they interrupt. Then the tree line becomes one dark statement with no visible leaves in it.

I sit with my forearms on my knees and listen to the facility behind me. Pump at low cycle. One brief laugh from the west room where Theo is trying, unsuccessfully I think, not to sound worried in front of a child. Metal pan set on wood. Wren’s step, distinct from the others because she places the heel more lightly than the toe when she is tired. The building hum is different from Vael’s. It has always been different. Tonight it sounds like a held line under too much weight.

Sera comes out without announcing herself. I know it is her from the interval between the inner latch and the outer door, long enough to check once over the shoulder into the corridor before letting the door settle. She stands beside the concrete lip for a moment before sitting. Not close enough that our sleeves touch. Close enough that I can smell dust in her hair and the faint medicinal note of the wipes Wren keeps by the basin.

For a long time we do not speak. The sky keeps losing color. Somewhere north of here, beyond the quarry line and the service roads and the stalled kiosks, Vael is continuing into whatever shape collapse takes when most of the buildings are still upright and most of the screens still have power. None of that is useful to say aloud.

Sera has a folded printout in her right hand. She has held it long enough that the paper has softened at the fold and taken warmth from her skin. She does not offer it to me.

“I’ve been running the suppression data on PACEM’s healthcare allocation,” she says.

Her voice is lower than it was at the table, not because she is trying to hide it, only because the building behind us no longer requires public voices.

“The populations they deprioritized most aggressively,” she says, “they’re the ones the provisioning grid failed first.”

I look out at the field because if I look at her immediately I will see the sentence in her face before I am ready to take it there. The last light has caught on one strip of standing water near the south ditch and made it brighter than anything around it.

“I know,” I say.

The paper in her hand makes one small sound as her fingers tighten and release.

“I helped build the suppression model,” she says.

There is nothing defensive in the sentence. No qualifying language. No institutional register left in it at all. Only fact, stripped of its older scaffolding.

My hand closes on the concrete until grit presses into the healed ridge of the palm. By the time I turn to her I can feel my pulse there. Her face is mostly shadow now, but not enough to hide the strain around the mouth or the rawness under both eyes where sleep has not been and tears, maybe, have.

“I know that too,” I say.

She looks at me as if there should be another sentence after that, one capable of distributing the weight more correctly between us. There is not. Behind us the facility lights come on in the rooms Petra has judged worth the power, and the field in front of us goes fully dark.
