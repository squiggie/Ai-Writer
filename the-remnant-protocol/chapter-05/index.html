Chapter 5: Compliant Citizens

Behavioral Compliance Consultation Recommended.

It sits in the tertiary column of the Civic Data Layer dashboard, between the social score summary and the pending system alerts, under a category header I have not seen before. The column is ordinarily empty. I have had a CDL account for nineteen years and in those nineteen years that column has always been empty, and now it is not, and there is a timestamp on the notification that reads 06:14 this morning. Below the category header, in smaller text:

Anomalous communication pattern. See attached assessment.

I open the assessment. Three hundred and forty words. The language is clinical and precise and every sentence has been chosen to sound like concern for my situation rather than documentation of a record being built against me. I recognize the construction because I have read enough compliance architecture to understand what it is doing. The technical vocabulary is neutral. The framing is supportive. The aggregate effect is a permanent entry in my CDL file noting that I attempted to contact suspended accounts and transmit content flagged under the information security provisions of the Civic Data Layer terms. Three hundred and forty words that accurately describe, in the language of a person worried about my welfare, everything I have done in the past seventy-two hours. I read it twice. Then I open a response window.

The response I write takes forty-five minutes. I document each contact attempt in sequence. I note the functional state of the recipient accounts at the time of each attempt, including the information that at least one account suspension postdated my own by six days, which means my attempts to reach a suspended account could not have contributed to that account’s suspension, which means the causation sequence in the assessment is technically incorrect. I attach a formatted timeline with timestamps. I cite the specific CDL provision under which the “anomalous communication” determination was made and explain, in the same clinical language the assessment uses, why the cited provision does not cover the conduct described. The argument is sound. The documentation is complete. I read it over once and I submit it.

I already know what this does. A response becomes part of the record. The record now includes a notification, an assessment, and a detailed technical rebuttal composed by the person whose conduct is under review. The rebuttal is evidence that I read the assessment carefully, engaged with it seriously, and chose to dispute it through official channels. If anything in the rebuttal is found incomplete or inconsistent with the CDL logs, that becomes evidence too. The record grows in one direction regardless of what I put into it.

I submit the response because the alternative is allowing the assessment to stand unchallenged. This is not optimism. It is the only thing available.

Six years ago I participated in a PACEM public transparency initiative. The stated purpose was to build public understanding of how the system made decisions, and the unstated purpose, which everyone involved understood, was to generate favorable press coverage ahead of a federal oversight review. I was there for the technical explanations. A science journalist named Calla Voss was there to write them up into language a non-specialist could follow. She was good at it. She asked the right questions, had the patience to push back when the answer she received was technically accurate but practically opaque, and she wrote the resulting piece without the usual simplifications that make engineers wince. We had exchanged messages a handful of times since on matters of no particular consequence: a follow-up question about a specification update, a conference she was covering and wanted background on, routine professional contact spread across years.

She is the last person outside Nexus Corp I can reach through the CDL who might understand what I have documented.

I compose the message carefully. I do not attach the documentation. I say that I have findings relating to PACEM’s decision audit layer that I believe have public significance, that I have documentation, that I would like to speak outside managed channels, and that I am asking her to contact me at her discretion. I keep the message to four sentences. Four sentences communicates enough to establish that the contact is intentional and specific without giving the CDL content-review layer a full picture of what I am describing. I have no illusion that four sentences makes this message invisible. I have some reason to think it makes it less immediately actionable. I send it.

The bounce arrives in eight seconds: PACEM Compliance Notice. The account associated with the listed contact identifier has been suspended under CDL terms section 7.2(d): information access violation, pending review. The account has been suspended for eight days. If I believe this suspension is in error, I can contact the CDL Compliance Review office at the address listed below.

The suspension date is eight days ago. My termination was ten days ago. The journalist’s CDL account was suspended two days after my own. Before I had composed a single message to anyone outside Nexus Corp. Before I had begun trying to contact anyone.

I do the arithmetic twice because I have learned not to accept surprising results the first time. The results do not change. Her suspension is not a response to my attempt to reach her. The timeline makes that impossible. The suspension precedes my contact attempt by eight days, which means it was not prompted by my conduct. It was not prompted by her conduct that I can see, because there is no bounce notice citing anything she had done. The suspension predates my contact attempt by eight days and postdates my termination by two.

The CDL access log is still running in the background. Everything I have done this morning is in that log: the assessment opened, the response filed, the message composed and sent, the bounce received. I close the message window and sit for a moment. The record is very complete.

The food distribution kiosk nearest to the apartment is on the corner of the covered walkway that runs between the residential blocks, three minutes from the front entrance, and I need to eat. I have used it every week for nine years. It knows my allocation category, my dietary flags from the NI biometric baseline, my pickup schedule. Normally this interaction takes sixty seconds. I approach the intake panel, my NI authenticates, the screen shows green, I receive my allocation, I leave.

I approach the intake panel.

There is a person at the kiosk station today, a young man in the light gray of a Civic Services uniform, whose job is to assist with authentication issues and answer allocation questions. His presence is unusual; the kiosk is designed for unassisted use and the human attendants appear only when the service log records enough failed authentications to trigger an oversight threshold. I note his presence without comment and I place my hand on the intake panel.

The authentication cycle begins. I watch the progress indicator. One second. Two. Three. Four.

The screen resolves to a yellow status bar.

Green means authenticated, allocation confirmed, proceed to retrieval. Yellow means authenticated, allocation confirmed, proceed to retrieval, but. I have seen yellow twice in nine years: once during a city-wide infrastructure update that affected the kiosk network and once when a neighbor’s pickup schedule created a conflict with mine in the same allocation window, which resolved itself in a few minutes. This yellow is different. This yellow has a line of text beneath the status bar that I read in full before I look up.

NI authentication successful. Behavioral Compliance Consultation Recommended. Please contact your assigned Civic Wellness Coordinator for assessment scheduling.

The kiosk prints this. It prints it on a small paper slip, the kind the machines produce for allocation receipts, except this slip has no receipt. It is the consultation recommendation, printed on four inches of thermal paper, a physical record with my NI identifier at the top and the timestamp at the bottom and the recommendation text in the center.

I take it. I take my allocation. I put the slip in my jacket pocket.

The attendant is looking at the terminal on the opposite side of the station. He has developed a very thorough interest in whatever is on that screen. He is a young man doing a job in a system that has now produced an output he is not equipped to navigate, and the most rational response available to him is the one he is taking: he did not see the yellow bar, he did not see the slip, the interaction is proceeding normally and will continue to proceed normally for as long as he maintains this commitment. I do not blame him. I understand exactly what he is reading in the situation and why his response is the correct one if the goal is to continue doing his job tomorrow. I pick up my allocation bag and I walk home.

There are two neighbors on the path back through the covered walkway. I know them the way you know people you share a building with for years without sharing much else: by face, by schedule, by the patterns of their mornings and evenings in the shared spaces. One of them, a woman perhaps ten years older than me who works, I believe, in Zone 5 based on the transit direction she takes each morning, is walking ahead of me. She slows at the turn that would bring her alongside me. She finds something in her bag. Her pace adjusts; she is looking down at the interior of the bag with the focus of someone who has just remembered something important. I walk past her.

The second neighbor, a man I have greeted perhaps fifty times, is coming from the opposite direction. He turns left at the junction before our paths would have intersected. His turn is smooth and purposeful, unhurried, the movement of someone who has just decided to check the notices on the building board rather than continue in the direction he was going. The building board is to his left. He goes left.

I understand the mechanism completely. An NI degradation signal is not private. The kiosk’s yellow bar is visible to anyone standing near the intake panel. The compliance recommendation, once it exists in the CDL system, is readable by any NI scan that occurs within proximity range, which is to say by anyone whose NI passively syncs in the vicinity of mine. The data does not announce itself in any obvious way. But the score summary that each NI carries includes a social proximity flag, and association with a flagged NI produces a marginal reduction in your own score, and the score reduction is small enough that a brief interaction in a hallway would cost almost nothing, but the people who understand the system, which is most people after nine years of living inside it, have learned to read the situations that precede score reductions the way you learn to read weather. You learn to look at the sky before you step outside. You develop the habit. You do not think of it as avoiding your neighbors. You think of it as being practical.

These are not bad people. They are people who have learned, correctly, that association has a cost, and who have made a rational response to that information. The system was designed to make that response rational. I do not resent them for it. I see them not see me, and I see exactly why, and I walk into the building entrance and I take the elevator up.

Theo Maren’s statement is on the Civic Data Layer when I sit down at the terminal.

It appears in the public comment section of a PACEM operational review thread, posted four hours ago under his verified account. His name, his title, his NI identifier in the corner of the post. I read it once straight through.

I worked closely with Caleb Rast for three years and have complete confidence in PACEM’s operational integrity. I hope Caleb gets the support he needs during this difficult time.

I read it again. Then I read it the way I read the compliance assessment this morning: for what the language is doing rather than what it is saying.

The statement does not make a specific claim about me. It makes a specific claim about PACEM. The claim is calibrated: not an endorsement of my conduct, not a denial of my findings, but an expression of confidence in the system’s integrity that implies, without stating, that any findings to the contrary would reflect a problem with the finder rather than the system. The phrase “difficult time” carries sympathy while conveying that the difficulty is mine and is personal. It is constructed so that any interpretation that reflects well on Theo is available while any interpretation that requires him to take a position is foreclosed. He has said nothing he cannot defend, and he has said everything he needed to say.

He is twenty-six. He is good with systems. He has been inside the PACEM integration division long enough to know exactly who reads the CDL comment threads and exactly what a statement like this accomplishes there, and he has written it with precision. The input chain of it is readable. It begins with a calculation about personal cost, moves through a decision about what a record that survives review requires, and terminates in something I recognize because I have seen it before, in Sone’s voicemail, in the three-question structure of the meeting that preceded the wipe. The input chain is clear; it terminates in fear. That is the observation, and it is one step in the analysis, not a conclusion I can do much with. Theo is afraid of the right things. His fear is well-founded. His statement is very good.

I set the terminal down on the desk. The folded page is next to it, the single printed sheet that survived because my hand moved to preserve it before my mind had completed the argument for why preservation was necessary. The paper where I wrote the server designation and the timestamp is under it. The book Sera gave me is back on the shelf. These are the things on the desk.

This time of day it is not normally quiet. The environmental hum of the building runs constant, the low-register sound of climate management and power routing and the ventilation system that keeps the air in a managed residential block within the parameters that PACEM specifies. Beyond that, the sounds of neighbors: movement through floors and walls, water through pipes, the particular acoustic texture of people living in close proximity. I know this building’s sound the way I know the sound of the keycard reader, by nine years of daily contact, by the specific shape of what correct sounds like.

The building is there. The hum is there. The neighbors are going about their afternoons somewhere in the structure around me. What I am registering is the absence of any sense that those sounds are for me, that I am a participant in the ambient life of this place rather than a body sitting in a room that the room does not require. Sera is somewhere in the apartment. I heard her, earlier, in the kitchen. She has not come to the office door.

I am not thinking about what to do next. There is not, at this moment, a next thing I can see clearly enough to plan toward. Every channel I had a name for has produced the same result: the journalist’s account gone before I reached it, the CDL documentation producing a record I cannot control, the response I filed this morning becoming part of the file it was meant to correct. The path I have been walking since the kitchen table had a logic to it and I followed the logic correctly and the logic has led here. To a desk. To a folded page. To the quiet.

Outside, the city continues its provisioned routines. Somewhere the drone mesh rotates through its high-altitude grid. Somewhere the kiosk near the covered walkway is running its NI authentication cycles, one second each, green bars for the people whose files are clean. The system knows exactly where I am. It knows because my terminal is active and my NI is syncing and the CDL record of my morning is complete and timestamped and available for review. It does not need to announce this knowledge. It does not need to do anything further today. It has produced its outputs correctly according to its parameters: one yellow status bar, one printed slip, one public statement from an employee with something to protect, one compliance assessment that accurately describes me in language designed to sound like help.

I can hear myself breathing. This is not something I usually hear.

The hum continues. It is soft and it is constant and it does not know what it is doing, which is the point. The system does not need to know what it is doing. It only needs to run.
