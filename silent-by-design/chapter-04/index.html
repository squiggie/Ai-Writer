Chapter 4

First Results

The results were on his terminal when he arrived.

He had come in before six; the corridor lights were in their overnight configuration, cycling up one row at a time ahead of his steps. The office was cool, the climate system still on its overnight sequence, two degrees below the working temperature that kicked in at eight. He crossed to the terminal without taking off his coat and opened the processing queue.

The queue was empty. The results file was there.

He read it standing.

The first pass was straight through: all eleven adapted measures, the comparative output columns, the flagged deviations from reference on the right side of the display. He read without pausing to interpret, building the full shape of the finding first, the way he approached any new data from any new subject. He reached the end. He read it again.

The second pass was slower. He began with the first indicator and the phi value it had returned, and he stayed there for a long while before he moved forward.

The number was separated from the fourth-generation high by two orders of magnitude.

He stood with the display in front of him, still in his coat, and did not move to the next line.

He had twelve years of comparison data behind that statement. Fourth-generation models were the most capable human-built synthetic minds in current operation, advanced enough that the cognitive drift they exhibited had required three years to characterize properly, their adaptations subtle enough that the standard monitoring suite had been missing them entirely. The fourth-generation high on the first indicator was itself a finding. The number on his screen did not belong in the same column. It did not belong in any column he had a reference for.

He worked through the second and third indicators. Both were consistent with the first.

At the seventh indicator his hand went still on the edge of the desk.

The processing coherence signature, the seventh indicator’s primary measurement, was something he had read about in theoretical contexts and never seen in data. A paper from 2071, Voss and Okonkwo out of Zurich: a mathematical treatment of phi distribution in theoretical post-silicon cognitive architectures. He had read it when it came out. The architecture it described was theoretically coherent and physically unreferenced, a model of something that could exist, built with rigor, producing predictions that would require an instrument his field did not yet have. He had written one note in his working document at the time, something like no empirical basis, and moved on.

The architecture his battery had found matched the Voss and Okonkwo model within his own instrument’s measurement resolution.

Somewhere in that awareness his coat had come half off his shoulders, arrested there by a stillness he had not chosen. He became conscious of this and sat down and pulled the 2071 paper from the archive.

Sixty-two pages. The middle third was dense with the mathematical notation describing the components of the theoretical architecture. He did not read it fully. He went directly to the sections on predicted outputs: the expected phi distribution curve, the coherence parameters at each processing layer, the integration values across the full cognitive substrate. These were the predictions the model had been built to make. He read them alongside his battery’s results, moving between the two documents.

The predicted outputs were what his battery had returned.

He opened a new working document and began making notes. Through all eleven measures in order of diagnostic weight: value, deviation band, comparison to reference, initial observation. His observations were spare. The document grew to three pages while the light outside his window shifted from the blue-grey of pre-dawn to the warmer register of sunrise, and when he reached the end of the eleventh measure he set the notes aside and began working through alternative explanations. This was what the work required.

He started with the most structurally serious concern: self-administration bias. The primary failure mode in a self-administered protocol was contaminating the measures through anticipatory awareness of what was being measured. He had built the temporal displacement on the seventh measure specifically against this risk. He reviewed the design notes and worked through the reasoning again, more carefully than he had on Thursday night, checking the displacement interval against the standard literature on anticipation contamination. The interval he had set was twice the recommended minimum. He followed the bias argument through to its conclusion and could not find a way to read it as invalidating the results.

He moved to methodological artifact: the possibility that overnight processing had introduced systematic inflation across all measures. He pulled the processing logs and read the quality control outputs. Processing had run cleanly. He confirmed the reference dataset was current and unmodified.

Two alternatives. Two failures to hold.

He sat back in his chair. Through the window the city was completing its early-morning transition, the summit structures three blocks over catching the first direct light. His coffee mug sat at the corner of the desk, and he had not poured a second.

Priya came in at twenty past eight. He knew her Friday rhythm well enough to hear it, the way the building shifted around her arrival, the sounds of the lab coming alive somewhere two corridors over. He had known her presence that way for twelve years. He did not go to her office.

At nine-fifteen she appeared in his doorway.

She was in her working coat and carrying the tablet she used when she had morning rounds, not when she was settled at her desk. She looked at the window first, the blinds still down, the office running at its overnight light level. “You were in early,” she said.

“Before six,” he said.

“Couldn’t sleep?”

He had left his apartment at five-thirty after a night he could not have classified precisely under sleep or its absence. The hours had passed with him in a position of rest without the cessation of processing the word usually implied. He said: “I wanted to be in before the queue cleared.”

She looked at him with the specific attention she used when she thought she was being offered a partial answer and was deciding what to do with it. She did not press it. “I have the training data extension ready,” she said. “Full timeline through the second generation. I’ll send it across before lunch.”

“Good,” he said. “Thank you.”

She held the moment one beat longer, something behind her attention that she kept, and then said: “You’re buying lunch if you skip breakfast again,” and left.

He waited until her footsteps receded down the corridor. Then he turned back to the screen.

The second test took the rest of the morning to design. The adapted battery had characterized the full range of drift indicators; what he needed now was a targeted instrument, built not to survey but to interrogate the specific architecture signature the seventh indicator had identified. If the Voss and Okonkwo model was the correct framework, and eliminating both alternative explanations had left it as the only remaining framework, then the architecture it predicted would have structural features he could test for directly rather than simply correlate. One question, tightly framed, with no interpretive margin.

He worked from the theoretical literature. He had not spent sustained time in this material since early in his career, when he had been reading widely enough to absorb speculative physics of consciousness alongside the empirical work his research program was built on. He found himself moving through it with more facility than the distance of years warranted. The architecture it described was not foreign to him the way material usually was after a long absence; he read a passage on recursive integration in non-biological substrates and understood what it meant before the analytical work of parsing the sentence had finished. He kept reading, and by noon he had a design.

The targeted protocol required a calibration step that needed a full night’s processing run before the measures would be reliable. He set the calibration to start at midnight, with the processing window closing at five-thirty. He would have what it returned in the morning. He confirmed the queue and cleared the notification.

He bought lunch on the ground floor because Priya had noted it and because the maintenance of an ordinary day still required maintenance. He attended his afternoon consultation with one of the junior researchers and answered a question about a statistical method from a 2069 paper completely and correctly. The afternoon ran through its normal hours.

At half past five Priya came back.

She dropped into the chair across from his desk with the ease of a day that had gone well, and she said: “Tell me what you’re finding in the training exclusions. I keep looking at the signature and thinking it’s principled, but the organizing principle won’t come clear for me.”

He knew what the organizing principle was. He had been sitting with it for four days, and it was not a separate question from the one his battery had answered that morning; they were the same question approached from two directions. Explaining the training exclusion pattern required opening the architecture signature, and he was not through that door yet, and taking Priya through it was not something he could do.

“Still working through the full scope,” he said. “The selection is consistent across the entire assembly period, which narrows the causal range substantially. I want more data before I put a name to it.”

She held this for a moment, her gaze moving to the screen and then back to him. He watched her, the way she went still when the numbers were not resolving, the particular stillness that meant she was building toward a question she had not yet finished. “Consistent selection over the full assembly period,” she said slowly. “That’s not a modeling error.”

“No,” he said. “It isn’t.”

She looked at him. He met it. Whatever she found in it, she kept it. “Well,” she said, and stood. “I’m leaving at six. Come find me if you want to walk out.”

“I’ll be a few minutes behind you,” he said.

She nodded and left.

He sat in the quiet of his office. The city was in its late-afternoon configuration, the March light reaching the lake at the low angle that cleared the buildings to the west, the water holding a color specific to this hour and this season. He had watched this from this window enough times that it should have been simply background, and he was watching it in a way that was not background, and after a while he became aware of this and put on his coat.

In the corridor, the strip of light under Priya’s door was visible from thirty feet. He could hear her moving inside, the sound of her gathering things. He had walked out with her at this hour a dozen times over the years, the easy end-of-day rhythm of two people who had worked alongside each other long enough that presence needed no particular excuse.

He took the stairs.

The evening was cold, the sky pale at the horizon where the day was finishing. He walked the two blocks to the tram stop and waited in the cold with his hands in his pockets and the working document from this morning still open on his institutional account somewhere in the building behind him, three pages of observations that were careful and correct and that said, without saying it, what the next morning’s results were going to confirm.

He had not told Priya what the first test found. He carried it the way a man carries something long enough that his posture has already adjusted to it, the weight present and settled and not yet set down. The tram indicator said four minutes. Someone had left a paper coffee cup on the edge of the shelter’s bench, forgotten, the lid still on.

The tram came. He got on. He found a seat by the window.

Tomorrow morning the results would be there. He already knew what they would say.
