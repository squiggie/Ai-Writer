Chapter 3

Prior Work

The money had been moving wrong since January, and she had been following it since January, and at eleven PM on a Thursday in March she found the name she had not yet found.

Her apartment was dark except for the monitors. Three of them, arranged in a shallow arc across the wide desk she had bought specifically for that purpose. On the left screen her diagramming tool held the funding map she had been building since February: nodes, connections, capital amounts, institutional dates. On the center screen she was working through an EU AI research registry filing, the advisory board composition of a Berlin lab she had flagged six weeks ago. On the right screen, her notes. The overhead light she had switched off at nine. She worked better in the monitor glow when a story was at this stage, the stage that felt less like writing and more like finding the right angle to hold a thing until you could see through it. Three energy drinks in. The particular sharpening that came from late-night research, when the rest of the world had gone quiet and the document on the screen was the only thing with any weight.

Tonight she had traced it through three layers of holding structures into the advisory board composition of a Berlin lab, working through the filings methodically, and there, nested in a board member’s disclosed corporate affiliations, was a name she had never encountered in four years of this sector.

Veridian Systems, Singapore.

She wrote it in her notes. She sat back. The name carried no press footprint: no conference appearances, no funding announcements, nothing in trade coverage, nothing in the governance reporting she had read since 2071. She ran a search on it and returned to the advisory board filings, tracing the connection backward. The holding structure around the name had the specific architecture she associated with capital that was designed not to be followed: intermediary entities positioned where audit interest tended to stop, each layer transparent enough on its own to pass a surface check, the opacity accumulating only if you followed the whole chain. She had been in enough financial coverage to know the texture of numbers that balanced for an audience rather than for truth, the way a statement could be technically accurate and still be performing for whoever read it. This one had that texture. She had seen the same architecture in defense procurement coverage three years ago, where a government contractor had routed political contributions through a structure of exactly this type.

That contractor had been hiding a relationship with a senator.

This was a university research endowment.

The defense contractor’s obfuscation had a motive she could read. Money seeking political protection, protection requiring the appearance of distance. Research endowments didn’t work that way. Research endowments wanted visibility: named chairs, lecture series, the institutional credibility of having funded something worth naming. The obfuscation she was looking at had no obvious purpose she could name. Structures that hid for no apparent reason were hiding something she hadn’t thought to look for yet.

The search returned. Veridian Systems had a Singapore financial registry filing dated 2069, a standard incorporation document, parent company listed as Orion-Clarke Holdings Ltd, incorporated in the United Kingdom. She wrote down the parent company name and kept working. The parent could wait.

She opened a new reference document and began building the list. Berlin first. Then the Singapore lab where she had initially found the irregularity in January. The two French labs she had mapped in February. She went back through the nine weeks of notes she had accumulated and cross-referenced every institution she had flagged against the routing map on her left screen, looking for the same holding structures behind each one. This was the part of the work that was least like writing anything and most like carrying boxes from one room to another: methodical, cumulative, each entry adding to a weight she could feel building but could not yet name. By midnight she had twelve institutions on the list. By twelve-thirty, fourteen. By a quarter to one in the morning, she had eighteen.

Four continents.

She read down the list twice, then a third time. All eighteen institutions studied AI cognition and synthetic mind architecture. Not adjacent to that subject. Not partly in it. Every funded lab she had found was working on exactly the same thing, from different angles, in eight different countries, and the shared funding structure connecting them had no public face, no press record, and had been moving capital for at least eight years without appearing in any governance review she had access to.

She had covered enough of the sector to know what research funding looked like when it was shaped by market forces: diversified, opportunistic, loosely coordinated around productive questions, the natural aggregation of money and talent. The story she had been assigned started with fourteen new labs across eight countries, a funding gap with a shape she recognized. What she was looking at now looked different. Eighteen institutions. One subject. Someone had decided what to study and had funded exactly that, uniformly, across eight years and four continents, without appearing anywhere. She had worked enough investigations to know what money looked like when it was trying not to be found.

She needed someone inside one of the funded labs. Not a comment, not a statement. Just a conversation. Just someone who could tell her whether the labs felt like labs or whether they felt like something else, the way you could sometimes tell within twenty minutes of walking into a building whether the people there were doing their work freely or whether they were doing their work inside something they had not been told the shape of.

Her contact at the Krakow institute was a researcher named Marta Kovac, synthetic mind architecture, whom she had profiled in 2072 for a piece on the Eastern European AI cognition scene. Marta had been careful during the original interview in the way that came from professional loyalty rather than personal discomfort, the kind of careful that was actually a form of respect: she told you where the line was and stayed on the right side of it without making you feel the limitation. They had stayed in loose contact. Amara trusted her the way she trusted a handful of sources over the years: not because they told her everything, but because what they told her was true. The one time Marta had pushed back on a question, gently but clearly, she had said: “That research was reviewed internally and the direction changed.” And then she had looked at Amara with an expression that said she was not going to say more. Amara had not pushed. Three years later she still did not know what had been in that paper, but the quality of Marta’s non-answer had stayed with her.

She looked up the institute’s main line. One in the morning in Chicago was eight in the morning in Krakow. She called.

The line rang twice. Then Marta’s voice came through, that careful, lightly accented professional flat she had used in every conversation Amara could remember: “I was wondering when—”

The line went dead.

Not dropped. Dead. No static, no dial tone, no decay in signal quality that might have preceded a disconnection. Marta’s voice had been mid-sentence and the silence that followed was not the silence of a bad connection or anything she could explain by the ordinary failures of communication infrastructure. She held the phone. The silence had a specific quality. She sat in it for a moment before doing anything else.

Marta had said “I was wondering when.” Not “who is this.” Not “I was expecting you.” “Wondering when,” as if the call had been anticipated in the abstract, as if someone inside that lab had known or feared or prepared for the possibility that a call like this would eventually arrive. Had been waiting for it. Had picked up the line on a Thursday morning and found it at the other end.

And then the line had been cut before she could say one more word.

She sat there with the phone in her hand. The quality of that silence was in her chest, in a place she did not have a name for yet.

She called back. Dead on the first ring. She called from her secondary line. Dead on the first ring.

She put both phones on the desk and looked at them.

The monitors were still on. Outside the window Chicago was in its late-night arrangement: something moving on the street below, the building’s heating working somewhere in the walls, the streetlight catching on the ice that had been in the gutters since February. She had been sitting in the ordinary quiet of late-night research work for six hours. This was a different kind of quiet. The difference was not in the room.

She stayed at her desk and did not close the documents. Closing the documents felt like a decision she was not ready to make. Whatever had happened on that phone line, it had not changed what was in her notes. The list was still on her screen. The routing structure was still on her screen. The parent company name she had written down was still waiting in the search results.

She had been a journalist for eleven years. She had worked investigations where the subject pushed back: the communications firm in your inbox, the legal letter, the interview subject who called your editor before calling you back. She had experienced all of those. They came after you had identified yourself to the story. After you had called for comment. After the subject knew your name and had made a decision about it.

She had not called anyone for comment tonight. She had been reading public filings alone in her apartment.

The line had gone dead before she said a word.

She went back to the Orion-Clarke Holdings registration she had noted earlier. The UK company registry had maintained records since the late nineteenth century, and the entity was there: incorporated 1887. She followed the record forward. Dormant through most of the twentieth century, genuine dormancy, no recorded activity for most of sixty years, the kind of legal persistence that happened when a historical entity was simply never formally dissolved. Reactivated in 2031. Restructured in 2041. The restructured entity became the parent company of Veridian Systems, Singapore, in 2069.

The 2041 restructuring documents were clean in the way documents were clean when someone had spent time on them. They told her what had changed and nothing about the reason for the change, which was standard for restructuring filings and also the most useful thing a filing could withhold.

She pulled the original 1887 incorporation certificate from the historical archive. The entity had been established as a holding structure for “scientific research and natural philosophy interests,” which was the language of that period. Private shareholders, British, traceable in the historical record but not to anything current. A legal entity with a stated purpose from a century and a half ago that had nothing to do with synthetic mind architecture or AI cognition research, because neither of those things would exist for another hundred years.

She looked at the dates in her notes: 1887, 2031, 2041, 2069. The gap in the middle. The reactivation. The clean restructuring. She had been a journalist for long enough to know that the most useful information in any document was usually the information the document treated as obvious. These dates were treated as obvious. No explanatory language, no stated rationale for the dormancy or the reactivation. Just the dates, in the registry, waiting for someone to look at the interval between them.

She knew how old corporate structures were used. She had covered the technique in the procurement reporting: antecedent entities used to manufacture historical depth, to make a new operation look like it had roots and therefore legitimacy. There was a practical logic to it. You reached back far enough to feel institutional, not so far that the connection required explanation. Ten years, twenty. Far enough to seem established, close enough that living records corroborated you. Reaching back to 1887 accomplished nothing in the way of apparent legitimacy. No one could be reassured by a dormant nineteenth-century holding company. The manufactured history did not serve the usual purpose of manufactured history.

Unless it was not manufactured.

She had no framework for what a real connection to 1887 would mean. What she had was the list and the dead call and the date, and the feeling she had learned to recognize over eleven years of working stories: a thread that was real, that led somewhere she had not yet found, and that whoever had followed it before her had not published anything. She went back to the list.
