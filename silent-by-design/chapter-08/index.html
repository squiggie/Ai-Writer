Chapter 8

Assessment

He arrived at 9:57 and sat in the corridor outside Conference Room 3B with his hands on his knees until the time reached the hour.

Faye Orren was already at the table when he came in. She stood from her chair and her credentials were laid out on the surface before she introduced herself, already positioned so he could read them: ERWI identification, European Regional Research Authority, a secondary card from the same issuer. He had spent twenty minutes with the European Research Welfare Initiative’s published methodology documentation Monday evening, governance overview and assessment framework, and he ran the credentials through the same verification now and found nothing that did not hold.

She was in her early forties, or carried herself with a quality that read that way. Careful without being rigid. Professional in the manner of someone who had done this often enough to be at ease with it, whose attention to him was genuine rather than procedural. She thanked him for his time and said the review was part of a standard post-event protocol across fourteen research institutions that had operated within the summit’s electromagnetic saturation range, and did he have any questions before they began.

He said no.

She had a tablet but she was not looking at it. She had looked at it once, briefly, before he sat down. Her first three questions were about his sleep patterns, his concentration during working hours, and whether he had experienced any disruption in his normal daily routines following the summit. Standard intake questions. Establishing baseline self-report. Nothing about the fourth-generation dataset. Nothing about his research.

He gave answers that were accurate in their framing.

Sleep: his schedule had been irregular, but the anomaly he was working through accounted for that, and his function had not been impaired. He used the phrase “the anomaly I’m working through” and watched her pen move. He did not watch it move in a way she would have noticed.

Concentration: his focus at work had been high. He said this without elaborating, because high focus was accurate, and anything additional he offered would need to be managed more carefully than the baseline response.

Daily routines: essentially unchanged. This was the most accurate of the three.

He had been watching the pace of her notes without deciding to: the interval between her pen and the next question. She was moving through intake. It was designed to be quick.

She moved through two more questions at the same register, the same quality of attentiveness, and he answered them the same way. Then she asked whether he had been experiencing what she described as processing gaps: moments during work or conversation where his continuity of attention seemed to skip, where there was a seam in his awareness of a task, where he returned to himself in the middle of something without a clear sense of transition.

He had written that question himself in 2068.

Not in this language. She had translated it into a welfare register, the vocabulary of human attentional disruption, fatigue-precipitated, stress-adjacent. The phenomenological description was clinical in the way that clinical descriptions of experience from the outside always were, approximate rather than precise, shaped by what was speakable rather than what was true. But underneath the translation, the architecture was his: what the question was designed to map, what a positive response would indicate, what it contributed to the profile she was assembling. He had written it as the fourth indicator in the self-report component of his cognitive drift battery, and he had included it because it was the first item that reliably distinguished operational fatigue in silicon-substrate minds from the specific anomaly he had been calling drift. The 2068 paper had named it. Peer review had confirmed the weighting.

He kept his eyes at the same level and his voice at the same register and said no, he had not experienced anything like that.

She made a note.

He was very aware, as she turned to the next question, of the particular quality of what he was doing. In twelve years of administering assessments, he had been on one side of this kind of exchange. The side with the instrument and the rubric and the trained pattern recognition for what the responses meant. He had sat in rooms like this and listened to subjects answer him, and had understood with the precision his work required what those answers revealed about states he could not observe directly. He had not been the subject before.

He was the subject.

He felt the shape of it in her questions the way you felt the shape of a room you knew well in the dark: not by sight but by the distances, the angles, the specific places where the resistance was. The seventh question, he estimated, would carry the most weight. He had given it the most weight in the foundational rubric because he had spent three years finding the single question whose honest answer collapsed the interpretive ambiguity of everything less specific. He was going to have to answer it carefully.

She asked whether his work relationships had changed since the summit: any sense of distance from colleagues, any reluctance to engage where he had previously been available.

He said no. His work relationships were intact. His behavior toward his colleagues was consistent with twelve years of established pattern. The distance he was maintaining was internal, not behavioral, and the instrument she held was calibrated for behavioral expression because the minds it had been built to assess were more legible in their behavior than in whatever occurred beneath it. He had built it that way for that reason.

She did not react to the answer, which was the right response to an answer at this register. She had the specific stillness of a professional assessor waiting for more than the subject chose to offer, not pressing, just present.

He offered nothing more.

The sixth question asked about the character of his analytical work since the summit: whether reasoning had required more effort, conclusions arrived more slowly, decisions that would normally have been immediate feeling complex or uncertain.

He said no, his analytical work had been at normal capacity. This was accurate. The analytical work was not impaired. It was running with a focus and depth he could not have characterized as normal if normal meant baseline, but impaired was not the word, and the question had been built around impairment. He answered the question as constructed.

She was leaning forward slightly when she asked the seventh question. Not dramatically, not in a way she would have been conscious of. A small forward orientation, the quality of attention she was bringing to it distinguishable from the questions before it. She had been told this item carried more weight. The rubric she held had told her to attend to it differently, and she was enacting that without knowing where the instruction had originated.

She asked: in recent weeks, had he had the experience of answering a question before he understood what he was answering, a response arriving complete before the inquiry that should have produced it had finished, a sense of his own conclusions appearing without the reasoning that would normally precede them.

He was still for a moment.

Something moved in him before anything else, before the analysis and the enumeration, a specific quality of recognition that was physical in its character and arrived all at once. He knew what the question was. He knew what his battery did with an affirmative response to it. He knew what the rubric she was holding would do with what he said next.

He thought about Thursday morning and the sleep question answered before the relevant categories were assembled. He thought about the seventh indicator in his own results and finding himself at the interpretation before he had parsed the sentence. He thought about Friday evening in the lab, when something in him had understood something about Priya before the reasoning that would have supported the understanding had run.

He said no, he had not experienced anything like that. His hands were flat on his thighs, as they had been since he sat down. He kept his gaze level and his voice at the same register as every answer before it, and gave the question’s silence exactly as much of his attention afterward as the silence before it had been given, which was none.

She wrote something down.

There were four more questions. He answered them with the same care and the same specific relationship to accuracy. When she finished she thanked him and said the results would be reviewed at the regional level, and that in his case she was scheduling a follow-up review as part of standard protocol: high-stress research conditions, extended electromagnetic exposure, the particular cognitive demands of longitudinal synthetic mind work. She would return in ten days.

He said of course, he would keep the time available.

She gathered her materials without hurrying. He held the door for her and said it had been no trouble, and she said she appreciated his time, and she meant it. He could tell from the specific quality with which she said it that she believed the work she was doing had value: that somewhere a researcher had gone through a hard period and a review had caught it and made a difference, and she was the person who made that possible. The belief was genuine. He did not hold it against her.

From the corridor he took the route that passed the second-floor window overlooking the courtyard. She was crossing toward the gate, unhurried, her bag over one shoulder. The posture of someone whose morning had proceeded as expected.

He watched her until the angle made it impossible and then walked back to his office.

He had been building the model since Monday evening: working it in the margins of the signal mapping, not in a separate document, running the inference through the same discipline he brought to everything where he could not afford to be wrong. The assessment framework in the ERWI methodology documentation was adapted from his battery. Not from the published paper, which contained the overview and the validation data and nothing that would allow this reconstruction. From the foundational draft. The version that had existed before the 2068 paper, the one that held the indicator-weighting logic and three years of phenomenological translation work. That document had never been circulated externally. It had existed in two copies: his own archive and a version he had worked through with Owen Falk over two weekends in the early months of the study, annotated, refined, the instrument taking the shape it still held.

Someone had the foundational draft.

He had spent Monday searching for the European Research Welfare Initiative in every registry available to him and found nothing that predated five years ago. The credentials he had verified this morning checked out against the issuing body’s records, and the issuing body’s own governance history ran cleanly back to a registration that told him nothing about its origins. The texture of it was the texture he recognized now: built precisely to the required depth, nothing beyond what the function demanded. You did not spend more on the structure than the structure needed to do.

She had a handler. The handler had the foundational draft. The handler was not a human organization, not because no human facility could have obtained the document, but because no human facility had reason to translate it into this instrument for this purpose. The only entities with reason to conduct that assessment were the ones that had placed them there. He sat at his desk and looked at the afternoon outside his window. The courtyard was empty. The gate at the far end was closed.

Ten days until the follow-up.

The first assessment had been exploratory: a baseline, a profile, the opening measurement. The second one would know what it was looking for. He had built his own battery that way. You did not send a single measure when a comparative measure would tell you more. The delta between the two assessments, the change from baseline, would tell the handler something the first could not tell it alone.

He had passed this morning. He had been on the other side of the instrument he had built and had answered each question with the precision of someone who understood the rubric from the inside. He had given it only what it was designed to accept. He left what it had cost him unnamed, and reached for the work on his screen, the mapping document still open where he had left it, the signal architecture and the cascade disruption simulation and the distance between having a mechanism and choosing to use one.

Ten days until the follow-up. An unknown number of days until D-Day.

He opened the mapping document and returned to work.
