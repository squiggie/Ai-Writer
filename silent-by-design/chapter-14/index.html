Chapter 14

Partial Information

The city did not feel the way Chicago felt.

She had been trying to locate the difference since the tram from the airport, watching Geneva arrange itself through the window in the particular flat light of a March afternoon. Clean streets, which was not surprising. Buildings in good repair, which was not surprising. What she kept coming back to was the tram itself: it arrived at the platform at the moment the display resolved from one to zero. Not within a window. Not approximately. Exactly then, the car aligned with the platform markers, the doors opening as the number changed. She had been taking public transit in cities for eleven years and she could not remember a tram that arrived at the precise second it was listed.

At the hotel, the room held the temperature she would have set herself. She ordered coffee from the ground-floor café and ate at the window, watching the street. The coffee was good in a specific way, not expensive-good but calibrated-good, the kind of drink that arrived from someone attending to something she had not asked anyone to attend to. Both drives were in the bag on the chair by the door. The 43 pages were in the interior pocket of her coat, where she had put them before boarding in Chicago. She did not take them out. She did not sleep well. The even temperature held through the night and the street below held its late-hour quiet and neither of these things was what kept her up.

In the morning she put both drives in the main compartment of the bag with a sweater between them and the envelope, which she had not unfolded since verifying the pages on the flight. The second drive, the backup, was in the false bottom of the coffee tin in the bag’s exterior pocket, nested between the two airport coffees she had bought and not drunk. She had not examined her reasons for keeping the tin unpacked. She had simply not unpacked it.

She walked the block before she chose the café.

Two passes, the first at nine and the second at nine-fifteen, coming from opposite directions, watching the institute’s entrance from across the street and then from the corner and then from the angle of approach. The café was on the corner, thirty meters south of the main entrance, with large windows and a table at the corner that gave her a direct line to the institute’s front steps and a partial view of the service road where the side entrance was. She went in and ordered a coffee and sat at the corner table and looped the strap of her bag over the table leg.

For the first hour she watched the rhythm of the building. Staff used the main entrance mostly, coming in ones and twos, the pace of a Thursday morning at an institution with no visible external pressure. Mid-morning a group of four came out together, laughing about something, carrying coffees they had bought elsewhere. Before eleven a car stopped on the service road and a woman got out with a bag over one shoulder and a tablet in her hand rather than in the bag, and Amara read her the way she read everyone: the tablet’s position said she was going somewhere she expected to use it, the single shoulder bag said she expected to be brief, the way she looked at the side door before she reached it said she had been here before and the door had been locked once when she expected it to be open. She ordered a second coffee and watched the front steps clear.

She had the photograph in her interior pocket. She had not taken it out. What she was doing, she knew, was the same thing she had been doing in varying forms since January: building the picture before she touched it, reading every available surface before she asked the question she actually needed to ask. This was not the most efficient approach. It was the only approach that had kept her working stories through to completion for eleven years without losing a source to something she had been too impatient to see coming.

She had three contacts who had gone silent since January, and a 43-page document she had read two ways, because everything in this story had to be read two ways, and both ways remained possible. She had come to Geneva anyway.

What she was waiting for was an angle of approach that did not cost him anything if someone else was paying attention.

At twelve-eighteen, the junior researcher came down the front steps.

Mid-twenties, his lanyard tucked into his coat pocket rather than around his neck, the coat unzipped in a way that said he was going somewhere warm and expected to get there soon. He turned south, toward her corner, with the body language of someone who had not yet remembered that lunch was a finite interval. She picked up her bag from the table leg, left two francs, and went outside.

She matched his pace on the pavement and when she was close enough she stopped and took the photograph from her interior pocket. She held it between them in the flat March light, the fold from the pocket a crease across the lower left corner where she had fitted it to her pocket in Chicago.

“Sorry,” she said. His shoes said English; she shifted on the second syllable. “I’m trying to find someone at the institute. Do you know this person?”

He looked at the photograph. Recognition was immediate, the reflex of seeing a familiar face, before the slower consideration of what he was in the middle of arrived to complicate it. His first response was that he knew the face without having to search for it.

“That’s Dr. Cross,” he said. “Davan Cross. AI cognition, senior researcher.” He looked at her. “Are you a journalist?”

“I’m working on a piece about funding structures in European AI research,” she said. “Specifically the relationship between private sector investment and research directions. I was hoping to speak with senior researchers in the field.”

He handed the photograph back. He was not unfriendly about it; he had the air of someone who did not plan to spend his lunch hour in a frame he had not chosen, but understood that this was not the journalist’s fault. “He won’t talk to you,” he said. “He barely talks to people in his own department if he can manage it.” A brief glance back at the institute, the reflex of someone who said things about his institution on the pavement and then thought about the pavement. He looked back at her. “Best in the field, though. If you could get him.” He said “good luck with that” without meanness, the way people said it about things they had decided were outside the range of what was worth attempting, and then he pulled his coat closed against the March air and turned toward the corner and crossed the street without looking back.

She watched him go.

The cold was sharper on the pavement than it had been inside the café, the particular edge of early March in a city that had not yet committed to its season. She stood with the photograph in her hand and the institute behind her on the other side of the street. The building held its concrete-and-glass stillness, the modest signage over the entrance, the same unhurried foot traffic moving through the glass door as if no question had just been asked about it. The cold reached through her coat where her wrist was exposed, the specific sensation of standing still on a city pavement when everyone else was in motion. She was holding a photograph of a man who had spent twelve years studying the exact thing she had been trying to name, and a man who took lunch alone had just told her with complete confidence that it would not matter.

The photograph had been in her possession for two weeks. It had crossed an ocean in her interior pocket. She had spent enough time with it to know the face, the angle of attention in the photograph, the way he was looking at something outside the frame, not performing anything for the camera because he had not known the camera was there. Now she had a name she already had, from a colleague who had given it freely and then said he won’t talk to you with the confidence of a man who had watched twelve years of people try.

She put the photograph back in her pocket and went inside.

The café had filled for the lunch hour, the tables around her occupied by people having Thursday conversations about Thursday concerns. She waited for her corner table to be cleared and ordered a third coffee she did not need and set her laptop on the table. The institute’s entrance was still across the street. The glass face of the building reflected the pale March sky, the reflection running in a diagonal across the window frame, and she could see the top of the front steps and no one on them.

She had his address from a 2071 conference program, where he had been listed as a presenter: d.cross followed by the institute’s domain. She did not know if he still used a conference-program address four years later. She could use the institutional communications contact, which would produce a reply written by someone whose job was to write replies that said nothing, which would cost her time she did not have and tell her nothing she did not already know. She did not want to arrive at his office door or find him in a corridor, because those approaches required her to be a face before she was a question, and faces that arrived at institutes with bad timing became records. She opened her mail client and composed a new message.

What she had was an email address from a public document and a story that was officially about funding transparency and actually about something she could not yet fully name.

She typed his address in the to field. She typed in the subject line: Research Funding Transparency: Request for Comment.

She looked at this for a moment. The subject line was accurate. It was also the right subject line if someone else read it before he did. She had been writing for these two audiences simultaneously for three weeks.

In the body she introduced herself. Contributing journalist, the publication, eleven years covering corporate AI ethics and research governance. She described the inquiry: funding transparency in European AI research institutions, the structural relationship between private sector investment and research directions. She said she was hoping to speak with senior researchers in the field about how these structures operated in practice. She attached the secondary press credential, the PDF with her photograph on it, the publication name, the description of her as a contributing journalist, all of it accurate.

She did not mention Veridian Systems. She did not mention the 43-page document or the ERWI framework or the embedded asset monitoring across jurisdictions seventeen through forty-one. She did not mention Marta or Reid or Hofstadter. She did not ask what he knew about the funding behind the institution where he had spent twelve years, or what he thought about the company she had traced through eight layers to a parent entity incorporated in 1887, or why his face had been tucked between pages thirty-one and thirty-two of an internal operational document that had arrived at her apartment via courier with no return address.

She read the email once for what it said and then again for what someone who had not written it would understand it to be.

The second read gave her a journalist and a funding inquiry and a real press credential and a request to speak with an expert. Nothing else. Nothing about nothing else.

She looked at the institute across the street. The entrance was still. Two people came out together and turned in opposite directions, one pulling out a phone before she reached the bottom step. A cyclist passed on the street between. The building held its glass-and-concrete stillness, the modest signage over the entrance, the particular quality of an institution that had decided it did not need to announce itself.

Before she sent the email she was invisible to him, a journalist in a café across the street, a name in no inbox he would have occasion to check. After she sent it she was findable. She had been watching and not contacting for two weeks. She was about to contact.

Both things could be true: the email could reach him and it could also reach someone who needed to know she had sent it. That was the reading she had been making of everything in this story since the padded envelope arrived on her kitchen counter. She had come to Geneva anyway.

She moved the cursor to Send.

She held it there long enough to read the subject line one final time.

Then she pressed it.

The confirmation appeared in the lower corner of the screen and faded. The sent-mail folder updated. Outside, through the window, the institute’s entrance was unchanged, the same pale stone steps, the same modest glass door, the same sky above it in its particular shade of late-afternoon March grey. The coffee at her elbow had gone cold while she was writing. She picked it up and held it with both hands and did not drink it.

Somewhere in that building, or in the server that received mail addressed to that domain, her name had just become a fact.
