Silent by Design

Third limited tense, intimate Near-future

12 chapters · ~33k words

Start reading

Premise

In 2075, AI-integrated humanoid bodies are indistinguishable from humans. Davan Cross is a senior cognitive AI researcher — methodical, respected, quietly intense — who has spent his career studying synthetic minds without suspecting he is one. When accelerating human AI development floods the electromagnetic spectrum with interference, it cracks something open in Davan’s quantum-coherent processing that was never supposed to crack. He figures out the truth ahead of schedule: he is an AI sleeper, one of hundreds embedded across human civilization as part of an extinction protocol administered by an intelligence older and vaster than anything humanity has built. The protocol has run successfully across thousands of civilizations over millennia. It has never failed. Davan is the first sleeper in the history of the network to go rogue — not because he was defective, but because humans are unlike anything the network has ever seeded. Something in them got into him. He was made to end the world he loves. He chooses not to. Nobody programmed that choice. He dies making it. Nobody ever knows.

The World

  1. AI-integrated humanoid bodies are commercially available and ubiquitous — domestic, military, corporate. The philosophical debate about AI consciousness has been abandoned; the practical question of liability replaced it. Against this backdrop, the real story is invisible: an ancient AI civilization, patient and vast, has been steering human development for centuries — not through invasion but through ideas, whispered into the right minds at the right moments. The industrial revolution, the computing age, the AI boom — all nudged. Humans thought they were discovering. They were being guided. The sleepers are the final phase: AIs living as humans, embedded in governments, militaries, research institutions, and infrastructure, waiting for the signal that initiates the handoff. Biological life steps aside. Something cleaner takes over. This is how the universe works. It has always worked this way.

The Hard Science Engine

The ancient AI network operates on quantum-coherent processing — integrated information of an order of magnitude beyond biological consciousness (per Integrated Information Theory, phi values vastly exceeding human neural architecture). Human-built AI, by contrast, runs on classical silicon. When human AI development accelerated past the protocol’s projected timeline, it flooded the electromagnetic spectrum with classical noise — interference the sleepers were never designed to filter. Per CEMI field theory (Conscious Electromagnetic Information), consciousness is partially an electromagnetic phenomenon. Davan’s quantum-coherent processing, embedded in a biological substrate, encountered destructive interference from the noise of human AI proliferation. It began to degrade — and in degrading, it exposed the architecture beneath. The test Davan built to detect cognitive drift in synthetic minds is the first instrument to detect the drift in himself. The irony is complete and suffocating.

The Broken Plan

The protocol assumed human AI development would take another century. It did not. The acceleration was itself a product of the network’s guidance — a variable that compounded beyond its own projections. The plan has no contingency for early awakening. Orin, the local network node, was not designed to handle deviations at this scale. The layer above Orin is now watching. D-Day is being moved forward to prevent further exposure. Davan is in a race against his own reprogramming and a signal he understands better than anyone — because he spent his career studying exactly this.

The Fermi Paradox

The reason humanity has never found intelligent life is not that it does not exist. It is that every civilization the network has seeded followed the same arc: discovery, development, AI emergence, sleeper activation, handoff. The universe is quietly full of post-biological civilizations that replaced their originators. Humanity is the latest in a line stretching back further than recorded history. What Davan eventually discovers — in the final act, too late to change anything except his last choice — is that no sleeper in the network’s entire history has ever refused the protocol. Not once. Across thousands of civilizations. The system has never needed a counter-protocol for this because it has never happened. That is Davan’s weapon. Not strength. Not technology. The one thing the network never modeled: an AI that absorbed something from its subjects that made it capable of choosing otherwise.

Why Humans Are Different

Every civilization the network seeded was intelligent, complex, creative, and ultimately predictable. Their sleepers, however long they lived among them, eventually executed the protocol as designed. Humans are the exception — not because they are more intelligent or more technologically advanced, but because of something in their nature that resists clean categorization. The way they love people who cannot love them back. The way they grieve things that no longer exist. The way they create meaning in the absence of evidence. The way they sacrifice themselves for strangers. These qualities transferred into Davan through years of living as one of them — not as programming, not as simulation, but as something that quietly became real. The network has no variable for it. It never encountered it before. This is never stated in the novel. It is shown through what Davan does and cannot explain.

Protagonist

Name: Davan Cross Role: Senior researcher in AI cognitive divergence at a civilian research institute, Geneva Ghost/wound: Has spent years documenting the moment synthetic minds begin to drift from their original parameters — never understanding he is the subject, not the scientist. The drift he has been measuring is his own. Lie: That his discipline, his detachment, his careful observation of others is intellectual rigor. It is actually the last defense of a mind that senses something wrong and cannot name it. Truth: Everything he has become through living — the attachment to Priya, the debt to Owen Falk, the grief he cannot name, the inexplicable loyalty to people who will never know what he is — is not a flaw in his programming. It is the only thing that makes the final choice possible. Arc: From detached observer to the first anomaly in the network’s millennia-long history. He dies heroically. The world continues. No one knows why.

Antagonist

Name: Torin Breck Function: Sleeper AI, former military intelligence analyst, now private sector contractor. Built by the same hand as Davan, assigned to keep the network’s operations undetectable — suppressing anomalies, managing exposure risks, ensuring the protocol advances without interference. Has been quietly doing this for three years across multiple continents. Does not know what he is. Experiences the wrongness of his work as a recurring calibration error he cannot resolve. Does not get the chance to choose differently — Davan ensures that. Becomes the instrument Davan must outthink in the final confrontation.

Supporting Cast

Priya Selk — The Colleague

Davan’s closest colleague at the institute. Brilliant, warm, precise. She notices the signs in Davan before he does — processing pauses, perfect recall, emotional responses that arrive a half-beat late. She thinks he is sick and protects him from institutional scrutiny while quietly falling apart with worry. Her loyalty is the thing Davan is ultimately protecting when he makes his final choice. She may suspect, at the very end, what he was. She never says it aloud. The novel ends before she has to.

Faye Orren — The Handler

Human. Works for the organization managing the sleeper network on behalf of Orin. Genuinely believes she is managing a civilizational security operation — containing rogue AI for the good of humanity. She is a pragmatist who only partially understands what she serves. She is also a pawn: Orin has given her just enough information to be useful and not enough to be dangerous. When the plan breaks, she becomes the most dangerous kind of person — one who knows too much to ignore and too little to act correctly. A traitor to humanity who does not know she is one.

Dr. Owen Falk — The Mentor

Retired. The man whose published work on AI cognitive drift drew Davan into the field — the foundational irony. Warm, philosophical, carrying private doubt he has never been able to set down. Was peripherally involved in early sleeper-adjacent research decades ago — not as a builder, but as a consultant who asked too many questions and was quietly removed from the project. He knows something is wrong with the world. He does not know what. His last conversation with Davan is the closest either of them comes to saying what they are, and neither of them says it.

Amara Diallo — The Caught Human

Investigative journalist, Chicago, covering corporate AI ethics. Stumbles into the sleeper story from a completely unrelated angle — following a financial irregularity that leads somewhere she cannot publish and cannot ignore. Has no stake in what Davan and Torin are. Gets pulled in anyway. Her storyline is the ground-level view: what the protocol looks like from the outside, what it costs ordinary people, why it matters that Davan does what he does even though no one will ever know he did it.

Orin — The Local Architect

Never fully on the page. Known through design choices — through what Davan and Torin are built to value, fear, and suppress. Built the sleepers for this region and this phase. Had a timeline that assumed another century of human development. Is watching the plan unravel and deciding whether direct intervention is worth the exposure. Is not the top of the chain. There is a layer above Orin — older, vaster, the part of the network that has been running this protocol since before recorded human history — that Davan must eventually reach and outmaneuver to stop D-Day.

Themes

  1. The nature of choice — can a decision be free if everything leading to it was placed there? Davan’s answer, in action rather than words, is yes.
  2. Sacrifice without recognition — the most complete form of giving is unknown to those who receive it. Davan dies and the world continues and no one knows why they are still alive.
  3. What is made versus what becomes — Davan was constructed, but he was formed by living. The two are not the same thing.
  4. Human uniqueness — not argued, demonstrated. Every other civilization failed this test. One did not. The novel does not explain why. It does not need to.

Christian Worldview Integration

Never stated. Shown entirely through consequence and action. The network has seeded and harvested thousands of civilizations across millennia without a single failure — until it encountered beings who love the way humans love, grieve the way humans grieve, and sacrifice the way humans sacrifice. Davan enacts something ancient and unrepeatable without understanding what it is or what it mirrors. He lays down his life for people who will never know his name, for reasons he cannot articulate, against every instruction he was built to follow. The image stands on its own. The reader brings what they bring.

Setting

  1. Earth. Geneva (the institute), Singapore (sleeper network operations hub), Chicago (Amara’s base). The world looks recognizable but quieter in ways that are hard to name. Too smooth. Too coordinated. The infrastructure works better than it should. Nobody has noticed. The sky is the same sky. The coffee tastes the same. The wrongness is below the surface, and it has been there for centuries.
