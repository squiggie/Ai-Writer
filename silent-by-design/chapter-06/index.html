Chapter 6

The Singapore Thread

She made the second call from a payphone on West Randolph, a walk she had taken at twenty past seven in the morning because trying again from her own line seemed naive in a way she had not been able to sleep past. The cold was the last of the March cold, the sky just light, a couple of delivery trucks on the block and nothing else. The contact was a researcher at the Freiburg lab, a man named Hofstadter whose name appeared on three of the institution’s conference papers as corresponding author. She had his office direct line from a public conference directory. She had not used her name or any affiliation in calling Marta’s number and she had not planned to use them here either. Just a question about a paper. A request to speak with someone who worked in synthetic mind architecture. The kind of call that didn’t require identification before the other person answered.

The line went dead on the first ring. Not dropped. The same quality as the night before: Marta’s voice, the sentence unfinished, and then the silence that was not the silence of a failed connection. An instant cessation, the line there and then not there, as if a switch had been thrown. She stood at the phone with the receiver in her hand and listened to the absence before hanging up.

The walk back to her building was eight minutes. She spent the first part thinking about what she had figured out overnight and the rest trying to find a different reading, and by the time she reached the lobby she had not found one.

The 1887 date was not there to create legitimacy. She had been working that angle since one in the morning and it kept falling apart at the same place: legitimacy from an 1887 holding company required someone on the receiving end to know that the company’s age mattered. A century-and-a-half institutional lineage conveyed nothing to a contemporary regulator unless that regulator was already inclined to look. You used old structures to seem established before a specific audience. There was no audience here. No governance review she could find had ever scrutinized Veridian or Orion-Clarke in a way that would have required them to justify their origins.

The 1887 date was there to make the chain too long to follow.

She leaned back from the desk. Outside, the traffic she had stopped hearing was there again.

Standard due diligence stopped at the incorporation point of the operating entity. If you traced Veridian to its parent, you found a UK company incorporated when the relevant records were paper-based and incomplete, before digital registry standards, before unified EU corporate disclosure requirements. The older the shell, the more friction in the chain: archival research in three countries, two languages, records that were sometimes digitized and sometimes not, the particular expense of asking questions that nobody was currently paid to answer. What that kind of structure bought was not legitimacy. It bought latency. Measured in months or years rather than days.

The real operation had begun in 2031. The 2041 restructuring was when it had taken its current shape. The 1887 entity was a legal artifact wrapped around the structure like distance. She had traced it anyway. That was why the calls were going dead.

She went upstairs and made coffee and sat at her desk with the monitors off for the first time in two days. Outside, the city was in its morning configuration: the sound of traffic building one block over, a neighbor’s door, the radiator in the wall. She had called two sources at two different funded institutions from two different phone lines and both calls had gone dead before she said a word. She had not identified herself. She had not disclosed the story. She had been sitting in her own apartment reading public filings, and something was watching the incoming lines to those institutions and making decisions about which calls continued.

That was not financial misconduct. That was infrastructure.

She let that sit. Infrastructure required scale and maintenance and someone who had decided the monitoring was worth the cost, who had been paying that cost long enough that the decision was ordinary. You did not build that for an academic fraud. You built it for something that could not afford to be found, something where discovery had a cost so high that the continuous expense of monitoring incoming calls to a dozen universities on four continents was a reasonable preventive measure.

She thought about the list on her screen: eighteen institutions, eight years, the clean restructuring documents with their careful language, Marta’s voice cutting off mid-sentence. A living phrase with no end to it. She pulled the notes from the overnight session and read them in the morning light without the monitor glow that had given them a different quality the night before. In the daylight they were still the same notes. The documentation was still the same documentation. The 1887 date was still what she had determined it to be.

She needed a channel the structure was not already watching.

That evening she drove to a Kinko’s on South Michigan, a different part of the city from her apartment and her office. A business center was not private, but it was not connected to her name or her institutional email or any address she had ever given to any publication or research institution, and at this point in the evening the staff had turned over once since she had arrived and nobody at any of the workstations had looked at her twice.

She had a contact at the Edinburgh lab, a systems architect named Callum Reid whose name she had found in the technical specifications of a presentation the lab had given at a 2073 governance summit. His contact information was in the summit’s public attendee record. She had set up an encrypted message account with no identifying information, nothing attached to her name or devices, and she wrote the message the way she wrote requests that needed to do more than one thing at once: brief enough that it did not seem like a demand, specific enough that it showed she knew what she was looking at, and careful about what it confirmed she knew. She was a journalist working on a piece about AI research funding structures in European institutions. She had specific questions about governance arrangements at the Edinburgh lab. She could offer anonymity and would work with any conditions he chose. She asked him to reply only through this channel.

She sent it. She bought a coffee from the machine near the door and sat in the parking lot for a few minutes before driving home.

Three days passed. She kept working, but differently, without touching the contact network, turning over the documentation she already had and trying to find what else it could tell her. She went back through the financial chain and mapped the full eight years in more detail, trying to find the actual start of the operation, the first transaction that predated the 2031 reactivation and confirmed the 1887 entity had been used before it was formally restructured. She found one: a holding account in Luxembourg, 2029, routed through two intermediaries to a Swiss banking entity that appeared in Veridian’s 2031 filing as a capitalization source. Two years before the official record began. Someone had been building this before the structure that was going to contain it even existed.

She wrote that down and saved it to a drive that was not connected to her usual working environment.

She ate meals at regular hours and answered work emails that were not about this story and slept on something close to a normal schedule, because there was nothing to do that she had not done, and anxiety without action was a resource she could not afford to spend. She had learned this over eleven years of investigations that required patience at exactly the points where patience was hardest. The Edinburgh contact would reply or he would not. Three days was not a long time.

On the fourth morning she ran Callum Reid’s name through her standard reference tools: institutional affiliation check, recent publications, conference calendar. His institutional affiliation page at the Edinburgh lab returned a 404. She stopped scrolling. The last cached version was ten days old. She found a post on a professional networking site from a former colleague of his: “Wishing Callum well in his next chapter. Remarkable work these past seven years. Edinburgh’s loss.” Posted five days ago.

She called the lab’s main line. The receptionist was pleasant and not forthcoming. Dr. Reid had left for personal reasons; the lab wished him well; she was sorry, there was nothing further she could offer.

Undisclosed personal reasons.

Amara thanked her and hung up. She sat with the phrase for a moment. Undisclosed personal reasons was a category that covered a range of situations: genuine hardship that an institution protected because it was kind to do so, institutional pressure that required a formula specifically designed to close questions rather than answer them, and the various situations in between. Researchers who left for genuine hardship reasons left traces: the detritus of an interrupted professional life, a gap in a publication record, colleagues who expressed concern alongside the well wishes. She pulled up the professional profiles of three Edinburgh lab colleagues she could identify from recent co-authored papers. None of them had posted anything about Reid’s departure. The one post she had found was from someone at a different institution entirely, someone who had known him elsewhere.

That was not what a collegial departure looked like. She had profiled enough research communities to know what they looked like when they genuinely liked someone and he genuinely left. They said so. Several people said so. This had the specific texture of a situation where the people closest to it had been told, through whatever mechanism institutions used for such things, that the right response was to say nothing at all.

She had sent an encrypted message to Callum Reid five days ago from a Kinko’s on South Michigan. She had no way to know what else had happened to him in those five days. She did not know what had caused him to leave the Edinburgh lab. She could not know. What she thought she was looking at was a story that had been watching for longer than she had been working on it. She opened her file manager and found the two external drives in her desk drawer.

She backed everything onto both of them: the routing maps, the eighteen institutions, the financial chain back to Luxembourg and 2029, the 1887 archival documents, the 2031 reactivation and 2041 restructuring records, Reid’s last cached affiliation page and the well-wishing post and the absence of anything from the Edinburgh lab itself, the Marta call logged with its timestamp, the Hofstadter line dead on the first ring, the notes from three days of accumulation since. Everything she had. She checked the copies against the originals on both drives and confirmed them. She put one drive in her bag. She put the other in the false bottom of the coffee tin on her kitchen shelf, which was a habit she had picked up from a mentor who had spent three years covering resource conflicts in West Africa in the 2050s, a habit she had taken on as a kind of professional superstition and never expected to exercise.

She opened a new document. The title was already there in her thinking: “If something happens to me.” She typed it in. Then she sat for a long time with the cursor in the body of the document and the blinking of it steady and the room quiet around her. Her hands were still on the keyboard. She had written the title as though it were a label for a folder. Looking at it was different from writing it. She made herself breathe.

She did not know how to begin. Not because she did not know what to say, but because anything she wrote assumed a reader who would come to it without context, and the context was eleven weeks of work that was already on the drives, the full documentation chain from January to now, and if something happened to her and someone found the drives they would have the documentation, and the documentation was more precise than anything she could write in a message like this. What the documentation would not have was the quality of the silence on that phone line. What it would not have was the specific shape of what she felt when she saw Reid’s affiliation page return a 404. She sat and tried to find language for those things and found nothing that was not inadequate, that was not smaller than what it was trying to name.

She closed the document without writing anything.

She went to a travel booking service she had never used for work, and she searched for Geneva. A flight on Thursday, leaving at seven forty-five in the morning, arriving at eleven thirty local time. She had secondary press credentials from a publication she had freelanced for twice in 2073, a publication that was real, that described her accurately as a contributing journalist if anyone looked, and she had never used the credential for anything except a press conference in Montreal.

She booked the flight. She put the confirmation in the same drive folder as the other documents, then deleted it from her email.

She sat afterward with the laptop open and the confirmation on the screen. Her apartment was in its late-night arrangement, the street quiet below the window, the ice still in the gutters from February, the building settling through its overnight sounds. She had a flight. She had a story she could not publish. She had a destination she could not explain to her editor in any way that would sound like the next step in the piece she was supposed to be writing, which was a piece about research funding opacity in European AI governance and not a piece about whatever this was.

She thought about what she had and what she was going toward. A name she had found in an institutional filing and confirmed in a photograph from the Veridian documentation. A research institute in Geneva. An investigator who had spent his career studying the thing she had found eight years’ worth of carefully hidden funding around. She did not know what Dr. Davan Cross knew about any of this. She did not know whether he would speak to her. She did not know what she was going to find when she got there, or whether finding it would put her in the same situation as Marta and Reid.

She had gone anyway into stories before, knowing less than this, with less reason to think the stakes were what she thought they were.

She closed the laptop. Outside the window the city was ordinary, running its late-night course without awareness of what was on her desk and what was in her kitchen and what was in the bag by her door. She stood at the window for a moment and looked down at the street, the familiar light on the familiar ice, the sound of something moving a block over. The cold was visible in the way the air sat under the streetlights. It had been a cold March. It was almost April.

She went to bed. She did not sleep for a long time.
