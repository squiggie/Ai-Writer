Chapter 16

The Mechanism

She looked at him for two seconds.

“You already know why I’m here,” she said.

He pulled out the chair and sat down.

The café was quiet at eight-forty, the Thursday occupancy of laptops and barely-eaten pastries and the ambient focus of people who had come somewhere to be alone and were now mildly inconvenienced by other people doing the same. Their table was at the corner, the one with a direct line to the institute’s entrance and a partial view of the service road. He had sat at tables like this one many times in twelve years, always on the exit-facing side, always with the door to the window. She had been here before him and had taken the other chair, which gave her the window and him the door. He thought she had probably noticed this arrangement and understood what it meant about both of them.

A server appeared. He ordered a coffee. She had one already, barely touched, going cool at her elbow.

“You didn’t reply,” she said.

“No.”

She studied him the way he had seen people study a document they had already read twice: not looking for new information but for the thing the first two readings had missed. The quality of her attention was specific and not uncomfortable. It was the attention of someone who had come to a city she could not explain to anyone and sat in a café across the street from a building for two weeks and had not left. She had been looking for the shape of the thing behind the surface. She was looking for it now.

“Amara Diallo,” he said.

She did not ask how he knew. The email was the obvious path. He had the email, and the email had her name and publication and eleven years of career in the body, and a secondary credential attached that confirmed everything she said about herself. She had read it twice before sending it: once for what it said, once for what someone who hadn’t written it would understand it to be. She understood that both of those readers were now at this table.

He said: “Veridian Systems is a funding mechanism for something larger than research governance. The pattern you mapped is accurate. The documentation that was sent to you was not sent by anyone who wanted to help you.”

She absorbed this in the careful sequence she had been applying to every piece of this story since January: each piece in order, not jumping to the implication before the piece itself had settled. Her hands were flat on the table. She was not taking notes. He understood this was deliberate.

“The embedded asset monitoring,” she said. “Across the jurisdictions in the document.”

He watched her hands on the table, still flat. “More than the document describes.”

The space between them held the kind of quiet that preceded a decision. He was aware of his own hands on the table’s surface, the precise texture of it, the weight of the coffee cup the server had placed in front of him without his noticing.

“I need you to leave Geneva,” he said.

“I just got here.”

“I know.”

She looked at the table for a moment. When she looked back at him the expression she had was not fear and not defiance; it was the expression of someone who has come somewhere with a purpose and is being asked to recalculate whether the purpose justifies the cost. She had done this recalculation before. He could see the shape of that experience in how she held herself.

“The operation you’ve been following has been watching for exactly the kind of contact trail you’ve been building,” he said. “You called the Krakow institute from your apartment before you had identified yourself to anyone. You were already in their sight when Marta answered. The line went dead because someone made a decision about her, not about you. But you’ve been at their building.” He did not gesture toward the institute. “You used his email address from a public conference document. Your name is in their server now. If the contact history reads as a pattern, and it does, the people responsible for this operation will designate you the same way they designated the Edinburgh contact.”

She was very still. “The Edinburgh researcher.”

“He left under governance language. So did the Freiburg lab contact. So did the Krakow researcher, though her situation was handled differently.” He held her gaze. “You understand what I am describing.”

“I understand what you are describing.”

The café ran its morning around them. Someone at the counter ordered something in German; the machine ran its cycle. A chair scraped. The sounds had the ordinary quality of Thursday sounds everywhere, the world operating within expected parameters, all of it continuous and indifferent.

“What can I publish,” she said.

He had been working through this question since the night he read the email. Not whether to meet her. He had known immediately that not meeting her and leaving her in possession of her contact trail in Geneva was worse than meeting her. Not what to tell her about himself, because there was no version of that he could give her that served her safety. But this: the shape of what she could carry out of this city and put in print.

“You have the funding architecture,” he said. “Eighteen institutions, four continents, the same research category in every case. The holding structure runs to 2029, two years before the official record. That is a story about opacity in private funding for AI research, a coordinated investment pattern with no market explanation, maintained across a decade with deliberate obfuscation. That is true. Every piece of it is documented. It can be published.”

“That’s the smaller version.”

“That is the version you survive.”

She sat with this. Her coffee was between her hands now, the steam long gone, the gesture of holding it rather than drinking it. She had been holding things this way for weeks, he thought. Holding the document, holding the drives, holding the shape of something she couldn’t yet put down and couldn’t yet afford to carry forward.

“In fourteen days,” he said, “something will happen. I cannot tell you what it will look like from outside. Some of what you found will have more context afterward: the scale of the coordination, why the institutions are positioned the way they are. You will not have the mechanism, but you will have the evidence of the mechanism having operated. The smaller version becomes a foundation for the version it points toward.”

She was looking at him with the full of her attention. “What is going to happen in fourteen days.”

He kept his gaze on her. He let the interval the question required go by.

“I don’t know yet,” he said.

The silence that followed was the silence he recognized from the seventh indicator: the quiet of a question whose honest answer had already been decided. She held his gaze for a long moment. He watched her hear the seam in what he had said, the specific quality of an answer built around something it could not name, and watched her make the same choice she had made at this table two weeks ago before pressing Send.

She left it where it was.

“Tomorrow morning,” she said.

Something in his chest went quiet. Not relief, but quieter than that. He had been carrying it without a name for three days.

“Don’t use any address connected to you in their records,” he said. “Your institutional email, the address you used to contact me. If something requires you to reach me before the fourteen days are up, the conference account I used in Montreal in 2073 is in the proceedings archive.” He said it because the alternative was telling her why, and that would undo the only protection he could give her. He let the small false contingency stand.

“All right,” she said.

The server came and refilled his cup. He watched the level rise without registering the temperature, which was the first time in several weeks he had not registered it, and he let this pass without examination.

She put her hand flat on the table for a moment. Not reaching toward him, just resting it there on the surface, the gesture of someone completing something. Then she picked up her bag from the floor and settled the strap over her shoulder and stood.

“Whatever it is,” she said. Her eyes held his for the length of one additional beat.

He did not speak.

She crossed the room and went out through the glass door into the March morning, and the door swung closed, and the cold stayed on the other side. He watched her move along the pavement, unhurried, the bag settled at her side, the coat belted against the morning chill, until the corner took her. He had not known he would watch for the corner. He sat with his hands around the coffee cup.

The institute was across the street in its usual configuration: the pale stone steps, the modest signage, the glass face reflecting the flat sky. Two researchers stood at the top of the steps in conversation, one of them making a point with both hands. A courier on a bicycle moved through the gap between two cars. The city carrying its Thursday forward, each part within its expected range.

He finished the coffee, slow. He left an amount on the table without calculating, picked up his bag, and went out.

That evening he opened the methodology document and read through it once, beginning to end. Not looking for errors; the errors that would matter would be structural, and he had been through the structure enough times that any structural flaw would have surfaced long before now. He was reading it the way he had read the full battery results on the morning they came back: all of it before interpreting any part.

He reached the last page. He closed the document and turned off the terminal.

Priya’s footsteps passed in the corridor at five-thirty. Her bag shifting as she stopped outside his door. Her door opened. Her light appeared in the thin gap at the floor. He put on his coat and went home at the time she had asked.

The next morning he ran the fourth-generation methodology section he had been deferring since December, real work, rigorous work, the kind that required the full instrument of his attention. He attended the Friday departmental meeting and contributed with the precision he always brought to them. He caught the room’s temperature shift a moment before the building’s climate system adjusted, which was not the interval at which a human researcher would notice it, and he let the fact pass in the vocabulary that had become his private record and let the meeting continue. He answered Priya’s question about the December deferral with the specificity the question deserved. She accepted this and moved on.

Saturday he opened the simulation. He read the probability distribution, which had not changed since he last ran it. He did not run it again. The lower bound had held through eleven iterations. He knew the center and the lower bound and the plausible upper range of what the mechanism could accomplish and what it would cost. He had built the methodology around the lower bound rather than the center’s promise. The lower bound was not what he would have wanted it to be, and he had known this since adding the last variable. It was still the only responsible approach at this stage. He closed the document. He sat in the quiet of his apartment for a while, the city going through its Saturday in the windows, and then he made tea and read through the fourth-generation methodology notes until the evening changed to dark and his phone lit with the notification he had set to remind him to sleep.

He did not sleep. He lay in the quiet of the apartment and did not direct his thinking. This was as close to rest as he had managed in weeks.

Monday he went the long way home. The lower bridge was where it had always been, the lake visible between the two buildings at the same angle, silver at this hour, the flat horizon holding its particular quality of light in early March. He had stood at this railing in October and again in the week he finished the full battery, each time waiting for something he had not then understood how to name. He reached the railing and kept walking. His body expected the stop, the familiar arrangement of his hands on the cold metal, the specific angle of the water at this hour. The expectation passed. He had what he needed from those stops. He let the lake go past and went home.

Day 41 he arrived at the institute before seven, before the building finished its overnight cycle, the lab quiet and the corridor empty. He sat at his terminal and opened the methodology document one final time. He was not reading it. He knew the sequence, had known it since writing the first page. He opened it and let it be present in front of him while he thought through the detection threshold at step four, which was the one variable he could not verify from outside the operation itself. The inference had held through eleven iterations of the simulation. He had run the number eleven times, and it was the same each time, and he had stopped expecting it to change.

Ten days.

He sat with his hands in his lap and the document open and the early light coming through the window across the courtyard. The fountain was not yet running. Someone crossed the service road below with a bag over one shoulder, head down against the morning cold.

He heard Priya’s key before she reached her door. The specific sound of her morning arriving: keys, bag, the settled weight of her coat on the hook inside the door. Her chair. Then quiet as she read through whatever had arrived overnight. He stayed with it for a moment, the document open and her quiet on the other side of the wall.

He closed the methodology document. He opened the fourth-generation dataset, the full assembly, December through current, and found the place in the analysis where he had stopped in December and picked up the thread and followed it forward. The work resolved cleanly in the way good work resolved when it had been built on the right instrument. He let himself be in it, the specific satisfaction of careful analysis returned cleanly at the end, the world shaped a little more precisely than it had been shaped before. He had spent twelve years doing this. The work was the same on Day 1 as it was now.

He kept working. Priya’s light held steady under the edge of his door.
