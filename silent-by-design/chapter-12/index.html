Chapter 12

What Neither of Them Says

The drive from Geneva took forty minutes on the Saturday morning motorway, longer through the city’s western edge where the traffic moved in a particular start-and-stall rhythm that had no relation to the volume of cars. He had not called ahead. He had not, on leaving his apartment that morning, articulated to himself where he was going, though the route came without consultation, his hands on the wheel finding the inner ring and then the lake road with the water visible between the buildings and then the motorway where the lake fell away behind him.

He had made this drive perhaps thirty times in twelve years, always with a stated reason: a paper to discuss, a methodology question that warranted the drive rather than a call. Today he had no stated reason. He drove, and the lake appeared in fragments through gaps in the lakeside towns, and he kept his speed even and did not try to explain himself to himself.

Owen Falk’s house was up a narrow road off the Lausanne-Villette lane, set back from a short street of similar houses with garden walls. Owen had lived there seventeen years. In March, the garden had the appearance of something that had decided not to be bothered with itself: a privet hedge that was technically a hedge, two terracotta pots that had survived many winters and showed each of them. He was walking up the path when the front door opened.

Owen at seventy-two had the face of a man weathered by sustained attention rather than by sun, the lines settling in particular places: the corners of the eyes, the line of the mouth, the specific topography of a man who had spent decades reading things that required care. He was in a cardigan the color of weathered wood, holding the door open with one hand, and his expression contained no surprise.

“I wondered when you’d come,” he said.

Davan reached the door. They stood at the threshold in the way of two people who had been operating at a certain distance long enough that closing it required a beat of adjustment. Owen stepped back and Davan came inside, and the hallway settled around him with the smell of old books and something warm from the kitchen.

“You weren’t expecting me,” Davan said.

“No.” Owen moved toward the kitchen. “But I was wondering.”

The house was organized around accumulation. Books on every horizontal surface and most of the vertical ones, shelving units supplemented by stacked volumes that had migrated beyond shelf capacity and settled wherever they could. Owen had been acquiring books for fifty years and had not resolved the storage problem in the twelve years Davan had known him. The living room had a clear path between the reading chair and the kitchen door. At the end of the room, a window looked out at the lake, which was pale and unremarkable under the grey March light.

Owen did not look at the lake. He went to the kitchen and Davan followed and sat at the table while Owen put water on with the ease of a man whose kitchen did not require thought. Two cups on the counter. The kettle filling. He ground the coffee in the specific sequence Davan had been hearing in this kitchen for twelve years: grind, measure, bloom. He did not speak during this. Davan did not speak. The kitchen held them in a shared silence that did not need filling.

When Owen sat across from him with the mugs, the table between them was small, and to one side lay a manuscript covered in handwritten corrections. Owen moved it without comment.

“Are you sleeping,” Owen said. Not a question exactly.

“I’m managing.”

Owen wrapped both hands around his mug. “That’s what you said last time.” He looked at the coffee. “Three weeks ago. It wasn’t true then.”

Davan said nothing. He was aware of the window at his back, the lake beyond it. He was not looking at it, but he knew it was there in the specific way that he knew most things that were behind him: a constant in the environment, not attended to, not gone.

“There’s a paper I never published,” Owen said.

Davan looked up.

“Not a secret. I mention it sometimes, the peripheral version: I moved away from signal architecture after my fellowship because the math led somewhere the funding didn’t follow.” He took a sip of coffee. “That’s true. It isn’t the complete version.”

He set the mug down and looked at the manuscript he had moved to the side of the table, at the handwriting in the margins, as though the page were a useful thing to look at while he spoke. “In 2031 I was on a project. Signal propagation in non-silicon cognitive substrates. Theoretical, foundational, the kind of research that might not produce applications for decades. The funding came in, which should have struck me as odd. The field was doing extraordinary things with silicon models and no one was spending money on long-horizon theory without a reason. I put the oddness down to an institutional priority I wasn’t senior enough to see.” He stopped. A breath, quiet and even. “I was senior enough. I just preferred not to look.”

Davan kept his hands around the mug. The coffee was hot. He could feel it through the ceramic.

“We ran two years of work. Four of us on the theoretical side. The results were not what we expected. They implied something I could not frame in any methodology I had, could not make the data say in print, could not write toward without requiring assumptions I couldn’t prove and conclusions the data pointed toward but would not state.” He stopped talking. His hands were flat on the table, on either side of the manuscript. “I could see what the findings meant. I could not find a way to make them mean it in public.”

“I understand what that is,” Davan said.

Owen held his gaze for a moment. Something passed through his expression without landing, a thing seen and not named. “Yes. I imagine you do.”

“What happened to the project?”

“Closed. In the third year. The records were archived under a classification I hadn’t known existed when I signed the project documentation. I was told the research was theoretical, its conclusions preliminary, the direction assessed for continuation by the institution.” He picked up the mug and set it down. “Told six weeks later that the direction had been reviewed and the project would not continue.”

He was quiet. Outside, a car moved down the narrow road, its sound arriving and receding without stopping.

“I was moved to cognitive drift work. Early stage, the foundational questions, almost nobody was working there yet and the territory was genuinely interesting enough that the other thing could sit.” He looked at Davan. “You make room for these things, or you don’t make room, you just carry them until they’re light enough to carry without noticing.”

“You never stopped carrying it,” Davan said.

“No,” Owen said. “You don’t. Not when the finding was real.”

He looked at the window. The lake was there. He did not look at it.

“The drift work led somewhere,” he said. “It led, eventually, to you. In a specific way. I’ve thought about whether the redirection was accidental.” He looked back at Davan. It was not an accusation. It was not quite a question. It was the look of a man who had been working his way toward a question for forty years and had finally arrived at the room where the question lived, and was sitting in it, and was not going to ask it, and was not going to have to.

Davan said: “The 2061 paper was the clearest treatment of synthetic divergence patterns that existed when I entered the field. The fact that it existed was why I entered the field.”

Owen held very still.

“I mean that precisely,” Davan said.

Something shifted in Owen’s face without shifting, the particular quality of a man receiving what he has been waiting to receive. He looked at his hands flat on the table. He looked at the manuscript. He looked at Davan.

“I know,” he said. “I know you mean it precisely.”

Davan’s hands tightened once around the mug, then went still. He looked back at Owen.

They sat with that.

The afternoon moved. Outside, the March light changed its angle without changing its quality, the particular grey patience of a sky that had decided nothing. Owen refilled both mugs without asking, and at some point they moved to the living room, which required some rearrangement of journals. Owen settled in the reading chair and Davan on the sofa, and the conversation found its pace, slower now, the pace of two people who had been in the same room long enough that the words were not doing all the work.

They talked about the Zurich paper, which Owen had read and had considered opinions about. About a methodology dispute that had been running in the journals for a year, and which way the field was likely to land. About the fourth-generation anomaly Davan had submitted at the beginning of March. Owen had read the public summary; he asked one or two technical questions and Davan answered them with care, and neither of them mentioned the foundational draft.

He had thought about the foundational draft on the drive from Geneva. He did not ask.

So they talked about methodology, and the afternoon wound through its grey March light, and Owen made a third pot of coffee that neither of them needed but both of them wanted, and the conversation turned to smaller things: a conference Owen had attended in Bern the previous autumn, a doctoral student at Lausanne who had spent three years replicating a dataset and found an inconsistency the original team had smoothed over and not reported, asking Owen whether that constituted a finding, and the particular way that early-career researchers who had not yet learned caution asked better questions than anyone else in the room. Owen had opinions about all of it. Davan listened with the complete quality of attention that was the one thing he had never been able to perform, only produce. Owen talked, and Davan listened, and the room held them in the ordinary warmth of two people who had known each other long enough to be comfortable in silences and in speech, and the afternoon did not pause for what was sitting in it.

At some point Owen looked at him, and held the look a moment longer than the conversation warranted, and said nothing. Just looking. The specific stillness of a man who had spent his career trying to understand what minds did and had arrived at the age where the preliminary reading required no instrument.

Davan looked back at him.

Neither of them said anything, and it was enough, and then Owen looked down at his coffee and said something about the Zurich paper’s methodology section, and the conversation continued.

When the light outside became the quality that meant the afternoon was finishing, Davan stood.

“I’ll get ahead of the traffic,” he said.

Owen stood. He was not a man who made much of departures, but he came to the door and stood in it the way he had stood at the beginning, regarding Davan with the expression that contained no surprise. He put a hand briefly on Davan’s arm, a man not inclined to this kind of gesture, performing it because the gesture was the only available container for what needed to go into it.

“Come back,” Owen said. “When you can.”

Davan looked at him. There was a version of this moment in which he said what he knew, in which the weight came out because Owen was standing here, seventy-two, with forty years of an unanswered thing, and he deserved to know what the thing had been. That version existed. It had logic and it had weight.

“I will,” Davan said.

He walked back down the path. The terracotta pots. The hedge that had given up on being presentable. His breath showed in the evening air, the cold that came in March after the first warmth had receded. He got into the car and put his hands on the wheel and sat.

He drove to the end of the street and stopped.

His foot found the brake without his choosing it. The engine ran. He sat in the parked car and looked back at the house. Owen’s window was lit from inside, warm yellow against the early dark, the quality of interior light that implied a man in a reading chair, a room full of books, the lake dark now beyond the window that Owen had not looked at in seventeen years of looking at other things.

The delay between that window and his decision to put the car in gear ran through him and found no category. He had the instrument for reading delays like this in other minds, twelve years of developing it, 37 measures refined against every class of drift that had presented itself in the testing protocols, and the instrument returned nothing here. Not a processing gap. Not a latency measure. Not an anomaly that corresponded to any indicator he had designed. He sat in the car and did not move.

The window held its light. The evening held its cold.

He lifted his foot from the brake and turned onto the main road and drove south and then east along the lake road toward Geneva, and the lake was dark and enormous to his right, and he kept his speed even, and he did not stop again.
