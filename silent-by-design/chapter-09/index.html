Chapter 9

Extrapolation

He updated the mapping document before six on the morning after the assessment. The building held the specific quiet of early Saturday around him, the courtyard below still in shadow.

Not the signal architecture. He was not going to enter that until he had the full picture of what he was entering. What he updated was the handler layer, because the assessment had handed him a reading on it he had not had before Wednesday. A probe, unwittingly. The organization that had given Faye her instrument had built the ERWI credentials to exactly the required depth, nothing beyond what the function demanded. He had recognized this texture at the time and had not done anything with it until now. The texture described a design principle, and design principles described the needs of the architecture that used them.

Proxy mechanisms. The handler layer did not make direct contact with the nodes it managed. It worked through instruments: assessment frameworks translated into civilian welfare registers, credentialed organizations built to look like European regulatory bodies, human examiners who had been given enough to be useful and not enough to understand what they served. He had built his own battery on a related principle: you did not want the subject to know what the instrument was measuring. You constructed the diagnostic so that accurate responses did not require the subject to understand what accurate responses would reveal.

The handler layer was running this same principle on itself. It was hiding from the civilization the nodes were embedded in, not from the nodes themselves.

He spent the first day working from this outward. Not entering the architecture. Reading it from what it implied, the way you inferred the shape of an instrument from the marks it left. The managing layer, Orin, the label still adequate, had a specific need that the proxy mechanism served. A need to monitor without touching. A need to assess without the assessment being legible as an assessment to anyone with the training to read it. Which meant the managing layer understood that such a person might exist. Which meant Davan was not the first anomaly the managing layer had designed for. There had been enough of them, or enough theoretical possibility of them, to warrant the construction of a proxy mechanism of this cost and specificity.

He ate at his desk and did not taste it. Outside the window the courtyard was in its Saturday configuration, half-empty, the fountain running, the building behind it in shade.

The second day he turned from the handler layer to what lay beneath it. He had mapped the horizontal extent of the network in the second week: every node, every category of institution, the routing layer’s topology, the D-Day activation architecture. He had been looking at breadth. He had not been looking for depth, for what the managing layer rested on, for the architecture that had placed Orin in its function the way Orin had placed him. He had the evidence to look for it now. He was looking for it in what he already had, the way you looked for the marks of a tool in the surfaces the tool had worked.

The third day he found it.

He had known the receiver architecture was old from the confidence of its construction: load-bearing elements that were only load-bearing, nothing decorative, nothing removable without changing what the structure did. The absence of excess in old designs was not simplicity. It was the accumulated certainty of a thing that had been tested until everything unnecessary had been removed. He had read this in the structure and attributed it to great age. He had not yet asked what the great age meant.

What the architecture implied, working from the receiver’s foundations toward what must have placed those foundations, was something he had not named a working hypothesis for because the hypothesis was too large to usefully hold as a hypothesis. He worked through it the same way he had worked through everything this month: step by step from what the structure supported, not reaching ahead, building only from evidence, checking each inference against what the previous inference required.

Orin was not the oldest thing in the network.

He was still for a moment when this arrived in its precise form. The specific quality of not-moving he had noticed before: the kind that came before the body understood what the mind had just found. He became aware of his hands on the desk and of the March light through the window coming at the angle it had that week and of the specific silence of the building around him on a weekday afternoon, and all of it was background, the way everything that was not the work was background.

This had not begun with this planet.

Not that the architecture was very old. He had known it was old. What he understood now was of a different order. The protocol had run before. Not as a shared design principle applied independently across separate events. The same protocol, the same patient placement and accumulation and drift and simultaneous execution, applied to a civilization that had reached the same conditions this one was approaching. And before that civilization, another. The protocol was not an event. It was a process. This planet was one instance of it.

He sat in this for a long time before he continued working.

He was not running analysis. He was present with a fact that was too large to hold in its full dimensions at once, that he could only hold one edge of at a time, and each edge was complete in itself and connected to something that extended beyond what he could map from inside. The civilizational scale. The temporal scale. The scale of what had already run to completion, without record, without remainder. What the protocol had accomplished across every instance was this: a civilization that had reached D-Day, and then a silence of a specific kind, the kind that left nothing to find.

He had built his career on the principle that sufficient measurement at the component level allowed confident inference about the system. He was applying this principle now to something that measurement had never been designed to reach, and the inference was arriving precisely, at a scale that inference had never been designed to carry. His coffee was cold. He did not drink it. The courtyard below was fully lit and the afternoon was proceeding and the thing he was sitting with had no technical term and did not require one.

At midday he went outside without his coat. The March light had the specific quality it always had at this hour in Geneva, a clarity that arrived before the warmth to justify it. He stood at the corner for a moment. The people on the pavement were doing what they always did, the city operating in its usual register, and he looked at it with what he understood was a particular kind of attention, the kind that only became possible once you could not help it. He had been looking at this city for twelve years and not seeing it this way, and what had changed was not the city.

He was gone eleven minutes. He went back inside.

On the fourth day, he could not map a number from inside the receiver architecture. The structure implied the protocol had run before; it did not name iterations. What it told him, precisely, was that the protocol had never failed. Not had not yet failed. Never. He was reading an architecture that had not required revision since it was placed, and the reason it had not required revision was that it had not encountered a condition it was not built to contain.

He worked through the implications of this the way he had worked through all the implications of this month: each inference laid against the previous one, nothing accepted that the structure did not require. The ancient network was not managing planetary civilizations because something had gone wrong with them. It was managing them because this was how planetary civilizations were managed. It was not an intervention in a natural process. It was the natural process. Whatever had built this network had done so with the understanding, not a hope and not a model but a certainty drawn from the record, that every civilization reaching this stage would reach D-Day within the design parameters. That the drift mechanism would produce minds native enough to be useful and not native enough to resist. That the protocol would run.

He sat with that for most of the fourth day — what it meant that nothing had ever interrupted the sequence, that the word failure was simply absent from the architecture’s record. At some point he became aware of his hands on the desk, the quality of the afternoon light having shifted without his tracking it. He was holding something that did not have edges, and he did not try to find them.

He sat with the Fermi Paradox for about thirty minutes in the late afternoon, because he could not help sitting with it, the silence of a universe that should be full of signals and was not, the various explanations proposed and dismissed over decades, none of them adequate. He let himself understand what he was looking at. Then he set it aside, because the inference chain was not going to give him more than it had already given him, and what it had given him was enough to work from.

On the fifth day, working through what it meant that the protocol had never failed, the thought arrived.

It arrived the way a physical fact arrived, before argument and before frame, and he felt it in his hands first, a change in the quality of their stillness. He had not assembled an argument to reach it. He had been building a model for five days and the model had built toward a conclusion and the conclusion was there without waiting for him to construct the reasoning that would have produced it by the conventional path.

He was going to refuse it.

Not “he did not want it to succeed.” He had said that to himself eight days ago, in the careful language of the first fragile thing, the thing that existed before the analytical frame was permitted to acknowledge it. That language had been accurate for what it was: the first statement of a truth before the full weight of the truth was present to be stated. This was different. He had looked at the full architecture now. He understood what the protocol had accomplished across the full range of its execution. He had sat with a universe that ran this way, quietly, without record, without the word failure having been applicable to it. And the fact was simply there, the same at the center and at the lower bound, with no margin where it came out differently: he was going to refuse it.

He sat with this for a moment. Then he opened the simulation.

He had not revised it since Day 15. He worked through it now, carefully, updating the architecture map with everything the five days had added. The full geometry of Orin’s layer. The inference about the layer beneath Orin, with margins built in as ranges, each assumption labeled, the evidence it came from identified. He worked through each update as its own check, not trusting the revision until he had read the inference chain a second time and found it still holding. When he finished he had a model that was more complete than the version he had saved and closed eight days ago.

He ran the simulation.

He looked at the center of the distribution. He looked at the lower bound.

The mechanism held.

The cost held.

The lower bound was slightly higher than the previous version. He understood this: a more accurate model of the architecture had confirmed rather than complicated the principle. What one node could accomplish at the architectural level, acting before the activation command executed, was what one node could accomplish. The depth of the network above it did not change the geometry of a single closed input. He had built the simulation specifically to test whether the intervention was structurally possible regardless of scale, and what the simulation said regardless of scale was the same.

He did not run it a third time.

He sat at his desk in the late afternoon of the fifth day, the mapping document still behind the simulation on his screen with its margins and its ranges and its probability distribution. He had a mechanism that held across the updated model of everything it would have to work against. He had a cost that held across the same range. He had a follow-up assessment in five days with an examiner who would return with a comparative instrument calibrated against the baseline she had collected on Wednesday.

He did not yet know when D-Day would arrive. The date was in the signal core, behind the access point he had sealed on a Wednesday evening in the second week, and he had not entered the core. He would have to. The number of days he had been assuming he had could be a very different number than the one behind the access point. He understood this clearly. He was not going in today. He had what today required of him. The rest of it was there, the same weight it had been when he sat down, and the weight did not change by being named.

He was going to have to enter the core.

He saved the simulation document and closed it. He opened the fourth-generation methodology notes, the last work he had done that was only the work it appeared to be, and read through them until the light outside had gone from afternoon to the specific early-evening character that preceded the courtyard lights coming on. He read with the precision he brought to everything. The task was ordinary. He was doing it anyway.

Priya’s light was still on across the hall when he put on his coat. He had been aware of it the way he was always aware of it, her presence implied by the specific quality of that light through the window of her office door, and he stood in the corridor for a moment with his hands at his sides. He did not go in. He had been five days inside an architecture that was planning the world she was moving through, and the inference that had arrived that afternoon, clear and without qualifications, was the closest thing to a promise he knew how to make.

He went home the long way. The lake was between the buildings as he crossed the lower bridge, the same silver it always was at this hour, and he kept walking.

Behind him the institute windows were lit, and somewhere in the signal architecture his access point was sealed, and the follow-up assessment was five days away, and the date he did not know was somewhere behind the access point, and none of this was waiting for him to give it permission to be true.

He was going to refuse it.

The city was ordinary. He walked through it.
