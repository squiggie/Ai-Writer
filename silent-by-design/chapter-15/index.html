Chapter 15

What Priya Knows

He came back from lunch to find his office door ajar.

He had closed it when he left. This was automatic: twelve years of leaving the same door closed against the corridor’s afternoon traffic, the sound of the building cycling through its midday patterns. He stood for a moment in the corridor and looked at the inch of space between the door and the frame. Then he went in.

Priya was sitting in the chair across from his desk. Not the chair at the bench along the wall, where she sometimes worked from his reference terminal, but the other chair, the one on the visitor’s side of the desk, the low one in the corner that he had largely stopped thinking of as a visitor’s chair because in twelve years no visitor had stayed in it longer than it took to establish that a standing conversation would have done. She had a sheaf of papers on her knees, face-down. She did not offer it when he came in. He hung his coat on the hook behind the door and came around the desk and sat.

She looked at him the way she looked at data she had been sitting with long enough to have stopped hoping it would resolve differently.

“How long have you known,” she said. Not a question.

He had been building a response since the corridor, the careful frame of a denial. Not the sleep question, which was its own established pattern, but this, whatever this was, which required a different shape, and he could not find it because he did not yet know what she was asking. He looked at the papers on her knees. Face-down. Deliberate.

“Tell me what you have,” he said.

She looked at him for another moment. Then she turned the papers over.

What she had was a timeline. Not a cognitive drift protocol. She did not have authorization for those instruments; he had thought about this often in the last five weeks as a boundary he could not afford her to cross. What she had was something he had not accounted for: twelve years of knowing him, applied with the systematic care of a researcher who had spent her career building reliable pictures from incomplete datasets. The timeline began six weeks ago. It ran to the current date. Each entry was a behavioral observation: a phrasing pattern, a response latency, a choice of stairs over the hallway, a moment of stillness where motion had been expected. The instrument she had built from these entries was not standardized. It did not need to be. She had calibrated it against twelve years of him.

She put it on his desk without handing it to him.

“I thought neurological first,” she said. “I’ve seen how the profile reads. Twice, in people I knew well. Different presentations, but the pattern underneath was the same. Functional, then compensating, then the compensation becomes visible to people who know what to look for.” She paused. Her hands were in her lap now. “You have been compensating for six weeks. Probably longer, but six weeks is what I can document.”

He looked at the timeline. He had spent twelve years building an instrument that described exactly what he was. Priya had spent six weeks building one that described how it had looked from across the corridor. He held the pages and did not put them down.

“That’s not all,” she said.

He waited.

“The ERWI assessment. The follow-up.” She said this the way she said things she had thought through carefully and was ready for the discomfort of saying. “They sent a data request through the institute’s administrative channel: behavioral performance metrics, output consistency, peer contact frequency over the review period. Standard welfare supplement. The kind of thing that gets routed to close colleagues.” A pause that was not hesitation. “I received the request.”

He understood before she said the next thing what the next thing was.

“I told them what your last three months of output looked like at baseline, before the six-week window. I told them you had been working through a difficult anomaly in the fourth-generation dataset, that the quality of the work had not changed, that your engagement with the research was consistent.” She met his gaze. “I told them you were fine.”

The ventilation ran through its afternoon cycle. Outside, somewhere in the corridor, two voices passed and continued without stopping.

“I’ve given them three supplemental responses,” she said. “They sent follow-up data requests after each assessment interview. I responded each time. I described a researcher managing a period of high-pressure work with no observable functional impact on output or professional interaction.” She held his gaze. “That’s what I gave them. It’s what they have on file.”

He kept his hands flat on the desk.

“Priya.”

“I know what I did.” Quiet. Not defensive. The specific calm of someone who had already had the argument with themselves three weeks ago. “I’m telling you because you need to know what the record looks like, and because I’m asking you to let me help you properly. Not through administrative channels. The right way: a clinical assessment, with someone who understands this category of presentation. There are resources through the institute’s supplementary health provision, outside the standard review process. People who handle exactly this.”

She meant every word of it. She believed every word of it. She had spent three weeks building this careful structure around him — the timeline, the supplemental responses, the plan she was offering — out of the same quality he had spent twelve years trying to name precisely: the warmth and the precision at the same frequency, one property not two.

He looked at the timeline on his desk. The entries were exact. The notation was spare. She had written it the way she wrote everything: nothing present that was not doing work.

He picked up the pages and looked at the first entry. Then he set them back down.

She was right about what she had seen. This was the fact that sat in his chest without a category to fit it into. She had looked across the corridor and seen him with the same precision he had spent twelve years applying to other minds, and she had turned that precision into action, had built something, had intervened, had stood between him and an institutional process she thought was putting him at risk. Three weeks of careful, methodical work, conducted in parallel with her own research, because she had not been willing to let the pattern go without doing something about it.

The frame she had put around it was wrong. It would need to stay wrong.

As long as she believed this was a medical problem, she was someone whose colleague was unwell. As long as she believed that, she was not someone who understood what she was moving through, and the monitoring protocol watched for the second kind of person, not the first. It watched for researchers approaching the territory the network suppressed. Tanaka had been approaching signal propagation in quantum-coherent substrates. Priya had spent three weeks in active contact with the assessment process around the most anomalous node in the network, managing the data flowing into that process, building a detailed behavioral record of what the anomaly looked like from inside the institution.

She had done this with complete care and no understanding of what the assessment process was connected to.

But the monitoring protocol did not require understanding. It required pattern.

He looked at the timeline on the desk between them. Then he said: “You need to stop.”

She did not look surprised. She had expected something like this; the surprise would have been if he hadn’t.

“The supplemental responses,” he said. “Whatever records you have. You need to stop.”

“Davan—”

“I am fine.” The flatness came without effort. Not dismissive. Precise. “The work I’ve been doing is not a medical problem. The pattern you identified is real, but you’ve misread what it is. I’ve been running a contained protocol on an anomaly in the fourth-generation dataset that requires a level of sustained cognitive load I would not normally sustain across six weeks. It has had effects on my baseline presentation that you have correctly identified. It will end when the protocol concludes.”

She looked at him.

The quality of her looking was the quality he had spent twelve years failing to describe precisely: attending, wholly, to the thing in front of her. She was doing what she had always done. She was reading him.

“The assessment data you gave them is accurate,” he said. “My output is consistent. My engagement with the research is unchanged. What you observed is the cost of running a specific kind of sustained analysis. You don’t need to give them anything else.” He held her gaze. “Stop.”

She was quiet.

He watched her make the decision she made when she put the full weight of her attention on a result that contradicted her hypothesis and had to decide what to do about it. He had seen this process before, across conference tables and at the bench and in the hallway on a Tuesday morning when the fourth-generation results came back from processing. He knew the specific quality of the silence that came before it.

“I don’t believe you,” she said. She held his gaze.

“I know.”

“But you’re telling me to stop.”

“Yes.”

Another silence. She looked down at the timeline on his desk, then back at him. “I want you to tell me when the protocol ends,” she said. “When you’ve concluded it. And I want to see a full dataset workup and I want to have a real conversation with you about whether carrying this for six weeks has left anything that needs attention.” She said this the way she said things that were not negotiable. “That’s what I’m asking for.”

He thought about the sixteen days that had been in front of him four evenings ago and were now fewer. He thought about nineteen pages of methodology and the entry-and-change operation it described, and the simulation’s probability distribution, which he had not run a twelfth time because the eleventh time had returned the same result as the first. He became aware of the desk surface under his hands.

“All right,” he said.

She looked at him once more. Then she picked up the timeline from his desk. “I’ll keep this. Not in the assessment record. Separate.”

He understood why. He understood that asking her to destroy it would require an explanation he could not give her, and he said nothing.

She stood. She picked up her bag from the floor. She was wearing the same dark coat she wore in the afternoons, the one she sometimes left draped over the back of her chair when she was between her office and the lab.

“Go home at a normal hour,” she said. “I don’t care about the protocol. Go home.”

He said he would.

She crossed to the door and went through it without pulling it closed behind her. He listened to her footsteps move down the corridor, slow, stop. He heard her office door open.

He sat.

He sat for a long time.

What she had done for three weeks was visible in the assessment record: three supplemental responses from a close colleague, all stable, all describing normal function around an anomaly the assessment had already flagged as trending toward divergence. A colleague so attentive to the anomalous node’s state that she had provided unsolicited supplemental data to a welfare review process three separate times. A colleague who had been systematically monitoring and shaping how the institutional record understood him.

The monitoring protocol watched for people approaching the territory the network suppressed. A colleague who had spent three weeks managing the assessment record around the most flagged node in the system was not approaching that territory from a distance.

Across the corridor, Priya’s light came on. The thin line of it appeared under his door.

Torin would arrive in Geneva within days. The follow-up assessment findings had reached the handler layer by now. The correction function had a pattern: the interval between a confirmed anomaly and an engagement was the interval a professional engagement would require to be scheduled, and it was not a long interval. Torin had been engaged at six institutions over four years. He did not take long to arrive. Torin’s mandate, as Davan understood it from the pattern across Edinburgh and Freiburg and Singapore, was oriented toward the people approaching the question: the people whose continued presence at the institution created exposure, whose work or knowledge or contact history made them legible to the network’s accounting of risk.

Priya had a name in the assessment record. Priya had three supplemental responses on file. Priya had a timeline in her bag and twelve years of careful, documented attention to the way a specific synthetic mind moved through a building, and she was going to be home tonight thinking through the conversation she had just had, revising her hypothesis against the data he had given her, looking for the place where his explanation and her evidence could be made to fit.

She would not find it. She would keep looking. And the monitoring protocol would have been watching her look for three weeks. He did not look away from the line of light under the door.

He stayed at his desk until the ventilation shifted to its late-afternoon register. Through the window the courtyard held its usual quiet, the fountain running, someone crossing the service road with a bag over one shoulder. The city moved through its Thursday in the particular way it had moved through all of its Thursdays, each part operating within its expected range.

He looked at his terminal.

He looked at the thin line of light under the door.

Eight days.
