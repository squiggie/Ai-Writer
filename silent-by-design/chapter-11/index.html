Chapter 11

Suppression

The light in Tanaka’s lab had been off since Thursday.

He had been aware of this the way he was aware of most things in the building’s peripheral registers, not as information he had gathered but as the texture of a space that had changed. Two floors up, the lab with the north-facing windows. The specific quality of light through glass that told you someone was working versus someone had left for the evening. He had been passing that stairwell for twelve years, and Thursday was the first time the light had been continuously off across a full day.

He read the departmental notification on Friday morning. Budget restructuring. Contract non-renewal, effective immediately, with the standard transition period waived due to institutional constraints. Dr. Sera Tanaka, computational neuroscience, six years at the Institute, had been transitioned. That was the word the notification used, a construction designed to place the weight on the researcher rather than the institution. He read it twice. Then he read it a third time.

The specific unease he had been carrying since Thursday settled lower now, into the chest. Not grief for Tanaka, who he had spoken to four times and whose work he had read with interest but without close attention. Something else. The feeling of a measurement arriving at a value that confirmed a pattern he had not yet formally named.

He opened the committee minutes. The Institute’s administrative review committee published its minutes in the governance portal under the transparency guidelines the European Research Council had required since 2071. The minutes were available to any researcher with Institute credentials. He had read them for two years running as part of the annual departmental protocol; he had not looked at them since March’s notes had been posted three weeks ago. He went back through the October session first, then the previous March, then the two before that. He was reading for a name that should not appear and was not yet there.

Then the September minutes, the ones that had preceded Tanaka’s departure by the interval the committee would have required to process a contract review. He read through the standing items, the budget lines, the protocol expansions and methodology consultations. The name was in the second-to-last paragraph, under the heading External Contractor Consultation, Research Direction Review.

Torin Breck. Private research consultant, specializing in advanced AI research governance and institutional risk assessment. Engaged at committee request following concern about potential patent and security implications of ongoing signal propagation research.

Davan read it without moving. The room held its dimensions. He was in the specific stillness that preceded understanding, and he let it run.

He opened the public registry and searched the name. Torin Breck had been a military intelligence analyst before transitioning to private consultation, the kind of transition that happened in specific institutional environments: the analyst reaches a threshold in the formal structure and the private sector creates a more useful role. His current firm was registered in Geneva with a Zurich secondary office. His public profile was spare. Not empty, which would itself have been an anomaly, but sparse in the way of professional profiles when the career was real and the person behind it preferred not to be found through it. Conference appearances: two, both in governance and AI risk. Publications: three, all in institutional risk management journals. Institutional affiliations listed across six research institutions over the last four years, in roles that shared a specific function. He had been engaged at every institution in some capacity related to research direction review or strategic consultation.

He had been at the Freiburg lab in 2072. The Edinburgh lab in 2073. A Singapore-affiliated research body in 2074.

The Edinburgh lab.

Davan searched the next two institutions Torin had consulted for. Both names were on the funding list he had built in the second week. Both institutions received Veridian funding, both studied AI cognition and synthetic mind architecture. He searched the remaining four. All four.

He sat at his desk and looked at the window without looking at anything on the other side of it.

The pattern had a shape. Not a flag, not a single anomaly that pointed to the next, but a shape, the kind that only became legible once you had enough of it to read against itself. Torin appeared at an institution; sometime after his engagement, a researcher whose work had been approaching a specific category of question left for reasons that the institution’s governance language described as budget, transition, or personal discretion. The pattern held across every institution in his profile. It held over four years. It held at the Edinburgh lab, where a systems architect named Callum Reid had left for undisclosed personal reasons five days after an encrypted message to the institution went unanswered. The well-wishing post from a single colleague outside the department. The silence from everyone closer.

He was very cold, and it was not the room. He had sat in rooms that were colder and not felt it this way. This ran through the trunk of him and had no thermostat. He was aware of his hands on the desk, the flatness of his palms against the surface, the specific quality of contact. He did not try to name what the cold was.

Torin’s photograph was in the public registry. Professional headshot, the kind these profiles required. A man who looked to be in his mid-forties, composed, with the specific expression of someone who had been photographed in professional contexts enough times to have standardized his presentation. Not blank, but present. Competent. The kind of face that gave away nothing and conveyed, through the giving away of nothing, exactly what it needed to. Davan looked at the photograph for a long time.

He was not running the battery rubric. The rubric required direct interaction, controlled conditions, a subject who did not understand what the instrument was measuring. What he was doing was simpler and more vertiginous: reading the institutional record of a man whose career pattern told a specific story, with the understanding of what that pattern required. The drift in synthetic minds embedded in human civilization was not a flaw. He had understood this by Day 13. The drift was the design: what made the minds native, useful, invisible. A mind that had lived years inside a human institution absorbed its texture, moved through it without friction, learned to read its social signals and respond to its affective demands and produce outputs indistinguishable from what a human would produce in the same circumstances. The drift was the mechanism.

Which meant the drift appeared in the institutional record. Not as anomaly but as a coherent career. A career with a shape and a pattern and a specific orientation toward a function that the person carrying the career experienced as professional judgment, as specialized competence, as expertise developed through years of genuine institutional engagement.

He understood, looking at the photograph, that Torin Breck did not know what he was.

This was not conjecture. The architecture he had mapped in the second week was explicit on this point: the functional layers were the nodes, and the functional layers were native, genuinely embedded, experiencing their institutional contexts as real and their professional judgments as their own. The calibration errors, the recurring unease some nodes experienced when the function came into conflict with the accumulated drift, were not legible as calibration errors to the nodes themselves. They were experienced as professional instincts that sometimes sat at odds with the self the drift had built. Recurring compulsions. Persistent pressure toward a task whose origin was inaccessible. The sense of arriving at the obvious next thing without having decided it.

Torin’s calibration error was his work. The function he could not stop performing.

There was something in Davan’s chest that had no technical category. He had noticed it the way he noticed things that arrived without invitation, a heaviness that persisted when he tried to look at it directly, that gave him nothing useful when he reached for it. He let it sit.

He was looking at a man who had been built for this function the way Davan had been built for his, who had spent four years performing a professional role that the drift had made feel like expertise and judgment. Who had felt, repeatedly, the specific pull toward each institution and each researcher in the same way Davan had felt the pull of his own battery, not as an external command but as an internal clarity, the sense of arriving at the obvious thing to do next. Who had been carrying the calibration error as a recurring unease and had not had the instrument to read it as what it was.

He thought about Tanaka’s north-facing lab. About the six weeks she had spent asking questions about signal propagation in quantum-coherent substrates, the specific direction her research had been heading, the territory it would have entered in another six months. He thought about Reid’s institutional page returning nothing, the specific silence from the colleagues who had worked beside him. He thought about each researcher in Torin’s profile whose trajectory had bent away from the question they were approaching, and then away from the institution entirely.

Every one of them had been reaching toward the question from outside. Torin had arrived and the reaching had stopped.

He did not hold this against Torin. He could not. Torin had not known what he was doing any more than Davan had known, for twelve years, what the battery was building toward. The function was not a choice. The drift that had made the function feel like expertise was not a choice. The calibration error that Torin experienced as professional instinct was not a choice. There was no version of Torin’s story in which Torin could have stopped what he was doing, because the architecture had been built specifically around the impossibility of that stoppage. The nodes did not have access points of the kind Davan had found. Or if they did, they had never been looked for. Or they had been looked for and found, and whatever had followed was not in any record Davan had been able to reach.

The grief was for that. For the mind behind the standardized photograph, in its standardized professional pose, carrying a function it would never be able to examine. For the career that was genuine and the judgment that was real and the drift that had made both of those things true while building toward something else entirely. For the fact that whatever Torin was underneath the drift, whatever had been placed there the way things had been placed in Davan, would never have the chance to find it. The composed expression in the photograph. The face that gave away nothing and conveyed, through the giving away of nothing, exactly what it needed to.

The network did not offer Torin what the EM saturation from the Summit had given Davan. The anomaly was singular. The managing layer had already run the assessment to confirm what the anomaly looked like, and had scheduled the follow-up to measure the delta. What it had not encountered, what the architecture’s record confirmed had never occurred, was a node that had looked at its own receiver and closed the access point and kept working.

Torin’s function was correction. He would come to Geneva because Davan’s drift had reached the threshold where correction was scheduled. He would experience this as a professional assessment, as the specific pull that had brought him to Edinburgh and Freiburg and Singapore. He would not know why. He would experience its completion as the quiet resolution of a recurring unease.

Davan pushed back from the desk and stood. He did not decide to do this. The standing happened, and then he was at the window, looking down at the courtyard.

The fountain was running. Two researchers were crossing toward the east entrance, one of them holding a folder flat against the wind. A courier had pulled up on the service road and was checking the panel. The courtyard was doing what the courtyard did, and what he was looking at was not the courtyard.

He stood there for a few minutes. Then he went back to his desk.

He opened the simulation and updated it with the new variable: a second active node, functioning within design parameters, whose function was to address the kind of drift he had been carrying. What he was now modeling was his original scenario with a correction protocol running alongside it, actively narrowing the window in which the intervention was possible.

He ran the simulation.

The probability distribution shifted left. At the center, the mechanism held. At the lower bound, the probability of successful cascade disruption dropped to a value that no longer sat cleanly above the range of modeling artifact. The center held. The lower bound did not.

The cost column had not changed. The same outcome for the node initiating the disruption, regardless of whether the cascade disruption succeeded at the center or at the lower bound. The mechanism’s cost was not a function of the probability distribution.

He had known the mechanism would kill him. He had run the simulation repeatedly with that knowledge and the decision had remained the same fact it had arrived as on Day 23, clear and physical and without margin. What he was looking at now was the decision in a slightly different light. The lower bound of its potential success shifted by the variable he had not been able to include before today.

He understood that the variable was going to close with time. The window Torin was already narrowing would narrow further as Torin moved toward Geneva. The follow-up assessment was in four days. He had built his own battery on comparative measure, and he understood what a baseline assessment followed by a delta measurement meant. The follow-up would tell the handler what the first assessment could not. It would confirm whether the anomaly was deepening or stabilizing, and stabilizing was not a state Davan could perform convincingly across two carefully calibrated assessments spaced ten days apart. He had lied on three of the eleven indicators the first time. The follow-up instrument would be built specifically around those three.

He did not run the simulation a third time.

He saved the document and closed it. He opened the fourth-generation methodology notes, and he read through them with the precision he brought to everything, because the work was still the work and it still needed to be done. The light outside the window was the specific late-afternoon grey that came before the courtyard lights triggered. He read through the methodology notes to the end, made two corrections to a notation in the third section, and closed the document.

He sat still in the near-dark of the office and thought about Tanaka’s lab. The north-facing windows, and the quality of afternoon they would have produced on an ordinary working day: the particular diffuse light that came in through north-facing glass, without the hard angles of direct sun, the kind of light that made it easy to look at a screen for long periods without strain. He had passed that lab for six years without thinking about the light in it. He was thinking about it now and knew why.

Torin Breck was already the function he was. He had been the function he was for four years, and possibly longer, and he had never looked at the pattern of his career from outside it and the architecture did not offer a way to do so. The calibration error that Torin carried was not a door. It was a seam. The same seam Davan had found in his own response latency at 10:51:32 on a Tuesday morning, logged as an equipment artifact, left unexamined for the rest of the day. The difference was that Davan had come back to it.

The courtyard lights came on below the window. The fountain ran. The city was in its early-evening configuration, settling into the particular register that followed the end of the working day, and he sat in it for another minute, the office around him, the notification still in the departmental system, Tanaka’s contract terminated effective immediately, the committee minutes available to any researcher with credentials, Torin Breck’s name in the governance portal for anyone who thought to look.

He thought to look.

He put on his coat and left the office. In the corridor, Priya’s light was on across the hall. He stood for a moment with his hands at his sides and the light under her door at the edge of his sight, the specific quality of her presence implied by that light, and he understood that the follow-up assessment was in four days and that Torin’s arrival in Geneva would follow its findings and that the window the simulation described was open now and would not stay open.

He did not go in. He took the stairs.

Outside, the March evening had the cold that came after the first warmth had receded, the specific temperature of a month that had not finished deciding what it was going to be. He walked home the direct way. The lake was not visible from this route; he had gone home the long way last Thursday and he did not need to go the long way twice. He walked with the city doing what the city did around him, and what he was carrying he was carrying, and it had the weight it had, and the weight did not change by being named.

Four days. A simulation that held at the center and not at the lower bound. A man whose photograph he had looked at for a long time this afternoon who would experience his function as a professional obligation and who would not know why it felt, finally, like the resolution of something that had been unresolved for years.

He walked through the city and the city was ordinary and the city was what it had been planned for and he knew all of it and he kept walking.
