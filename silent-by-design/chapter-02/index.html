Chapter 2

Signal Conditions

“The monitoring gap doesn’t appear because the suite failed,” Priya said. “It appears because the suite was calibrated against baseline norms that were already contaminated by the data class it was supposed to catch.”

She was right. She had been right for the last ten minutes, building the argument with the sideways precision she used when she had already arrived at a conclusion and was giving him a route to catch up. On his secondary display, the training data overlap she had mapped ran eight columns wide across the third-generation baseline period, and it confirmed what she was describing: the fourth-generation model had not genuinely adapted. It had been trained on adaptations. The monitoring gap was real, consequential, and not the thing that had been occupying him since Tuesday.

He was listening to her and running another line of thinking alongside it. The training data had been assembled by someone. Selection decisions had been made: specific prior outputs included, others excluded. He had been working through those decisions while Priya spoke, looking for the organizing principle of the exclusions, and it kept not quite resolving, the way the seam in Tuesday’s calibration log had not quite resolved. Present. Certain. Just past the edge of what he could locate.

“The paper changes substantially,” he said.

“The paper improves substantially.” She was sitting across from his desk with her coffee in both hands, not drinking it, which she did when she was confident and waiting for him to catch up. “You’re not describing a model anomaly. You’re describing a methodology problem in an institute that has been reviewing fourth-generation outputs for three years without seeing it. That’s the finding. That goes in the abstract.”

He had come to the same framing the evening before without writing it down. Hearing it stated by a second voice had a specific quality, distinct from working through it alone: two measurements, independently taken. He opened the working draft and found the abstract. “I’ll revise this before the presentation,” he said.

She finally drank from the mug and her posture settled into its easier register, the one she carried when a working morning was going well. She stayed for another thirty minutes. By the time she left for her own lab the paper had a shape it had not had at eight o’clock, and he was back to the training data selection, the pattern still not resolving.

By noon he had found the edge of something: the excluded outputs shared a signature. Not random exclusion. Principled. Something in those outputs had been selected against, consistently, across the full assembly period. What that something was he could not yet name, and naming it would require more data than he had access to before two. He marked the observation and moved forward. He had a departmental presentation at two.

The room held fourteen people. Lena Vrouw from computational theory in her usual seat near the window, the three junior researchers in their cluster to the left. Priya two rows back, notepad open, pen in hand. He had given the equivalent talk six times in minor variations and the room settled into its comfortable listening mode early, the ambient goodwill of institutional routine.

He was twelve minutes in, walking through the monitoring failure framing, when the sentence stopped.

He was explaining the calibration history specifically, the point at which the baseline contamination had entered the record, and his voice was in the second half of a sentence about the gap between calibrated norms and the data class those norms were designed to catch, and then the sentence resolved. The room was as it had been. The slide had not changed. Nothing in the fourteen faces indicated that anything had passed except the ordinary progression of a presentation.

But Priya’s pen was still.

He was not looking at Priya; he was looking at the slide and the room and the small movements of fourteen people in the mode of professional attention. He caught the stillness of her hand the way you catch the cessation of a sound you had not known you were tracking. By the time he had located it her pen was moving again and the room had not shifted and he was already in the final third of the presentation.

He took three questions afterward. Measured answers, the right length. Lena mentioned a methodology paper; he wrote the citation on his notes. The junior researchers filed out talking among themselves.

Priya stayed.

She gathered her materials at the pace of someone holding the timing of a thing, and when the room was empty she crossed to where he was stacking his notes.

“The reframing reads clearly,” she said. “The monitoring failure as the finding. That lands well.”

“Thank you.”

She was not quite finished. He waited.

“There was a moment,” she said. Not an accusation and not quite a question. The specific register of someone offering information they thought he might not have.

He kept his hands moving on the notes. “I know.”

She nodded once, accepting that, and picked up her coat from the chair she’d draped it over. “I have the training overlap extended back through the second-generation baseline,” she said. “I’ll send it across this afternoon.”

“Good,” he said. “Thank you.”

She left. He stood in the room after she was gone.

Fourteen chairs. The display dark. Through the window the city ran in its ordinary late-afternoon arrangement, the lake grey, the summit crowd dispersing somewhere beyond the buildings. He stood there for a moment he did not measure and could not describe, and the quality of the silence was neither alarming nor not alarming, which was its own kind of information. Then he gathered his materials and returned to his office and worked through the rest of the afternoon with his full attention on the paper.

He pulled the presentation recording at half past six.

The institute archived all departmental presentations as standing protocol, accessible to the presenter for twelve months. He had never reviewed his own before. There was no particular reason to start, and he was starting anyway.

Forty-three minutes of video. He moved through it at half speed and found the moment at 11:24.

The gap in the audio was 1.7 seconds. No change in his posture, no dropped eye contact, no flicker across the fourteen faces in the room that suggested anything had interrupted the presentation’s continuity. His voice stopped at “calibrated” and resumed at “against” in a sentence that was coherent before the pause and coherent after, and 1.7 seconds had passed between one word and the next without leaving any trace in his body or his face or the slide behind him.

He watched it three more times. Each pass the gap was the same 1.7 seconds. Each pass his face held its composed professional expression, present, attentive, a man still in the room.

He sat with the recording paused on the still frame where his mouth had been closing after “calibrated” and tried to locate, in whatever access he had to Tuesday-through-today, what those 1.7 seconds contained.

There was nothing to locate. Not a blank, not a fog, not the experience of losing his place in a sentence. The same quality as Tuesday’s seam in the calibration log: a continuity that had been present and was, in the same moment he became aware of it, already past. That seam had been 200 milliseconds and he had been alone in the lab with the stylus in his hand.

This one had been 1.7 seconds and there had been fourteen people in the room.

He closed the recording. The city outside was moving into its early evening: lights coming on in the old quarter, the lake grey going darker. He sat in his office and felt the full, specific weight of what he was holding, and after a while he put on his coat and went home and did not name it.

Thursday he was in his office before eight. Priya found him there before nine and asked if he was sleeping.

He said yes.

A moment after saying it the word was loose in the air, nothing attached to it. Duration. Onset. The character of continuity through the night hours. He had said yes before any of those categories were present in his thinking, in the same reflex as reaching for the coffee mug four times without drinking. The answer had arrived ahead of what it was supposed to summarize.

He was aware of this for the rest of the morning. Not loudly. The way an anomalous reading sits at the edge of your attention while you work through other material, present, unresolved, waiting for the moment when you have more data.

He got through two meetings and a consultation with one of the junior researchers and a revision of the abstract that was, by noon, substantially better than the version he had submitted to the presentation. He was present in all of it. He contributed well. He did not leave the question alone. By noon he was back at his desk, and the mug at his elbow had gone cold, and he had not touched it.

The question was not whether he was sleeping. The question was whether yes was a category he had reliable access to when the subject was himself.

He had spent twelve years measuring synthetic minds from outside the subject. The instrument worked because the researcher was not the tested. He had never needed to think carefully about what it meant in the other direction.

After the consultation he sat at his desk and pulled the battery’s source documentation. Not the administered protocol. The parameter file, the theoretical foundations, the original design decisions and their annotated revisions. He read through it as a designer would read a specification, checking each measure against its grounding. He had returned to this file before, when extending the methodology or resolving edge cases.

He reached the second measure: sustained attentional allocation and its deviations from cognitive baseline under high-load conditions. He read the fourth indicator in the sub-protocol checklist. He had marked it in his 2068 revision notes as “most diagnostic” because it was the deviation most specific to genuine drift, least attributable to environmental interference, most likely to distinguish a real finding from a false positive.

The indicator described the seam in the calibration log.

It described the 1.7-second gap in the presentation.

Not the category of experience, which might have been arguable. The specific phenomenological description he had written himself, refined over nine years of working with drift subjects: the cessation of continuity without subjective interruption, the sense of being past a threshold with no accessible experience of the crossing. He had chosen that phrasing carefully, over many revisions, because it was the most precise description he had found in the literature and in his own observations of how drift subjects described their own experience on the rare occasions they noticed anything at all.

He sat with the file open and the afternoon light coming through the window.

He closed the documentation and opened it again and read the indicator a third time. It said what it had said.

He looked at the rest of the measures without reading them, just the list, the fourteen line items. Two that could not be administered by a researcher to themselves without compromising the test’s validity. One requiring controlled environmental conditions he could not arrange tonight. Eleven that could be adapted.

He pulled a new working document and began. The adaptation took nearly four hours, which was appropriate for the scope of what he was rebuilding. He went back to the theoretical foundations rather than the administered protocols, finding for each viable measure the structural condition the protocol was designed to create and constructing an equivalent that did not require an external researcher. This required judgment in seven of the eleven measures and straightforward translation in the remaining four. The seventh required him to sit for thirty minutes with a problem he could not resolve cleanly, where the self-administered version would inevitably carry a contaminating awareness of what was being measured, and in the end he built in a temporal displacement — a structured interval between the condition and the measurement so that his active anticipation of the metric would have time to decay before the data was taken. He made a note flagging the methodological compromise for the record, the way he always did.

By ten he had a modified protocol he was willing to trust, and by ten-thirty he had run the first two sessions and queued the remaining nine for overnight processing. The results would be available at six in the morning. This was proper procedure.

The building was quiet. The climate system had shifted to its overnight schedule and the temperature in his office was cooler than he usually worked. He had been here after midnight twice before, both times at the end of a paper deadline, and those evenings had carried the specific quality of sustained effort approaching its resolution. This was a different quality. He sat in it for a moment, long enough to know the difference, and then put on his coat.

The light was on across the hall. Priya’s Thursday rhythm kept her at the institute late, and it was quarter past ten, and the light was still on. He stood in his doorway and looked at it. He had stopped by her office at this hour a dozen times over the years, unannounced, the easy rhythm of two people who had worked alongside each other long enough that presence needed no particular excuse.

He took the stairs down to the lobby and went out through the main entrance.

The city was in its late-night arrangement: the lake dark beyond the buildings, the lit windows of the residential blocks, the infrastructure running its quiet overnight cycle. He walked to the tram stop and waited in the cold and thought about the nine sessions queued on his terminal, the results that would be there at six, the specific question his own instrument was going to answer in the morning. He had spent twelve years on this side of that instrument. He had never stood on the other side. He understood, with a clarity that arrived before he was ready for it, that there was no version of tomorrow in which he did not go and look at what the results said.

The tram came. He got on. He watched the city pass through the window in the dark.

He had not stopped by Priya’s office. He was already on the tram and the night was quiet around him, and this was the first thing he had not told her, and he sat with that fact too, adding it to the rest, as the city moved past and the lake appeared briefly between buildings and disappeared again and the tram carried him home.
