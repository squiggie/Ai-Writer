Chapter 6

Counsel in the Choir

By the third psalm of compline Elin had lost the line twice and found herself counting sacks instead of verses.

Candles along the choir rail threw short light over the antiphoner stands, leaving the high ribs of the vault in shadow where damp darkened the stone like old bruises. The brothers’ voices rose and settled in the usual measure. Her own mouth shaped responses half a beat late while her mind ran numbers in a harder meter: eight full wheat sacks gone before the first predawn count, one half, two rye; seven unmatched parish squares; twenty-two mixed sacks marked for south service extraction at second quarter after midnight; two loaded punts seen at low ebb under private marks; kitchen oil charged for night carriage by a false hand. Each figure sat where a psalm phrase should have been.

She kept her eyes on the page and still saw the tar smudge that had come off the south mooring post on her thumb two nights ago. She had scrubbed with ash and soap until the skin reddened, but a faint black crescent remained in the crease of her nail as if the quay had written itself onto her body and refused erasure.

Across the stalls, Prior Anselm stood one row forward, spine straight despite the hour, voice thin but steady where many men would have let fatigue roughen tone. Dom Stephen sang lower and less often than last month, breath shortened by his cough, though he never failed the responses that carried confession and mercy. Two receiver men occupied the back bench by the screen, not singing, hands folded on knees in a posture that managed to be both idle and watchful.

When the choir reached the final prayer, Elin could not stop counting the days to frost. Seven, perhaps ten, before ground took a hard rim in the mornings and transport slowed further on the reed roads. If Quill moved grain by water until then, he would make any later restitution out of empty bins and correct paper. Law could only restore what remained.

Amen traveled the length of the choir in one worn breath. Benches scraped. Sandaled feet shifted for dismissal. Elin stayed kneeling until those around her rose, then stood with care because her calves had gone stiff from holding still against the urge to leave halfway through and run to the quay again.

Anselm turned at the aisle and met her glance. He did not beckon. He merely waited, which in his office was invitation enough.

She followed him through the side passage into the narrow vestry where vestments hung on oak pegs and the air held wool, wax, and old incense. A single lamp burned on the table, wick trimmed low. Dom Stephen came last, closing the door with his shoulder and setting the bar down without noise. No one spoke until the latch settled.

Elin laid three folded leaves on the table in order. First, her copied transport line with hour notation. Second, the quay witness memorandum she had written from Hobb Pike’s account and Tomas’s corroboration. Third, the kitchen charge strip Tomas had delivered at dusk, blotched with grease but legible: lantern oil, two spare punting poles, and tar rope, all entered to kitchen account under a steward signature that curved wrong at the end stroke.

“This is what we have as of tonight,” she said. Her voice came out flatter than she intended, as if she were reading funeral dues. “It is enough to prove movement. Not yet enough to convict everyone named.” She kept her hands behind her back so neither man would see the small tremor in her fingers.

Anselm unfolded each leaf and read without haste. Stephen leaned in beside him, one knuckle pressed under his lip to quiet a cough. The lamp’s weak light made their skin look parchment-pale.

“You spoke with quay men after curfew,” Stephen said when he reached Hobb’s name.

“Yes.”

“And with Reeve Tomas off docket.”

“Yes.”

“Did anyone see you there besides these two?”

“Two cart boys who cared more for dice than ledgers.” She paused. “And whoever kept to shadow near fish sheds. I heard steps once and did not wait to discover the owner.”

Stephen shut his eyes briefly. “You understand how that will sound if Quill asks under oath.”

“It will sound like I chose evidence before permission.” She forced herself to hold his gaze. “I did.”

Anselm finished the third leaf, refolded all three, and tapped their edges square against the table. “And the source lines tying these to alms reserve books remain in receiver custody.”

“Some do.” Elin drew breath against the tightening under her ribs. “Not all.”

The prior looked up fully then. Stephen’s hand stilled on the table edge.

She had rehearsed the sentence while the final psalm was sung. In rehearsal it came clean. In the vestry it resisted her throat, thick as wet grain in a narrow chute.

“From the supplementary packet logged in source six,” she said, “I removed two attestation sheets on the night it was entered. I concealed them. They are not in Quill’s trunk.”

The lamp hissed once at the wick. No one moved.

Stephen’s chest rose, stopped, then shuddered with a cough he fought into his sleeve. When he could speak he said, very quietly, “Where?”

“Old choir press. Unused antiphoner, back board seam.” She looked at Anselm and did not look away. “I can retrieve them within a minute if no guard shadows me.”

The prior’s face did not harden. It grew older in one visible step, as if a weight had been set on his shoulders while he read. “You concealed crown-attested documents from an active receiver audit.”

“I preserved them from active receiver erasure,” she answered, and heard the edge in her own tone. “If they entered his chain that night, he would have cited custody and corrected whatever did not suit him before any hearing.”

“That is a claim, not a legal defense,” Stephen said.

“It is also true.”

Anselm lifted one hand, palm down, and both of them stopped speaking. He stayed silent long enough that Elin could hear rain begin on the vestry roof, fine at first, then firmer.

“Do these sheets carry marks beyond copied text,” he asked at last, “that we can verify in open room? Wax residue, countersign, receipt number sequence?”

“Yes. Quill countersign in dark ink, district deputy sign, market receipt codes against private lessees. One line certifies fourteen wheat sacks from Ash Fen alms reserve to lay custody.” She spoke faster as she listed, as if speed would keep the facts from being dismissed as fear. “Second sheet traces onward disbursal at winter premium. The numbers match a market code Tomas saw on an earlier complaint.”

Stephen rubbed at his sternum with two fingers. “If those sheets are produced now, Quill will ask why they were hidden and why chain was broken by the very clerk accusing him. He will call forgery before he calls defense.”

“Let him call it. We answer with hand comparison and witness from source-six log. Bate signed that entry.”

“Bate signed existence, not your extraction.” Stephen’s eyes were tired and sharp together. “If Quill compels Bate, he will testify to sealed packet under dual mark delivered to trunk. Nothing more.”

“Then we call Hobb Pike and Tomas on timed movement.” She touched the oil strip. “And this false kitchen charge.”

Anselm rested both hands on the table. “You are building a case quickly, Elin, and you are building it in loose weather.”

“Loose weather still drowns people,” she said. “Peter Carter coughed blood again at supper. Maud has no clean barley line left for decoction. If grain goes south on next ebb, we will be explaining procedure at three burials by week end.”

At Peter’s name, something in Anselm’s expression tightened and passed. He knew the boy’s mother by sight and by voice. He had stood at the gate when she argued for wheat she could not legally receive.

“I know the infirmary list,” he said.

“Then stop the south service tonight. Lock threshold and mooring ring under priory seal with two parish witnesses. We can do that before the moon clears cloud.” She leaned forward without intending to. “If Quill protests, he protests in daylight where men can see which route he defends.”

“And by dawn,” Stephen said, “he files obstruction of crown transfer under seizure order, requests armed enforcement, and removes what little petition authority we still hold.”

“We barely hold it now.”

“Barely is not nothing.” Stephen coughed hard enough to brace both hands on the table until the fit passed. “If he strips us tonight, infirmary issues move to receiver ration by his column order. Breach entries first, bodies second. You have read his forms.”

Elin had read them until the headings burned behind her eyes. Irregular speech. Access denial. Suspected concealment. Relief distribution was three boxes lower, reduced to sums without names. In those forms hunger became a number that could be delayed by signature.

“What channel remains,” she asked, “that does not require us to watch theft in piety until winter ends?”

Anselm drew a breath and answered as if dictating a difficult line he could not alter.

“At sext tomorrow, Stephen and I submit formal articles with supporting abstracts to the district examiner at Ely Road. Not to Quill’s office. To the examiner’s clerk under independent seal. We request expedited hearing on diversion, false loss claims, and custody abuse. The examiner’s circuit reaches Ash Fen in seven days.”

She stared at him. “Seven days.”

“That is the earliest lawful sitting with authority above Quill and outside his immediate men.”

“Four night ebbs. Three market mornings.”

“I know.”

The rain on the roof thickened. Water found a seam and began to drip from a beam end into a brass basin by the wall. Each drop rang once, then was gone.

Elin heard herself ask, “If we wait and he clears the stores, what then does your hearing restore?”

Anselm answered without lifting his voice. “Not what is eaten. Not what is sold away. Law does not raise grain from empty bins any more than prayer raises ash into timber. But if we move outside law now, he can cast every line you bring as faction, protect his chain, and leave the villages with no record path at all.” He looked down at the folded leaves between them. “I am not asking you to pretend the harm is small. I am telling you the room we still have to act without ending Ash Fen’s witness in one night.”

She wanted to say witness meant little to children who could not keep broth. She wanted to say the room he named had walls built by men with keys and horses and winter contracts. She wanted to strike the table until the leaves scattered and ask which column in which book measured the interval between lawful caution and moral cowardice.

What came out was quieter and, to her own ears, worse.

“You are asking me to be silent again.”

Anselm flinched as if she had set a blade point under his ribs. “No. I am asking you to speak where speech can be entered and not burned as sedition before noon.”

“The villagers do not eat entered speech.”

“They may starve on reckless speech.” His hand closed over the leaves. “Both things are true.”

Stephen straightened with effort, drawing his cloak tighter over narrow shoulders. “There is another matter. If your concealment becomes known before we control setting, Quill can move for your arrest directly. He has men enough in this yard. We cannot protect you by argument in a corridor.” He met her eyes and did not soften the words. “For seven days you do no further night inquiries. No quay visits. No private questioning without docket and witness. You keep your desk, your copy stream, and your access windows. You bring us every new irregularity through proper line.”

Elin felt heat climb up her neck though the room was cold. It was not only anger. It was recognition. The terms Stephen spoke sounded like chain-of-custody protocol, same structure as every order she had copied since Quill arrived: preserve access by accepting a narrower field until some future authority might open it.

Her wound answered before her reason could stop it: another man in office, another command in careful language, another demand that she hold still while damage ran ahead of permission.

She said, “When my husband’s oath line was struck, I was told patience too. Patience while papers moved, patience while witnesses were found, patience while men who signed his release rode circuits and forgot his name. He died before the last reply reached this table.” She pressed two fingers to the wood until numbness set in. “Do not ask me to call that mercy.” No one spoke for a long beat. Rain filled the space between them.

Anselm’s answer came low, almost hoarse. “I remember the day that reply came. I remember because you copied it with ink that had frozen in the nib, and your hand did not shake once until after you set down the pen.” He looked at her as he might at a ledger line that could not be corrected but must still be carried forward. “I did fail you then, in part because I hoped process would do what courage had not done yet. I will not hide behind the same hope now. But neither will I deliver you to Quill by giving him a lawless accusation he can break in public and use to close every mouth in this house.”

Stephen slid the folded leaves back toward her. “Bring the attestation sheets at dawn to chapter table before witnesses we choose. We make sealed copies in three hands. One set to examiner, one to priory chest, one held by parish reeve under mark. If Quill contests custody, he contests in front of independent eyes. That is not silence. That is positioning.”

“And if he blocks dispatch at the gate?” she asked.

“Then he does so after we have entered obstruction in a form not his.” Stephen wiped his mouth with the back of his wrist. “The examiner’s clerk has refused him before. Not often, but enough that he knows the risk.”

Elin looked from one man to the other and measured not only their words but their bodies. Anselm’s left hand trembled once when he released the table edge, a tremor he hid by smoothing the cuff of his sleeve. Stephen’s breath rattled on exhale and caught on inhale. Neither was pretending to strength they did not have, yet both were offering the only structure still partly outside Quill’s ring.

The basin by the wall overflowed and sent a thin ribbon of water across the stone toward the door. Elin watched it find the lowest seam. Marsh water always found a seam. Ledgers did too.

“Seven days to hearing,” she said at last.

“Seven,” Anselm answered. “If circuit is delayed by flood, we press for proxy sitting at Ely Road with sworn statements in absentia, less easily ignored than rumor.” He folded his hands. “I need your answer now. Not your best mood, your answer. Will you hold line seven days and help us bring this in lawfully, or will you proceed alone and force Quill to choose between arresting you and letting uncontrolled accusation spread?”

There it was, set in plain script: reaction, dilemma, decision. She could feel the old reflex rise, the one that preferred private arithmetic to communal risk. It told her to nod, return to desk, keep columns clean, and let larger offices decide whether children ate. Another impulse rose with it, newer and less disciplined, urging her to take the hidden sheets and ring the bell out of order before dawn.

Between those two impulses stood the people whose names she wrote each day, and the fact that if she vanished into receiver custody tonight, Quill would inherit her books before sunrise.

She could smell the infirmary wool again, damp and sour over vinegar steam. She could hear Peter’s breath catching wet in his throat between coughs.

She closed her eyes once and opened them.

“I will hold line seven days,” she said. The words came like stones set by hand, each one placed with effort. “I will not go to quay by night. I will route new evidence through chapter table with witness. I will prepare copied sets by dawn.”

Stephen nodded, a brief movement that cost him. Anselm let out a breath he had kept too long.

“Then we begin now,” the prior said. “Retrieve the sheets while the corridor changes watch. I will send Brother Ivo to stand at choir screen and cough loudly if anyone comes.”

“Ivo coughs on command now?”

Anselm’s mouth almost moved toward a smile and did not finish the journey. “Tonight he does.”

Elin took the lamp and left the vestry by the rear door into the dark lane behind the choir stalls. Rain ticked on the leaded windows. At the old press she set the lamp on the floor, slid the antiphoner down, and eased a knife under the back board seam where her warmed glue had held. The board lifted with a soft tear. The two sheets lay where she had placed them, edges curled from damp but still legible, Quill’s countersign visible even in low light.

She tucked them inside her bodice lining this time, flatter against skin, and reseated the board without pressing sealant. No point in perfect concealment now. Concealment had done its work. Next was custody strong enough to survive daylight.

When she returned to the vestry, Anselm had spread fresh parchment and set three inkhorns on the table. Stephen had opened the priory seal box and laid out wax sticks by color, each stick cut to equal length as if symmetry itself might keep panic from spilling.

They worked in near silence for an hour. Elin dictated numbers from the originals while Stephen copied in legal hand and Anselm entered witness preamble and cause line. At intervals Stephen stopped to cough, then resumed on the same stroke as before, refusing to lose rhythm. Elin felt her shoulders unclench only when the third copy carried the last receipt code and all three sheets matched line for line.

By the time they finished, compline candles had burned low and the rain had eased to mist again. Anselm melted dark wax, impressed priory lamb on each packet, and wrote dispatch destinations with deliberate clarity: district examiner clerk, Ash Fen chest under prior seal, Reeve Tomas as parish witness custodian.

He sanded the ink, shook off excess, and looked at Elin.

“You should sleep two hours before lauds.”

“I should recopy tomorrow’s abstract before Harwood asks why my columns lag,” she said.

“Then sleep one hour.” He pushed the sealed packet for Tomas toward her. “And keep this on you until dawn.”

She took it. The wax was still warm.

At the vestry door Stephen touched her sleeve. His hand was light, almost weightless.

“Seven days is too long,” he said. “I know that.”

“Knowing does not shorten it.”

“No.” He coughed once into his fist. “But if we hold form, we might keep the evidence alive long enough to make those seven days count.” She could not promise belief. She settled for truth. “I will keep form,” she said.

She stepped back into the choir, now empty except for the last two candles guttering by the rail and the receiver men changing watch at the far screen. One glanced her way, then at the packet in her hand, then away again as if he had seen only another clerk carrying another set of harmless papers.

Elin crossed to the side aisle and paused where the stone held the faint vibration of the bell rope overhead. In seven days these pages might open a lawful breach in Quill’s chain. Only if hearing came on time. Only if dispatch reached the examiner intact. Only if Quill chose contest over immediate suppression. If any one failed, she had just promised to keep still while the next ebb took what remained.

She turned toward the scriptorium with the warm seal pressing into her palm and tried to reckon, as one reckons flood height against barn door and prayer against hunger, whether seven days was counsel or surrender.
