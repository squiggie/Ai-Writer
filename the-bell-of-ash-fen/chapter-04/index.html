Chapter 4

The Sealed Roll

By rushlight in the damp archive, Elin knelt beside the north wall chest and set both palms under a warped panel that had not sat true since the flood year. The wood stuck, then gave with a wet cough of old glue, and she drew out two mildew-stained account rolls wrapped in cloth gone soft at the corners. The hourglass on her table had already lost a finger of sand. She carried the rolls to the light, laid them beside the receiver’s abstract form, and wiped her hands on linen that came away green-gray.

The guard Quill had posted for tonight stood outside the inner door where he could see her table if she leaned forward, not if she crouched by the wall. His name was Bate, a broad man with a soldier’s calves and a drover’s patience, and he had learned by the second night that she would not waste speech on him unless the keys were in question. He had also learned she would count everything aloud if he let his attention wander.

“Five source rolls,” she said now, setting the mildew pair with the three volumes approved at sext. “One parish abstract quire, one transfer roll, one alms issue book, two flood-damaged residuals from north wall chest, docketed in my hand.”

Bate scratched the count with charcoal on his board. “Residuals were not on the request list.”

“Residuals were not legible at sext,” she said. “They are legible now that the panel has dried enough to lift. If you want them entered by the hour, you can either note them now or explain to Master Quill why figures were left to rot because his guard would not let me read them.”

He grunted, not liking the choice and liking less that she had stated it plain. “Read fast, then. You get one hour and no saint’s indulgence.”

She moved back to the table and set the first roll under a brass weight. The parchment had puckered where damp had bitten through, and ink had feathered into brown clouds along half the lines, yet the headings remained clear enough: Alms Transfer, North and East Marsh, St. Luke to St. Simon. The months named in the roll sat four years earlier than the current shortage, but old routes taught present theft. Men who skimmed grain changed office before they changed method.

She opened Quill’s Daily Abstract for Receiver's Office and filled the first three boxes as required: breaches of order, irregular speech, denied access requests. The form wanted misconduct first and hunger second. Elin wrote the words exactly and felt each line scrape. In the margin she entered infirmary reserve projection and fever households as she had last night, not because Quill valued it, but because she would not leave the body count to memory.

The transfer roll on her right held recent entries under receiver custody. The mildew roll on her left held older allotment routes drawn when marsh roads were still marked by willow posts now long gone. She aligned them by parish name, then by quantity notation, then by carriage description. Where one scribe wrote cart, another wrote flat punt; where one wrote four sack wheat, the other iiii sak whete, but numbers traveled even when spellings drifted.

At seventeen lines in, she found the first mirror that should not have existed. A current entry in Quill’s roll listed twelve sacks to Mere End poor relief by reed-road convoy, countersigned R.Q. and witnessed by a receiver clerk she had not seen in Ash Fen before this week. A line in the old mildew roll used the same route annotation in the same uncommon order, reed bend, second marker, ash post, and marked that road as impassable above half tide. Tonight’s weather sat well above half tide. No convoy could have made the journey in the hour claimed.

She marked the discrepancy in thin red and did not yet write accusation. Discrepancy could still mean false hour, not false transfer. She checked three more lines. Two were ordinary. One carried another impossible timing, this one from south causeway to North Bank in forty minutes with loaded sacks after rain. Even a light cart would need twice that unless the grain never traveled.

Bate shifted outside and rapped once on the frame. “Half glass.”

“Then hold your boots still,” she said. “The vibration shakes dust onto open ink.”

He looked down at his feet as if they belonged to someone else, then stepped back from the threshold. When he moved, she heard another sound beyond him in the corridor, a softer tread and the click of a staff ferrule. Dom Stephen appeared at the outer grating, shoulders bent under his cloak.

“You are still upright,” he said through the bars. “That surprises me.” His voice carried gravel from his cough and too little sleep.

“Surprise is no category here,” she answered, keeping her eyes on the columns. “Do you need a witness line?”

“Prior asks whether tonight’s abstract can include gate queue numbers by parish. Quill’s man copied totals but not places.”

“I can add in margin if Bate confirms no objection.”

Bate shrugged toward the grating. “You can add ten psalms in margin if the main boxes are filled. My orders are time and lock, not content.”

Stephen coughed into his sleeve, recovered, and passed a folded scrap through the bars with two fingers. “From infirmary. Agnes Carter’s boy coughed blood again at supper and cannot keep broth. Healer asks for dawn priority and one strip of linen if stores allow.” He held Elin’s gaze until she took the scrap. “Write him where Quill’s men can read it.”

She nodded once and tucked the note beneath the abstract weight. “I will.”

Stephen’s steps retreated, slow at first, then slower after the first turn, and she listened until the corridor gave him back to stone silence. She copied the infirmary line in the margin where Quill’s clerk would have to see it at close. Then she rolled the old parchment tighter, set it aside, and reached for the second mildew bundle.

This one bore no heading on the outside, only a faded knot and a smear of red wax impressed long ago with Ash Fen’s lamb seal. The cloth came apart in her hands. Inside lay six narrow strips of docket tags, two folded receipts eaten at one edge, and a short quire stitched in rush fiber instead of thread. She frowned at the stitching. Rush rotted faster than linen and was used only for temporary field copies, not permanent books. Temporary copies were made when a man expected to rewrite later. Men rewrote when they needed clean story from dirty numbers.

She flattened the first receipt with a bone folder. Received at East Dike Store: ii sack rye, i half sack wheat, witness mark. No household names, no parish mark, no matching line in the official alms book for that date. The second receipt repeated the pattern at North Spill chapel: quantity received, no names, no originating chest noted. The docket strip tied to both slips read for transfer proof only, in a hand she did not recognize.

She opened the rush-stitched quire and felt her pulse move higher in her throat. The first page was a table of dispatches with four columns, all in the same neat clerking hand: date, declared relief destination, quantity taken, quantity delivered. The delivered column differed from taken in nearly every row. Losses were marked as tide spoilage, road levy, emergency diversion, phrases broad enough to swallow whole barns. Beside five entries sat a narrow countersign in darker ink: R.Q.

For a moment she could hear only the scratch of sand through glass. Her hands stayed on the page while her mind ran measures: if these rows were true, then missing sacks were not random theft by carter boys or rats. If they were false, then someone had manufactured a second set of numbers and hidden them in a wall chest where flood and rot might erase them before any audit. Either way, Quill’s initials had touched the losses.

She laid the quire against the current transfer roll and checked dates. Three matched days precisely. Quantities matched at departure and diverged at delivery. The official roll ended each line with delivered complete. The rush quire reduced by one, by two, by half, then redirected remainder to places she did not recognize: Bramcote lease granary, Ely road lay store, South Quay tally house.

No lay store names appeared anywhere in Ash Fen’s alms protocol. Relief moved from priory chest to parish hands with household marks, not through private stops. She knew this as doctrine, not preference. To divert alms grain through lay stores invited levy, spoilage, and theft in one motion, and every prior since boyhood schooling had forbidden it except in flood rescue under witness. These lines claimed flood rescue on dry-sky dates.

Bate knocked again, sharper. “Quarter glass. Close what you are on.”

“I close when I can cite,” she said.

“You close when the sand says.”

She did not answer him. She copied six key rows from the rush quire onto her blank leaf, including date, quantities, and the R.Q. countersigns. She added a caution mark after each line: source unentered, custody uncertain, verify against witness dockets. Her training did not allow certainty from one hidden quire. Her pulse did not care about training.

When she reached for the next page, the quire shifted and showed a thicker fold beneath, stitched into the back cover. She eased the stitching apart with the point of her awl and drew out a packet wrapped in waxed linen, narrow as her forearm and heavier than its size should permit. A cord crossed it twice and disappeared under a fresh seal of dark green wax pressed with a signet she recognized before she let herself think: Quill’s ring, quartered field with small cross and hooked reed.

Below Quill’s seal sat another mark, older and partly smeared, the Ash Fen lamb cut through by a diagonal score as if someone had invalidated local custody when the packet was tied. Along the fold, in the same neat hand as the quire, was written: Supplementary transfer attestations, not for parish issue book. Deliver under receiver hand only.

The room seemed to tilt and settle. She set the packet flat and placed one finger along the seal edge to test whether the wax had already cracked. It held, but damp had lifted one corner where the linen did not adhere. She could slide two sheets free without fully breaking the impression if she used a thin blade and patience.

She listened first. Bate was still at the threshold, humming under his breath with the monotone of a man counting down someone else’s labor. No other tread in the corridor. No clerk’s voice. She slipped the blade under the lifted corner and worked the fold until two narrow sheets came free.

The first was a transfer attestation dated eight days before Quill arrived in person, signed by a district deputy and countersigned R.Q. It certified movement of fourteen wheat sacks from Ash Fen alms reserve to temporary lay custody for secure reallocation under reform authority. The second sheet listed onward disbursal of the same grain to three private lessees at winter premium, each line carrying a receipt number that belonged in market commerce books, not poor-relief ledgers.

Her mouth went dry. She read both sheets twice to ensure she had not transposed one number in haste. She had not. Receipt 17C on the attestation matched a market tally code she had seen once on a reeve’s complaint, then dismissed for lack of source. Here it sat under seal with Quill’s sign.

If she entered this in the official abstract tonight, Quill’s clerk would take the packet at once, claim receiver custody, and perhaps alter whichever leaf made him vulnerable before any open hearing saw it. If she concealed it and was found with it, she could be charged with withholding crown documents, which in seizure season could travel from felony to noose without lingering. The rule line from the chapter table beat at the back of her skull: no page leaves scriptorium without docketed control.

She held still, hearing blood in her ears and Bate’s thumb drumming wood at the door. A tremor started in her left hand; she pressed that hand flat to the table until it obeyed. Under her breath she named the choices as if dictating a charge: surrender and lose the proof, or hide and risk the rope. No folio tally marked the packet fold, only the seal line and the order for receiver hand.

She looked at the hourglass. The last sand clung in the throat. She still had to complete the required boxes, sign close, and submit copies for count. She tucked the two extracted sheets inside her left cuff, beneath the wool binding at the wrist where a quick pat would find only cloth. Then she slid the larger packet back into the rush cover and bound the quire with plain cord so it resembled any damaged residual awaiting catalog.

Bate stepped into the doorway and pointed at the glass. “Done. Pens down.”

She lifted her quill, set it deliberately across the inkhorn, and turned the receiver abstract toward him. “Main boxes complete. Margin notes include infirmary and parish queue. I require one extra line in source log for flood residual quire with sealed supplement, condition damp, chain uncertain.” She watched his face as she said sealed supplement. If he flinched, she would know he had been told to look for exactly this.

He frowned at the board. “No extra lines. You logged five sources. We close five sources.”

“Then you sign refusal in witness column,” she said. “I will mark that source six was denied entry by guard order.”

His jaw set. Men like Bate did not fear sin; they feared their names attached to the wrong line. He held out his charcoal. “Write.”

She entered source six in tight script: Residual quire, rush stitch, attached supplementary packet under dual seal, retrieved from north wall chest panel at second quarter hour. She left out Quill’s initials. She wanted the existence recorded before a name pinned blame. Bate squinted, scratched his mark beside hers, and jerked his chin toward the trunk.

She stacked the volumes for return in order of request list, leaving the rush quire fourth where it would pass under less attention than first or last. When Bate lifted it, the hidden packet inside shifted and bumped the board with a soft, wet-leather thud. He glanced back at her.

“Stone in that one?” he asked.

“Damp boards,” she said. “Flood rolls drink water and keep it.”

He accepted the answer because he wanted the hour ended. He locked the trunk, pressed Quill’s wax, and held out his palm for her blank leaves. She gave him the counted stack minus the two sheets in her cuff and kept her breathing even while he thumbed through. He found no missing number. She had copied two blank folios earlier and left them uncited, an old scribe’s trick she had never used to hide proof.

The corridor bell from chapel struck the quarter after compline close. Far off, someone coughed in a long, tearing fit that might have been Stephen or any winter lung in the house. Bate tied the trunk key to his cord and opened the inner door for her to leave. She gathered her board, her inkhorn, and the abstract, then set the warped chest panel flush with one push of her shoulder. A hollow knock came from behind the wood, then silence. There was space deeper in that wall than the chest should allow. She kept her face blank and stepped into the corridor.

Quill waited at the outer table with his chief clerk and a lantern that made his signet flash each time he turned a page. He looked rested. Men who ordered other hands to work by the hour often did.

“Mistress Marris,” he said, taking the abstract. “Any irregularity tonight beyond yesterday’s resewn leaf?”

“Residual flood material recovered from north chest, now logged,” she said. “Transfer timings continue to conflict with tide and road condition for three recent lines. I entered discrepancy marks for witness verification.”

He scanned the form, eyes moving quickly, then slowed at the source log addition. “Supplementary packet under dual seal,” he read aloud, almost conversational. “A useful discovery. We will inspect that with proper witnesses tomorrow.” He looked up at her and smiled in a manner that made warmth sound like threat. “You see why discipline rewards everyone. Had this remained in damp, accusations would outrun facts by sunrise.”

Her left wrist itched where the two hidden sheets lay against skin. “Facts outrun us either way when they vanish into locked desks,” she said.

“Closed desks preserve facts from frightened hands.” He set the abstract aside and held out her counted blanks. “You have done well tonight. Continue in this temper and no one need suffer unnecessary scandal.”

She took the leaves and inclined her head because refusal would accomplish nothing in open corridor. Bate locked the archive door behind her and passed the key ring to Quill. Metal met metal with the same clear sound as the night before, except now she could hear another sound beneath it, like the waterline sucking at reed roots before flood reverses. Quill turned to his clerk. “At first bell, schedule inspection of residual quire and supplement. Add sworn witness.”

“Yes, master,” the clerk said.

Elin walked away before she could hear more, counting steps to keep her pace steady: twelve to cloister turn, eight to infirmary door, fifteen to scriptorium stair. At the infirmary she stopped long enough to leave linen for Peter Carter and hear that the child’s fever had not broken. She entered the note in her pocket docket by lantern, then crossed to the scriptorium where her desk stood cold and ink-stained under a single candle.

She shut the door, set the board down, and unwrapped her wrist binding. The two hidden sheets slid into her palm, thin, damp at one edge from her skin, and more dangerous than any knife in the priory. She read the signatures once more to ensure memory and paper agreed. They did, with the same clean loops on each Q and the same market receipt numbers that had no lawful place in alms transfer.

She should have placed them in the sealed evidence chest and written a full disclosure line before sleep. She should have gone to Anselm at once and accepted whatever legal ruin followed from admitting concealment within the same hour. She stood at the desk with both sheets in hand and could not yet make either move, because once the pages entered ordinary chain they would pass through Quill’s ring before reaching any hearing that might stand against him.

A board creaked in the cloister outside. Another step followed, lighter than Bate’s, then another, then stillness directly beyond the scriptorium door. Elin folded the sheets together, slid them up the wide sleeve of her gown against the inner seam, and reached for her docket knife as the footsteps stopped and did not pass.
