I cinched the shoulder restraints on the maintenance skiff with fingers that already felt overused, though station morning had barely cleared the ring clocks. The harness webbing was older than the current mission patch on the bulkhead and polished smooth where too many gloved hands had dragged it into place. Outside the forward blister, Enceladus filled half the view in hard white curvature, the south-polar fractures braided in blue shadow. The plume itself was not a column from here so much as a density problem, a vertical thinning and thickening against black.

Linh floated in beside me and snapped her own restraints shut with less ceremony than the hardware deserved. She had a service cap in one hand and a sample cassette rack clipped against her thigh. The motion made the tools at her belt knock softly together. In the skiff's cramped cabin every sound had edges. Fan whine. Avionics tick. The faint intermittent tap from a valve that needed a maintenance note no one would write until it failed loudly enough to justify attention.

"Say again why command thinks this is safer than remote intake," she said.

"Command does not think this is safer than remote intake." I reached past the console and pulled the preflight checklist into primary view. "Command thinks this is a maintenance calibration pass attached to a particulate sensor fault report. Which is why the request was approved."

Her mouth moved toward something sharper, then stopped. "You falsified the request?"

"No. Bank C's near-field particulate sensor is reading low during high-density plume edge transit. That is a real fault report." I toggled the diagnostic fields one by one. Attitude jets green. Intake shutters responsive. Suit pressure nominal. Return lock handshake confirmed. "I omitted the part where we intend to learn something useful."

"Much more reassuring."

What I had omitted from the request packet mattered almost as much as what I had included. Serran had answered my blank preliminary classification field at 05:20 with a direct call to my berth, then a second to the lab when I did not pick up the first in time. He stated that until anomaly status was clarified he wanted all additional work kept inside the approved validation queue. Archive sweeps. Acoustic correlation. Modeled responses. Remote sampling only. By the end of the call I understood that "remote only" was not principally about safety. Remote collection would preserve ambiguity.

The skiff's guidance panel lit to full operation. I keyed through the authorization layers using the maintenance envelope Pavel had signed last week for an unrelated sensor swap. It still covered short-range departures from ring C if the craft was listed under instrumentation support.

Linh watched the authorization go green. "If this turns into a compliance hearing, I want it recorded that I recognized the paperwork smell immediately."

"Noted."

"Also recorded that I still came."

"Also noted."

She pulled the service cap over the back of her cropped hair and leaned forward to check the cassette rack again. We had loaded six cryo-trap filters, three mineral microgrids, and a paired electrostatic collector designed to separate heavier particulate from volatile plume fraction at close range. The station usually sampled from safer stand-off distances where the plume had already diffused. If the harmonic anomaly was affecting how matter traveled through the vent network, the signal would be strongest where the plume was still stratified by source and stress history.

That was the hypothesis.

I released the docking clamps. The skiff shuddered once as the magnetic latches disengaged, then drifted free of ring C with a slight yaw that the attitude software corrected half a second later. *Anselm* slid across the canopy, all truss and pressure hull and attached utility growths. Beyond it Saturn hung flattened and impossible, the rings angled thin enough from here to look like a ruled line drawn through black.

I pushed a burst through starboard aft and rotated us nose-down toward the plume shadow. Close work near Enceladus always made scale behave badly. The moon below looked serene from orbit until you brought up the overlays: fracture maps, shell stress predictions, altitude tolerances, particle hazard bands.

"Telemetry relay is live," Linh said. She had already partitioned the outgoing stream, one feed to maintenance archives as required, a second local mirror to my tablet through a private science buffer. "If someone in command gets curious, they'll see a fault-check pass skimming the acceptable edge of bad judgment."

"That description is accurate enough."

"You're calmer when you're breaking procedure for a reason than when you're following it."

I adjusted the descent vector by 0.3 degrees to account for a cross-current in the predicted particulate field. "That may be an observational bias."

"I've seen you in safety reviews. You get the look of someone trying to stop a machine with grammar."

I almost answered, then let it go. The skiff had entered the outer vapor fringe and the hull sensors began to register impacts too small to hear and too numerous to count individually. The instrument panel translated them into density gradients and velocity scatter. Ahead of us the plume was still mostly darkness textured by motion, a rising column of water vapor, ice grains, salts, silicates, organics, and unknowns accelerated through fractures no human hand had ever seen directly.

I tightened my grip on the control bars and felt the tendons in my wrist answer. Sleep deprivation sharpened some parts of me and coarsened others. People's motives blurred first.

"Range to target sampling lane?" I asked.

"Nineteen kilometers and dropping. Bank C fault window in three minutes." Linh zoomed the forward scatter plot until the densest flow bands stood apart. "If the low-reading sensor is going to embarrass itself, this is where it does it."

That part was true enough to survive an audit. Bank C's near-field unit consistently undercounted larger grains when the plume density rose too quickly across the inlet. Maintenance wanted an in situ calibration. I wanted a dense particulate profile paired with live harmonic timing and local stress-state data from the orbital model.

The first warning tone sounded as we crossed into the higher-velocity scatter region. Yellow, not red. Particulate flux above nominal maintenance envelope. I narrowed the inlet shutters by four percent to protect the collectors without collapsing throughput. The skiff responded with a soft judder as micro-impacts ticked along the bow shield.

Enceladus's limb curved under us, too bright to look at directly for long. The nearest tiger stripe flashed with specular glare where the sun caught fresh ejecta. Every model we had built of the vent system remained inference stacked on inference. Stress transfer through ice. Fracture permeability. Brine transport. Phase change. Load response.

Linh's display tightened to the sample stream. "Collector one is live. Two is live. Cryo-traps stable."

"Bring the mineral grids online in staggered sequence. I want time separation across the densest band."

"Already doing it."

Of course she was. The skiff dipped deeper into the plume shadow and the cabin light changed as reflected brightness from the moon fell off. At this altitude the plume ceased to look diffuse and began to show structure. The forward lidar returned filamented density lanes, transient braids where particulate-rich outflow folded through thinner vapor, then sheared apart again under local turbulence and orbital motion. Nothing mystical in it. Fluid dynamics under harsh conditions. But structure at close range always did something to the body before the mind finished naming it. My breathing had gone shallower.

"Harmonic lock?" I asked.

Linh thumbed the sensor crossfeed to my panel. "Weak but present. Same nested interval, riding below the usual noise shelf. You want me to tell you that is comforting?"

"No."

"Good. Because it isn't."

The interval markers advanced along the lower edge of the display, pale amber against black. Even after the icequake shift from the day before, my eyes kept wanting to interpret the pulses as abstract timing rather than event. That habit belonged to the job. Time-series first. Meaning later. Yet here, in the particulate lanes themselves, the pulses were not merely overlaid on transport. They seemed to coincide with changes in grain density sharp enough to notice in real time. Higher count on the coarse channel. Then lower. Then a redistribution between lanes that should have smoothed more gradually if all we were seeing was bulk turbulence.

I did not say that aloud. Patterns seen too early become suggestions.

The second warning tone came in red.

Linh's head snapped toward the nav pane. "Cross-shear spike. We just picked up lateral velocity from sector south-six."

I saw it a moment later in the scatter model, a fast-moving density ridge cutting across our planned exit vector. Not an icequake. More likely a vent-output fluctuation compounded by local turbulence. It did not need a dramatic cause to damage a maintenance craft.

"Vector solution?" I asked.

"Climb now and lose the dense band, or yaw thirty degrees port and ride the edge."

I looked once at the projected flux map, once at the warning stack, once at the timing markers still stepping across the harmonic pane. A close-pass sortie is always descent math in reverse. Not how to fall, but how much risk you can convert into resolution before the system calls the debt.

"Port yaw," I said. "Three-second burst, then trim manually."

Linh did not argue. She armed the auxiliary collectors instead, which was her way of objecting later. I pulsed the thrusters. The skiff slewed left and the cabin vibration climbed instantly, not dangerous yet, but high enough that I felt it in my molars. The density ridge crossed under us as a ghost on the forward display, particulate concentration flashing bright enough to saturate the coarse channel for half a second. Impacts rattled across the bow shield in a rapid dry run.

"Shutters compensating," Linh said. "Collector two holding. Grid three just took a load spike."

"How bad?"

"Interesting."

Interesting bad I could work with. I held the trim manually while the software tried to overcorrect around my inputs. The skiff rode the edge of the ridge, nose shivering as the local plume geometry changed around us. For seven seconds we sat inside the worst of it. Seven seconds was enough for the mineral grids to log a particulate fraction three times denser than stand-off collection ever gave us and enough for bank C's near-field unit to reveal, conclusively, that the maintenance fault report had not exaggerated.

Linh let out a short breath that might have been a laugh in a less hostile environment. "Congratulations. Your lie is now better documented."

"Adjust the report wording later."

Then the intake shutter on collector one stuck at seventy-two percent open.

The alert flashed amber then red as actuator response lagged. If it froze there during exit, larger grains could pit the internal mesh badly enough to ruin the sample and the hardware together. I felt the old professional narrowing take hold, the one that strips fear and leaves only ordered verbs.

"Route shutter control to manual bus."

"Already moved. It isn't the command path."

"Mechanical bind?"

"Looks like particulate icing at the hinge track." She zoomed the actuator current. "The flux spike packed the outer lip. If we cycle it hard we may shear the guide."

I checked our relative position. Still in the dense lane. Still with the cross-shear slipping aft. We could climb now and preserve the craft, but the collector would either stay compromised or take the load on exit. We needed ten more seconds for the final grid interval to complete.

"Can you pulse the de-ice filament locally?" I asked.

"On a maintenance skiff? What luxury do you think we're flying?"

"Then backfeed heater current through the diagnostics branch."

Linh looked over at me sharply. "That will trip maintenance logs."

"Yes."

"And maybe cook the actuator seal."

"For two seconds it won't."

She was already rerouting power. "You always sound most persuasive when you're asking equipment to forgive you."

The current spike hit. The shutter trembled on the status readout, stalled, then jerked inward another twelve percent. Not enough. Another second and the actuator temperature would pass acceptable limits.

"Again," I said.

"You get one more."

The second pulse did it. The shutter slid to forty percent open and held. We had the sample. More importantly, the instrument stream across the last nine seconds showed something I could not unsee even while managing the failure stack. The coarse mineral fraction had not merely risen with density. It had oscillated in step with the harmonic interval, enriched at one phase and depleted at the next, as if particulate transport itself were being sorted within the flow.

I climbed us out before the thought hardened into language. The skiff punched upward through thinning vapor, impacts easing from rattle to sporadic taps to nothing. Black opened ahead again. The warning tones dropped away one by one until only the sticky shutter fault remained blinking in the margin like a bureaucratic witness.

For several seconds neither of us spoke. My shoulders had gone rigid under the harness.

"That was the last time I agree to a maintenance errand with you before breakfast," Linh said.

"Noted."

"I mean it."

She stared at her board, breathing a little too fast, then forced the rate down by deliberate count. "You saw it."

I kept the skiff on return vector and did not pretend to misunderstand. "I saw a correlation."

"You saw grain sorting change on the pulse."

I looked at the stream again. Harmonic markers. Coarse fraction rise. Harmonic trough. Coarse fraction fall. Repeat. Then during the cross-shear disturbance, the relation loosened, shifted, and resumed in the new local density lane without randomizing. My chest had the unpleasant lightness it gets when the evidence begins arriving faster than the categories built to contain it.

"Yes," I said. "I saw it."

She leaned back against the seat shell and shut her eyes for two breaths. "Good. I needed to know I wasn't building religion out of telemetry."

"This is not religion."

"I know. That's why it's worse."

We docked under maintenance priority because the sticky shutter qualified as actual hardware degradation now. Two techs met us at the lock, irritated until the fault logs populated their tablets, then professionally aggrieved. I signed the transfer on the skiff with accurate omissions intact and carried the secured sample cassettes myself to plume analytics before anyone in command could notice what the maintenance pass had brought back.

By the time the first particulate lattice resolved on the lab table, my body had discovered what the sortie had cost it. The muscles between my shoulders had tightened into wire. A low headache sat behind my left eye where the skiff vibration had been worst. None of that altered the image growing across the analysis screen.

Linh stood opposite me at the microgrid station with her sleeves shoved up and a strip of sealant residue still stuck near one wrist from helping maintenance pull the shutter assembly. Pavel was not here. I had sent him the real maintenance fault report and the parts summary only. If the particulate data held, he would have to see it soon, and I wanted one uncontaminated read before his caution or my resentment entered the room.

The first grid image came in as a false-color lattice map of trapped mineral grains from the dense-band interval. Most plume particulate at this scale looks like violence frozen small: irregular ice inclusions, salt shards, silicate fines, fractured aggregates built by pressure, chemistry, ascent, and subsequent damage. These grains were not orderly in any naive sense. But order does not need neatness. It needs relation.

I tagged the capture windows against the harmonic timestamps from the sortie. Grid one, early interval. Grid two, mid-band. Grid three, cross-shear onset. Grid four, post-shift recovery. Linh ran an automated morphology classifier, then killed the smoothing before it finished.

"No black-box help," she said.

"Agreed."

We worked manually. Length-to-width distributions. Branching frequency in the silicate chains. Salt crystal orientation clusters. Trapped organic film prevalence. Charge retention variance across the electrostatic collector. The labor steadied me. Compare, reject, align, rerun.

By 11:40 station time the broad picture had emerged. The particulate population shifted with the harmonic phases in ways bulk turbulence alone should not produce. During high-amplitude intervals, the mineral grains showed increased lattice regularity in the silicate fraction and a higher incidence of brine-film retention around microfractured cores. During the trough phases, the same sample stream broadened into more chaotic aggregate shapes and lower retained film. Not absolute alternation. Statistical drift with teeth.

Linh projected the phase-separated lattices onto the wall. "Tell me geology is just being elegant again."

I enlarged one cluster until the grain boundaries pixelated. "Geology is often elegant."

"Not my question."

No, it wasn't. The issue was not whether mineral transport could vary under changing flow conditions. Of course it could. The issue was whether the variation was merely downstream of plume mechanics or whether the same harmonic relation we had tracked in timing and stress response was now appearing in what the plume carried and how it carried it.

I pulled the orbital stress model from the previous pass, then aligned the local particulate shifts against both the harmonic markers and the predicted load state in the subsurface coupling zone. The fit was ugly at first because the sortie cross-shear had added local turbulence the broad model could not resolve. I corrected for it using the skiff's own motion record and intake orientation. Better. Not perfect. Better enough.

Linh crossed her arms, then uncrossed them immediately as if stillness cost too much. "Say it carefully so I don't have to hear Serran saying it badly first."

I kept my eyes on the fit residuals while I answered. "The harmonic intervals correlate with changes in particulate morphology and mineral retention during near-field transport."

"And the changes are phase-dependent."

I nodded.

She looked back at the wall. "Then command doesn't get to call this a timing oddity anymore."

Not quite. Harder to ignore can still be buried if the institution has time to classify and fragment. But she was right about the practical effect. If the harmonic relation was embedded in particulate transport, then this was no longer a case command could frame as a merely interesting timing anomaly. Something below the shell was imposing patterned constraints on what reached space.

I brought up the electrostatic collector data last because I distrusted how much I wanted it. Across the paired collector plates a second-order periodicity appeared in the denser lane, synchronized with the harmonic pulses and strongest in the same intervals where the lattice regularity tightened. The effect was small until I normalized for the cross-shear event. Then it remained.

Linh read the trace over my shoulder. "That's not random charging."

"No."

"And it isn't just the skiff shaking the collector differently?"

"I modeled the attitude contribution. It doesn't account for the phase lock."

The lab prompt chimed once, sharp enough to cut through the fan noise. A command-priority header opened itself in the upper corner of the wall display before I could stop it.

**COMMAND REVIEW REQUEST: upload full sortie telemetry and provisional findings to anomaly oversight queue within fifteen minutes. Classification hold pending.**

Linh read it and went still.

"There it is," she said quietly. "Ambiguity with a deadline."

I did not answer. My mouth had gone dry. Fifteen minutes was long enough to seize the data and short enough to keep us from building a case around it first.

I looked from the prompt to the phased lattice maps, then to the electrostatic trace, then back to the capture windows from the dense lane where the grains had tightened and loosened in time with the pulse. My right hand was still resting on the bench. I had not noticed how hard I was pressing until the heel of it began to ache. When I lifted it, a clean crescent of blanched skin marked where the edge had cut into my palm.

My pulse had moved up into my throat. Not fear exactly. Not only fear. The same pressure I had felt in the skiff when the plume stopped behaving like background matter and started behaving like constrained report. Here it was again, only slower and harder to dismiss. The room smelled faintly of hot dust from the overworked lattice projector and solvent from the shutter sealant on Linh's wrist. Ordinary station air. Ordinary tools. My next breath still came shallow.

If the relation had held through pressure, fracture, ascent, particulate suspension, charge separation, and export into vacuum, then the plume was not merely reporting conditions below. It was carrying arrangement under constraint. Not a message in any human sense I could defend yet. Not intention I could certify. But not accident either, not in the form command would prefer. Something in the ocean was using every available physical route to persist through the ice.

Linh did not push me to name it this time. She was watching my face instead, as if waiting to see whether I would retreat into procedure or step through it.

I opened the command request, split the queue window from the local analysis feed, and felt my hand hesitate over the upload authorization. The hesitation was small. Measurable. Enough to tell the truth.
