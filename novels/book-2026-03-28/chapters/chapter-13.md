The certification package arrived at 07:18 station time with command priority and a checksum chain three levels deeper than the ordinary readiness forms. I opened it at the restricted analytics terminal they had assigned me after the review, a narrow station bolted against the inner wall of plume analytics where the camera angle saw my hands, the screen, and most of the door. The room was colder than it needed to be. Not environmental drift. Command economy. Keep people alert, keep processors cool, keep bodies slightly uncomfortable so no one mistakes a controlled space for privacy. On the main wall the live plume map had been replaced by a reduced feed without raw harmonic overlays. Beneath the ice, whatever was there still moved inside the data. The display simply no longer admitted it.

The form expanded in quiet panes. Mission identifier. Harvest window. Array status. Legal review reference. Then the authorization block opened, and my hand stopped over the pad.

`RESPONSIBLE SCIENTIFIC OFFICER ATTESTATION`

Below it sat the same architecture I had signed on *Khepri* nine years earlier, though the consortium had changed fonts and added cleaner spacing as if typography could launder consequence. Attestation of reviewed evidence. Attestation of known uncertainties. Attestation that no currently ratified hazard or protected-biology trigger required suspension of operational readiness. A final field for reservations. A signature pad beneath.

My thumb hovered three centimeters above the glass. The tendon in my wrist tightened first. Then the old memory came in by procedure rather than image. Elias leaning over my shoulder in the prefabricated survey bay, tapping the uncertainty field with one grease-dark fingernail.

If a line can fail under load, he had said, do not let them make you sign as if load were theoretical.

I had signed anyway. Not because I believed the numbers were good. Because the caveat language looked adequate, the schedule was already tight, the structural drift was still within modeled tolerance if the weld distortion did not propagate, and the mission lead wanted a yes that could survive audit. Three days later the ice-interface gantry failed in a cascade no one called unforeseeable once the dead were counted.

The terminal chimed once. New instruction appended.

`Certification response required within 00:40:00 to maintain extraction readiness pathway.`

The clock started in the corner. I read the fields once without touching anything, then again, slower. The final activation queue from the observation-ring board had become a completed entry. Array warm state achieved. Guidance lock stable. Skim corridor prediction inside tolerance. Next threshold was not hardware readiness anymore. It was operational certification, the narrow bureaucratic throat through which all the station's previous arguments now had to pass.

The hatch opened behind me. I knew the cadence before I turned. Director Serran did not hurry when he wanted the room to absorb his arrival as part of its governing structure.

He came in alone, which was more forceful than bringing security would have been. Dark command jacket, collar seal in place, tablet already awake in his left hand. He took in the terminal, the timer, my unmoved hand, and the fact that I had not stood.

"Doctor Vale."

"Director."

He stopped beside the second station rather than across from me. Not intimate. Not adversarial in the obvious way. An angle designed to suggest we were jointly examining a problem with unfortunate constraints instead of reenacting the same moral argument in different software.

"This package is narrower than the one you seem to be bracing against," he said. "Read the reservations section."

"I have."

"Then read it as written, not as memory."

I looked at the block again because refusing to do so would only theatrically flatter him. The reservations field had been expanded beyond normal certification length. It allowed attachment of analytic concerns, continued-review requests, and a recommendation for post-skim restrictions pending annex adjudication. A compromise built in advance. My shoulder line shifted toward the keyboard before I told it not to.

Serran touched his tablet and mirrored his version to the side screen. "You are not being asked to retract your finding. You are being asked to certify that current ratified thresholds for mandatory suspension remain unmet. That is true. You may append any reservation you can defend. You may request expanded review after skim acquisition. You may remain lead scientific officer on the anomaly under restricted authority."

"After skim acquisition."

"Yes."

"After the first intrusive pass."

"After a limited material pass conducted inside existing environmental tolerances."

The sentence made my thumb dip a fraction toward the glass before I caught it. Limited. Material. Tolerances. Committee-safe words, all of them. I had written versions of that sentence before. That was what made it dangerous.

"You want me to certify the distinction between harm and unratified harm."

"I want you to certify the distinction between evidence and policy trigger."

"That is the same sentence with a different insurance team behind it."

He set his tablet down on the empty station with careful fingers. He always became more precise physically when his argument needed to appear cleaner than it was. "No. It is the distinction that keeps stations functioning when evidence arrives ahead of law. The review remains open. You have recorded your reservations. I am offering you a route to keep this inside process rather than turning yourself into a compliance casualty before the real decision even arrives."

Compliance casualty. The phrase was dry enough to pass for administrative concern. I noticed my left molars had already locked against each other. I eased them apart and tasted metal where I had bitten the inside of my cheek without meaning to.

"The real decision," I said, "is whether we begin before the review closes."

"The real decision includes Earth."

"Earth is not on the form."

"Earth is in every form on this station."

That would have sounded grandiose in another mouth. In his it landed as operational accounting. Millions of lives abstracted into throughput models, then pressed down through signatures until the pressure became personal and deniable. I brought up the attachments in the certification package. Mortality projection appendix. Procurement impact statement. Legal concurrence memo still marked provisional. My own finding quoted once, narrowed again to possible intelligence, then cross-referenced to threshold ambiguity. They had built the package exactly as they should have if the goal was to make refusal look like a private failure to cooperate with complexity.

"You included procurement in a scientific attestation."

"I included mission consequences."

"You mean consequences to recognized humans."

His face changed by almost nothing. Only the small compression at the corner of his mouth that appeared when he decided bluntness would cost less than persuasion. "Yes."

The honesty of it moved through me like cold fluid in a line. Not because it surprised me. Because the package on the screen had been designed to let that preference hide behind mixed authorities. Hearing him state it cleanly reduced the available fictions.

I opened the reservation field and watched the cursor blink. The pad glowed at the bottom of the form, blank and patient. If I signed with a narrowed warning, I kept access. I kept the feeds they had not yet hidden. I kept a chair at the table when Nadir certified what I would not. My wrist knew that before the rest of me did. The muscles along my forearm pulled tight, already rehearsing the downward turn of a signature I had no right to give.

Potential distributed adaptive process unresolved under inadequate annex criteria. Recommend immediate suspension pending external scientific review.

I did not type it. Not yet. The form's timer kept counting. Serran read over my shoulder without pretending not to.

"That recommendation will be rejected on scope," he said.

"So will any honest sentence."

"Not any. The one you insist on because it converts uncertainty into veto."

I turned in the chair enough to face him. The motion pulled a line of pain across my lower back from too many hours at stations built for shorter bodies and better sleep. "No. It converts uncertainty into restraint."

The timer clicked down another minute. On the side screen his mirrored package highlighted the reservation field in procedural blue, as if the software itself were trying to guide my hand toward the acceptable compromise.

"Listen carefully," he said. "If you sign with reservations, three things remain true. The skim proceeds. The review continues. You stay inside the chain that can still influence what happens next. If you refuse, the skim still proceeds, the review continues without you, and command gains no reason to distinguish principled disagreement from procedural noncompliance."

There it was. The temptation, properly engineered. Sign and keep access. Sign and stay proximate to the data. Sign and preserve the ability to fight later with cleaner standing. Sign and avoid becoming the story instead of the finding. Every part of it had structural merit. That was why it worked.

I scrolled farther into the form until the attestation language reappeared in full.

I certify that all currently available evidence known to this office has been reviewed in good faith and that no ratified environmental, biological, or contact threshold presently compels suspension of scheduled extraction readiness and associated first-pass operations.

Good faith. Presently. Ratified. Compels. The sentence had been machined so that each qualifier carried the weight the noun could not survive alone. I had written enough reports to admire the workmanship even while recognizing what it was for. My thumb drifted toward the pad again. I pulled it back and flattened my hand on my own knee until the tendons stopped jumping.

The hatch cracked open. Sergeant Solis leaned in only far enough to satisfy protocol. "Director. Command is asking whether the attestation will clear inside the current queue."

Serran did not look away from me. "We are still in discussion."

Solis's gaze flicked to the countdown in the corner and then to my untouched signature block. Something in her expression settled, not approval, more like recognition of the geometry. "Understood," she said, and left us to it.

I checked the timer. 00:28:14.

"What happens at zero," I asked, "if I do not sign."

"The queue escalates."

"To whom."

"Compliance review and deputy certification authority."

"Meaning Haleh Nadir."

"Meaning the station does not allow single-point analytic obstruction."

"That is a flattering phrase for replacement."

"It is a necessary one."

I looked back at the form. The signature pad waited with the indifference of polished hardware. The room's environmental fans moved cold air across the back of my neck. Behind the reduced display wall the live data stream continued to populate hidden layers I no longer had authority to see without request. Somewhere below us the array held ready over Enceladus, all that hardware poised inside tolerances written for inert matter.

"If I sign," I said, "and the first pass perturbs the field before external review, your record will show that the lead analyst with the strongest evidence package cleared the operation."

"With reservations."

"Your summary bars will not lead with the reservations."

He took one slow breath through his nose. "Doctor Vale, institutions cannot operate at the pace of every grave inference."

"Then they should not be allowed near things they can injure before they can name."

His eyes held mine. "That is not a station policy argument. It is a civilization argument."

"Yes."

We stayed there a moment, both still enough that I could hear the faint processor whine inside the restricted terminal. He wanted me to recognize scale as a reason to yield. Instead it clarified what he was asking the form to do. Convert a civilization argument back into local compliance by giving one exhausted analyst a reservations field and a clock.

I turned to the keyboard and began typing.

Current evidence supports unresolved risk to a probable distributed adaptive process in the plume-coupled ocean system. Existing annex criteria are structurally inadequate to assess first-pass harm under these conditions. I cannot attest that proceeding now is scientifically responsible.

Serran read it in silence. I could feel him measuring where the sentence might still be sanded down into usefulness.

"If you attach that," he said, "command will reject the certification and proceed to deputy review."

"I know."

"You will lose the only remaining lane in which your judgment still carries formal operational weight."

The cursor blinked at the end of scientifically responsible. One edit and I could make it survivable. Potential instead of probable. Recommend review instead of cannot attest. I felt the trained reflex in my hand, the old committee instinct to leave behind a sentence that could pass upward without breaking anyone's schedule. My breath shortened. The cold air made the inside of my nose sting. The pad sat below the text, warm and waiting.

"No," I said. "I will stop lending it to yours."

The words entered the room before I had time to improve them. They were blunt enough to sound almost unlike me, which meant they were probably the most accurate thing I had said all morning.

His expression went very still. Then, unexpectedly, some of the pressure came out of it. Not softness. A different kind of calculation abandoned. "You think refusal preserves you from complicity."

"No."

"Then what exactly do you think it preserves."

I looked at the signature pad, at my own hand reflected faintly in the black glass. The hand was steady now. That was new. "The record."

He picked up his tablet. "Records are written by the systems that survive the event."

"Only if everyone signs."

The timer reached 00:19:02. He watched it for two seconds, then keyed something on his tablet. My terminal chimed.

`Directive note appended by command: Certification with reservations remains available. Failure to attest within allotted interval may trigger immediate compliance inquiry under operational obstruction code.`

"Last offer," he said. "Sign with your language narrowed to unresolved interpretive concern. Keep the probable language in review. Keep your access. Keep your chance to influence the next decision."

I reread what I had typed. There was room to sand it down. Replace probable with potential. Replace cannot attest with recommend reconsideration. Give the station something it could metabolize. The urge was physical. I could feel it in the tendons of my forearm, in the small forward tilt of my body toward the signature pad, in the trained instinct to leave a sentence that survives committee rather than a sentence that tells the truth at first contact with power.

Elias had once told me that steel fatigue rarely announces itself at the point of break. It starts earlier, in the repeated acceptance of loads the structure was never designed to carry, until the final failure looks sudden only to people who ignored the cycles.

I deleted one word and retyped the same word. Not because the sentence needed correction. Because my hand needed to prove to my own body that I was still the one moving it.

Serran saw that too.

"Mara."

He had not used my first name in command space before. It did not sound intimate. It sounded expensive. "If you do this, they will formalize it as obstruction. They will remove your authority before the skim even completes. Nadir will certify. The operation will continue. The only immediate result will be the collapse of your standing."

My throat tightened once. I waited it out. "Then the form is not asking for judgment. It is asking for endorsement."

"It is asking whether you understand the cost of refusing the only compromise available."

I touched the send-preparation field but not the signature pad. "I understand it better than you need me to."

The countdown dropped under fifteen minutes. His tablet signaled again. He checked it, jaw setting in that brief hard line I had started to recognize as command under schedule insult.

"Very well," he said. "If you will not clear the pathway, you will answer for the delay."

"I already am."

He turned and left without another word.

After the hatch sealed, the room seemed to gain volume and lose oxygen at the same time. I stayed in the chair, watching the cursor blink after my final sentence. The certification package remained open. Sign. Submit unsigned reservation. Request extension. Escalate for clarification. None of the verbs matched the situation exactly. The station still believed enough in forms to pretend choice could be discretized into buttons.

I opened the evidence attachments one more time. Raw return windows. Manual decompositions. The failed passive branches from Pavel's last model. My own narrowed privileges left me unable to duplicate them, but not yet unable to review them. I checked three values I already knew by memory, the way I had once recalculated *Khepri* load margins long after the collapse because arithmetic offered the illusion that repetition could alter history. The numbers held. The transformed interval still returned by relation, not decay. The geological alternative still failed the re-entry path. Below the ice, the thing we had not ratified still refused to become inert just because the forms required it.

At 00:07:31 the terminal shifted from blue countdown to amber warning.

`Pending certification pathway. Nonresponse will trigger compliance referral.`

I set my thumb on the glass pad at last, not to sign but to wake the identity field and let the system confirm I was present. The pad warmed under my skin, awaiting attestation. I left the reservation text in place above it like a fracture line no amount of smoothing could erase.

Then I took my hand away.

The last minute ran louder than the previous thirty-nine, though the room made no new sound. I heard my own breathing. I heard coolant move in the wall. I heard, or imagined I heard, a distant station-wide status chime from the command spine as some adjacent readiness task completed without me.

00:00:10

00:00:09

The system opened a final prompt.

`Attest now to preserve primary certification authority.`

I did not touch it.

At zero the form sealed itself with a flat administrative tone. The signature field grayed out. My reservation text remained visible for one beat, then shifted into read-only pending review. New headers populated faster than I could read them.

`PRIMARY CERTIFICATION LAPSED`

`DEPUTY AUTHORITY NOTIFIED`

`COMPLIANCE INQUIRY INITIATED: POSSIBLE OPERATIONAL OBSTRUCTION`

For a moment the room stayed perfectly still around the notice, as if the station wanted to make sure I understood that collapse could occur in software before it arrived anywhere else. Then the hatch lock changed state behind me with the same soft relay sound that had begun containment the day before.
