# Style Guide: The Bell of Ash Fen

## 1. Genre Profile
This novel is **Historical Fiction** (from `genres.md` profile 4). It fits because the central conflict is impossible outside 1539-1541 Dissolution England: Elin's choices are constrained by oath politics, priory seizure law, grain scarcity, and a religious world where institutional corruption and lived faith coexist. The period is not backdrop; it is the pressure system that makes silence, witness, and mercy equally costly.

## 2. Comp Authors
- **Hilary Mantel**: Contributes the model for rendering power as daily texture, so Elin's fear lives in inventories, signatures, and who is allowed to touch which records, not in theatrical villain speeches.
- **Geraldine Brooks**: Contributes emotionally precise interiority for a woman navigating institutional constraint, shaping Elin's conflict between communal care and personal guilt after disavowing her husband.
- **Patrick O'Brian**: Contributes technical authenticity as narrative pleasure, guiding how tithe barns, account rolls, marsh roads, and grain weights become story propulsion rather than decorative research.
- **Anthony Burgess**: Contributes period-conscious diction without parody, helping the prose feel historically situated while still readable to a modern audience.

## 3. POV and Narrative Distance
Third-person limited, **single-POV through Elin Marris only**. Narrative distance stays close enough for free-indirect shading of her judgments (ledger-minded, guilt-burdened, risk-calculating) but does not become confessional first person. No head-hopping, no omniscient historical asides, and no scenes where Rafe Quill's private motives are directly narrated; his intent is inferred through documents, behavior, and consequence.

## 4. Sentence Rhythm
Default rhythm is deliberate and tensile: medium-to-long syntactic lines with concrete nouns (tools, stores, measures, routes, offices), reflecting Elin's scribe discipline and the procedural drag of seizure-era bureaucracy. Under pressure (raids, confrontations, evidence exposure), rhythm contracts into short clauses and clipped beats with fewer qualifiers, marking the shift from record-keeping survival to immediate moral action. That transition signals moments when Elin stops managing damage and starts choosing witness.

## 5. Tone and Emotional Register
Primary tone is weighty, textured, and morally alert, with restrained irony about official language versus lived suffering. Chapter endings should leave the reader with tightening dread and costly hope at once: the sense that truth may repair the village, but only by increasing immediate danger to people Elin loves.

## 6. Protagonist Voice Signature
Elin's prose-mind moves in inventories: she notices sequence, custody, quantities, and omissions before she notices rhetoric. Vocabulary register is literate but practical, mixing ecclesial/legal terms (oath, alms, seizure, restitution, priory rule) with material specifics (grain damp, wax seals, reed-road timing, winter stores). Metaphor sources come from ledger practice, marsh hydraulics, and liturgical habit: accounts that will not balance, channels that backflood, bells that summon judgment and mercy together.

She does **not** state directly: "I betrayed my husband," "I am afraid," or "I need forgiveness." Instead she revises entries, over-specifies facts, measures food twice, tracks other people's risk, and frames feeling as procedural necessity. At maximum pressure, this control-framed voice cracks into bare, plain constructions with fewer abstractions; she names acts and consequences directly when she finally chooses public truth over protective silence.

## 7. Scene Break Policy
Maximum **2** scene breaks (`---`) per chapter; **1 is preferred**. A break is warranted only for:
- A real time jump that changes operational stakes (for example, from pre-dawn tallying to evening enforcement).
- A location-and-power shift that changes who controls risk (for example, priory archive to commissioner hearing room).
- Deliberate white space after an irreversible moral act (for example, disclosure of the account-roll fraud in public).

No breaks for minor movement, atmospheric reset, or to avoid writing transitions.

## 8. What This Novel Is Not
- Not a modernized resistance fantasy where Elin sounds like a 21st-century activist in Tudor costume.
- Not a cozy mystery puzzle-box where the grain fraud is entertainment detached from famine consequences.
- Not grimdark collapse fiction where corruption is total and grace is absent or naive.
- Not action-adventure swashbuckling that sidelines documents, oaths, and food logistics in favor of set-piece violence.
- Not generic AI-historical prose full of museum-tour exposition, archaic-thesaurus dialogue, or interchangeable "mud-and-candle" atmosphere.
