# World Bible: The Bell of Ash Fen

## 1. Physical World

### Geography and Climate
- **Region**: Eastern English fenland around Ash Fen Priory, with marsh villages connected by reed roads, levees, causeways, and seasonal footpaths.
- **Terrain Logic**: Land and water exchange control by tide, rain, and drainage condition. A path usable at dawn may be submerged by dusk.
- **Seasonal Pressure**: Main story runs from late October 1539 to February 1540. Cold, damp, and flood-prone winter conditions make transport, storage, and survival harder each week.
- **Key Locations**:
  - Ash Fen Priory: cloister, chapter house, scriptorium, archive, bell tower, lower granary.
  - Tithe barns: storage points for local grain obligations.
  - Reed-road market: private exchange node where scarcity pricing is visible.
  - Eel trench and abandoned weigh house: hidden logistics corridor and evidence cache.
  - Ely Road hearing site: regional legal checkpoint where records are tested.

### Technology and Material Systems
- **Technological Baseline**: 1539 Tudor-era hand tools and manuscript culture.
- **Records Technology**: Ledgers, account rolls, seals, signatures, copy books, lockboxes, waxed wraps, tally sticks.
- **Transport**: Horse carts on raised roads, small river/fen barges, hand-carried sacks, and tide-timed movement.
- **Food System**: Grain is measured, weighed, and stored physically; loss through damp, theft, spoilage, and diversion is common.
- **Communications**: Bells, messengers, rumor chains, public readouts, and posted writs.

### Systems in Place
- **Inventory/Custody System**:
  - Whoever controls keys, seals, and witness lists controls legal reality.
  - Duplicate ledgers can contradict official books; custody chain determines credibility.
- **Relief/Alms System**:
  - Priory stores are expected to support villages in scarcity.
  - Transfers can be reframed as legal compliance while functionally starving locals.
- **Enforcement System**:
  - Crown-appointed officers operate through local clerks, armed escorts, and procedural language.
  - Public order justifications can be manufactured ("riot prevention," "incitement" claims).

## 2. Social and Political Structures

### Power Ladder
1. **Crown policy and reform offices**: define legal authority for dissolution and seizure.
2. **Commissioners/receivers (e.g., Rafe Quill)**: execute seizure, convert sacred/charitable assets into state-managed property.
3. **Priory leadership (e.g., Prior Anselm)**: retains moral authority and local trust but shrinking legal power.
4. **Parish officers/reeves**: mediate local disputes, ration claims, testimony.
5. **Villagers (laborers, widows, families)**: absorb risk from policy decisions without procedural protection.

### Social Dynamics
- **Oath Politics**: Prior declarations and signatures define safety, loyalty, and exposure.
- **Gender Constraint**: A widowed lay scribe can access information but has limited public force; her influence comes through documents, witnesses, and moral credibility.
- **Class Leverage**: Officials and factors can turn logistical control (storage, route access, market timing) into coercive power.
- **Reputation Economy**: Informers, confessors, and protectors are all remembered; memory itself is social currency.

## 3. Rules and Limits

### No-Magic Rule
- There is no supernatural mechanism that resolves plot conflict.
- Faith matters through choices, conscience, ritual, and communal practice, not miracles that suspend consequences.

### Technological Limits
- No fast transport, instant communication, modern medicine, or forensic certainty.
- Documents are fragile, alterable, and vulnerable to damp/fire.
- Proof requires corroboration: matching tallies, physical stores, witness testimony, and legal venue.

### Institutional Limits
- Law can be weaponized by those with office, but not endlessly; records plus public witness can still constrain power.
- Priory cannot stop macro-level dissolution decree by moral argument alone.
- Private truth without public process cannot protect the hungry at scale.

### Moral/Thematic Limits
- Justice without process collapses into vendetta.
- Mercy without truth hardens into complicity.
- Safety strategies (silence, delay, false signatures) always carry downstream cost.

## 4. Cultural and Religious Elements

### Religious Life
- Liturgical calendar and offices (matins, compline, vespers, Candlemas) structure daily and seasonal time.
- Bell-ringing signals authority, assembly, mourning, and alarm.
- Confession, penance, and restitution are lived practices, not abstract doctrine.

### Cultural Practices
- Households track food by basket, measure, and week-to-thaw.
- Public reading and witnessed signatures are civic theater and legal mechanism together.
- Memory preservation (names kept or erased in ledgers) is both spiritual and political.

### Christian Worldview Expression
- Grace appears as costly protection of the undeserving and repair after wrongdoing.
- Moral clarity emerges through consequence: lies can preserve short-term order but amplify communal harm.
- Hope is practical and embodied: reopened stores, corrected names, and accountable custody.

## 5. Historical Timeline (Story-Relevant)

### Pre-Story
- **1534-1538 (background pressure)**: Oath and reform enforcement reshapes local loyalties and punishments.
- **Before Chapter 1**: Elin's husband is executed for refusing a forced oath; Elin survives by publicly disavowing him.
- **1538-1539**: Crown seizure practices intensify; local officers learn to profit through procedural control.

### Main Story Window
- **Late October 1539 (Ch. 1-2)**: Granary shortfall discovered; commissioners' approach announced; Elin assigned seizure inventory.
- **Early November 1539 (Ch. 3-7)**: Quill arrives, secures archives, and concealed transfer evidence emerges.
- **Mid to Late November 1539 (Ch. 8-10)**: Informer alliance forms; surveillance tightens; punitive arrests begin.
- **Early December 1539 (Ch. 11-14)**: Duplicate ledgers reveal network-level fraud; Elin is pressed to sign false inventory and does.
- **Mid-December 1539 (Ch. 15-19)**: Starvation consequences crest; confession and public witness; violent fallout; mercy test.
- **January 1540 (Ch. 20-21)**: Regional hearing yields interim writ freezing grain contracts; supervised redistribution begins.
- **February 1540 / Candlemas (Ch. 22)**: Priory closure advances; communal ledger custody established beyond institution.

### Post-Story Trajectory (Known)
- **By summer 1540**: Ash Fen Priory is dissolved despite local restitution gains.

## 6. Required World Details from Outline
- Great bell can summon outside normal liturgical order; doing so is a deliberate act of defiance.
- Archive control is materially represented by keys, lock replacement, and seal authority.
- Low-tide routes (eel trench) create temporary access to hidden logistics nodes.
- Fire can erase legal reality by destroying custody records; smoke damage matters to evidentiary integrity.
- Grain fraud is not random theft; it can be structured through contracts, private marks, and rerouted barges.
- Public proceedings hinge on witness visibility: who hears the reading, who holds the ledger, who can seize it.
- Institutional victory is partial: one fraud network can be constrained while dissolution still proceeds.
