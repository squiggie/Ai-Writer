## Premise
In 1539, as royal commissioners move through eastern England to dissolve monasteries, Elin Marris, a widowed lay scribe at Ash Fen Priory, is ordered to inventory relics and lands for seizure while famine creeps across the marsh villages that depend on the priory's granaries. When she uncovers a set of sealed account rolls proving that a celebrated reform official has been diverting church alms into private grain speculation, Elin must decide whether to expose the fraud and ignite violent reprisals, or keep silent and preserve a fragile peace built on theft; either choice may cost the lives of the people she has spent years quietly feeding.

## Protagonist
Name: Elin Marris
Role: Lay scribe and records keeper for Ash Fen Priory
Ghost/wound: Her husband was executed for refusing a forced oath, and she survived by publicly disavowing him
Lie: Survival requires silence, and truth only gets the faithful killed
Truth: Truth joined to courage and mercy can protect others even when it cannot protect her
Arc: She moves from private, fearful maintenance of order to costly public witness that restores justice to her village at personal risk

## Antagonist
Name: Master Rafe Quill
Function: Crown-appointed receiver whose legal authority and grain network make him the human face of institutional seizure and moral compromise

## Themes
1. Memory can be an act of resistance against sanctioned forgetting.
2. Justice without mercy becomes vengeance, and mercy without truth becomes complicity.
3. Faithfulness is measured less by safety than by what one is willing to preserve for others.

## Christian Worldview Integration
Christian belief appears through lived practice rather than speeches: shared prayer before dangerous choices, confession that leads to restitution, and acts of costly preservation for neighbors who cannot repay. Grace is shown when Elin extends protection to a former informer, breaking the cycle of retaliation, while moral clarity emerges through consequences: lies secure short-term safety but deepen communal ruin, and sacrificial truth-telling becomes the means by which both wronged and wrongdoer can begin repair.

## Setting
Fenland England during the Dissolution of the Monasteries (1539-1541): tidal marsh villages, winter roads, tithe barns, and a priory converting from sacred house to contested property. Social rules are defined by oath politics, parish allegiance, harvest scarcity, and crown law enforced through local intermediaries, so every personal decision carries legal, economic, and spiritual consequence.
