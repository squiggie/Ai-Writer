# Themes Map: The Bell of Ash Fen

## Theme 1: Memory as Resistance Against Sanctioned Forgetting
- First appears: **Chapter 2 (Ash on the Register)** when Elin confronts her husband's crossed-out oath line, and **Chapter 3 (The Receiver Arrives)** when archive custody shifts to Quill.
- Develops: **Chapters 4, 9, 11, 17, 21** through the sealed roll, replaced locks, duplicate ledgers, public reading of evidence, and restitution records restoring erased names.
- Resolves: **Chapter 22 (After the Bell)** when Elin records her husband's name without erasure and moves accounts into communal custody.
- Chapter map: **2, 3, 4, 9, 11, 17, 21, 22**.

## Theme 2: Justice Without Mercy vs Mercy Without Truth
- First appears: **Chapter 2 (Ash on the Register)** when rule-bound refusal of alms preserves procedure but harms neighbors.
- Develops: **Chapters 8, 12, 14, 15, 16** through Matthew's morally compromised offer, Quill's coercive bargain, Elin's false signature, lethal downstream consequences, and confession with a charge toward truthful mercy.
- Resolves: **Chapters 19-20** as Elin blocks mob vengeance, insists on due process, and gives full testimony (including her own wrongdoing) in formal hearing.
- Chapter map: **2, 8, 12, 14, 15, 16, 19, 20**.

## Theme 3: Faithfulness Measured by Preservation for Others, Not Safety
- First appears: **Chapter 1 (Bell Before Dawn)** with winter shortfall and Elin's imposed inventory duty; reinforced in **Chapter 2** by her survival-through-silence posture.
- Develops: **Chapters 5, 7, 11, 13, 14, 15, 17** as evidence mounts, village suffering sharpens stakes, Elin commits to public witness, then fails under duress, then turns back toward costly courage.
- Resolves: **Chapters 21-22** with reopened stores, restitution practice, and ongoing stewardship after institutional loss.
- Chapter map: **1, 2, 5, 7, 11, 13, 14, 15, 17, 21, 22**.

## Christian Worldview Moments by Chapter
- Grace:
  - **Chapter 8**: Elin accepts Matthew's help despite revulsion.
  - **Chapter 16**: Elin chooses to protect Matthew after confession.
  - **Chapter 19**: Elin publicly restrains retaliation and shields Matthew from lynching.
  - **Chapter 22**: Elin appoints Matthew as witness-clerk under supervision.
- Redemption:
  - **Chapter 16**: Confession leads to renewed moral agency.
  - **Chapter 19**: Wrongdoing is named alongside restitution.
  - **Chapter 20**: Elin admits perjury under oath while pursuing truth.
  - **Chapter 21-22**: Material and civic repair (reopened stores, corrected records, shared custody).
- Moral clarity:
  - **Chapter 5**: Fraud is empirically confirmed.
  - **Chapter 11**: Diversion is revealed as deliberate policy.
  - **Chapter 15**: Elin sees the lethal result of compromise.
  - **Chapter 17**: Public witness aligns truth, risk, and responsibility.
- Providence (non-miraculous, through timing, community, and faithful action):
  - **Chapter 6**: Prayer context and counsel frame discernment under pressure.
  - **Chapter 16**: Confession and charge reorient Elin's choices.
  - **Chapter 17**: The bell gathers a decisive public witness moment.
  - **Chapter 21-22**: Limited but real provision and continuity despite dissolution.
