By midday the scriptorium brazier had burned low to a red basin of ash, and Elin sat with a pumice stone in one hand and the oath ledger open beneath the other. The folio she worked from had taken damp years ago, then smoke, then rough handling by men who turned pages with wet thumbs and no patience for edges. Ink had bled where signatures were crowded, one corner had blackened and curled, and the lower margin held three lines she could still salvage if she cut around the burn and recopied them into the clean register with witness note and date.

She shaved crusted soot from the page edge and brushed the powder into a horn cup so it would not stain the fresh sheet; the cup already held a finger's depth of gray. Dom Stephen coughed at the opposite table and sorted seal tags by parish, his breath catching on the third cough as it always did. Brother Ivo stood at the chest by the north wall with the lock-smith's iron in his lap, counting keys against Elin's docket while the smith waited with shoulders bowed and cap in hand. Outside, carts rattled through the yard.

Elin set the damaged folio aside and drew the oath register close. Its board was newer, calfskin uncracked, pages still stiff enough to spring if left unweighted. She ruled a guide with lead point, copied place and feast date, copied names, copied marks of assent, then reached the line that had split the old sheet where the smoke ran hottest.

Marris, Jon.

The surname stood first in a clerk's old habit, then a neat slash through both names from left shoulder to right hip of the letters. Beside the slash was a marginal note in a hand she knew as well as her own: Released from oath, by order.

She held her quill above the clean page and felt the nib gather one dark bead that would drop if she paused too long. Her fingers tightened, her throat worked once against nothing, and the bead fell anyway, a round blot just below the ruled line where his name should have gone.

"Mistress?" the smith said, too loud for the room. "Is this key to remain with prior, or with your chest?"

Elin wiped the nib, sanded the blot, and answered without looking up. "Prior keeps one. I keep one. No third copy. Mark it so."

The smith nodded as if she had favored him with mercy, though it was only order. She trimmed a new quill, reset the line, and copied the entry exactly as damaged source required: surname, given name, strike-through, marginal release. She did not write why. Once, when men asked whether her husband had stood where he swore on a marsh road two winters before, she had supplied hour and tide mark and witness list because accuracy was asked. By sundown his oath had been judged void, his holding rights suspended, and households tied to his ferry route had gone three days without safe crossing.

The line in this register needed only what the line demanded: name, strike-through, order noted. She copied and moved to the next hand.

The outer door opened on a gust that bent candle flames thin. Prior Anselm came in with the commission letter folded under his sleeve and mud drying in pale flakes at his hem. He had been to gate and back since prime, and the set of his jaw told Elin before he spoke that no petition for extension had been entertained by anyone who mattered.

"Brother Ivo, leave the smith to his work and come to chapter table," he said. "Dom Stephen as well. Mistress Marris, bring the morning docket and alms variance sheet."

Elin blotted her fresh lines, laid a board over the open register, and carried three tied bundles to the long table by the brazier. Anselm stayed standing. He preferred that when talk would not be brief.

"I have sent Tomas to gather six weeks of parish ration slips," he said. "He returns by vespers if roads permit. Infirmary disbursements to North Bank were entered?"

"Entered at prime," Elin said. "Half measure each household, with names and ages. No wheat drawn from lower granary."

Anselm nodded once. "Good. Now we set rule for the next fourteen days and do not wander from it, whatever noise the yard makes."

Dom Stephen lowered himself to a stool and pulled his cloak tighter. "Noise is already beginning. Two reeves asked whether they should hide vestments with kin before King's men arrive. I told them hiding is confession. They looked unconvinced."

"They may be unconvinced," Anselm said. He flattened the folded letter beneath his palm. "They may not be obeyed. Read the custody points as drafted."

She untied the first bundle and read from her own hand. "Item: all books touching grain, rents, and poor-relief to be logged on withdrawal and return, hour marked. Item: key custody split between prior and scribe, witnessed at first bell and close bell. Item: any correction to figures entered in margin with cause, initialed by two witnesses. Item: no schedule copied from memory where source leaf survives. Item: no leaf removed from binding without two clerks present and notation of damage." She looked up. "Added item: old tally scraps to be preserved in sealed sack until reconciliation is complete."

One corner of Anselm's mouth twitched, almost approval. "Keep it."

Dom Stephen tapped the table with one finger. "Add one more. No speech in market beyond assigned statement. If fear runs faster than fact, we lose order before commissioners arrive."

Ivo shifted his weight and rubbed at a wet cuff. "People ask if there will be bread at Martinmas. If we answer with silence, they hear no."

"Then answer with what is true and no more," Anselm said. "The house is under inventory order. Relief continues by recorded measure. Accounts are being reconciled. Petition for aid will follow lawful channel if needed. Nothing beyond this."

He took up Elin's variance sheet and read the figures again though he had heard them twice since dawn. "Forty-nine full sacks entered, forty-one in chest," he said quietly. "Nine half-sacks entered, eight present. Twelve sacks of rye entered, ten present."

No one answered, and the brazier snapped as a knot in the wood gave.

Anselm laid the sheet flat and put two fingers on the missing line as if he might press it back into being. "These numbers are not to be spoken as accusation without proof. Do you hear me? We do not name thief, fraud, or conspiracy on rumor and fear. We do not feed panic with words we cannot yet carry before a magistrate. Accuracy first. Restraint with it." His gaze settled on Elin and held. "Especially you."

She kept her hands open on the table. "I have named no man."

"You have a gift for precision," he said. "And a temper that sharpens when hunger is in the room. Quill's clerks will welcome any loose phrase as evidence of disorder. Do not give them such charity."

Heat rose in her face and then fled, leaving a chill along her spine. He was not wrong. At dawn she had said liar before matins and wished the word back before the bell finished ringing; she had also watched children queued at gate with hollow wrists and knew silence could serve theft as neatly as speech could. She pressed her thumbnail into the table edge until it hurt and fixed the resolve in one plain line: hold speech for now, hold proof first, keep the stores in priory hands long enough to show what had been taken.

"What do you require of me exactly?" she asked.

"That you keep to the page," Anselm said. "You write what can be witnessed. You refuse what cannot. You correct error with mark and reason, not with anger. If pressed in yard or market, you send them to me or Dom Stephen. You do not argue policy in public."

Dom Stephen gave a rough laugh that turned to cough. "In short, do not save us by burning us."

Anselm ignored the phrasing. "Can you do this?"

Elin thought of the line she had just copied with her husband's name struck through by order, and of another order now folded under Anselm's sleeve with Rafe Quill's name written in cleaner ink. Orders looked similar on paper. Consequences did not.

"I can do it," she said.

He inclined his head. "Then we may yet keep standing long enough to seek remedy."

Over the next hour they assigned witnesses and columns. Ivo took noon and dusk granary openings with lay brothers from different parishes so no household could claim favored hands. Dom Stephen would witness seal wax at close and mark beside Elin's entry. Anselm would hear petitions only at fixed hours, each summarized before decision. The lock-smith returned with two new hasps and a bill that made Ivo mutter; Elin entered the amount under emergency maintenance and made him sign by the number.

When the room cleared, she copied oath entries until her wrist stiffened. Villages east and west, names marked fulfilled, lapsed, remitted, punished, dead. She kept each fate inside narrow legal words. Order did not mend hunger, but it could prevent the second ruin of false record.

Near sext, light thinned behind cloud and turned the windows from white to pewter. Reeve Tomas came in with his hood soaked dark to the shoulder and a bundle of damp slips tied in blue thread.

"Road at willow bend is half flood," he said, setting the bundle down. "I lost one page to mud and wrote it again from Widow Nery's tally before leaving." He stripped off his gloves and looked from Elin to the open oath book. "You heard, then, that men in market say the receiver brings soldiers."

Elin untied the slips and spread them near brazier heat, weighting corners with smooth stones. "I heard men in market say three different things before prime," she said. "None signed."

"This came from Carter Pell's cousin who carts for Ely house." Tomas lowered his voice. "He says Quill's men take inventories as if counting for sale, not for safeguard."

She did not lift her head. "Our statement stands. House under inventory order. Relief by recorded measure."

"That sounds like priest words when children are thin."

"Priest words are not my trade. Entries are."

He watched her a moment, then leaned on the table, rain spotting the wood from his sleeve. "I was at gate this morning. I saw North Bank turned to half measure from infirmary. I know why. But I also know what folk will do if they believe barns are being emptied onto south barges while they wait." He tapped the blue thread. "These slips are six weeks. Look hard. There is loss between what leaves granary and what reaches cook stones, and not all of it is mice."

She met his eyes. Tomas was never subtle. He pressed like a man mending dike with bare hands while water rose, too urgent to care if mud ruined his boots. She respected him for it and feared the same quality.

"I am looking hard," she said. "What I cannot do is accuse from a wet bundle and market breath."

"No," he said. "What you cannot do is speak before you have standing."

The sentence landed and stayed. She set down her quill. "What I cannot do is waste the only ground we still hold. If books fail, Quill takes keys and calls it reform. If books stand, we may force a hearing."

Tomas rubbed his jaw, then nodded once, unwilling and real. "Then force it soon. Frost does not wait on hearings." He turned to go, stopped at the door, and added without looking back, "Hob Carter's wife is asking you by name at gate. She says it concerns her boy's chest and no broth left in house."

Elin closed her eyes briefly, then opened them on the columns before her. Hob Carter's allotment had been drawn three days past, complete and signed; additional issue required infirmary mark or prior's exception, both stretched since dawn.

"Tell her petitions are heard at none bell," she said.

"She has already waited since terce."

"Then she may wait one bell more and be entered."

Tomas exhaled through his nose, not agreement and not refusal, and left mud prints along the stone as he went.

Elin held still until his steps faded. Then she drew the ration slips closer and reconciled row by row: parish issue against household marks, marks against reeve copies, reeve copies against bakehouse draw. Red cross where figures matched, blank square where they failed. By none, seven blank squares stared back from two parishes.

Prior Anselm returned for petition hour. Three requests were granted at half measure from infirmary chest under healer's note, two deferred pending reeve verification, one denied for false duplicate mark. Elin entered each outcome with date and witness, then copied them clean into daily abstract so no one could claim later that choices had shifted in retelling. Hob Carter's wife was not among the petitioners.

At vespers the sky darkened early and rain came in a thin, steady sheet that turned yard clay into black glaze. Elin carried the day's bundles to the alms chest with Ivo and Dom Stephen at her heels. They checked each docket against physical folio before locking. Ivo held the lantern while she placed wax on cord knots and pressed her seal. Dom Stephen added his mark beside hers, hand shaking on the first attempt until he cursed, reheated wax, and set a cleaner impression on the second.

"If I die before Advent, let the record state I was killed by seals and not by plague," he said, rubbing his chest.

"If you die before Advent, I will still require your replacement to sign straight," Elin said.

He gave her a look that might have been offense or affection, then coughed into his sleeve and waved them toward the inner door. "Go eat while broth is warm. I will bring this abstract to prior."

Elin stayed by the chest long enough to feel wax cool hard under her thumb. She carried the key ring to her desk, entered close hour, and took up the oath register again. The page with Jon Marris's struck name waited where she had weighted it flat. A drift of brazier ash had settled at the lower margin, fine as flour; when she brushed it away, gray smeared across the fresh ink beneath and softened the final letters of a witness name.

She stared at the smudge. Not ruin, not even serious damage. A wet cloth and patient scraping would clear most of it. Yet for a breath her stomach drew tight and she saw only what ash did after heat had passed: it hid edges and made all losses look alike.

She cleaned the margin with spit and linen, recut one letter, initialed correction, and sanded dry. Then she closed the book, tied it, and stood, because if she stayed seated she would work until full dark and miss gate hour altogether.

The cloister smelled of wet wool and leek broth. Kitchen boys hurried past with stacked bowls. A lay sister from infirmary nodded to Elin, face drawn with fatigue, and said two fever cases had worsened since morning. Elin logged the note in memory as she walked, setting it beside ration figures and lock times. Entries first, even when the entries were names of the failing.

At the outer gate, a narrow awning had been rigged against rain. Petitioners stood beneath it in a line that bent around the post and into open yard. Tomas held the board and called names. Prior Anselm spoke with each household head while Elin recorded decision and measure. Children shivered against skirts, and no one complained loudly. Desperation had already learned manners.

When Hob Carter's wife came forward, she carried a boy of perhaps six wrapped in a blanket gone threadbare at the corners. The child's skin had the waxy look of fever sweat dried and dried again, and his breath rattled at the end of each draw, shallow and fast. The woman bowed once, more because her knees gave than from ceremony.

"Agnes Carter, North Bank," Tomas read from the board. "Petition for wheat from alms store outside weekly allotment."

Elin checked the slip stack. "Allotment drawn three days ago, signed by mark."

Agnes lifted her chin. Rain shone on her lashes. "My eldest took half to my sister's hearth when flood rose and we had to share fire. What remained went to broth for this one." She shifted the boy higher, and he moaned and turned his face into her shoulder. "He cannot keep roots. He needs bread thickened with milk if he is to hold till morning."

Anselm's eyes moved to the child, then to Elin's ledger. "Infirmary note?"

Agnes shook her head. "Healer came at dawn to three houses ahead of us and did not reach ours."

"Then infirmary cannot issue by protocol," Elin said. She heard how flat her own voice sounded and gripped the ledger edge until the skin at her knuckles blanched. "Out-of-allotment wheat requires healer mark or prior exception entered with witness and offset source."

Agnes stared as if Elin had spoken in Latin. "Offset source?"

"Which household gives up equivalent measure," Elin said. "Or which chest is reduced in writing, with consequence entered."

"Consequence is my boy does not breathe by morning," Agnes said.

The line behind her shifted, cloth brushing cloth, the soft impatience of hungry people who feared delay more than rain. Tomas looked at Anselm. Anselm looked at Elin. Dom Stephen, under the eave, pressed knuckles to his mouth and coughed once.

Elin turned a page, found the infirmary reserve line, and read the remaining figure: two days if current fevers worsened, three if no new cases. Below it, the alms variance still stood unresolved, missing sacks blank as open mouths. If she issued now without mark and offset, she broke the rule she had written all day to keep their books defensible. One exception made under rain and witness could become five retold by dark, ten by morning, and by week's end the record would show generosity with no source and no chain. Quill's clerks would call it concealment and seize by statute. The house would lose control of every chest in one stroke. Hunger would not lessen. It would move from queue to decree.

"Prior may grant exception," she said, eyes fixed on the page because Agnes's face made the words harder. "If granted, it is entered with witness and source reduction now, not later."

Anselm drew a long breath and let it out. "What source can bear reduction tonight?"

Elin answered at once because she had run the numbers twice. "None without cutting infirmary fever ration below minimum."

Agnes's mouth opened, then closed. She shifted the boy again, and this time his head lolled before she caught it. "You are telling me to stand aside."

"I am telling you the rule," Elin said.

"Rule," Agnes repeated, and the word came raw. "Your rule keeps ink fat and children thin."

No one in line spoke. Rain thudded on awning cloth. Elin felt her pulse hammering at her wrists and tasted metal at the back of her tongue, but she held the ledger steady with both hands.

Anselm's voice was quiet when he answered, and that quiet carried farther than shouting. "Agnes Carter, I cannot issue wheat outside protocol without taking from fever stores that are already short and recorded. I can send healer now. I can authorize warm broth from kitchen this night. I can move your household to first call at dawn for infirmary review."

Agnes stood motionless for three breaths. Then she gave one short nod that was neither assent nor gratitude, only exhaustion shaped into movement. "Send healer," she said. She pulled the blanket tighter around the boy and stepped away from the table.

Elin wrote the decision: petition denied for alms wheat outside allotment; healer dispatched; kitchen broth authorized; household advanced in morning queue. She marked witnesses, prior, reeve, legal vicar, scribe, and her hand did not shake until she blotted.

As Agnes passed, she stopped beside Elin's table and looked down at the open ledger, rain running from her hood to the page edge. "Write this too," she said. "He coughed blood at noon."

Elin lifted her quill. "Child's name?"

"Peter Carter."

Elin added the line beneath the petition entry, date and hour exact, and sanded it dry while Agnes walked back into rain with the boy pressed tight to her shoulder.

By the time the last petitioner left, dark had settled over the yard and the gate torch smoked in the damp. Tomas collected the board and went to confirm the healer had truly been sent. Dom Stephen asked for the key ring and waited while Elin locked the gate chest. Anselm stood a moment longer under the awning, watching the lane where Agnes had gone until she was no more than movement in gray.

Elin closed the ledger and tucked it under her cloak. The page felt warm against her ribs from her own body heat, as if ink might still be changed by keeping it close. It could not. The entry was made, the rule held, and the boy's name was now a fact in the book that might save the priory's standing and still fail the child before dawn.

She turned toward the scriptorium with mud pulling at her shoes and counted what remained for night: seal the petition folio, copy exception abstract, reconcile seven blank squares from Tomas's slips, recalculate infirmary minimum if fever rose, check whether healer returned with mark. Keep to the page. Keep speech narrow. Keep stores from vanishing in rumor and pity and fear.

Ahead, beyond the cloister arch, the bell rope hung motionless. She could not tell from where she stood whether the hemp was wet from rain or only looked that way in torchlight. Either way, it waited for a hand and an hour. So did the next line she had to write.
