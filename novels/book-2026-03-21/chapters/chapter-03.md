The first cart struck the outer stones hard enough to wake the pigeons in the transept roof, and by the second rattle of iron-bound wheel the cloister had changed its breath. Elin looked up from the parish slips spread on her desk and held still long enough to count the sounds: four horses at least, two heavier axles, men calling distances in London cadence rather than fen speech, and the sharp clink of polearms carried upright so the blades would not dull on gateposts. The yard should have held broom work and wood splitting, not harness chains and command voices.

She set her thumb on the line she had been checking, North Bank against west bake draw, and finished the column before she stood. The seven unmatched squares from Tomas's bundle had become six by midnight and were back to seven by dawn when a substitute mark proved false in one household row. She had circled that row in red and written `verify at source` in the margin with Dom Stephen's witness initials. No reason mattered now if the men at the gate were Quill's.

Brother Ivo crossed the scriptorium at speed, one hand on his key ring as if he feared the links might jump from his belt and run toward the noise. "They are here," he said, not yet out of breath. "Receiver's party. Ten, maybe twelve with him if you count cart men and armed watch. Prior asks you in the yard now. Bring the commission copy and your custody docket."

Elin closed the slip bundle, tied it in blue thread, sealed the knot with a dab of wax, and entered the hour on her desk slate before she moved. It steadied her more than any prayer line she could summon with boots already thudding outside.

In the cloister walk she passed two lay sisters from infirmary carrying pails, both staring toward the gate with faces set between fear and curiosity. A novice came running with a broom still in his hand. Dom Stephen's cough sounded ahead, rough and persistent, then cut short as if he had pressed it down by force. By the time Elin stepped into the yard, Prior Anselm had taken his place beneath the chapter-house arch with three canons to either side and Tomas the reeve planted near the gate post, jaw hard, counting strangers the way she did.

The receiver entered without haste and without apology for the mud his carts cast across the swept stones. Master Rafe Quill was taller than rumor had made him, clean-shaven save for a narrow beard at the chin, cloak of dark broadcloth cut for city weather and lined better than any wool worn at Ash Fen. He dismounted before the horse had fully settled, gave his reins to a younger man in clerk's cap, and looked once around the yard as if taking inventory by sight: gate width, granary line, windows, men fit to carry chests, men too old to resist.

Two carts rolled in behind him. The first held lockboxes and writing desks strapped under oilcloth, the corners branded with crown marks. The second carried three iron-bound trunks, a rolled chest of survey chains, and a rack of spare polearms. Four armed men walked beside the wheels in quilted coats with badges stitched in red thread. They were not soldiers in field order, but they knew where to put their hands and how to narrow movement in the yard without anyone admitting it.

Quill removed one glove and bowed as if arriving to dinner. "Prior Anselm," he said, voice smooth and loud enough for every ear in range. "By authority of the commissioners for ecclesiastical survey and receivership, I thank you for your readiness. The King's reform desires order, not injury. Where books are true and officers cooperative, we preserve what may be preserved. Where confusion reigns, waste follows." He turned slightly, including the canons, the brothers at the pump, the kitchen boys frozen with kindling in their arms. "No honest house should fear an honest count."

Anselm returned a formal bow. "Ash Fen has prepared the inventories commanded. You are received lawfully."

Elin stood one pace behind the prior's right shoulder with the docket boards under her arm. Quill's gaze found her and remained half a breath too long.

"Mistress Marris," he said. "Your hand copied the return witness on my first notice. Clean hand. Prompt hand. I am told the same hand has held your grain and alms books these three days."

"I hold custody by prior order," she said.

"As you should." His smile was small and did not reach his eyes. "We will make good use of such diligence."

He signaled with two fingers. One of his clerks stepped forward and unrolled a parchment list on a board, reading out assigned stations in a fast practiced rhythm: north gate watch, granary weigh verification, chapel plate inspection, archive room seal check, kitchen stores tally. Each item carried two names from Quill's party and one requested local witness. It placed his men everywhere before anyone had accepted a single term.

Dom Stephen moved beside Anselm and spoke low but not low enough to hide from Elin. "He has come with placements already drafted."

"Yes," Anselm said.

Quill clasped his hands. "Before we begin, a word for your people, if you allow it."

Anselm's jaw tightened once. "Speak briefly."

Quill stepped onto the low stone by the pump and raised his voice toward the gathered workers, brothers, and petitioners who had begun to cluster under the outer awning. "Hear me clearly. Relief will continue where entries support it. No child goes hungry because a ledger has changed keepers. No elder is turned from broth for want of a seal. The Crown seeks faithful stewardship, not spectacle." He let the sentence settle, then added, gentler, "There has been much alarm in these marsh parishes. Alarm feeds rumor. Rumor feeds hoarding. Today we cut rumor at the root with lawful sight."

Tomas shifted beside the gate and muttered without turning his head, "Lawful sight with polearms."

Elin kept her own eyes on Quill's clerks. While he spoke mercy, one of them was already marking door lintels with chalk numbers, another counting lock positions on the archive corridor, a third noting names of households in the petition line before any alms case was called.

When Quill stepped down, he faced Anselm again. "Now, my lord prior, I request chapter house for private review of custody provisions before the first chest is opened. My chief clerk and legal witness with me, your legal vicar with you, and Mistress Marris as record officer."

Dom Stephen opened his mouth to object. Quill lifted a hand, courteous as a host pouring ale. "Only routine clarification, reverend sir. Better a quarter hour in private than twelve disputes in public."

Anselm gave one tight nod. "Chapter house, then." He turned to Elin. "Bring the commissioner letter, our custody protocol, and the variance sheet." They left the yard under the eyes of both parties and entered the chapter house, where the cold was sharper than outside, trapped in the stone through two nights of rain. Quill chose the far side of the table where the window gave him light on every page. He removed both gloves, laid them parallel, and waited until Elin had set out the requested papers.

"Let us start with compliment before correction," he said. "Your protocol is stricter than most houses I have visited this quarter. Split key custody, witnessed corrections, hour logging, no leaf removal without notation. If all priory officers feared God and arithmetic equally, my profession would be smaller."

Dom Stephen coughed once into his fist. "Correction implies fault discovered. You have not yet examined a line."

Quill inclined his head. "Fair. Then let us say adaptation to present conditions." He touched the variance sheet with one fingertip. "Present conditions include a declared shortfall in alms wheat and rye before my arrival, together with unresolved parish mismatches. Under receivership law, that raises suspicion of meddling and requires receiver custody of sensitive books until reckoning is complete."

Anselm's hand flattened on the table edge. "Central custody here means what, exactly?"

"All relic inventories, plate lists, alms ledgers, and transfer rolls held under my seal." Quill's tone remained mild. "Books may be consulted by your officers in supervised session. Originals do not sleep in private desks. Keys rest with receiver."

Elin felt the words as a physical shift, like footing giving at reed-bank edge. Her palm found the table edge before she meant it to, and the chill in the oak ran up her wrist. "Our commissioner order names preparation by house officers under seal," she said. "It does not void internal custody pending inspection."

Quill looked at her with open approval, as if she had answered a catechism correctly. "Mistress Marris reads clauses. Good. Clause seven also authorizes receiver discretion where discrepancy suggests danger of concealment or defacement. Your own variance line activates it." He slid a folded sheet from his case and set it beside the commissioner letter. "Supplementary instrument signed at Ely two days past. You may copy the text when we are done."

Dom Stephen leaned forward, squinting. "This carries one signature, not three."

"One is sufficient for interim custody in district operations." Quill turned the sheet toward him, patient, almost kind. "You know that."

Anselm said, "Interim does not mean arbitrary."

"Nor do I practice arbitrary rule." Quill sat back and folded his hands. "I keep rule by record. You fear seizure without witness. I fear alteration before witness. We may satisfy both fears if we arrange labor correctly." He nodded toward Elin. "Mistress Marris remains primary copy clerk and reconciler. She reports the state of inventory to my desk at close each day. Requests for access go through her to my chief clerk."

"She answers first to this house," Anselm said.

"Of course." Quill's smile returned, thin as a blade edge. "In spiritual matters and household discipline. In matters of this receivership, she answers to the receiver. The distinction keeps us all legal."

Elin looked down at the variance sheet where her own ink had named the missing sacks. The line had been her leverage for truth and now served as warrant against her. Her tongue tasted of iron. She could object, refuse, force a written protest. Quill would retain keys anyway under armed witness and add obstruction language to the margin. Every later petition for relief would carry that mark.

She lifted her eyes to Anselm. "If custody transfers, we need conditions entered now."

Quill gestured. "State them."

"Daily access hours fixed in writing. Full log of every leaf moved, by whose hand and for what purpose. No scraping, no recutting, no rebinding without two local witnesses. Any new corrections in our books must carry my mark and one of our witnesses beside your clerk's."

"Accepted," Quill said at once.

She added, "Alms distribution cannot halt for audit theatre. Petition hour proceeds under prior's authority with recorded measures as already set."

"Accepted with observer present," Quill said. "I would be a poor servant of reform if I stopped soup to count spoons."

Dom Stephen made a dry sound that might have been laughter or cough. Anselm did not move.

"And relic lists?" Anselm asked.

Quill folded his hands. "Immediate sealed custody. I have seen too many houses where devotion and opportunism share a chest."

"You suggest theft by our clergy."

"I suggest temptation by uncertainty," Quill said. "It is a gentler accusation and usually true."

The room held silence long enough for the wind to rattle the casement. At last Anselm said, "Enter my objection: that Ash Fen has maintained lawful custody and offers no evidence of relic defacement."

"Entered," Quill said, and nodded to his clerk.

Elin wrote the minutes herself because she trusted no other hand to preserve each phrase. By the time the agreement was read back, the ink on the first lines had already dried to dull brown.

When they returned to the yard, Quill's party had built a working court beneath a canvas fly stretched from gatepost to granary eave. Trestles held scales, counterweights, sealing wax, and three open ledgers in different hands. One armed watch stood by the archive corridor, another by the alms chest, two by the carts. Petitioners who had come for fever broth waited farther back than usual, pressed toward the rainline by men who said it was for order.

Quill announced the custody transfer in the same warm public voice he had used on arrival. "For the protection of this house and the King's trust, sensitive books will now sit under neutral seal while local officers continue their good labor. Mistress Marris, already your careful scribe, will coordinate the flow so no rightful relief is delayed." He smiled toward the queue. "You see? Continuity, not chaos."

Agnes Carter stood near the awning post with Peter wrapped against her shoulder. The boy's cough reached Elin between the clink of scales. Agnes did not return Elin's look. She watched Quill with the expression of someone listening to a physician describe weather while a fever climbs.

The transfer began at once. Elin and Ivo carried the alms ledgers from scriptorium chest to the inspection trestle, each docket called aloud by Quill's clerk and repeated by Dom Stephen for local record. Quill checked seals, nodded, passed each volume to his chief clerk, who wrapped it in waxed cloth and laid it in an iron trunk. Relic lists followed, then plate registers, then old transfer rolls Elin had meant to reconcile against Tomas's slips before nightfall. Each bundle received a tag in red ink with receiver mark and sequence number.

At noon the granary verification started. Quill walked the bays himself with Elin at his side and two armed watch behind, not touching weapons but close enough that Ivo kept glancing over his shoulder while he untied sacks. Quill asked measured questions in polite cadence.

"This seam darkened by damp last week or earlier?"

"Earlier," Elin said. "Logged three days ago."

"Rat sign active?"

"Minor by east wall. Not sufficient for declared variance."

"Households currently on half measure by infirmary exception?"

"Nine by yesterday's close, two under review at first bell."

He nodded at each answer and made no comment until they reached the alms bay where the missing line stood obvious in the stacked gaps. There he crouched, touched the packed earth, and held up two fingers with dust on the tips.

"You see the burden this places on me," he said quietly, only for her. "If I leave such a room under ordinary custody and another three sacks vanish, London will call me either fool or partner. I am neither."

"If records vanish under your custody," she said, "what will London call that?"

He looked at her with something like interest. "They will call it impossible, because my logs are better than yours." He stood and brushed his fingers clean. "Do not mistake force for carelessness, Mistress Marris. I take hard measures so I can survive soft accusations."

By midafternoon the rain thickened and ran from the fly edges in cold ropes. Petition hour was moved under the cloister arch, but Quill kept one observer at the table where Anselm heard each case. Elin wrote as before: names, household size, fever marks, grant or defer, source chest. The observer copied her entries into a separate book with narrower script. Twice he asked her to repeat an hour because he had missed it. At the third interruption she said, "If your hand cannot keep pace, stand closer." He stood closer.

Between petitions Quill's chief clerk delivered a new form headed `Daily Abstract for Receiver's Office` and placed it on Elin's page. "Close report in this format each night," he said. "Signed by you and one of ours."

She scanned the columns. They were not foolish. They simply reordered significance: first line for breaches of order, second for irregular speech in yard, third for access requests denied, and only then measures issued to poor relief.

"This omits infirmary reserve projection," she said.

"Not required in district abstract."

"It is required if anyone wants these people alive in January."
He gave a small shrug. "Add a note in margin if you must." She added it before he stepped away.

By vespers the last of the trunks had been sealed and chained. Quill held the ring of new custody keys on his belt. The old split pair that had rested with Anselm and Elin for three days now lay in a cloth bag on Quill's desk, tagged as superseded.

Inside the chapter house, Quill reviewed the day's close with formal civility. "Efficient progress," he said. "No obstruction worth noting. Your house should be commended." He signed two receipts, handed one to Anselm and one to Dom Stephen, then turned to Elin. "You and I must settle copying schedule for continuity of source lines."

Anselm said, "She has worked since before dawn. Schedule can wait until morning."

"Morning is full with plate appraisal and east barn weigh." Quill's tone remained respectful and immovable. He turned one receipt between thumb and forefinger before laying it flat. "Night offers quiet, fewer interruptions, better concentration for delicate hands." He faced Elin directly. "One hour nightly in the archive, after compline bell, under my guard's supervision. You may copy lines needed for reconciliation. Originals remain in place. At the hour's end, pens down, books returned, room sealed."

Elin felt the day in her body all at once: ache between her shoulders, two cramped fingers on her right hand, grit under her eyelids from damp smoke and dust. She set both hands flat before speaking so no one could read the tremor in them. In one hour she could copy perhaps six pages clean if no interruptions, four if cross-checking figures, fewer if she also had to complete the new abstract and petition dockets. The unresolved transfers alone covered more than fifty leaves.

"One hour is insufficient for lawful reconciliation," she said.

"It is sufficient for first necessities." Quill adjusted his cuffs. "Choose what matters most."

"That choice itself can falsify outcome."

"Then choose carefully." He looked at Anselm and Dom Stephen as if inviting them to admire her seriousness. "I trust Mistress Marris to distinguish urgent truth from clerical vanity."

Dom Stephen's cough broke loose, hard enough to bend him at the waist. When he straightened, eyes wet, he said, "If she falls behind by this limit, we enter protest tomorrow."

"Enter what you please," Quill said. "I will answer in writing."

When they left the chapter house, full dark had already settled in the cloister, and Elin carried Quill's one-hour limit like a weight between her shoulder blades. Rain eased to mist. One lamp burned at the archive corridor where Quill's chosen guard stood with halberd butt on the stone and a bored, vigilant face. At the strike of compline's close, the guard unlocked the outer door and let Elin pass with one inkhorn, three quills, and a stack of blank leaves counted before entry.

The archive smelled of wet leather and old glue. Trunks and shelves that had stood familiar now wore fresh tags in red ink, each number corresponding to Quill's custody list. At the center table lay six volumes allowed for tonight's hour: alms issues, transfer rolls quarter three, relic sidebook, plate list, parish abstracts, and the new receiver form to be filled before lock.

She set the hourglass the guard had provided and turned it. Sand began to fall with an indifferent whisper.

She worked in sequence, not because sequence was fastest, but because sequence was the only way to keep from lying by omission. Alms issues first: she copied the last four days including Agnes Carter's denied wheat and Peter's blood-cough note, then flagged households now on infirmary edge. Transfer roll next: three entries with Quill's countersign from earlier in the month, quantities marked as dispatched to south parish relief with no corresponding parish receipt in Tomas's slips.

Half the sand gone.

She moved to parish abstracts and found one leaf missing from the section that should have held the day between the first and second market floods. Not torn, not moused, not water-lost. The stitching holes showed a deliberate lift and resew with cleaner thread than the rest. She touched the holes, counted them, and wrote on her blank leaf: `parish abstract quire II, leaf between dated entries removed before current custody, resew visible at stitch 4-7`. Then she paused, looked again, and added: `verification witness required`.

Three quarters sand.

At the door the guard shifted his weight and cleared his throat. "Quarter left," he said.

"I can read sand," she answered without heat.

She took up the receiver abstract last. Breaches of order. Irregular speech. Access requests denied. She filled each required box in the narrow terms Quill's office demanded, then used the margin to record infirmary reserve projection, fever households, and pending source checks on the seven unmatched squares.

When the last sand fell, the guard stepped inside. "Hour done."

"One line." She finished the word `unstable`, blotted, and laid down the quill.

He counted her copied leaves, counted her blanks, compared the numbers to his entry board, then locked each source volume back into the tagged trunk while she stood with ink-stained fingers at her sides. He sealed the trunk with green wax and pressed Quill's stamp so hard the wax ridged at the edge. At the corridor table outside, Quill waited in dry cloak though the night was wet.

"Productive hour?" he asked.

"Less productive than three hours would have been," she said.

"I admire persistence under narrow limits." He held out his hand for her copied leaves. She did not give them. He smiled faintly. "Keep your copies. Originals are what concern me." He glanced over the top page where she had noted the resewn leaf gap. "You see why custody matters. Missing leaves happen in anxious houses."

"This one may have happened before your arrival," she said.

"May have," he agreed. "Your note is cautious. Keep that habit."

He nodded to the guard, who locked the corridor door and handed Quill the ring. Metal touched metal in a short clear sound that carried farther than it should have in the stone passage.

"Same hour tomorrow," Quill said. "If you need special volumes, submit request at sext."

He turned and walked toward guest lodging with the keys at his belt, two armed men falling in behind him as naturally as shadows. Elin stood under the lamp until their steps faded and the corridor settled back into priory quiet, thinner now than before, as if quiet itself had been inventoried and reduced.

She looked down at the copied leaves in her hands. They held enough to prove danger, not enough to prove order. Behind the locked door sat every line that could join those pieces or break them. She could not tell yet which loss in that room had happened before Quill came and which would happen because he had.
