The stair to the bell loft held night's cold like a stone cistern, and Elin took it two steps at a time with her tally board tucked beneath her arm, her lamp hooded against the draft. The rope for the great bell brushed her sleeve at each turn, damp with mist drawn in through the louvers, and she judged from that wetness what the yards and reed roads would be by noon: slick plank, slow cart, twice the labor for every sack brought from west barn to gate. Matins would ring soon. Before the first stroke she wanted the lower granary count in ink, not in memory, because memory softened edges when men with seals began asking what had been where and when.

She came out of the stair and crossed the yard under a paling sky. Frost silvered the horse trough rim and the pump handle. Smoke from the bakehouse chimney lay low, not rising, pressed down by fog so thick it seemed another wall set around Ash Fen. At the granary door she set the lamp on the threshold, tested the hasp, and felt the familiar grit of rust and old flour on the iron. The key turned hard for the last inch, then gave with a scrape that sounded too loud in the hour before prayer.

Inside, the air changed. Dry chaff, mouse droppings, a sweet rot from one split corner stack, and beneath all of it the clean mineral cold that rose from packed earth. She shut the door behind her, raised the lamp, and let light run across rows: parish tithe barley to the left, priory table oats behind, alms wheat marked with the burnt cross on the right-hand bays. She did not begin with the alms stores. She never began with what troubled her. She counted in order.

"Bay one, six full, one broken," she said softly, and chalked the line. "Bay two, five full, one half." Her finger traced the sewn marks and pinched twine for damp. At the end of the row she knelt, held the lamp low, found no rat scrape at the wall seam, and marked two droppings by the door.

A scrape sounded behind her, not rat this time but boot leather at the threshold. Brother Ivo ducked his head through the door with his shoulders up against the cold, his tonsure hidden under a wool cap that had once been black and now matched ash.

"You are ahead of the bell again," he said. "If I were younger I would take it for zeal and not suspicion."

"If you were younger you would still sleep at this hour," Elin answered, keeping her eyes on the sacks while she wrote. "Hold the door shut. Draft skews the lamp."

He eased it closed with one hand and came down the aisle, breathing through his mouth. "The bakehouse girl says we have enough for All Saints and after that God will provide. I told her God has provided already in the form of measures and locks, and she may honor Him by not dipping from the wrong bay."

Elin moved to the alms side at last. She set the board on a barrel head, took a fresh stub of chalk, and read yesterday's figure from the prior sheet pinned beneath wax at the post: forty-nine full sacks wheat, nine half, twelve rye for mixed loaves when wheat ran thin. She looked to the floor where forty-nine should have stood and counted forty-one.

Cold lifted along her spine. She set the chalk down because her hand had begun to shake, pressed her thumb into a split at her palm until it stung, and counted again from the far corner. Then by rows. Then by sewn marks at the mouths. Forty-one full. Eight half. Ten rye.

"Ivo," she said, too quiet. She swallowed and called again. "Ivo."

He came slower than she liked, rubbing his hands together under his sleeves. "What is it?"

She pointed. "Yesterday at none there were forty-nine full alms wheat and nine half. Who drew between none and now?"

"No one by my hand." He frowned at the stack as if numbers might climb out of it if he stared hard enough. "Kitchen took one half at supper and one half this dawn, both signed. Not full sacks."

"Then where are eight?"

"Eight what?"

"Eight full wheat, one half, two rye."

His face went blank first, then wary. "Perhaps you read the wrong sheet."

She pulled the pinned slip free, held it to the lamp, read each line aloud including his mark at bottom. He listened, lips moving over the sums as if the numbers might confess to him if he mouthed them like prayer.

"Could be spoilage discarded," he said at last, too quickly.

"Where? Show me discarded grain enough to fill eight sacks."

"Could be moved to upper store while floor was mended."

"No boards were lifted." She touched the hard-packed earth with her boot toe. "Could be your novice forgot to enter transfers. Could be mine forgot. Could be a liar wrote on my post. Which is it?"

Ivo's shoulders sank. "Do not put liar in your mouth before matins."

"Then give me another word that keeps children from hunger in January."

He did not answer. He took the slip from her and folded it twice, too neat for fear, and handed it back. "Ring first. Count after. If we miss office because of sacks, the house hears of it before noon."

Elin bent over the board and wrote a second set of figures beneath the first, marked with the hour and lamp sign so she could testify to light conditions if asked why she had erred or not erred. Her fingers had begun to ache where chalk dust crept into skin cracks. She flexed them, set her jaw, and copied one line from memory of last winter's ration table: one household of five, three half-loaves each day, seven days, one and one-third pecks flour equivalent. She extended the column to Candlemas and stopped before the final sum because she already knew it. If the lower number stood, they would run short before deep frost. Even if no road flooded. Even if no rat touched a grain.

The bell rope thudded once above them, a novice testing slack, then again with intention. Elin capped the inkhorn, blew the wet lines dry, and pressed the board flat against her chest to keep the pages from curling as she climbed.

The first stroke of matins rolled out over fen water and came back in pieces. By the second stroke, shapes moved in the cloister walk: brothers in dark wool, two lay sisters from the infirmary, kitchen boys carrying kindling. Elin set her board on the scriptorium table and went into the choir with numb fingers tucked in her sleeves. She tried to follow the psalm and found she was counting, not chanting: forty-one, eight, ten, and the empty spaces where numbers should have stood.

At the second lesson the outer door banged, then shut hard against wind. She did not turn, but she marked the interruption by habit, the way she marked tears in parchment or a blot above a man's name. A minute later the sacristan appeared at the choir rail and bent toward Prior Anselm. The prior's head lifted once, sharply, and then lowered again. He did not break the office. He finished the lesson to its end and gave the response in the same grave measure as always.

When the dismissal came, he held his place by the stall and looked along the line of lay clerks. "Mistress Marris," he said. "Bring your boards to chapter house. Now."

She bowed, gathered her papers from the table, and felt Ivo watching her as she passed the crossing.

Outside, dawn had not yet burned through the fog. The chapter house door stood open, and a rider waited in the yard with his horse blowing white. Not one of theirs. The cloak was good wool, brown dyed nearly black, and the mud on his boots was pale where it had dried from chalk roads west of Ely, not the peat-black Ash Fen spat up. He held a tube case of oiled leather under one arm, sealed with red wax impressed by a stamp she did not need to see close to know.

Inside the chapter house, Prior Anselm stood by the long table with one hand resting on the back of the carved chair that no one had sat in since the last visitation. His face had the color of old paper and the stillness of a man counting cost before he speaks. Beside him stood Dom Stephen, their legal vicar, rubbing at a cough in his chest, and two canons called early from beds by the look of their hair and the speed of their breathing.

The rider entered when beckoned, pulled off one glove, and placed the tube on the table without kneeling. "For the house of Ash Fen," he said. "By order of the King's commissioners for ecclesiastical survey and seizure administration." He looked at Elin only once, quick and measuring, then back to the prior. "Return witness mark required before terce."

Anselm nodded to Elin. "Cut it. Read."

She set down her board, warmed her knife blade over a candle a heartbeat so cold wax would not shatter, and sliced the seal clean. The parchment inside crackled as she unrolled it. Formal hand, chancery-trained, black ink not yet fully cured. She read the first lines silently, then aloud when Anselm gestured.

"To the Prior and officers of Ash Fen, in execution of His Majesty's ordinances concerning houses religious and their goods, you are hereby commanded to prepare and present within fourteen days full inventories under seal of lands, vestments, plate, stores, granary holdings, rents due, alms distributions, and all accounts touching poor relief for the present year..."

Dom Stephen coughed into his sleeve. One of the canons swore under his breath, then crossed himself as if to take the word back from the air.

Elin continued. The letter named receiving officers to arrive in person. It named penalties for concealment, defacement, delay. It named custody transfer procedures for keys, boxes, and record rooms. It named no appeal that could be heard before winter.

At the bottom, under three signatures, was the appointed receiver for their district.

Master Rafe Quill.

Elin had seen the name in copied decrees and in rumors passed with fish baskets at market. Never in her own house papers. She read it once and felt the weight settle, the way floodwater settled into a ditch and did not move for days.

Anselm held out his hand and she passed him the sheet. He read the middle clauses again while Dom Stephen leaned close enough to fog the ink.

"Fourteen days," the vicar said. "If weather holds."

"Weather does not hold in late October," Anselm answered.

"Then we request extension on grounds of transport impediment."

"Granted by whom? The same office that wrote this?" He tapped the page once with a finger bone-thin from fasting and age. "No. We comply."

The rider shifted his weight. "Witness mark, my lord."

Anselm gave a short nod to Elin. She drew the return slip from the tube, wrote date and hour, listed the men present as witnesses, and pushed the sheet for him to mark. He made his sign, took back the tube, and left as briskly as he had come, boots thudding over stone, horse tack clinking away into fog.

For a moment no one spoke. Only the candle guttered in a draft from the ill-fitted casement.

Anselm turned to Elin. "What have you in granary?"

She opened her board and gave the numbers without adornment: present sacks by category, measured losses from prior figure, rat sign minimal, damp moderate, estimated household reach at current ration. She kept her voice level. She did not explain where her throat tightened when she said "short before Candlemas" because that was not a figure, and figures were what men in offices recognized.

Dom Stephen rubbed both palms over his face. "If these numbers stand in a seized inventory, they will read as misrule."

"If they do not stand," Elin said, "they will not become grain by being denied."

One canon snapped, "We may not hand wolves a map of our weakness."

She looked at him, then at the board. "Wolves do not ask for maps. Receivers ask for signatures."

Anselm lifted a hand before the canon could answer. "Enough. We are not saved by shouting in this room." He paced once along the table, slow because of his knee, then stopped at Elin's side and put his finger on the line where she had written the loss. "Can you prove this variance by prior entries and witness marks?"

"Yes, if entries have not been altered since yesterday."

"Can you reconcile every house account in fourteen days?"

"With two clerks copying and no interruptions, perhaps. With ordinary interruptions, no."

"There will be no ordinary interruptions," he said. He looked from her to Dom Stephen and back again. "Mistress Marris, you will prepare the full seizure inventory yourself. Granary, alms, plate, vestments, rents, all. You will take first custody of every book in this house that touches stores or relief. No page leaves scriptorium without your hand on the docket. Brother Ivo will open every barn at your request. Dom Stephen will witness seals at close of each day."

He paused, and his voice lowered by half a tone, enough that the others had to lean in.

"You will write true figures. You will also write a chain of custody so exact that no man can claim these accounts were muddled by fear or zeal. If there is hidden loss, we discover it before the commissioners do. If there has been theft by any hand, we name it in record before they name it for us."

Elin felt the board edge press hard into her ribs. "My lord, if the losses prove greater than this morning's count, the parishes will not hold winter."

"I know." His eyes did not leave hers. "And if our books fail scrutiny, we lose not only stores but standing to petition for relief elsewhere. We become liars first and hungry second."

Dom Stephen gave a thin, unhappy laugh that was not amusement. "Hungry first, if this receiver is as swift as rumor says."

Anselm ignored him. "From this hour, you answer to me on inventory and to no other voice in corridor or yard. Not even mine if I speak without witness. Do you understand?"

Elin understood too well. It was protection and burden in one cord. If she kept the books true and they damned the house, she would be the hand that wrote the damnation. If she softened lines to buy days, she would stain the record and hand Quill a ready rope for all of them.

She set the board down and flattened the page with both palms so the curl would not hide a number. "I understand. I need three things before prime. New quills cut hard for small hand, two locks changed on the alms chest, and a runner to summon reeve Tomas with parish ration lists for the last six weeks."

Anselm inclined his head once. "Granted."

She added, "And no one burns old tally scraps until I inspect them."

One canon opened his mouth in irritation. Anselm spoke over him. "Granted."

Dom Stephen drew a stool and sank onto it with a wince. "God keep us from the King's clerks," he muttered.

"God keep us truthful," Anselm said.

He gathered the commissioner letter and held it out. "Copy this by noon into our register and a second hand for parish witnesses. Include names of all officers expected to arrive. We do not receive strangers without knowing which name to write beside each key."

Elin took the parchment. The ink smelled metallic and raw, as if the words had been cut from ore and not mixed from soot. She scanned again the list of demanded schedules: barn by barn, chest by chest, altar by altar. The clauses were neat. The threat was neat. Even the mercy line was neat, a sentence offering consideration for houses that cooperated fully and promptly, as though winter itself could be petitioned to wait while clerks sorted seals.

The chapter house door opened without knock. Brother Ivo stood there, cap in both hands, and did not cross the threshold.

"Forgive the interruption," he said, eyes on the floor. "Reeve Tomas is at gate with two women from North Bank. They ask alms wheat this morning, outside allotment. One child fevered. One old man cannot keep broth."

Silence fell like dropped cloth. The canons looked to Anselm. Dom Stephen stared at the table grain. Elin's jaw locked. The skin between her shoulders tightened until breath came shallow. She set two fingers on the board edge and felt the wood grain under her skin. Then she looked at her own figures, at forty-one where forty-nine should have been, and at the line where she had marked short before Candlemas.

Anselm closed his eyes a brief instant, then opened them and spoke as if dictating a will. "Give from infirmary stores first. Half measure each household. Record names and ages. Tell Tomas I will meet him at gate after prime." He turned to Elin. "Add this disbursement to today's opening. No informal gifts. No unentered grain."

"Yes, my lord," she said.
Ivo bowed and withdrew. Anselm looked around the table, each face in turn. "You all heard the order. We proceed lawfully and without panic."

The canons murmured assent with little strength. Dom Stephen pushed himself upright and reached for the letter to read yet again the clause on penalties, as if the words might soften if worried enough.

Elin gathered her board, the commissioner parchment, and her own rough ration table. At the door she stopped because a thought struck with the cold clarity of water under ice: by noon the whole house would know commissioners were coming. By evening every village within bell-hearing would know. By tomorrow men who bought cheaply at flooded roads would know how many sacks stood between Ash Fen and hunger.

She stepped into the cloister walk. Prime bell had not rung yet. Frost still held the flagstones where shadows lay, and her breath came quick from the chapter house heat into open air. She walked toward scriptorium and counted tasks in sequence to keep her hands steady: copy royal notice, seal return list, call reeve, inspect alms chest locks, reconcile yesterday's entries, recalculate winter reach by parish and household size, prepare witness docket, begin seizure ledger folio one. Near the infirmary door, one lay sister shifted her bundle of kindling, lifted two fingers in a brief blessing, and hurried on. Elin answered with a nod and kept walking.

At the crossing she looked up through fog toward the bell loft she had climbed before dawn. The rope hung still now, dark with damp. By habit she imagined it in ledger terms, line under tension waiting for weight. Once pulled, it could not call back what it had summoned.

When prime struck, she would begin writing the inventory that might keep them legal long enough to beg for relief, or else hand their weakness to the men coming down the road. The first page lay blank on her table, and by nightfall it would carry her hand, her seal, and numbers hungry people might have to survive.
