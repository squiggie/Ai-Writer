At first light Elin stood in East Barn with a spoilage rod in one hand and a wax tablet in the other, while rainwater dropped from the beam joints in a measured count that would have suited an hourglass if the room had believed in fairness. Brother Ivo had opened the doors before lauds and now held the tally board against his chest. Behind him, Receiver Guard Bate watched in his usual stance, feet set wide, jaw slack with boredom that did not soften into trust.

The order for this inspection had come under Quill's seal just before sleep: condition survey of all grain bays for damp loss and preservative redistribution. It was ordinary enough to disarm objection, broad enough to justify movement through every store room she needed. Quill had called it prudent stewardship in front of witnesses. Elin had copied the heading in a neat hand and understood it as the first useful permission he had given her.

An hour earlier, in the archive at first bell, he had inspected the logged residual quire with Dom Stephen and Harwood present. The supplementary packet remained inside, wax damp at one corner, Quill's signet still legible. He had praised her source entry, had it tied, tagged, and moved into his second iron trunk, then remarked that disciplined clerks were the soul of lawful reform. Elin had answered with the phrase required by office and felt the two extracted sheets, already hidden elsewhere, burn in memory like coals under ash.

Now she set the rod into the first stack and checked seam depth, mold bloom, and floor rise where damp wicked into burlap. Ivo called figures. Bate repeated them for witness. She marked each official value in black, then added dots at the page edge where no one but she would read them as a fourth column waiting to be filled.

"First bay loss," Ivo said. "One half peck to damp and fray."

"One half peck," Bate echoed without interest.

Elin nodded and wrote the line, then touched the rod to the next sack. The grain slid inside with a dry hiss, clean and firm. No sour smell. No warm clump. Nothing in this bay spoke to the losses declared in Quill-linked transfer lines, where whole sacks had vanished between departure and receipt as if water had taken them on clear days.

By prime she had finished East Barn sample counts and two full-weight checks on stone balance. Both sacks held within ordinary variance. She entered `localized damp, no broad spoil` in the receiver summary box and copied private arithmetic to the tablet's lower edge: declared transfer loss seven and a half sacks for this window, physically plausible loss less than one.

The two attestation sheets were no longer on her person. Before dawn she had lifted the back board from an unused antiphoner in the old choir press, slid the leaves between board and linen pastedown, then pressed the seam closed with warmed glue until age swallowed the repair. If Quill searched her sleeve, her desk, or her bed chest, he would find forms and clean blanks. He would not find fourteen wheat sacks moved to lay custody under his own countersign.

They crossed to North Barn as lauds faded through mist. Under the cloister arch she passed Agnes Carter with Peter on her hip, the boy's cough dry enough to sound like bark from split oak. Agnes did not ask for wheat now. She held Elin's gaze with a look that spared no courtesy and said, low and steady, "He took broth at dawn and put it up."

"I will enter him first in infirmary line again," Elin said.

Agnes shifted the child and looked toward the granary doors where Bate waited. "Do not enter him where men with keys can count him and not feed him."

Elin could not answer that with language fit for daylight. She touched Peter's blanket edge once, then moved on before her face broke order.

North Barn still smelled of old flood and mouse oil. The tide mark on the back wall stood shoulder-high from the winter three years past, dark under new limewash. Here she expected real damage and found it: two split sacks, one mold patch at floor level, minor weevil near the east post where straw had not been turned. Honest loss, all of it visible. Not the kind that erased deliveries whole.

Harwood joined them before terce, boots too clean for marsh work, receiver register tucked under his arm with red ribbon markers ready. He watched her with a narrow smile and asked if she preferred his clerking hand to note all observations direct, to save her effort.

"You can copy from my board at close," she said. "Field marks stay in one hand until witness is complete."

"That sounds like mistrust," he said.

"That sounds like chain of custody."

His smile thinned and returned. He stood near enough to read her lines and far enough not to touch anything that might later bear responsibility.

By terce North Barn lay closed with witness marks from Ivo and Bate. Elin's official summary remained modest and true: damp present, no catastrophic rot, no levy beyond known spoilage bands. Her private line widened the gap: declared losses in transfer book now exceeded physical loss by nine sacks and a quarter across the two barns.

The lower granary under the bell stair held colder air and finer dust. This was where she had first counted the missing sacks before Quill's arrival. The gaps were still there, but someone had re-stacked the rows so absence looked planned, a neat rectangular void at the back like a chest removed for cleaning. Men who needed lies to travel often straightened the floor first.

She ordered four sacks marked for afternoon dispatch to be weighed in full. Ivo and Bate lifted each to the hanging scale while Harwood wrote. First true. Second true. Third light by a quarter stone, still ordinary for burlap dry-out. Fourth true. Nothing near the deductions entered as road levy and tide spoilage in the diverted lines.

While Bate reset the hook she crouched by the south service threshold and ran her fingers along fresh grooves in the wood planks, twin wheel cuts deeper than morning cart traffic should make. In the groove lay a tar fleck and wet hemp fiber.

"These marks were not here last week," she said.

Bate glanced down. "You checked this board last week?"

"I check every board that carries food."

"Might be your own carts."

"My carts run parish issue by day, not heavy pull after compline."

Bate looked at Harwood before he answered. "I keep night post at archive door, not south service. Put what you please in your note."

She wrote `threshold wear accelerated, heavy extraction probable` in the official survey line, then added a private caution mark beside it. Probability was not proof. But probability plus false loss figures plus hidden attestations did not leave many roads open.

At sext she transcribed the three-house survey into Quill's required abstract. The sums were small enough to be plausible, large enough to avoid accusation of obstruction. Quill read the sheet in chapter house session with Anselm, Stephen, two canons, and Harwood present. He paused at `no broad rot event observed`, then looked up.

"Either Ash Fen stores better than most," he said pleasantly, "or your definitions of spoil differ from district custom."

"Rot spoils grain whether district custom agrees or not," Elin said. "Measured loss from damp and vermin does not account for declared transfer reductions on the dates flagged."

He tapped the page with one finger. "Declared reductions include transport hazard, levy, emergency diversion. Your rod does not weigh those."

"Then we should weigh them another way."

"We do. In office review." He folded the abstract and handed it back. "Continue your diligence. It serves everyone."

Anselm watched Quill leave before he spoke. "Did you get what you sought?"

"I got limits," she said. "And numbers that refuse to sit still."

Stephen's cough caught him hard enough to bend at the waist. When he straightened he said, "Bring the numbers to me after nones. Not accusations, only lines."

"Only lines," Elin said, though she no longer believed lines stayed only lines.

Nones brought rain and gate pressure. Petition hour moved under cloister cover while Quill's observer copied every request into his parallel book. Elin wrote household names, ages, fever signs, granted measure, denied cause, source chest. She added Peter Carter at first position by infirmary request and watched Harwood's junior copy the note without expression.

Between petitions she sent a kitchen novice to fetch Reeve Tomas from weighing shed, then met him by the bakehouse door where steam and yeast smell gave some cover from ears.

"Tell me straight," she said, unfolding a narrow strip where she had written dates only. "These three issue lines were marked delivered to Mere End and North Bank by parish hand. Did your wardens receive them?"

Tomas bent over the strip, lips moving as he counted dates. Rain drummed the eave hard enough to hide his first curse. "No carts on these nights. I remember because we kept lanterns for them and wasted oil."

"Any emergency diversion I do not know?"

"Only the one from God's sky into our fields." He wiped rain from his beard. "Mere End got half issue two days later. North Bank got none. I signed complaint in your office and got told to await reconciliation."

"By whose mouth?"

"Harwood, with Quill three steps behind him pretending to inspect horses."

She showed him the threshold note, not the hidden attestation values. Tomas read, then looked toward the south service lane.

"You think they ran sacks out by night."

"I think the floor says so."

"Floor does not speak at hearing."

"No," she said. "But boatmen do, if they will."

Tomas gave a short, grim nod. "South quay men talk for coin and safety. You can usually buy one and never both."

She folded the strip back into her sleeve. "Find me which man tied night loads two nights past."

"If I ask open, word travels to Quill before dark."

"Then ask crooked."

Tomas's mouth moved as if he might laugh, then settled. "You always did prefer numbers to people, Elin. Numbers are easier to keep alive."

She met his eyes. "Not this winter."

He left by the rain side, shoulders up against the weather, and did not look back.

At infirmary close Elin carried Maud healer's requisition list to scriptorium: oat meal low, honey nearly gone, linen strips down to narrow widths. Peter Carter had taken broth and coughed blood at both dawn and midday. Maud wanted one more handful of barley meal for night decoction and had no legal source line left to request from.

"I can write need until my hand breaks," Maud said, wiping a basin with cloth already too red. "If there is no grain, your writing turns to weather."

Elin copied each item in clean script and felt the page grow heavier than parchment had right to be.

Vespers bells began. Quill's men moved through the corridor in pairs, keys and wax in hand, closing day books under receiver tag. Elin returned to the scriptorium table with her strips and built a four-column reckoning: physical spoilage, declared transfer loss, witnessed parish receipt, unexplained variance. The fourth column, once started, widened with each line.

By candle mid-height she reached the scratch-corrected entry in a receiver transport log Harwood had left among copy leaves: `south service extraction, xxii sacks mixed grain, external water carriage.` The line bore one clerk initial she did not know, then overwrite in darker ink. No parish destination. No church witness. Time set at second quarter after midnight, two nights past.

Her hands went still. She read the line twice, then aligned it with threshold grooves, tar flecks, and Tomas's missing deliveries. Suspicion had served for days because she could not carry certainty without proof. This line was not certainty, but it was direction with date and hour.

She folded the strip and slid it into her left sleeve.

The safe choice was to wait for Stephen, pass him the column set, and let procedure grind through whatever teeth it had left. The lawful choice might still have been the same. But next ebb would come before dawn, and if the line meant what it said, another night movement could empty what infirmary needed by tomorrow noon.

She blew out the candle, took her cloak from the peg, and left by the side stair.

South quay lay below the fish sheds where the fen widened into black current and tide mud held light in broken streaks. Mist sat low on the water, cutting men at the knee. Elin kept close to wall shadow past two cart boys dicing by a post and a drover asleep against a wheel. Neither lifted his head.

The tally hut lamp still burned. Old Hobb Pike sat on a stool inside mending rope splice, his hands twisted thick from years of wet hemp and cold iron rings. He looked up when her shoes hit plank.

"Mistress book-hand," he said. "Priory owes me for three rope lengths since Michaelmas if you've come for debts."

"I came for tide times and who used them," she said.

He snorted and kept splicing. "That costs more than debt."

She set two pennies on the board between them. "I need one answer true and one answer fast. No names if that chokes your throat. Marks and direction will serve."

Hobb eyed the coins, then the doorway behind her, then the tide board on the wall. "Fast I can do. True depends whether you can hear ugly things without dropping them."

"Try me."

He pointed with the splice needle to a notch scratched under second night past. "Low ebb, second quarter after midnight. Two shallow grain punts took off from lower tie. Both loaded heavy. One had a private reed-hook mark burned near stern. Other had no parish sigil at all, just fresh pitch over where mark should sit."

Elin followed his needle to the direction marks. Both lines ran south channel, not east parish cut.

"Whose men loaded?" she asked.

"Not mine. Cloak men from priory side and two strangers with receiver belts. They paid lock boy silver to keep his eyes on his own boots."

"You saw sacks?"

"Saw enough to know grain from stone. Burlap dark with damp at the base, tarred ropes, no blessing line called." He looked at her straight for the first time. "Parish loads always call line. These did not."

She felt cold climb from her wet shoes into her calves. "Did they return before dawn?"

He shook his head. "One came back near noon next day light and empty. Other not till following dark, near as empty. Men said south road levy took part and spoil took rest. Men say many things when paid."

From the walkway outside came the slap of hurried feet. Tomas ducked through the doorway, breathing hard.

"I looked where you said," he told Elin without greeting. "Lock boy swears he never saw night carts. Then he saw my face and remembered receiver men bought lantern oil and two spare punting poles two nights past. Oil was charged to kitchen account by false mark."

Elin turned back to Hobb. "Kitchen account?"

Hobb grunted. "Harwood's lad signed with a curl trying to look like your kitchen steward. Bad imitation. I laughed when I saw it."

Tomas swore softly. "If they are charging transport to kitchen, they are already burying trail."

Elin set one penny into Hobb's palm and pushed the second toward Tomas. "Take this to lock boy. Tell him I need the oil chit if it still exists."

Tomas did not take the coin. "You should be nowhere near this hut after dark."

"And yet here we are."

"I mean if Quill hears you asking tide questions, he will not wait for hearing to break your desk."

"He already has my desk." She gathered her cloak tighter. "What he does not yet have is one line where floor mark, boat mark, and household hunger meet at the same hour."

Tomas stared at her, then nodded once. "I will get the chit if it has not burned."

He left. Hobb pocketed his penny and returned to the rope as if court and gallows were weather in another parish.

Elin stepped onto the quay. Mooring posts stood dark and wet, each ring rimmed with fresh rope burn. She touched one groove and brought away tacky tar mixed with wet fiber, the same fleck she had lifted from south granary threshold that morning. The water below sucked at piling roots with a hollow draw, tide turning.

Physical measure. Night extraction. Water carriage. Missing parish receipts.

Fraud was not buried in old flood books waiting for scholars and dry weather. It was moving now under private marks while infirmary broth thinned and Peter Carter coughed blood into cloth.

From the priory hill the compline bell began, softened by mist into round iron strokes. Each note arrived too late for the loads already gone and too early for whatever she would have to choose before next dawn. Quill's private barges had carried grain south two nights earlier, and the next ebb would come before the legal hour for patience.
