## Criteria Review

### 1. Sera's four questions — woven naturally, each answer making situation more alarming

PASS. The four questions are embedded in conversation, separated by Caleb's replies and the physical beats of the scene (cup lifted, cup set down, posture held). The questions in sequence:

1. Where did you find Wren — answer confirms Caleb was using off-CDL navigation methods, which is operationally alarming.
2. Her CDL status — answer confirms Wren arrived at the same pattern independently, which confirms the pattern is real, which makes the situation more alarming rather than less.
3. What does she want to do with what she's built — answer reveals Caleb has already formed an operational relationship with an unregistered off-CDL facility.
4. How long before the sync degradation affects the household flag — answer confirms Caleb has already calculated this, which confirms he is not in reactive crisis but is planning ahead, which from Sera's professional perspective is the most alarming answer of all.

The escalating pattern works as specified. Each technically valid answer confirms more deliberate thought, not less. Sera is not watching a man break down. She is watching a man who has been building something in front of her for months and has now arrived to describe it.

### 2. Sera's decision — clinical, prepared, not cruel; tragedy in the register itself

PASS. The decision sequence is well-executed. Key markers:

- She explicitly says she is not calling him broken and not disputing the evidence.
- The language of her decision is behavioral psychology vocabulary used precisely: "behavioral profile," "threshold I can manage responsibly," "professional obligation to my own assessment." This is her professional register. She is using the tool she has.
- The preparation is visible in the sentence structure. The pauses are deliberate, not searching. She has built the sentences before today.
- The line "I have a professional obligation to my own assessment" is doing significant work: it locates her decision in her professional identity rather than her personal one, which is the most honest and most devastating frame she could give it.
- Caleb correctly identifies the preparation: "You've been building this for longer than two weeks." She confirms without elaboration.
- She does not become cold. Her final line — "I don't want you to think I believe you're wrong" — is the closest she comes to breaking the register, and she still holds it.

The tragedy is in the register, as specified. She is not protecting her daughter badly. She may be protecting her in exactly the wrong direction. Both are true.

### 3. The daughter's goodbye — specific not symbolic; observational register doing emotional work

PASS. This is the chapter's most difficult execution requirement and it holds. Points of note:

- She is described through specific physical inventory: the blue sweater, the yellow cuff detail, the two braids Sera did this morning, the scar on the left cheekbone, the gray-going-brown eyes.
- The "weight of her hand" is rendered explicitly as unquantifiable, which is correct: Caleb reaching for his register and finding it insufficient is the right beat for this moment.
- Her dialogue is minimal and age-appropriate: "I know we're leaving." "Are you coming?" Nothing too knowing. She is performing gravity because she can feel gravity is required, not because she understands what is happening.
- The squeeze of her hand is not dramatized. It is described as the specific pressure of a child saying something she does not have words for yet. This is Caleb's observational register doing what it should.
- She does not look back. This is correct. Do not over-explain it; the draft does not.

The instruction "rendered exactly, in Caleb's observational register, which is the most devastating way to do it" is met. The section earns its weight without announcing it.

### 4. Caleb's prose register — plainer under emotional load; sentences shorter in later sections

PASS. The prose goes shorter and more primary in the departure and aftermath sections. The opening and exposition sections (coffee, NI degradation, describing the facility to Sera) have longer subordinate-clause constructions appropriate to Caleb in processing mode. The daughter's departure section: shorter sentences, primary vocabulary, no subordinate clauses in the key moments. The closing section is structured around short declarative inventory, which is the correct register for a man who is very afraid.

No ornate late-chapter passages. The rule holds.

### 5. The drawing — appears twice correctly

PASS. First appearance: Caleb notices it during the opening coffee sequence and does not dwell on it. The observation is absorbed into the paragraph, not isolated. Second appearance: in the final section, the drawing reappears after everything else has left the apartment. Caleb looks at it and considers what she might have meant by it, then declines to resolve the question. The draft does not over-explain this. The garden in careful parallel rows sits next to the description of the rows in Wren's soil beds, which is already established in Caleb's mind from Chapter 7. ### 6. Chapter ending — closes on specific unchanged things, not a statement

PASS. The final section is a list of unchanged things: chart, coffee cup, folded page, lock, windows, hum, drawing. It ends on "Nothing has moved." This is not a statement of feeling. It is an observational data point that carries the full weight of the chapter's emotional charge precisely because it refuses to name that charge. The ending obeys the rule.

### 7. Sera as non-villain — protecting their daughter; may be wrong; both truths held simultaneously

PASS. The draft holds both truths without editorializing. Caleb does not accuse her. He identifies that the decision was prepared before today. He understands what it is. He does not argue with it. The reader is not directed to judge Sera. What she is doing is defensible under her professional framework. What the novel will eventually show is that her professional framework has been built inside the system that is the problem, which means her measurement of what constitutes a threat to her daughter is itself contaminated. But the draft does not say this. It lets both truths be true simultaneously. This is correct.
