# Chapter 14: Acceleration

At 4:58 a.m., the north distribution line answers with a thin whistle when I crack the valve, then settles into the low, even channel sound that means flow has stabilized across all active beds. I hold my thumb against the clear segment near the manifold, watching for microbubbles and pressure flicker. Bed one is steady. Bed two is steady. Bed three runs clean after yesterday's nutrient flush. Bed four, the extension we brought online in late winter, stays inside the low-flow range I marked in pencil on the wall chart and have not erased.

The floor plates are wet from condensation at the seams. Cold has come off the night but not fully. My socks are damp at the edges where water found the stitching before I noticed. I crouch to check the threaded coupling at the east feed and stop with my hand half-extended because the coupling is dry, the new clamp is seated, and a tag of cut cardboard hangs from the pipe with baling twine tied in a clean square knot.

NORTH FEED SLOW LEAK. CLAMP REPLACED 04:21. TESTED 04:29.

The handwriting is blocky and upright, letters formed like they were learned on forms that allowed no overflow. Gideon writes the way he packs seed bins, exactly to line.

I straighten and listen. No drip at the old leak point. No ticking from thermal expansion where the clamp sits. Someone else found the problem before first light and fixed it without waiting for assignment.

This is what five people sounds like. Less silence between failures and correction.

I move down the row and run the rest of the checks by touch. Relay panel warm but not hot. Inverter hum flat. Pump draw within baseline. At the west corner, two trays of spinach show the pale stippling that means micronutrient drift is starting again, small enough to correct with today's mix if we catch it now. I mark it on the clipboard and smell wet compost, metal, and the faint medicinal edge of sulfur that still rises from D3 whenever morning air sits low in the room.

When I push through the inner door, Gideon is already outside at the wash station, sleeves rolled to the elbow, forearms dark with old scars and new mud. He is filling a bucket from the hand pump in measured strokes, full extension up, full compression down, never slamming the handle at the base of travel. He nods when he sees me, then checks his watch before he speaks.

"You heard the leak," he says.

"I saw the tag. Clamp looks good."

He wipes both hands on a rag folded into four equal panels. "The old band had a hairline split near the screw seat. I kept the failed part on the bench if you want to see it."

"I do."

"You get coffee first," he says. "You look like a man reading pipe threads instead of words."

His voice is rough from sleep and gravel dust. He says it as a field observation, not a joke.

Petra steps out of the annex carrying two meter housings against her hip. The left housing has a cracked bezel she repaired with resin and wire mesh. She sees me looking at it.

"It will hold another month," she says. "Maybe two if nobody drops it."

"You said that last month."

"And I was right last month." She sets the housings on the outer table and rolls her shoulders once. "Gideon says north feed stayed dry through ten-minute test. I want twenty before we call it solved."

I nod. "Add it to first block."

She pulls the slate toward her and writes with the pencil stub we keep tied to the table leg.

05:20-05:40 north feed retest (Petra)
05:40-06:10 nutrient batch A (Caleb)
06:10-07:00 west trench line check (Gideon + Theo)
07:00 breakfast

At two people, task lists are short and personal. At five, they become infrastructure.

The inner door opens again and Theo comes out with his notebook already open, thumb hooked under the back cover, pages folded from use and weather. He has grown stronger in the shoulders since winter, less visible shake in the hands, better color under the eyes when he has slept. He is still dressed half a layer too light for dawn and still moving too quickly through small spaces, as if speed can reduce consequence.

"I need ten minutes with you," he says.

I look at the notebook before I look at him. There are names on the visible page, stacked in a column with marks beside each.

"After first block," I say.

"This is first block."

He flips the notebook toward me. Nine entries, each with district code, old team affiliation, and a route note in the margin.

"I built overlap maps," he says. "No shared paths in the first three. If one goes dark, the others don't inherit exposure."

Petra does not look up from the slate, but her pencil stops moving.

Gideon sets the bucket down. Water sloshes against the rim and darkens the wood at his boots.

I close Theo's notebook gently and press it back into his hands. "After first block," I repeat. "Leak retest, nutrient mix, trench line. Then we talk."

He holds my eyes for half a second, then nods once. "Fine," he says. He turns, shoves the notebook into his jacket pocket hard enough to bend the cover, and walks toward the west yard where shovels hang on the rack.

Wren comes through the main door as he reaches the rack, taking in all four of us in one sweep the way she reads a room before she reads any sentence spoken inside it. Her scarf is damp at the ends where she has washed quickly in cold water and not fully dried. She looks at Theo's back, then at me.

"He brought names," she says.

"Yes."

"You told him later."

"Yes."

She nods and ties her scarf tighter. "Good. People who feel urgent before breakfast usually stay urgent all day. Put a shovel in his hands first."

By 6:12, I am at the mix bench weighing trace minerals into batch A while Petra calls out pressure numbers from the north feed retest and Gideon works the west trench with Theo under Wren's eye. The bench surface is steel over plywood and stays cold enough at this hour that my fingertips stick briefly where water has pooled. I split the magnesium load by grams, add iron chelate in a slow stream to avoid clumping, and stir with the long paddle until no powder skims the top.

Through the open annex door I can hear Theo and Gideon at the trench.

"Not from the shoulders," Gideon says. "You drive from the leg and let the handle transfer."

"I know," Theo answers, breathing hard.

"Then do what you know."

Metal bites packed soil in a steady rhythm. Theo is no longer new enough to be unfamiliar with the work. He is new enough to rush it when his mind is elsewhere.

At 6:47 a drone passes high west to east. Rotor texture is thin at this distance, almost filtered by cloud.

I do not write it immediately. I stir the batch, set the paddle down, rinse my hands, and step to the side table for the notebook.

06:47 high pass W->E, western corridor.

I circle the time once.

Breakfast is oats, preserved apple, and yesterday's bread reheated on the stove plate until the crust softens. We eat standing because two benches now serve five and no one has rebuilt furniture as a priority. Theo finishes first, wipes his bowl with a thumb, and keeps glancing toward me as if the chapter after breakfast has already started and the rest of us are delayed.

Wren takes the spoon from his hand before he can set it in the sink wrong side up. "Rinse first," she says. "Soap if you touch your face."

He does it. She hands the spoon back to him and does not soften the look.

After cleanup, we move to the main table. Petra sets meter logs to the left of the basin. Gideon lays out seed viability sheets. Wren brings the medical inventory. Theo remains standing with his notebook open, one finger marking his list.

I start with operations. "North feed repair holds through twenty-minute retest. East elbow dry. Bed four pulse cycle remained within night target. D3 sulfur still elevated. We keep no-plant markers in place until deeper excavation."

Gideon points to the seed sheet. "Tomato lot B loses viability curve in nineteen days. If we want full germination we start trays this week, not next."

Petra taps the cracked meter housing. "If this bezel fails before replacement, we lose accurate read on barrel two draw for at least a day while we swap by hand. I can build a temporary sight tube with clear line and marks, but it will drift."

Wren tears a strip of tape with her teeth and pins it over the curling map corner. "Insulin ampule count is unchanged. Two broken in transit, still stable supply for current people only. No margin for heat loss in summer if fridge relay fails."

Each line lands where it belongs, plain, specific, no one adding commentary that does not change action.

Then Theo sets both palms on the table and slides his notebook into the center.

"Nine names," he says. "All from inside systems that still touch allocation logic or compliance routing. I built them by access value and likely conversion."

He points to the first entry, tapping the page with a fingernail clipped short to the skin.

"Name one is analytics, healthcare triage mirror team. She can pull live prioritization drift if she comes out with her keyset. Name two is infrastructure audit, knows where they moved manual overrides after revision six. Name three is field compliance, has direct visibility on detainment queues before public classification."

Petra crosses her arms, meter logs still in one hand. "You ran contact with them already?"

"No," Theo says. "I built dormant paths only. Nothing sent."

Wren keeps her eyes on him. "Dormant means what, exactly."

He rubs the side of his thumb along the notebook edge until the paper starts to fray. "Means I confirmed route viability through legacy checks without opening message content. Timing only. Handshake latency. Presence, not contact."

"Through what device," I ask.

"Offline relay and two burned units."

"Recent?"

"Within three weeks. Not this week."

Wren's jaw shifts once. She looks at me without turning her head. I know the expression. We are both doing the same arithmetic from different disciplines.

At five people, failures that once consumed full days now close in hours. Beds are full. Trays turn. We are no longer one injury from immediate collapse. At five people, trust edges increased from one to ten.

Theo flips to another page and turns it so all of us can see. He has drawn route trees in pencil, each with a primary and two alternates, cross-out marks where paths overlap and are discarded.

"I know what happened when I moved too fast," he says. He does not look at anyone when he says it. "This is not that. One contact at a time. Forty-eight-hour quiet windows. Dead-drop only, no live exchange. We can add two people by midsummer if we start now."

"Add two mouths by midsummer," Gideon says, "and where do those calories come from until fall harvest."

Theo points to the west board. "Bed five frame is ninety percent done. If we finish manifold extension, we can support increased greens inside six weeks."

"If power holds," Petra says.

"If power holds," he agrees. "And if we stop operating like we have all year to decide."

He finally looks at me. His eyes are steady and too bright.

"They are not sleeping," he says. "People inside know something is wrong. Every day we wait, the ones with access get boxed deeper."

No one speaks for three seconds. In the silence, water clicks inside the stove kettle as heat builds under the metal. Wren reaches over, turns the knob down, and the click slows.

"You do not set tempo here because panic feels like clarity," she says.

Theo's shoulders rise, then settle. "I am not panicking."

"Then stop breathing like you are running," she says.

He exhales through his nose, longer this time, and puts his hands in his pockets so we cannot see whether they shake.

I close his notebook and keep my hand on it.

"We will review each name tonight," I say. "No sends before review. No viability checks before review. Until then, you stay on assigned work."

His jaw tightens. "Tonight is another day lost."

"Tonight is the schedule."

He looks at the notebook under my hand, then at the wall clock, then back at me. "Fine," he says. "Tonight."

We break to tasks. Wren takes Gideon to the south field edge to stake the no-plant zone around D3 before rain can blur markers. Theo goes with Petra to clear corrosion at barrel two's lower bracket and run continuity checks on the backup pump relay. I take the west corridor route to log drone intervals from the old tower and inspect the outer drop markers for disturbance.

By 11:14 I am beneath the transmission tower's shadow, notebook open on my thigh. Wind is from the northwest. The ground still holds spring damp under the top crust.

At 11:32 a high pass crosses west to east, nearly identical line to morning. At 12:41 a second pass comes lower, same corridor, wider lateral sweep before correction. At 14:03 there should be a gap if the old pattern holds. There is no gap. A third pass cuts north to south over the western approach and banks east in a turn radius tighter than baseline by visual estimate.

I write each time and draw arrows in the margin.

The pattern over the last month has been one western corridor pass approximately every four hours with minor weather noise. Today's spacing trends toward three. The adjustment is small enough to deny in conversation and large enough to matter in planning.

At 15:19, the lowest pass of the day clears the ridge line and drops enough that rotor pressure arrives as a chest vibration before the sound reaches full texture. I flatten against concrete without thinking and feel grit press through jacket fabric at shoulder and hip. The drone does not pause or circle. It moves through, but its camera angle is downward over the exact corridor Theo proposes for first contact packet transfer.

I wait two full minutes after sound loss before lifting my head.

15:19 low pass over west corridor, angle down, possible route learning.

I underline possible twice.

When I get back, Petra and Theo are at the north mount with both meter housings open on a tarp. Petra holds a flashlight between her teeth while she seats a wire at the terminal block. Theo steadies the housing with one hand and keeps the other ready with a screwdriver, following her pace instead of driving his own. It is good work when he lets it be good.

"Read me that value," Petra says around the flashlight.

Theo squints at the meter. "Point eight seven at idle."

"Again."

"Point eight six."

"Good. Hold there." She seats the wire and nods once. "Now tighten, not hard enough to crack resin."

He tightens to exactly the point where resistance changes and stops. She glances at him, then at the screw head, and says nothing, which from Petra is approval.

At 17:05 we close field work and move inside for evening review. Gideon brings in the marker stakes, boots caked with sulfur-stained clay from D3. Wren washes at the sink and scrubs under each nail with the brush reserved for soil work. Theo has already laid his notebook on the main table beside my operational logs. He has rewritten the nine names in cleaner print, one page per person, with known skills, likely motivations, and proposed approach windows.

The room smells of wet wool, metal filings, and onion from the pot on the stove. Outside, light has flattened to gray-blue across the yard.

I start at name one and read silently, then aloud for the table.

"Mira En. Analytics mirror team. Family in District Six. Prior corrective notices for unauthorized model critiques."

Theo leans forward, forearms on the table, hands clasped. "She flagged bias in pediatric queue weighting two years ago and got buried in reassignment. She knows where the bodies are in healthcare triage."

"And she has children still inside," Wren says. "Meaning she has leverage against her from first contact."

"Everyone has leverage," Theo says. "We do too."

Gideon folds the corner of a seed envelope until it forms a sharp point, then smooths it flat again. "Leverage is not a reason to recruit."

Theo opens his mouth, closes it, then taps name two.

"Rafi Cole. Infrastructure audit. No dependents. Burned once for refusing sign-off on a patched compliance report. He is already out at the edges."

Petra nods slightly. "No dependents matters."

"It does," Wren says, "and it is still a person, not a capacity upgrade."

We go through all nine this way. Skills. Routes. Risks. Who has family inside. Who has medical dependencies that cannot be met here yet. Who has access we could use and wounds that could turn access into damage. Theo has done the technical work. The logic of sequence is clean. The assumptions under urgency are not.

By name seven, his knee is bouncing under the table hard enough that the notebook shifts each time it hits wood. He catches it with his palm, forces the leg still, and keeps talking.

"If we wait until summer," he says, "these people will either be flagged or gone silent."

I keep my voice level. "If we move through a watched corridor, these people will be flagged faster."

He looks up sharply. "Watched how."

I feel Petra's eyes come off the page. Gideon's hand stops on the seed envelope. Wren stays still, waiting.

I could put the notebook from the tower on the table and change the chapter of this room in one sentence.

I say, "Watched in the way every corridor is watched now."

Theo's mouth tightens at one corner. "That is not an answer."

"It is the answer for tonight."

He leans back, chair legs scraping concrete. "You keep asking me to trust process while process keeps narrowing."

Wren sets both palms flat on the table. "He asked you for one thing. Tempo."

Theo turns to her. "Tempo is the whole decision."

"Exactly," she says.

The room goes quiet again. Outside, something taps once against the siding and rolls, probably a loose washer from the workbench edge where Theo left parts this morning. No one moves to pick it up.

I draw a line down the final page and write the only decision I can defend tonight.

NAME REVIEW COMPLETE
SEND WINDOW: DEFERRED 72 HOURS
RECHECK ROUTES + DEPENDENCY FLAGS
NO PRE-CONTACT ACTIVITY

I slide the page to the center.

"Seventy-two hours," I say. "In that window we finish bed five frame, validate barrel draw after today's repair, and run fresh route checks by ground observation only. Then we decide first contact, one name, one packet, one path."

Theo stares at the page. His breathing is audible again. He presses his thumb into the notebook spine until the skin blanches.

"Seventy-two," he repeats.

"Yes."

He nods once, small and controlled. "Then I want the first send."

"No," Wren says before I answer. "First send is mine or Caleb's."

Theo looks between us, then at Petra and Gideon, both of whom are deliberately studying their own papers. He closes the notebook, squares the edges against the table, and stands.

"Fine," he says. "Then tell me what to build in those seventy-two hours that actually changes the decision."

"West path decoys," Petra says without looking up. "You wanted action. Build that."

Gideon adds, "And trench reinforcement at the south markers before rain."

Wren points toward the tool wall. "Then sleep. You are no use if your hands shake tomorrow."

Theo picks up his notebook and hesitates by the door. "I am trying to make this place large enough to survive what is coming," he says, not turning around.

Wren's voice is quiet when she answers. "Then learn the difference between speed and capacity."

He leaves.

We clean in silence. Petra shelves the meter housings with cloth padding. Gideon labels two seed trays and stacks them in the dry cabinet. Wren banks the fire and turns down the lamp.

At 21:03, I sit alone at the main table with my operational notebook open to the tower log. The page is damp at the corner from my glove where mud had not dried. I recopy times in clean print.

06:47 high W->E
11:32 high W->E
12:41 lower W->E sweep
14:03 no expected gap
15:19 low west corridor angle down

Trend: western corridor interval compression, approx 4h -> 3h. Possible route learning.

The inner door opens. Theo steps in wearing a thermal layer and holding his notebook against his chest with both hands. His hair is still wet at the temples from a quick wash.

He does not sit until I nod toward the chair.

"I rewrote the nine," he says. "Removed the ones with dependent risk too high for first round."

He sets the notebook down and turns it toward me. Five names remain, each with two route options and a short line under motivation that reads like a diagnosis, not a pitch.

I rest my hand on the page. The paper is warm where his palms were. Outside, a drone passes far enough north that only pressure reaches the glass, not full sound.

Theo watches my face, waiting for permission, or refusal, or a word that can tell him whether tomorrow begins one way or another. I look at the first name, then the second, then at the line where he has written FIRST SEND WINDOW 04:40 if approved.

I keep my hand on his notebook and do not tell him what I wrote at 15:19.
