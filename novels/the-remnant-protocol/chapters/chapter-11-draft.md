# Chapter 11: Site

Wren points at the tank mount before she says good morning. The mount sits on the north wall of the former processing bay, above shoulder height, a steel frame bolted into poured concrete that has hairline fractures from freeze and thaw cycles older than either of us. One bolt on the lower right bracket has split through the shank and pulled two millimeters away from the washer. The load is still held, but only because the left side is taking weight it was not designed to take.

"That one fails first," she says. She taps the cracked bolt head with a wrench. The ring is higher than a sound bolt. "Re-brace before we add the second barrel."

She turns the wrench in her hand and holds it out to me. I take it. The handle is cold enough that the skin at my palm sticks for a moment before grip heat resolves it. It is a standard box-end, seventeen millimeter, steel worn smooth where years of use have removed the factory texture. My hand does not know this weight. It has known keyboards, stylus pens, glass touch surfaces, paper. Not this.

Wren has already moved to the ladder by the time I look up. She plants one boot on the first rung and tests it with a short bounce. "You staying through dusk, or riding back?"

I put the wrench in my coat pocket and take off my gloves. The dawn cold in this room has two layers, concrete cold at shin height and metal cold at hand height. "I am staying," I say.

She nods once, like a person acknowledging a measurement that is now confirmed, and climbs. "Then we work the order right. Tank, line, soil. The rest when there is time."

The Bible is inside my coat against my ribs. The single folded page is inside the paperback in my bag under the worktable. The notebook with one word written in dark pressure is in my left pocket. Build. I follow her up the ladder.

The bracket failure is visible at close range. The crack in the bolt is not fresh, which means it has held through multiple load cycles and then started to propagate after the temperature drop this month. I put the box end on the nut and test torque with light pressure. It gives too easily, then catches on deformation in the thread. Wren passes me a clamp from below, then a short segment of angle iron with two drilled holes.

"We sister the load," she says. "Do not trust the existing anchor."

"Material class?" I ask.

She squints at the iron and scrapes one edge with her thumb nail. "Mild steel. Salvaged rack support from the east bay. Strong enough if we spread the force across two points."

I hold the angle iron to the mount. The holes are not aligned for this bracket. "We need a third hole."

"We need a fourth as well," she says, and climbs down for the hand drill.

The drill is manual, brace type, with a crank and a chuck that closes by key. I have seen one in archive photographs. I have not used one. Wren tightens the bit, sets the point where I marked with a pencil line, and rotates the crank with short hard turns until the bit bites. Then she hands it up to me.

"Keep pressure vertical," she says. "If you walk the bit sideways you oval the hole and it will chatter loose by January."

I start the cut. The first turns are smooth. At eight turns, resistance increases and the handle pushes back into my palm. At fifteen, my shoulder starts to burn in a narrow line under the right clavicle where repetitive load lives when you are not conditioned for it. At twenty-two, the bit breaks through and my wrist drops because I put too much weight into the final push.

Wren catches the drill before it falls. "Less force at breakthrough," she says. She does not say anything about my hand shaking. "Second hole."

We cut the second hole and seat the brace. She holds the plate flat while I drive the bolt through and thread the nut from underneath. The nut catches at halfway because I am cross-threading. She reaches up, puts her fingers over mine, backs it out a quarter turn, and reseats it on the true thread without speaking.

"Now tighten," she says. "Not all the way. Alternate sides."

I alternate. Quarter turns, left then right, until the brace draws flush against the mount and the sound of the wrench against the nut changes from free ring to dull strike. We test by hanging half our weight from the lower frame and watching for movement at the anchor. No movement.

Wren leans back on the ladder and exhales through her nose. "Good. That buys us two weeks, maybe three."

"Two weeks to what?"

She climbs down and wipes her hands on her trouser legs. "To adding barrel two and moving the intake screen before freeze shuts the ground hard." She points toward the inner door. "Line next."

By 8:10 we are at the south wall trench where the current water line enters from the collection tank outside. The line is polyethylene, older than the current valve assembly, and routed too close to the upper slab where night cold reaches it before dawn. Wren has laid out the replacement section: twenty meters of newer line, two couplings, one check valve, one shutoff, one clamp kit, a coil of insulation wrap salvaged from transit HVAC ductwork.

The trench is shallow and narrow. We can re-route inside without digging exterior ground, which would take half a day and announce itself in ways soil disturbances announce themselves. We shut the feed and open the relief valve. The line spits once, then dribbles for forty seconds before pressure drops.

"You cut here," Wren says, kneeling by the marker line she drew in chalk. She puts the pipe cutter in my hand. "Straight cut. If you angle it, the coupling seals unevenly."

I kneel. Concrete cold comes through the fabric at both knees. The pipe cutter has a ratchet head with a spring return. I set it on the line and squeeze. The first bite dents but does not sever. The second bite goes through with a soft snap and a small spray of water the relief did not clear. The water hits my sleeve and runs toward my wrist.

I look at the exposed end. The cut is two degrees off perpendicular.
"Again," Wren says.

I cut one centimeter back to square it. This time the edge is clean. We fit the coupling, seat the gasket, tighten the clamp to stop point. My fingers are clumsy on the tiny set screw because cold has flattened sensation in the last joint of each finger. I pull my hand away, breathe into it twice, and continue.

We move down the trench in one-meter sections. Cut, seat, clamp, test alignment. The sequence has no interpretation layer and no persuasion layer. There is no one to convince. There is only whether the line seals.

At 9:37 the first full run is assembled and wrapped. We open the feed valve by one quarter turn. Water enters with a low rushing sound, then a higher note where it passes the new check valve. A bead forms at the third coupling and starts to travel.

"Leak," I say.

Wren is already there with the wrench. She tightens the clamp one eighth turn, then another. The bead stops growing. She wipes the joint with her thumb and waits for another bead. None appears.

"Again," she says.

We do this six times across the run. Three joints hold on first attempt. Two require adjustment. One requires disassembly because the gasket folded under insertion. By the time the line stays dry under full flow, my lower back has developed the deep hot ache that starts at the spine and radiates around to the abdomen when you have been bent for hours in a posture your body does not recognize as normal.

Wren stands and stretches both arms overhead until her shoulder joints click. "Eat now," she says. "Soil in forty minutes."

We eat at the worktable with our coats still on. Bread from yesterday, dense and slightly sour, plus preserved carrots and a cup of tea that tastes faintly of copper because the kettle sits on a coil that needs descaling. Wren tears the bread with both hands and sets half on my plate.

"If you stay," she says, looking at the loaf instead of at me, "you do not do Vael runs except emergency. Your NI is past red. In-city ping puts you on active watch, then physical pickup."

I hold the cup with both hands. The heat at the base reaches into my palms slowly. "I know the ladder."

"Knowing is not the same as accepting." She chews, swallows, takes another piece of bread. "When your address misses compliance inspection, housing code escalates. They will classify you as non-resident or evasive depending on which bot reaches the file first. Either way, your record hardens."

"It already hardened."

She looks at me now. There is dirt under one of her nails from the trench. "Harder than this. Eliminated classification is not theory."

I set the cup down because my hand is gripping too hard. The tin rings against steel. "If I return, they have me anyway. If I stay, I work until they have me."

Wren nods once. "Correct." She wipes crumbs from the table into her palm and drops them in a metal bowl for the hens in the west enclosure. "Then stop naming the choice like it is still open."

I do not answer immediately. My throat is dry from the cold air and from not sleeping. I drink the rest of the tea though it has gone bitter at the bottom. "Understood," I say.

She pushes back from the table and stands. "Good. Soil."

The adjacent field is east of the facility, beyond the concrete apron where transport trucks used to reverse into loading bays before integration rerouted all freight through automated corridors. The field has been unmanaged for years. Dead stalks from last season's volunteer growth stand in uneven rows where wind and runoff deposited seed. The top layer is frozen crust over damp loam. In sections near the old drainage ditch, the ground is compacted enough that a boot heel barely marks it.

Wren has marked a sampling grid with stakes and orange string. Four by six points, ten-meter spacing, plus two control points near the existing grow area for baseline comparison. She hands me a soil auger and a roll of sample bags.

"Depth at fifteen centimeters and thirty," she says. "Label both. If you hit rubble, offset half a meter and note it."

I press the auger into point A1. The first ten centimeters go easily. Below that, resistance changes from root mat to clay. I rotate the handle and lift. The core comes up striped: dark organic top, lighter brown below, then gray flecks at the base from old concrete aggregate. I place the first segment in a bag, mark A1-15. The second core at thirty contains more gray flecks and one rust-red shard I cannot identify.

By point A4, my hands are blistering under the base of the fingers where the auger handle rubs on each turn. The skin is intact but hot and tight. I switch grip orientation. By point B2, the first blister on my right palm opens. I wrap it with tape from the med kit and continue.

The wind shifts from northwest at around noon. It carries colder air off the empty lots beyond the boundary trees and pushes fine grit against the side of my face. The sensation is like static at first, then abrasion. I pull my scarf higher.

We move through the grid without talking much. Wren calls out point numbers; I repeat and bag. At C5 the auger hits metal at twelve centimeters, hollow sound, likely buried conduit from the old processing line. We offset and note it. At D3 the lower sample smells faintly sulfuric, not strong enough to rule out use but enough to flag for treatment if we plant there. At F1 both depths are clean and dark, crumb structure good, likely highest organic content in the set.

"That corner first," Wren says when I read out F1. She points with her pencil toward the southwest quadrant where drainage slope is gentler. "If we break ground there in spring, we can add three beds without changing current irrigation pressure."

"How many calories at full yield?"

She pulls a folded chart from her pocket and smooths it against the sample crate. "With current seed stock and weather averages, twelve hundred extra kilocalories per day in peak season, six hundred shoulder season, two hundred winter if we preserve right and nothing fails." She looks up. "Nothing fails is not a realistic variable."

"No," I say.

"Write with failure included."

I write it.

By 14:30 we have forty-eight sample bags labeled and stacked in two crates. We carry them inside and set them by the sink for pH and nitrate strip testing. The strips are older stock with expiration six months past. We run control tests against known soil from current beds to adjust interpretation drift.

The first strip set reads high acid at points A2 and B4, moderate acid elsewhere, near neutral at F1 and F2. Nitrate is low across the top row, moderate in the lower two. Phosphorus is inconsistent because one batch of strips has degraded dye at the edge, but trend suggests deficiency in half the field.

I tabulate on graph paper. Point, depth, pH range, NPK estimate, contamination flag, remediation requirement, estimated labor hours to correct. The columns expand as I work: compost volume needed, ash requirement, lime requirement if we can source it, transport burden from existing pile, person-hours at two workers versus three.

Wren watches me for a minute, then starts scraping mud from her boots with a flathead screwdriver. "You make spreadsheets in your head," she says.

"I make failure forecasts."

"Same thing if you are pessimistic enough." She stands, carries her boots to the door, bangs them twice against the threshold to dislodge packed clay, and returns in socks. "Do one more table. Water draw increase if we add those beds."

"I need pump performance at current temperature."

She points toward the panel by the sink where she has recorded daily pump readings in pencil for three years. "You have it."

I walk to the panel. The sheet is full to the edge, dates in a narrow hand, morning and evening readings, notes when values drifted: seal wear, intake clog, relay lag. Three years of data in graphite and paper tape. No cloud backup. No query interface. No abstraction layer between event and record.

At 15:10 we check the tank mount again. The new brace holds. At 15:32 we run full flow through the re-routed line and inspect each coupling dry. At 16:05 we move the second empty barrel into position below the mount but do not add water. Wren marks tomorrow's task list on the wall slate: mount reinforcement pass two, intake screen cleaning, compost turn, relay inspection.

I take my bag to the former seed storage room that is now my bunk. The room is narrow, concrete walls, one small window taped with translucent plastic to reduce heat loss, one cot with two blankets, one crate for personal items. I put the Bible in the crate, wrapped again in oilcloth, and set my notebook on top of it. The folded page stays in the paperback in my coat pocket until I decide where it can live without becoming easy to lose.

When I pull my gloves off, I see the beginning of dirt lines in the creases of my fingers that soap will not fully remove tonight. There is dried blood at the edge of the tape on my right palm where the blister opened. I peel the tape slowly. The skin beneath is raw, half-moon shaped, painful when exposed to air.

I wash at the utility sink. The water is cold enough to ache at first contact. Soil runs off in brown threads. Metal smell from the pipes remains on my fingers even after scrubbing with lye soap. I dry my hands on a towel that has been washed too many times and stiffened at the edges.

By dusk the temperature has dropped again. We close the outer vents to the night setting and check latch points on both service doors. Wren walks the perimeter with a headlamp set to red filter. I follow with the clipboard and note points that need repair: west fence section loose at post three, greenhouse panel crack widening on upper seam, one hinge on the south gate binding under cold contraction.

When we finish, she stands at the threshold and rolls her neck once to release stiffness. "You did not drop anything important," she says.

"I dropped the drill."

"I said important." She reaches past me, takes the clipboard, and hangs it on the hook by the door. "Eat, then write what you saw. If it stays in your head, you lose half by morning."

We eat lentils and root vegetables from a pot that has been simmering since afternoon. No one speaks for several minutes. The room sounds are enough: spoon against bowl, low hum of inverter, one short cluck from the hens settling in the enclosure. My forearms are heavy in the way they get after repetitive load. Each time I lift the spoon, the tendons at my wrist pull over inflamed tissue and complain.

After we clean the bowls, I go outside with the notebook and sit on an overturned crate facing the east field. Light is almost gone. The horizon holds a thin strip of steel gray above black ground. The facility behind me gives off a low warm glow through patched windows. I turn to a blank page and start the list.

Water system: secondary barrel installation, intake screen redesign, redundancy for check valve, winter insulation over exposed line, replacement gasket stock, manual pump backup.

Power: battery capacity margin at current draw, relay replacement for heater circuit, spare inverter board, fuel reserve for emergency generator even though we avoid running it, insulation repair at east wall to reduce overnight loss.

Food production: spring field remediation southwest quadrant first, compost volume expansion, seed viability testing by lot, preservation schedule, rodent-proof storage bins, calorie model by season with failure ranges.

Shelter: bunk expansion if third and fourth occupants arrive, weather sealing for north door, mold inspection in lower annex, spare blankets, clothing repair station.

Medical: inventory antibiotics by expiration, suture stock, analgesic ration plan, splint materials, infection protocols when water pressure is low, documentation for triage if arrivals exceed capacity.

Security and movement: route table updates by week, dead drop protocol revisions, light discipline chart, noise discipline after dusk, fallback points if facility compromised, burn plan for sensitive notes, signal system for approach without names.

Work allocation: daily minimum tasks, weekly maintenance rotation, contingency if one person injured, training sequence for anyone new so failures do not cascade through ignorance.

Governance: decision protocol when two disagree, allocation protocol when resources tighten, recordkeeping standard that survives if one of us is taken.

The list keeps growing. Every item generates two more items when traced to dependency. Add beds requires water. Add water requires mount and seals. Mount and seals require materials we do not yet have and routes we may not be able to run once patrol density changes. More people require food. More food requires soil correction and labor hours we only have if one of us is not sick and not hiding and not gone.

By the time I stop, my hand is cramping along the thumb pad. I flex it and watch the tendon move under skin that is already rougher than it was this morning. The page is full front and back. I turn it and continue on the next page, then the next.

When I finally look up, Wren is standing in the doorway with one hand on the frame, waiting without calling my name.

"You coming in?" she asks.

"In a minute," I say.

She studies my face for a moment, then nods. "Do not stay out long. Temperature drops hard after full dark." She goes back inside and lets the door close most of the way, not latched, leaving a thin bar of light across the dirt.

I read the list once from top to bottom. It is not a plan. It is an inventory of requirements for a thing that does not exist yet and may not exist in time. The first line in this notebook is still one word. Build. Everything after it is consequence.

I put the pen down and hold my hands out in the last light. Dirt is worked into the skin along the cuticles and around the new tape on my palm. The callus has not formed. The pain has.

This is a very long list for a man who started today, and the system that erased me in Vael has already started counting how long I have been gone.
