# Chapter 10: What the Water Told Noah

By the time my NI moved from Restricted pending to full Restricted classification, I had already stopped sleeping through the sync window. The third missed cycle produced the expected notifications and the expected language about restoration pathways and the expected sequence of access changes: kiosk refusals first, then building authentication failures outside registered residence, then transit friction, then the category the documentation calls provisioning non-availability and the people I worked with used to call, in rooms with closed doors, hard eject. The language for this is careful at every level. The outcome is not careful. You eat where you planned ahead, or where someone already outside the system has planned ahead for you.

I made three trips to Verdant after the classification changed. I made them at night, by routes that do not exist on current city maps and therefore do not exist to anyone who has not spent enough hours with pre-integration survey material to learn where the maintenance roads used to connect before the corridor expansions and the fencing and the drone mesh regularization. Wren gave me her cycle table for the rural boundary sweeps. She had three years of observational data in a weatherproof notebook, line after line of dates and times and cloud cover and vehicle noise and the specific kind of silence that means a unit has passed and the next one has not yet started its sweep. The table was accurate. It was also imperfect in the way all field data is imperfect. There is no perfect table for an active system.

On the fourth trip, I leave the apartment at 2:11 a.m. The hall lights in Threshold East are at nighttime low power, enough illumination to keep liability metrics inside standard range without paying for full-output operation. The entry lock resolves on the first cycle because inside registered residence I am still authenticated. The lock sounds different from before the downgrade. I noticed the change two nights ago and confirmed it against memory this morning. The second harmonic in the actuator is lower. Old mechanical wear, probably, not policy. Not every change in a system is surveillance.

Outside, the air has the dry metallic quality that arrives when the atmospheric stations have reduced particulate suppression for the overnight low-traffic period. You can taste the city less. My bicycle chain ticks once on the first full pedal turn and then settles. I counted six full mesh passes between the north corridor and the old service route at Sector Twelve. I waited through two of them behind a concrete noise barrier that used to surround a distribution yard and now surrounds nothing but gravel and weeds and one rusted sign whose lettering has been removed by weather down to a uniform matte gray. The drones do not look for men crouched behind concrete at 3 a.m. unless those men have already crossed a threshold in the queue.

Forty-three minutes from the boundary marker, the managed road surface ends and the old agricultural service cut begins. The temperature drops in a way that is immediate and honest. The cold here is not the calibrated cold of transit corridors. It is ground cold, drainage cold, the cold of air collecting where the land dips and holding there. My gloves are thin at the fingertips from two weeks of night riding and work I am not trained for. The brake lever bites into that thin place and I can feel the shape of the metal through the worn synthetic fabric.

I kill my bike light before the final rise and walk the bicycle the last three hundred meters. No engine noise, no voice traffic, no rotor note. One dog, far to the east, then quiet again. Verdant sits low from this angle, dark except for one panel of muted green light at the far side where the lowest grow setting keeps the winter trays alive through the night. Wren is either asleep or in the south room checking the water line, where she goes when the temperature drops below the range she prefers. I do not call out. We do not use names outside walls anymore.

The cache is in the cistern annex, in the old concrete chamber where the first filtration array failed before she replaced it with the current gravity sequence. We use it for exchanges when one of us is on a route the other cannot risk that night. The chamber smells of iron, wet stone, and old algae trapped in seams no cleaning brush reaches. I kneel, unseat the second panel, and reach into the dry section behind the intake pipe.

My fingers touch canvas first. Then paper.

For a moment I think it is one of Wren's notebooks wrapped against moisture, and then I pull it clear and the shape is wrong for a notebook. Too thick at the spine, too narrow in height, sewn signature bulge visible even through the oilcloth wrapper. The object is heavier than I expect for its size. I know what it is before I unwrap it because there are only a few objects built this way. A book.

The wrapper is two layers, oilcloth over waxed paper, tied with ordinary twine in a knot that can be opened without cutting. This matters. If you expect the receiver to preserve a thing, you tie it so it can be tied again. I untie it with gloves still on and then remove a glove because I cannot feel enough through the fabric. The cover is dark, almost black, with no jacket, edges worn pale where hands have taken it on and off shelves for years. The paper edge is uneven from use. The spine has been repaired once with cloth tape the color of old bone. The title is embossed and nearly rubbed away. Holy Bible.

I stand in the chamber with the book in my hand and do nothing for longer than I intend. Water moves through the active pipe to my left with the low continuous sound of pressure and stone. The floor is cold through the soles of my boots. I have not held a Bible in twenty years. I have seen scripture in archived text blocks, in digitized fragments, in the quotations people still attach to obituary posts before those posts vanish under moderation protocol. That is not the same thing. This is a physical object built to be opened. I rewrap the oilcloth loosely, keep the book under my coat, and go inside.

The main room is in night configuration. Three overhead fixtures off, one on at minimum. Grow banks in rows along the interior wall, each strip running at low power, enough photosynthetic input to hold the seedlings in maintenance without draining the battery reserve. The air has two temperatures: cold above shoulder height, warm near the tables where compost heat and electrical ballast collect. The humidity is higher than outside and carries the smell of damp soil, copper from the old intercom coils, and yeast from yesterday's bread cooling rack. Wren's boots are by the inner door. She is here. I hear no movement.

I set the book on the steel worktable under the weakest light that still allows reading. The table has scratches from years of knife work and wrench slips and one burn mark where a soldering iron rolled and lay for six seconds before someone moved it. There is an empty cup on the corner with a ring of dried tea at the base. Wren's handwriting is on a page clipped to the wall behind the sink: valve rotation schedule, tomorrow's notation already filled in. She did not leave the book there. Someone else did.

I sit. My knees complain from the ride when I bend them past ninety degrees, the specific stiffness of repeated cold exposure and too little recovery. I hold my palms around the tin cup for a minute even though there is no tea in it. The metal warms slowly. I open the book.

The first pages I see are not the beginning. They are maps, then thin columns of names, then a section header whose top corner is torn away. I turn forward, then back, then stop doing both and let the pages fall where their wear wants them to fall. The book opens near the middle of the first quarter, where repeated use has loosened the binding memory. Genesis 6.

I read standing first, then sit again because my balance feels wrong. The words are familiar in shape before they are familiar in meaning. I remember my grandfather's kitchen table, pine top worn smooth where elbows had polished it over decades, his thumb flattening a page while he read aloud in a voice that sounded like gravel and summer dust. He held the book with one hand and tapped lines with the knuckle of the other when he wanted to mark structure. Specific instruction, he would say, before reading a number. He liked numbers in scripture. Said you could test a person by what they did with numbers.

I read the passage through once without stopping. Corruption, violence, decision. Then the instruction sequence: make an ark of gopher wood, rooms, covering, length, breadth, height, window, door, levels. Three hundred cubits. Fifty. Thirty. I read it again.

There are many ways to read this section. The one that arrives first for me is architectural. The dimensions are not symbolic in presentation. They are operational. The text does not ask the builder to feel the right feelings and then improvise. It specifies load-bearing structure, material class, and envelope treatment. Seal it inside and outside with pitch. Place the door in the side. Build three levels. The instructions are complete enough to begin and incomplete enough that beginning still requires judgment. No section provides rainfall projections. No section provides expected duration under load. No section provides a model for population behavior once inside the structure. No output documentation appears before construction.

I put my index finger on the line with the dimensions and hold it there while I calculate rough scale from memory. Cubit conversion varies by period and source. Use conservative modern approximation and the hull length exceeds anything a private builder in this context could justify absent an external mandate. The ratio, however, is stable. Long enough for distribution, narrow enough for structural integrity in moving water, height sufficient for deck separation and air volume. It is not random. It is not poetic. It is work.

The water line in the south wall knocks once and settles. Somewhere in the inner room a hinge moves, then stillness again. My hands are on the page and I realize I have tightened them enough to crease the thin paper near the margin. I release pressure and smooth the page with the side of my palm.

I have spent fifteen years tracing outputs back to inputs. I have spent the last five months tracing false outputs to hidden inputs and hidden inputs to people who built systems pretending those inputs did not exist. The phrase I have used for this, in reports and in my own notes, is input chain. The input chain in a valid system terminates in a source that can be named, tested, and questioned. The input chain in PACEM's audit deception terminates in null. That was the point. That is still the point. I look at the page again.

Here, the input is instruction. The source is named. The output is not described except as event: waters, flood, destruction, survival by obedience to specification. No proof set accompanies the instruction. No pilot case. No independent review board. No statement of intermediate success metrics. Build this, then enter it.

I do not know what to do with that in theological terms. I know what to do with it in engineering terms. A man received parameters and built a structure in advance of a condition he could not verify. He acted on source trust.

I close my eyes for a moment and the room does not go dark because the grow lights are still visible through my eyelids, a diffuse green-red field that pulses when I move my head. My back hurts from the ride and from the last month of sleeping in positions that keep one ear available for the hall lock and the stairwell and anything that sounds like purposeful footsteps outside my door after midnight. My jaw is tight. I unclench it. The muscle jumps once.

The sentence from my kitchen table in Vael returns exactly as I said it there: What would it mean to stop trying to be believed.

At the time I asked it as a question about audience. I thought the variable was people. If not Sone, then who. If not the archive contacts, then who. If not formal channels, then who can still receive evidence and act on it. The question was still pointed outward. This text points it elsewhere.

Stop trying to be believed by people who are built to disbelieve what threatens their continuity metrics. Build for the people who will need a structure when those metrics fail them. Build before the water, not after. Build without confirmation that anyone will come. I open my notebook.

It is the same notebook I have carried since October: weatherproof cover, graph paper, half the pages filled with route times, lock-cycle observations, provisioning counts, battery draw estimates, rough lists of materials that might be moved without drawing query flags in the old months when query flags still mattered to me. The last page has numbers from two nights ago, pump output versus barrel loss at near-freeze temperature. My handwriting in cold is narrower, letters leaning right as the hand stiffens.

I turn to the next blank page. I start to write what I usually write: objectives, dependencies, constraints. I stop. I write one word: Build.

The pen point catches at the end of the d where the paper fibers rise. I press the tip down again and darken the line until the word is almost black compared to the rest of the page. There is no list under it. There are no numbered items. The absence is not confidence. It is not certainty. It is sequence. One action before all the others.

I sit with the notebook open and the book beside it and listen to the building. The low hum of the inverter. Water moving through the gravity line in intervals as the tank equalizes. A branch on the north side touching sheet metal with each push of wind. Two small clicks from the heating relay as it cycles to maintain the minimum range for the trays near the east wall. The room has the particular quiet of a place being kept alive by work that does not stop because no one is watching.

At 5:02 a.m. by my watch, first light starts to unseal the highest edge of the east windows. The interior is still mostly dark, but the dark has direction now. I stand, close the notebook, and read the passage once more, slower than before. I do not search for argument against it. I do not search for emotional confirmation. I read the dimensions again. Length. Breadth. Height. I close the book.

For a moment I hold it the way my grandfather held his, palm under the spine, thumb on the page edge, weight centered as if the object could slide if held carelessly. The memory is exact enough that my hand adjusts itself before I decide to adjust it. He used to pause like this before setting the book down at the end of morning reading, not for effect, just to make sure the page he wanted would open easily the next day.

I do not know who put this book in the cistern annex. I do not know if the person meant it for me specifically or for whoever reached into that compartment first. I do not know if there is another note somewhere I have not found. I do not know if Wren knows more than I do and has chosen not to say.

I know that someone carried a physical Bible across monitored ground and put it where our hands would meet in the dark.

I slide it inside my coat, against the inner layer where body heat will keep the paper above the cold point on the ride back. The cover presses flat against my chest. I can feel the corners when I breathe.

Outside, the sky over the eastern fields is lifting from black to iron gray. The route back to Vael is still possible for another hour before traffic and light narrow the options. I pick up my gloves, my notebook, and the wrench I forgot to return to the pegboard last week. I set the wrench back where it belongs.

Then I step into the morning with the book under my coat and the word in my notebook and no plan beyond it.
