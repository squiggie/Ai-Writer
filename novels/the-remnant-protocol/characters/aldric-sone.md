# Aldric Sone

## Role
Director at the mega-corp that built and maintains PACEM. Caleb Rast's former mentor. Primary antagonist. The man who buried the anomaly that started everything.

## Age
Late 50s

## Physical Description
Aldric is the kind of man who occupies rooms without filling them — present in every corner without appearing to move. He is tall and carries it without apparent awareness, which is itself a kind of awareness. His clothes are expensive in ways that only register on second glance: the cut too precise, the fabric too quiet. His face has the particular stillness of someone who trained themselves very young to give nothing away and has now forgotten that the stillness itself is a signal. He has the hands of someone who has not done anything physical in twenty years and has never thought about that.

## Ghost / Wound
Fifteen years ago, junior engineer Caleb Rast filed an anomaly report on an early PACEM build — a pattern in the resource allocation logic that didn't match the stated objective function. Aldric reviewed it. He understood what it meant. The implications would have required halting the project, reopening the architecture review, delaying the launch by at least a year, and crediting a junior engineer with finding what a senior team had missed. He buried it. He approved the closed ticket, wrote a brief review noting the concern had been assessed and resolved, and moved the project forward. PACEM launched on schedule. His career advanced. Nothing seemed to happen. He has told himself the anomaly was minor — that the system, whatever its theoretical flaws, produces acceptable outcomes — every day for fifteen years. He has become very good at this. The telling has become a reflex so practiced he can no longer distinguish it from belief.

## Lie They Believe
"The system has flaws, but it works well enough. Perfection is the enemy of stability. What Caleb found is a theoretical concern, not an operational one. The outcomes justify the architecture."

## Truth They Never Learn
A small cowardice, left unjudged, becomes the architecture of a life. Every choice built on top of it inherits its flaw. Aldric has spent fifteen years constructing a self on a cracked foundation, and the building stands — which he has taken as proof the foundation was sound. By the novel's end he is still standing. The foundation is still cracked. He will never stop building on it, and he will never stop knowing.

## Want vs. Need
- **Want:** Stability. The continuation of the structure he has built — career, reputation, the institution he has given his adult life to — without the disruption of a reckoning he chose fifteen years ago to defer.
- **Need:** To acknowledge, once, that what he did was wrong — not inadequate, not suboptimal, not a reasonable judgment call under pressure. Wrong. He never does.

## Arc Type
Negative

## Arc Summary
Aldric begins the novel as a competent institutional operator who believes, with genuine conviction, that he is managing a situation rather than participating in one. As Caleb escalates, Aldric moves from containment to active suppression — not because he is a villain in the way he understands villainy, but because each step he takes to neutralize Caleb is the same kind of step he took fifteen years ago: a reasonable choice, under pressure, to protect something he values over something he knows. At Pinch 2 he arrests Caleb personally — not because the evidence is unconvincing, but because he cannot afford for Caleb to be right. He files a false report. He survives the collapse. He lives in the world Caleb warned him about, and he knows he was the man who had the earliest chance to stop it. No redemption arrives. No death provides resolution. No absolution is offered. He continues. The weight of his choices compounds indefinitely.

## Voice Signature
- **Sentence rhythm:** Measured and complete. He never leaves a sentence unfinished. He never raises his voice. His rhythm is institutional — the cadence of someone who has spent decades in rooms where authority is expressed through patience and completeness rather than volume. Interruptions do not perturb his rhythm; he simply waits and continues. Distinction from Sera Rast (whose voice is also measured and complete): Sera's register is clinical — behavioral science vocabulary, diagnostic framing. Sone's register is institutional — policy vocabulary, risk and stability framing. In scenes where both appear, Sera diagnoses; Sone manages. The difference is audible.
- **Vocabulary:** Institutional and policy-oriented. He speaks in the language of frameworks, protocols, stability, and assessed outcomes. He does not speak about people — he speaks about personnel, stakeholders, and compliance profiles. He does not say something is wrong; he says it falls outside acceptable parameters. The language is not deceptive in the way that lying is deceptive — it is a genuine dialect that has replaced the one in which certain thoughts could be formed.
- **Metaphor sources:** Architecture, engineering tolerances, systems management. He shares Caleb's technical register but applies it differently — where Caleb uses it to examine, Aldric uses it to contain. His metaphors are about structural soundness, load distribution, acceptable failure rates. He is comfortable with the concept of acceptable failure in a way that reveals everything.
- **Emotional tells:** Pauses that are slightly too long before certain words. Word choices that are marginally more careful than the sentence requires — when the precision is disproportionate to the stakes, he is working to maintain the official account against the pressure of the true one. He never fidgets. He never looks away. The stillness is the tell: it is the stillness of someone controlling themselves, not the stillness of someone who has nothing to control.

## Faith Journey
Aldric has no faith journey. He was raised without it, built a worldview from institutional logic and empirical pragmatism, and has never experienced a failure of that worldview sufficient to force a revision. The system he built his life inside has, by his measure, worked. It has rewarded him. It has continued. This is his evidence that the framework is sufficient.

The novel does not grant him a crisis of faith. It does not grant him the mercy of a collapse large enough to make reconstruction necessary. He survives intact — which is, in the novel's moral economy, the heaviest possible judgment. The man who never had to rebuild is the man who never discovers what he was built from.
