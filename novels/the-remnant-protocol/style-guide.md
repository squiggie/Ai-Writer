# Style Guide: The Remnant Protocol

## The Core Problem

Across all nine completed chapters, the prose operates at one consistent level of compression. Events are reported with precision. Caleb observes everything — the amber light on the entry panel, the fold of Sera's hands, the oxidation level on Wren's roof cladding — but the reader observes alongside him without being inside the observation. The writing *describes* experience rather than *rendering* it. The result is a novel that reads as intelligent and architecturally sound but does not, in its current form, create the kind of physical immediacy that makes a reader lose track of the room they are sitting in.

The failure is not voice. Caleb's observational register is consistent and interesting. The failure is presence. The reader is never quite IN the room — never in the quiet office at 11pm on a Tuesday when Caleb finds the null reference, never in the cold forty minutes outside Vael when he first smells unmanaged air, never in the kitchen at the moment the door closes and his daughter is gone. Every scene is reported from the same careful distance, and that uniformity erases the distinction between scenes that should move fast and scenes that should stop time.

The corrective is not to abandon Caleb's analytical voice — that voice is the novel's greatest asset. It is to let experience arrive in his body before his mind has finished processing it. It is to let some scenes breathe, slow, become physical. It is to let the emotional content find form in objects and sensory detail rather than in analysis.

---

## Guiding Principle: The Body Is First

Caleb's mind processes everything. That is the novel's premise. But the body receives information before the mind names it. The goal of this style guide is to restore the body's priority — to let physical sensation, sensory detail, and the specific weight of objects arrive on the page before Caleb's analysis catches up with them.

This does not require sentimental language or interiority-speak. Caleb will never say he is afraid or sad or grieving. He will say the building's ambient hum, which he has heard for nine years, sounds different now. He will say his hands, which moved to save the file before his mind finished the argument for saving it, are doing something his mind has not yet caught up to. The body is the novel's access to emotion. Use it.

## Techniques by Comp Author

### Kazuo Ishiguro — *Klara and the Sun*, *Never Let Me Go*

**The principle**: Objects and routines reveal what the narrator won't say about themselves. The most emotionally significant facts in an Ishiguro novel are communicated through what the narrator notices, not through what they disclose.

**Applied to this novel**: Every time Caleb catalogs objects in a room — the chart, the folded page, Sera's coffee cup in the dish rack, his daughter's drawing on the refrigerator — he is telling the reader where his attention goes when he has no place else to put it. This is already working in the novel. The problem is that these catalogs often arrive without the physical texture that makes them accumulate weight.

*What Ishiguro does*: He gives you the specific temperature of an object, the sound of a door closing in a house that is about to be left, the smell of a room that a character has just walked out of. The mundane is not merely noted — it is fully sensed, because for the narrator it is everything.

*Concrete technique*: When Caleb describes an object that carries emotional weight, give it one additional sensory dimension. Not "the chart on the table" but "the chart on the table, the tape at the far corner giving up its hold in the morning warmth." Not "the coffee cup in the dish rack" but "the coffee cup in the dish rack, the small ring of soap film at its base still wet."

### Cormac McCarthy — *The Road*, *No Country for Old Men*

**The principle**: The body under pressure is rendered without explanation. Pain, cold, hunger, fear — these are physical facts, not emotional states. McCarthy never tells you his characters are afraid. He tells you the man's hands moved to the rifle in the dark before he had identified the sound.

**Applied to this novel**: Caleb's body has been under sustained pressure since the termination. He has been cold (the forty-minute bicycle ride in December). He has been still for hours at a time (the night at the kitchen table). He has been afraid, which in him manifests as heightened precision of observation. The novel describes these states but does not fully render them.

*What McCarthy does*: He slows a moment — a man moving through dark, a father watching his son sleep — and gives you the physical data of that moment with no interpretation. The reader does not need to be told what the moment means.

*Concrete technique*: In key scenes, stop the analysis and give three to five lines of purely physical data. No interpretation. What does the skin feel. What does the air contain. Where is the weight in the body. What sounds are present. Then let the analysis resume. This technique is most needed at: the moment of the null reference discovery (Ch. 1), the moment the wipe completes (Ch. 4), and the moment the door closes on Caleb's daughter (Ch. 8).

### Walter M. Miller Jr. — *A Canticle for Leibowitz*

**The principle**: Institutional absurdity is rendered with the gravity of sacred text. The mundane administrative machinery of power is described with the same patience and specificity as liturgy. This is both satirical and genuinely reverent — Miller treats the bureaucracy as a serious thing because it is.

**Applied to this novel**: PACEM's outputs — the compliance notices, the NI notifications, the yellow kiosk bar, the compliance officer reading a record in the order it appears — are already rendered with this quality. The forty-one-word bone-conduction notification in Ch. 9 is excellent Miller territory: the narrator dissecting a bureaucratic text with full attention, finding what the text is doing underneath what it is saying. This technique should be applied more consistently to physical space. Miller's institutions have weight, smell, and specific light.

*Concrete technique*: When Caleb enters an institutional space — the compliance office, the transit hub, the lobby of Nexus Corp — give it one or two details that establish the specific quality of the light and the specific quality of the sound. Not for atmosphere. For the sensory signature of what power looks like in physical space. The compliance office waiting room in Ch. 9 is close but thin.

### George Orwell — *1984*, *"Politics and the English Language"*

**The principle**: Plain language is not simple language. It is exact language. The most devastating sentences in Orwell are completely plain and completely precise. "It was a bright cold day in April, and the clocks were striking thirteen." No adjective does extra work. Every word is load-bearing. The flatness is the effect.

**Applied to this novel**: Caleb's analytical voice is already Orwellian in its plainness, and this is a strength. The problem is that the plainness is uniform — it does not vary with the emotional register of the scene. In Orwell, the flattest sentences appear at the moments of maximum pressure. The prose is stripped not because the content is unimportant but because the content is so important that any ornamentation would be a lie.

*Concrete technique*: Identify the three or four sentences in a scene that carry the most weight. Strip them to pure declarative minimum. No qualifications, no commentary. Caleb's line "The door closes" at the end of Ch. 8 Section 3 is correct Orwell. The line "She goes" at the end of Sera's departure is correct Orwell. Find more of these moments — they are the emotional punctuation of the chapter — and make sure the prose is building toward them rather than already at full compression throughout.

## Scene Break Policy

`---` breaks in draft files render as white-space breaks (typically a centered `•`) in a formatted book. The standard for literary fiction is zero to two per chapter.

**A break is warranted only when:**
1. A significant time jump occurs (hours or days, not minutes)
2. A location shift combined with a genuine emotional register change
3. The white space itself is doing rhetorical work (a held breath, a rupture)

**A break is not warranted when:**
- One action follows directly from another in the same scene
- A transition sentence could carry the reader between moments
- The writer simply didn't write the transition

**Maximum:** two per chapter, one preferred. When in doubt, write through it.

## The Five Cardinal Rules

These rules apply to every scene in every chapter.

### 1. Key scenes must slow down.

Not every scene — the channel-testing sequence in Ch. 6 should move fast; the compliance office visit in Ch. 9 can maintain its procedural pace. But the moments of maximum emotional or narrative weight must slow to a pace that is different from the novel's baseline.

The novel currently moves at one consistent speed. A reader can't tell the difference between the scene where Caleb finds the null reference and the scene where Caleb sends letters that won't reach anyone. Both are important. They should not feel the same.

Slow down: the discovery scene (Ch. 1), the wipe discovery (Ch. 4), the first meeting with Wren (Ch. 7 — already partially done), Sera's departure (Ch. 8 — already partially done), the moment after the door closes (Ch. 8), the final question (Ch. 9).

### 2. The body must be present in emotional scenes.

Every time Caleb's body is described doing something — his hands still on the keys, the cold coat, the weight of his daughter's hand — that is the novel's emotional access point. These moments are not enough. The body needs to be present more consistently in scenes of high pressure.

Specifically: in scenes where Caleb has been sitting for a long time (the night at the table, the hours after the wipe), acknowledge the physical cost. Stiffness. The specific quality of a person who has been very still for a very long time. When he rides back from Wren's facility on a bicycle after four hours in December, his legs are carrying a specific debt — the novel mentions it briefly. It should be felt.

### 3. Dialogue requires one physical beat per exchange.

Current dialogue is precise and serves the story, but exchanges often proceed as pure information transfer. Every significant exchange should have at minimum one moment where a character does something with their body — picks up a cup, sets down a page, does not move when they normally would — that tells us something the words don't.

The Sone interview (Ch. 4) is improved by the dialogue additions, but the physical beats are thin. The Wren meal scene (Ch. 7) moves past too quickly. Sera's departure speech (Ch. 8) is the novel's best dialogue work — use it as the template.

### 4. Sensory data must be specific, not generic.

"The cold" is not enough. "The specific cold that means the sun has not yet reached a surface" (Ch. 7 opening) is enough. The distinction is the kind of observation Caleb would actually make — not emotional color, but precise physical taxonomy. He categorizes everything. Let his categorization of sensory experience be as precise as his categorization of audit layer anomalies.

Generic: "The air was cold." Specific: "The air had the mineral edge of the building's overnight temperature differential, the kind that means the climate system hasn't yet completed its morning cycle."

### 5. The most important line in a section is usually the last one. Build toward it.

The chapter and section endings that work in this novel — "The door closes," "Nothing has moved," "What would it mean to stop trying to be believed?" — work because the preceding prose has been building toward them with increasing compression. The sections that don't work as well are the ones where the final line is at the same compression as everything before it.

Map each section to its final line. Is everything before it building toward that moment? If not, the pacing of the section needs adjustment.

## Caleb's Voice: Expanded Guidelines

### What makes it work
Caleb processes experience as data. Every observation is a measurement. Emotional content arrives in him as a change in what he notices, not as a direct feeling. This is both the character's defining trait and the novel's central technique.

### What it must not become
A voice that processes everything equally is a voice with no dynamic range. Caleb's analytical register must have gradations. Under normal pressure: long constructions, full analysis, complete sentences. Under maximum pressure (McCarthy principle): the analysis falls away, the sentences become spare, declarative, short. The body's data takes priority over the mind's.

The key moments where the McCarthy principle applies:
- The null reference discovery: analysis should arrive late, after the body has already responded
- The wipe discovery at 4:14am: the technical language of partition tables and low-level diagnostics is correct — but it should feel like a man reading a medical diagnosis in his own chart
- The moment his daughter lets go of his hand: the blue sweater, the yellow cuff, the weight — this is already correct. Don't add analysis here. Let it be what it is.

### Sentence rhythm rules
- Default rhythm: subject → verb → precise qualifier → observation about what the observation means
- Under pressure: subject → verb. Full stop.
- The transition between these rhythms marks the emotional peak of a scene. Use it deliberately.

### What Caleb never says
- How he feels ("I felt afraid," "I was sad")
- What something means to him directly ("This mattered because")
- Evaluative statements about other people's emotional states ("She was hurt")

### What Caleb does instead
- Notes what changed in his perception ("I noticed the building's hum for the first time")
- Notes what his body did before he decided to do it ("My hand moved to save the file before my mind had finished the argument")
- Reads other people as data patterns ("Her eyes had the quality they get after a long Thursday when she has not managed them")
- Acknowledges gaps in his own processing ("I do not know what she meant by it. I do not know if she meant anything")

## Chapter-by-Chapter Evaluation

### Ch. 1 — "The Wrong Sound"
**What works**: The keycard opening is strong. The structure of the chapter — present day on the plaza, then flashback to the discovery, then return to the plaza — is correct. The final line ("Behind me, the building does not change") is earned.

**Priority revision target**: The discovery scene at 11pm. This is the most important moment in the novel's first act, and it is compressed to summary. We need to be IN that office — the quality of the light at 30% standby, the cold coffee two hours gone, the specific sound of the building at eleven on a Tuesday. When the null reference appears, the reader should feel the moment before Caleb names it. Currently we are told what he found. We need to experience the finding.

**Specific scene to expand**: Lines 66–114 (the discovery). Add physical grounding before the first anomaly. Give the moment of recognition — the second run of the check — its proper weight. The mind is the last thing to catch up.

### Ch. 2 — "The Severance Terms"
**What works**: The voicemail analysis is the novel's best early example of Caleb reading a communication for its inputs. Sera's entrance and the scene where they sit together is the best dialogue work in the first three chapters.

**Priority revision target**: The apartment itself. We spend significant time here in this chapter and throughout the novel, but the space is thin. Give it the Ishiguro treatment — not description for its own sake, but the specific details that tell us who lives here and what they have made of the nine-floor west-facing apartment at Threshold East.

**Secondary target**: The four bounce notices (messaging client sequence). Currently they arrive at the same emotional pace. The third one — his own secondary account — should have a different quality. His own address, suspended. That is a different kind of information.

### Ch. 3 — "The Table"
**What works**: The all-night-at-the-table structure is the novel's most sustained scene. The physical work — thermal printouts, blue ink, the bad coffee, the calculation method — is the most grounded writing in the first three chapters. The dawn ending is the first genuinely sensory moment in the novel.

**Priority revision target**: The line of warm light under Sera's door. Caleb looks at it longer than he means to. That moment contains most of the chapter's emotional charge and it is crossed in a sentence. The reader needs a beat here — not interiority-speak, but the specific quality of Caleb standing with a cold coffee looking at a line of light under a door, and then going back to the chart. The duration of that pause tells us everything.

**Secondary target**: Entry 31 (the childcare subsidy, Zone 3, forty percent). This is the moment where the pattern becomes human. The sentence "Forty percent. For a family in Zone 3 with children" is doing good work, but it needs the preceding calculation to land harder. Let the arithmetic arrive at that number slowly enough that the reader feels it land.

### Ch. 4 — "Internal Report"
**What works**: The three-question structure of the Sone interview, with dialogue added, is correct. The wipe discovery logic (partition table, low-level diagnostic) is architecturally sound. The fifteen-year-ago callback is essential.

**Priority revision target**: The moment of the wipe discovery. Caleb wakes at 4:14am. "The file structure was gone." This is the correct sentence. But the moment before that sentence — the quality of 4am waking without cause, the specific character of a person who cannot place why they are awake and goes to the terminal anyway — is where the chapter's dramatic peak lives. Give that moment twenty lines instead of five.

**Secondary target**: The fifteen-year flashback (the closed ticket). This is told entirely in summary. It is important enough to deserve at least one scene image — Caleb at twenty-nine in the conference room with the whiteboard, the specific weight of being a junior engineer who has found something and is being told it is nothing, the way the ticket closing produced a feeling he named practicality. One concrete image here would transform the passage.

### Ch. 5 — "Compliant Citizens"
**What works**: The compliance assessment analysis (reading the 340-word document for what its language is doing) is excellent. Theo's statement analysis is the chapter's best passage. The kiosk sequence with the yellow bar and the attendant who doesn't see it is well-constructed. The chapter's final three paragraphs are the novel's best meditation on systemic power.

**Priority revision target**: "I can hear myself breathing. This is not something I usually hear." This is the closest the novel comes to direct emotional disclosure, and it deserves to be a fully rendered moment rather than a two-sentence note. The silence in that apartment — Caleb sitting at the desk, the building's hum around him, the city going about its provisioned routines — should be a scene, not a summary of a scene. Three paragraphs of the hum, the particular quality of breathing you notice when you can hear it, the way the office has stopped belonging to him. Then the final meditation on the system.

**Secondary target**: The neighbor evasion sequence. It works as analysis. It would be stronger if one of the neighbors had a specific identifying detail — something Caleb has observed for nine years — that makes the not-seeing more particular. Not a name. A detail.

### Ch. 6 — "The Closed Channels"
**What works**: The chart description as a method of analysis (three colors, three domains) is strong. The information-topology paragraph is the chapter's best line ("A topology does not feel like suppression from inside it. It feels like silence"). The Sera scene at the end — particularly "The eyes have that quality they get after a long Thursday" — is the chapter's emotional peak.

**Priority revision target**: The radio station revelation. Caleb locates a community radio station in a pre-integration directory, finds the frequency, discovers it is now a PACEM public information service. This should hit harder than it does. It is not just a closed channel — it is a name replaced with an institutional voice, a space that once hosted something outside the system absorbed entirely into the system. Let that specific loss register.

**Secondary target**: The forum testing sequence (3h18m, 2h4m, 47m). These three failures currently arrive at the same emotional pace. The contracting time should be felt as something mechanical and indifferent — the system is learning from his attempts, and the efficiency of that learning should feel like being watched. The third submission (47 minutes) should have a different quality than the first.

### Ch. 7 — "Wren"

Bread made from grain she has been growing since spring. What that tastes like to someone who has been eating managed allocation for twenty years.

### Ch. 8 — "Separation"
**What works**: The daughter goodbye is the novel's emotional peak and is correctly rendered. The blue sweater, the yellow cuff, the crescent scar, the weight of the hand — the McCarthy principle is operating here. Sera's clinical speech is the best sustained dialogue work in the novel. The apartment inventory at the end ("The coffee cup that Sera used is in the dish rack, rinsed, upside down, its shadow on the steel") is exactly right Ishiguro.

**Revision priority (low — this chapter is largely correct)**: The chapter opening (the drawing, the coffee). It moves at the same pace as every other chapter opening. The drawing on the refrigerator — which will become the chapter's emotional anchor — is noted and set aside before the coffee is even poured. Let Caleb stand with it for a moment longer at the opening. He doesn't understand it. He will think about it throughout the chapter. Establish that weight at the first sighting.

**What to protect**: The daughter goodbye scene is working. Do not add to it. The Ishiguro principle is operating correctly — the specificity of the yellow cuff detail and the weight of the hand carries what cannot be stated directly. The apartment inventory ending is correct. Do not revise these.

### Ch. 9 — "The Weight of One"
**What works**: The bone-conduction notification opening is the novel's most physically specific single passage — a voice in the structure of the skull. The compliance officer's reading of the record in order (not by severity, but in order) is excellent Miller territory. "Thank you. I do not know why I said it." is the chapter's best line.

**Priority revision target**: The nine-day deadline analysis section. This is the chapter's weakest passage — a compliance document that Caleb is reading aloud to himself. The operational analysis of sync vs. no-sync is accurate and necessary, but it is rendered in the same bureaucratic register as the compliance officer's speech. It should read as a man calculating his own extinction options at a kitchen table alone in December, not as a technical assessment. The information is the same; the human texture of the calculation is absent.

**Secondary target**: The transit hub passage after the compliance office. The M-series drone hardware specification, the bandwidth assessment — this is Caleb doing what he always does. But the moment when he realizes he is not important enough to be noticed yet — "You are not that interesting, until you are" — should register in his body, not only in his analysis. He is standing in a transit hub full of people going about their morning routes, and he is alone in a way that none of them are. Let that land for two sentences before the drone specification resumes.

## What This Novel Is Doing Right

This assessment focuses on deficiencies because that is the purpose of a style guide. The following strengths should be preserved and not corrected away:

1. **The analytical voice is consistent and interesting.** Caleb reads the world as data. This is working. The solution is not to make him warmer or more expressive — it is to make his analytical observations more physically grounded.

2. **Technical accuracy creates credibility.** The null reference architecture, the CDL compliance structure, the NI authentication degradation ladder — this detail is doing essential work. Do not simplify it.

3. **The system is rendered without editorial judgment.** The compliance officer reading the record in order. The kiosk attendant who develops a thorough interest in his terminal. The model learning from Caleb's submissions. These are correct — the system is not evil, it is optimized, and that optimization is rendered without commentary. This is the novel's central moral architecture. Protect it.

4. **The supporting cast is economical.** Sone, Wren, Sera, Theo — each one is precisely characterized without backstory inflation. Their scenes are as long as they need to be. This is correct.

5. **The motif management is working.** The chart, the folded page, the drawing on the refrigerator — these objects accumulate weight across chapters. The careful tracking of what is on the desk, what is on the shelf, what has been set down and not picked up — this is Ishiguro technique working correctly. Continue it.
